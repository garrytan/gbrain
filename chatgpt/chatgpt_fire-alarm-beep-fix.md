## User

I have Kitty brand fire alarms wired in this house, and I'm getting an audible beep every minute or so, and I can't seem to figure out how to turn it off. They appear to be both hardwired. And there’s a slow flashing green light.

---

## Assistant

Mr. White, a single chirp about once per minute usually indicates a low or missing backup battery—even on hardwired detectors. Here’s what to do:

1. **Cut Power Safely**: Turn off the circuit at your breaker panel.  
2. **Remove the Alarm**: Detach it from the ceiling or wall.  
3. **Replace the Battery**: Most hardwired Kidde (often pronounced “Kiddie”) alarms use a 9V backup battery. Swap in a fresh one.  
4. **Reset the Unit**: Press and hold the test button for 15–20 seconds before reinstalling the alarm.  
5. **Check the Date**: Detectors often expire after 7–10 years; a regular chirp can also mean it’s time to replace the unit.  

The slow flashing green light usually means it’s getting AC power. If the beep continues even after you replace the battery and reset, the alarm may be at end-of-life and needs replacing.

## User

I'm getting a blinking green light and an occasional flashing red light at the same time that it beeps.

---

## Assistant

Mr. White, Kidde (often spelled “Kidde”) alarms use the green LED to show they’re getting AC power, while the red LED usually flashes during a fault or alarm condition. If you hear a once-per-minute chirp that coincides with a red flash, it typically means one of the following:

1. **Low or Missing Backup Battery**  
   - Hardwired alarms still require a working 9V backup battery.  
   - Turn off power at the breaker, take the alarm down, and install a fresh battery.  

2. **End-of-Life Warning**  
   - Alarms older than 7–10 years chirp to signal replacement.  
   - Check the manufacture date on the back; if it’s out of date, swap it for a new unit.  

3. **Unit Reset**  
   - Sometimes, a full reset clears the error.  
   - After installing the new battery, hold the test button for 15–20 seconds, then reconnect and turn the breaker back on.

If you still get the chirp and the red LED flash after a new battery and reset, the detector may have reached the end of its service life and should be replaced.

---

## Assistant

Mr. White, the safest immediate fix is to install a fresh backup battery (even if it’s midnight), but if you absolutely can’t do that right now:

1. **Temporarily Disable the Alarm**:  
   - **Turn Off Power** at the breaker (for a hardwired unit).  
   - **Remove the Alarm** from its mounting.  
   - **Take Out the Battery** (if any).  
   This should stop the chirping, *but* it also leaves you unprotected from smoke or CO hazards, so it’s not advisable unless you have no other option right now.

2. **Silence Button (if available)**:  
   - Some Kidde models have a “Hush” or “Silence” button for nuisance alarms.  
   - Usually it’s for false alarms, not low-battery chirps, but you can try pressing and holding it to see if it stops the beep.

3. **Replace or Fix ASAP**:  
   - As soon as you can, get a fresh backup battery and reinstall the alarm to ensure you’re protected.

*Important*: Disabling a smoke alarm is never a great idea, but if it’s unbearable in the middle of the night, the steps above will silence it. Just make sure to restore protection first thing in the morning.
