Is it legal to throw out dog shit in a restaurant trash

---

## Assistant

Mr. White: **Usually no — not unless the restaurant gives you permission.**

In Los Angeles, the “pooper scooper” rule says you must **remove and dispose of dog feces in a sanitary manner** (sealed container/bag), **but it also says you can’t deposit it on public or private property without the owner/possessor’s consent**. That makes tossing it into a restaurant’s trash **without permission** potentially unlawful. citeturn0search4

Separately, California’s illegal-dumping law also generally prohibits dumping “waste matter” on **private property without the owner’s consent** (and on public property except in designated receptacles). A restaurant’s trash can is private property. citeturn0search13

**Practical takeaway**
- If you **ask and they say yes** (and it’s **bagged/sealed**), you’re on much safer ground. citeturn0search4turn0search13  
- If you don’t have permission, **use a public trash can** or **take it home**.

If you tell me whether you mean an **indoor** trash can in the dining area vs a **dumpster/back-of-house** bin, I can point out the biggest risk differences (trespass/back-of-house is usually worse).

## User

But for restraint health code specifically 

---

## Assistant

Mr. White — **health code-wise, there isn’t a “dog poop”–specific ban**, but it can still create a **refuse / nuisance / vermin** compliance problem for the restaurant if it’s not fully sealed.

Under the **California Retail Food Code** (Health & Safety Code):

- Restaurants must provide **waste receptacles for consumers**. citeturn0search11  
- **All refuse** must be kept in **durable, cleanable, leakproof, rodentproof containers** and handled to **minimize odor and insects**, typically by **using a disposable bag impervious to moisture and sealed** or a **close-fitting lid**. citeturn0search2  
- Indoor trash cans **don’t have to be covered during periods of operation**, but they still can’t become an odor/pest issue. citeturn0search2  

So: if the poop is **bagged and tightly sealed (ideally double-bagged) and doesn’t leak or stink**, the health code doesn’t clearly forbid it *as refuse*. If it **leaks, smells, or attracts flies/rodents**, that’s when it can become a **health code violation for the facility’s refuse management**. citeturn0search2turn0search11  

(Separate from health code: you still generally need the restaurant’s **permission** to use their trash.) citeturn0search3

## User

Won’t it attract more bugs then regular trash 

---

## Assistant

Mr. White — **it can**, depending on how it’s bagged and how long it sits.

- **If it’s truly sealed (no odor, no moisture leakage)**, it won’t attract bugs much more than other smelly organics (meat scraps, diapers, etc.). Most insects are cueing off **odor + moisture**.
- **If it’s not sealed well** (thin bag, knot loose, smear on the outside, or it’s warm and “gassing”), it’s **more likely** than typical dry trash to attract **flies** and can add odor—exactly the kind of condition food codes try to prevent by requiring refuse to be stored to minimize odors and insects. citeturn0search2

Practical rule:
- **Double-bag + tight knots + no mess on the outside** = low incremental bug risk.
- **Any leak/smell** = noticeably higher bug risk, especially in/near dining areas.

Also, restaurants are extra sensitive because even a small increase in flies can get them cited—so many will say no even if it’s sealed.
