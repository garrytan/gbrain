Mr. White — the reason this *can* be legal is that the law treats **(a) detaining a person** very differently from **(b) refusing to release property (your car) until a charge is paid**.

## 1) “They can’t keep me here” vs “they can’t keep my car”
### Detaining *you* (person)
In California, **false imprisonment** is the unlawful restraint/detention of a person. citeturn1search0  
Most stores/garages therefore avoid physically detaining people unless a specific privilege applies.

California’s “shopkeeper’s privilege” lets merchants **briefly detain a person** only under specific conditions (probable cause + reasonable manner + reasonable time) to investigate shoplifting. citeturn0search2  
That’s why you hear “don’t chase the TV thief” — it’s about *liability*, not that detention is always illegal.

### Holding *the vehicle* (property)
A parking operator is usually relying on **contract terms** (“you owe $X to exit”) and sometimes a **possessory lien concept** (if someone is lawfully in possession of property while providing safekeeping/storage services, certain liens exist in CA law). citeturn1search1turn1search6turn1search2

That doesn’t mean they can do *anything* they want — but it does explain why a gate being closed isn’t automatically treated like kidnapping.

## 2) Are they legally obligated to open the gate and “bill you later”?
**No.** There’s generally **no rule** that forces a private parking operator to extend credit or invoice later. If their posted terms say “pay to exit,” they can try to enforce that.

But they still have legal limits:
- They **can’t use unreasonable force or threats** against you.
- If the only exit is gated and they keep it closed in a way that **effectively restrains people**, that can drift toward **false imprisonment** territory (fact-specific), especially if someone is stuck and trying to leave. citeturn1search0
