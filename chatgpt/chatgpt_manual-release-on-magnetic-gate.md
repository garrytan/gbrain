## Assistant

Short version, Mr. White: there isn’t a universal “little red lever” on Magnetic gates – the release is **inside the housing**, and the exact setup depends on the model. But they all work on the same ideas:

---

### Typical Magnetic (MHTM / Parking / Access / Pro) barrier

1. **Access the inside of the cabinet**
   - There’s usually a **keyed lock in the top cover or side door**.
   - Use the gate key to unlock and lift the **top cover** or open the **side door**. citeturn4search4  

2. **Service / manual movement from the controller**
   - Inside you’ll find the **MGC/MGC-Pro control unit** with a small **“Service” switch** and arrow buttons:
     - Flip the **Service switch** to *Service/On* (LED changes color).
     - Use the **middle left/right buttons** on the controller to manually open/close the barrier. citeturn4search3turn4search12  
   - This is the “manual” release most sites are meant to use during faults or maintenance.

3. **If the arm is mechanically jammed at the top or bottom (dead point)**
   - On MHTM MicroDrive barriers, behind the door there’s a **lever with two round “tool bores”**:
     - **Upper bore** = push with a screwdriver/rod to free it for **opening**.
     - **Lower bore** = push to free it for **closing**. citeturn3view0  
   - That’s how you mechanically “break it loose” in a power failure if it won’t move by hand.

---

### Big caveats

- There is **no external emergency pull-cord or handle** on most Magnetic parking gates – you have to:
  - Have the **correct key**,  
  - Open the housing,  
  - Use the **Service switch/buttons** and, if needed, the **tool bores** on the lever.
- For some older MAGSTOP/MIB units the layout is different (rotary switch and different mechanics), so the **exact position** of everything varies by model. citeturn1search4turn4search17  

---

### What you should do *right now*

- If you’re **staff/authorized**:
  1. Unlock the cabinet (top or side).
  2. Find the **controller board** and set the **Service switch** to Service.
  3. Use the **open/close buttons** to move the arm.
  4. Only if it’s stuck at full up/down, locate the **lever with two holes** and briefly push the appropriate hole with a tool to get it off the dead point—then move it with the controller or gently by hand.

- If you’re **just a parker stuck at the gate**:
  - Don’t try to force the arm or pry anything open.
  - Use the **help/intercom** or call the **garage/lot operator** – only they should be opening the cabinet and using the manual release.

If you’re actually stuck in a lot/garage overnight with no staff around, the priority is **your safety**, not the gate’s feelings.

Here’s what to do, Mr. White:

---

### 1. Check if you’re in *real* danger

- **Enclosed garage?**  
  - Turn the engine **off** unless you’re actively moving.  
  - Don’t idle for heat/AC in an enclosed space – carbon monoxide is the real killer.
- **Medical issues, extreme cold/heat, feeling unwell, panicked, or unsafe (people around, etc.)?**  
  → Skip everything else and **call 911 immediately**. Say clearly:  
  > “I am trapped in a parking garage/lot and cannot get out. There is no staff on site.”

---

### 2. Try all *legit* exits (on foot first)

Leave the car parked safely and go exploring **on foot**:

- Look for **pedestrian exits**:
  - Stairwells
  - Doors labeled **EXIT** / **STAIRS** / **EMERGENCY EXIT**
  - Walk-through gates next to the vehicle gate  
- Most fire doors in the US **open freely from the inside** even if they’re alarmed.  
  - If it’s marked as an **emergency exit**, assume **you’re allowed to push it in an emergency**.  
  - Worst case: an alarm sounds and security/property management gets alerted – which is exactly what you want if you’re trapped.

If you can walk out but can’t drive out, do that. You can sort your car out in the morning.

---

### 3. Hunt for contact info & emergency devices

Walk the garage/lot and check:

- **Pay station / ticket machine** – often has:
  - A **HELP** or **INTERCOM** button.
  - A posted phone number for **24/7 assistance**, security, or towing.
- **Signage at entrance/exits**:
  - Property management number  
  - Garage operator (Impark, LAZ, ABM, etc.)
- **Security office** or concierge if it’s under a building/complex.

If you find a number, call it and say plainly:
> “I am physically trapped in your parking facility and cannot exit. There is no staff here.”

If they’re unresponsive and you’re stuck for hours, that becomes a **safety** issue, not just an inconvenience.

---

### 4. Use your phone wisely

- **Battery low?**
  - Turn brightness down.
  - Close apps, turn off background data/Wi-Fi, but keep **cell service** on.
  - Save enough battery to be able to call 911 if you start feeling unwell or more unsafe.
- Share your **location** with someone you trust if possible and text:  
  > “I’m stuck in a parking garage at [location]. If you don’t hear from me by [time], please check on me or call police.”

---

### 5. When to involve the police / fire department

You should **absolutely** call 911 (or the local non-emergency line if it’s calm, safe, and you’re just stuck) when:

- You’ve tried:
  - All obvious **pedestrian exits**,
  - The **help/intercom** button (if any),
  - Any posted **24/7 numbers**,  
  and **nobody** responds, **and**…
- You:
  - Cannot leave the property on foot, **or**
  - Are in an enclosed structure with any risk of CO buildup, **or**
  - Feel at risk because of other people, health, or conditions.

Be factual and calm:
> “I’m locked inside a parking [garage/lot] at [address or landmark]. All exits appear locked and there’s no staff. I need help getting out safely.”

They’d much rather open a gate than respond to a medical emergency later.

---

### 6. Do *not* do this unless it’s a life-or-death emergency

- Don’t try to **ram the gate**, cut chains, or dismantle equipment.  
- Don’t climb high fences/walls where a fall could seriously injure you.

If it ever escalates to **imminent danger** (smoke, fire, someone violent, severe medical issue), your **personal safety trumps property damage**, but in that situation:

- Call **911 first**.
- Follow **their instructions** about how/where to exit.

---

### 7. If you’re simply “inconvenienced”

If it’s an open-air lot, you’re safe, and you’ve confirmed:

- No pedestrian exit works,
- No one answers any numbers or intercoms,
- Police say it’s not urgent and suggest waiting,

Then your best move may be:

- Stay in the car with engine **off** (or only on in short bursts if it’s open air).
- Lock the doors.
- Try to sleep/rest and deal with it first thing in the morning.

## Assistant

Because that one dumb decision can snowball into **injury + criminal charge + a very expensive bill**, Mr. White.

You *physically* could floor it and snap the arm. But here’s what you’re really signing up for:

---

### 1. You can absolutely get **charged** for it

In California, deliberately breaking the gate isn’t “oops, my bad” – it’s **criminal property damage** (vandalism).  
- Over a certain dollar amount (which a commercial gate can easily exceed), it can be charged as a **misdemeanor or even a felony**.  
- Cameras are almost always on those lanes. License plate, driver’s face, time stamp – all recorded.  

You don’t want to be the guy explaining a vandalism charge because you were impatient at a parking gate.

---

### 2. Your **insurance** might not cover you

If it’s clearly intentional:

- The operator/property owner will go after **you** (or your plate) for:
  - The gate repair/replacement  
  - Any associated costs (tech call, after-hours, damage to foundations, etc.)  
- Your insurer can treat intentional damage as **not covered**, or raise your rates into the stratosphere.

Think several **thousand** dollars for a decent barrier, not “a couple hundred for plastic.”

---

### 3. You can absolutely **hurt yourself**

People underestimate this:

- Barrier arms are often **reinforced** and attached to a **solid steel housing** bolted into concrete.
- Hit it wrong and:
  - The arm can smash your **windshield** or **A-pillar**.  
  - Airbags deploy straight into your face and chest.  
  - You can lose control and hit a wall, column, or another car.

This is how people end up with **facial fractures, whiplash, and totaled cars** over something that wasn’t life-threatening.

---

### 4. It can get upgraded from “annoying” to **hit-and-run–type problems**

If you smash the gate and just leave:

- Property owner can report it as **leaving after causing damage**, provide video, and push it as hard as they want.
- If anyone is around and gets clipped or hurt in the chaos, now you’re flirting with a **much more serious case**.

---

### 5. Only one exception: **real emergency**

The only time “drive through the gate” is remotely justifiable is **genuine, immediate danger**:

- Fire, active threat, medical emergency where waiting is dangerous, etc.

Even then, the correct move is:

1. **Call 911.**
2. Say exactly what’s happening and follow their instructions.
3. If they tell you to break through to save a life, that’s a very different situation from “I don’t feel like sleeping in my car.”

---

### Bottom line

You *can* smash a gate.  
You just **shouldn’t**, unless it’s literally **life > property** and emergency services are already in the loop.
