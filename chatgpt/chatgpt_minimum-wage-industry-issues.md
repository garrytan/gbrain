## Assistant

Raising the minimum wage for just one industry—like fast food or hotels—creates several key problems:

1. **Distorted labor market**: Workers in other low-wage sectors (e.g., retail or janitorial services) may shift to the higher-paying industry, creating labor shortages in the others and wage pressure without productivity gains.

2. **Unfair competition**: Businesses in the targeted industry bear higher labor costs than comparable businesses, potentially leading to closures, price hikes, or job cuts if they can’t pass on costs.

3. **Encourages automation**: Companies in the singled-out industry may accelerate automation (e.g., kiosks in fast food) to reduce reliance on more expensive labor.

4. **Legal and political backlash**: It can invite lawsuits or lobbying by other industries demanding the same wage hikes, complicating labor policy and regulatory enforcement.

5. **Unintended regressivity**: If prices rise in industries heavily used by lower-income people (e.g., fast food), it can disproportionately hurt the very people the policy is meant to help.

A uniform policy across all industries avoids these distortions.

## Assistant

Mr. White, targeting a higher wage floor at only fast-food or hospitality firms creates several distortions:

1. **Cost-driven substitution**  
   When fast-food outlets alone face higher labor costs, they invest disproportionately in automation (kiosks, order apps) or cut hours to maintain margins. Meanwhile other low-wage sectors can keep staffing levels, shifting employment and investment away from the intended beneficiaries.

2. **Competitive imbalance**  
   Hotels or chains within the “covered” industry see their unit labor costs rise relative to full-service restaurants, retail or home-care employers. That divergence encourages businesses and workers to migrate to the lower-cost sectors, undermining the policy’s distributional goals.

3. **Reclassification incentives**  
   Employers respond by reclassifying workers as contractors, outsourcing roles to third-party vendors or fragmenting job duties to avoid the mandate—all of which reduces job quality and erodes tax revenues.

4. **Spillover and equity concerns**  
   Benefits accrue only to workers in the regulated firms, while adjacent industries (e.g. food trucks, small motels) remain exempt. That narrow targeting fails to address broader low-wage labor market pressures and can exacerbate inequality among similarly skilled workers.

In short, industry-specific wage hikes lead to relocation of jobs, investment and workforce into exempt sectors, distort competition and incentivize avoidance—making a broad, economy-wide minimum wage adjustment more effective for raising living standards.
