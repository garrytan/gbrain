Stopping around 10–12% BF ensures you’re in the ideal range to build muscle efficiently【6†L112-L117】 (more on why below) without getting **“too skinny”** for your frame.

In practice, even 2–4 weeks at maintenance can help you **“reset”** hunger and energy levels. ## Managing Fat Distribution Concerns

You noted uneven fat distribution (estimated ~22% BF on legs vs. ~14% on abs and ~12% on arms). **Key point: fat distribution is largely genetic and cannot be changed by diet or exercise selection**【13†L139-L148】【13†L149-L154】. In other words, where your body prefers to store fat (and in what order it burns it off) is pre-determined – some people have stubborn belly fat, others hold more in their thighs. In your case, it appears you have a **“pear-shaped”** pattern (more lower body fat storage, relatively leaner upper body). Research confirms that many individuals (especially women, but men too in some cases) lose fat in the upper body first and the lower body last【10†L83-L90】. Practically, this means as you cut, you’ll likely see your waist, chest, and arm definition improve **before** your legs get really lean. You might reach your target body fat % yet still see a bit of softness in the legs – that’s normal given your distribution.

**What you can do:**  
- **Get Leaner Overall:** Ultimately, reducing your total body fat further will lean out every region, including your legs. Stubborn areas may only lean out when you get to the **tail end of a cut** (for many men, lower abs clear up only below ~10% BF; for you, legs might require getting to very lean levels). Menno Henselmans notes that for these stubborn regions you don’t need a special approach – *“it’s the exact same process… it just takes longer”*【13†L149-L154】. So if leg leanness is a top priority, you might choose to diet down a bit more aggressively (e.g. aim for ~8–10% BF in some cycles). Just be cautious: going too lean can risk some muscle loss and isn’t maintainable year-round. It may be something you do briefly (e.g. for a photoshoot) then return to a more sustainable ~10–12%. 
- **Prioritize Muscle in Legs:** Building muscle in your legs will improve their appearance at any given body fat. More muscle will push out against the skin, giving a tighter look, and when you do cut, muscular legs will show shape (quads sweep, hamstring line) even if a thin layer of fat remains. Continue to train your lower body hard during both bulking and cutting – it will not make fat go away from legs directly, but it will ensure that when the fat does come off, your legs look toned rather than just “smaller.” *(Bonus: A well-developed lower body also helps fill out pants and creates a balanced physique for modeling.)*  
- **Accept Some Imbalance:** It’s worth noting that minor fat distribution quirks are usually not very noticeable to others when you’re overall lean. If your abs and upper body are sharp at ~12% BF, your legs might still hold a bit more fat, but unless you’re in bodybuilding contest shape, that’s perfectly fine. Many fitness models, for example, maintain around 10% BF which yields visible abs and decent definition, even if legs aren’t paper-thin. So, don’t chase perfectly even fat % across all sites – instead focus on the **total package**. As long as your waist is slim and muscles are well-defined, slightly softer legs won’t detract from your look. Over multiple cycles, you might find your legs do get relatively leaner once you’ve added muscle and cut down several times (some anecdotal reports suggest previously stubborn areas *may* improve after repeated cycling, possibly due to improved local muscle tone or blood flow, but results vary).

**Bottom line:** You can’t spot-reduce leg fat, but by *overall fat loss* and *time* (patience through those last pounds of a cut) you will eventually lean out the legs. Use strategic **cardio** if needed to help create the deficit during cutting phases – it can aid in burning extra calories, but it won’t specifically target leg fat. High-rep leg exercises or “toning” workouts won’t directly burn off thigh fat either (spot reduction is largely a myth)【10†L79-L87】【10†L83-L90】, so keep your leg training geared toward strength/hypertrophy. Trust that **consistent diet and training** will handle the distribution issue as you get leaner.

## Additional Tips and Summary

- **Training Alignment:** Adjust your training volume and intensity to match phases. During bulks, you can push volume (recoverability is higher with a surplus); during cuts, focus on maintaining strength with slightly lower volume to avoid overtraining. Mike Israetel often emphasizes matching training to diet – hard training drives muscle gain during bulk, while in a cut the goal is mainly muscle retention, so avoid excessive junk volume that could impede recovery when calories are low.

- **Nutrition Details:** Continue to prioritize protein (1g per pound of body weight is a safe target, more in a cut is fine) and nutrient timing around workouts. Menno Henselmans highlights the importance of a high protein diet with strength training when cutting to maximize fat loss and minimize muscle loss【11†L79-L87】【11†L115-L123】. Your planned rates of loss/gain are reasonable enough that you likely won’t need drastic macro swings – small caloric deficits/surpluses will do. For example, a ~0.75%/week loss for you might be a ~750 kcal/day deficit to start (adjust as weight drops), and a 0.5%/week gain might be a ~250–300 kcal/day surplus (adjust as weight increases).

- **Monitor Body Composition:** Since you have specific fat distribution concerns, consider tracking **skinfold measurements** (e.g., thigh vs. abdomen) or circumference (waist, thigh) through cycles. As Menno suggests, these can be more practical than relying on absolute BF% readings, which are often inaccurate【8†L104-L112】【8†L112-L119】. You want to see your waist stay relatively controlled during bulks and your thigh measurement gradually shrink with cuts (knowing it might lag behind waist changes). The trend matters more than any one measurement. 

- **Health and Consistency:** Incorporate the **maintenance phases** as needed for both physiological and psychological relief. RP’s approach of including maintenance every few months is there to **“preserve muscle and stay consistent”** long-term【5†L115-L119】. Think of these as consolidation periods – they allow you to solidify gains, enjoy a normal diet intake, and get ready for the next push. Consistency over many months will always beat any “rapid” bulk or cut. You’re essentially recomposing your physique in a sustainable way, which is exactly what Menno, Mike, and other evidence-based coaches preach.

- **Psychological Aspect:** Since you’re aiming for aesthetics, don’t underestimate the mental challenge of seeing your body change. When bulking, you might see a bit of fluff coming on and need to remind yourself it’s part of the plan (as long as you’re within the set BF range). When cutting, you might feel scrawnier especially at 6’4” – but remember, it’s temporary and reveals the muscle you’ve built. Having planned **bookends** (e.g., knowing your cut will last 6 weeks, or your bulk until you hit X weight) can help you stay on track without constantly second-guessing (“Should I stop now?”). Of course, listen to your body – if you feel you've overshot (too chubby in bulk or too flat in cut), you can adjust phase length a bit.

In summary, **cycle between lean bulking and cutting** in a structured manner: bulk in the ~10–15% body fat zone for 2–4 months at a time, insert maintenance or mini-cuts to keep things on track, and cut down to ~10% when needed to stay in peak aesthetic condition【16†L1-L4】. Over time, this will let you gradually increase your lean muscle mass while keeping you photoshoot-ready or close to it year-round. You have plenty of potential for added muscle (given your FFMI and height), so strategically spend a good portion of the year in mild surplus – just never so long that you accumulate excess fat. By following these principles from RP and Menno Henselmans, you’ll optimize your **mass-to-fat gain ratio** and ensure each bulk/cut cycle enhances your physique’s look. Stick to the plan, be patient, and you’ll develop a taller, bigger, and leaner body that fits both the **aesthetic model** criteria and healthy, evidence-based fitness standards.
