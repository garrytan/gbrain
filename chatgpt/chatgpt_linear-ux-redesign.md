Here’s a concise overview of **Linear UX / linear redesign in UX** and how it’s approached in practice:

### 📌 What “Linear UX” Means
**Linear UX** (also called *linear experience design*) refers to structuring a user’s interaction with a product so it moves in a clear, sequential way from **start to finish**, minimizing friction and complexity. The goal is to help the user complete a task or reach a goal with as few cognitive hurdles as possible — essentially a **smooth, guided journey**. citeturn0search1turn0search4

Key characteristics:
- A **defined beginning, middle, and end** in the user flow. citeturn0search4  
- Removal of unnecessary steps and choices to **streamline progression**. citeturn0search1  
- Focus on user objectives, not product features for their own sake. citeturn0search1  

### 🎯 Why It Matters
The core idea is that users should **never feel lost or stalled** — the experience should feel natural and predictable, helping them complete what they set out to do. citeturn0search4

### 🛠 How It’s Applied (Examples & Best Practices)
Some real-world takeaways from professional redesigns and trends:

**1. Remove visual and UX noise:**  
Designs like the recent *Linear app* redesign focus on reducing clutter in sidebars, navigation, and panels to let the user flow easily from task to task. citeturn0search0

**2. Reduce cognitive load:**  
Linear designs often lean minimalist (clean layouts, clear typography, consistent hierarchy) so the user’s eyes and attention move in a predictable path. citeturn0search2

**3. Support logical reading/interaction direction:**  
Especially in Western products, following a top-to-bottom and left-to-right progression makes interfaces easier to scan and act on. citeturn0search2

**4. Balance simplicity with utility:**  
Too much minimalism can feel boring or undifferentiated; good linear UX maintains clarity *and* strong brand identity. citeturn0search2

### 🧠 UX Design Principles Related to Linear UX
Even if a UX process isn’t strictly linear (most design workflows are iterative), **clarity, simplicity, and usability** are central to good UX overall — they align with linear UX thinking. citeturn0search5

In summary:

- **Linear UX = sequential, goal-oriented user flows** designed to minimize friction. citeturn0search1  
- **Redesigning for linearity** often means simplifying UI, improving hierarchy, and letting users progress logically through tasks. citeturn0search0  
- It’s not exactly a design *trend* but a **UX philosophy** prioritizing task success with minimal fuss. citeturn0search4
