## Assistant

A loud crackling sound from an AC unit usually indicates one of the following issues. Follow this order.

### 1. Turn It Off First
If the crackling is loud or sudden, switch the thermostat to OFF and cut power at the breaker. Electrical arcing is possible.

---

## Common Causes

### 1. Electrical Arcing (Most Urgent)
**Sound:** Sharp, fast crackling or snapping  
**Cause:** Failing contactor, loose wiring, capacitor failure  
**Risk:** Fire hazard

Check:
- Burning smell
- Unit struggling to start
- Breaker tripping

If present, do not restart it. Call an HVAC technician.

---

### 2. Debris in Outdoor Condenser

**Sound:** Constant crackling or rattling  
**Cause:** Leaves, sticks, or plastic hitting the fan blade

Check:
- Look through the top grille
- Inspect side panels for lodged debris

Turn power off before inspecting.

---

### 3. Ice Buildup Melting

**Sound:** Crackling or popping as ice melts  
**Cause:** Frozen evaporator coil

Signs:
- Weak airflow
- Unit running constantly
- Visible frost on refrigerant line

Turn system OFF and let it thaw for several hours. Likely airflow issue or low refrigerant.

---

### 4. Duct Expansion / Contraction
**Sound:** Light crackling or popping when system turns on/off  
**Cause:** Metal ducts expanding with temperature change  
**Risk:** Low. Common in older homes.

---

## Quick Diagnostic Questions
- Is the sound coming from inside or outside?
- Does it happen only when starting?
- Any burning smell?
- Is cooling performance normal?

## Assistant

Continuous “wood-in-a-fire” crackling for weeks, no burning smell, and no breaker trips strongly points to **mechanical contact or debris**, not electrical arcing.

Most likely causes, in order:

---

## 1) Debris Hitting the Outdoor Fan (Very Common)

Dry leaves, twigs, seed pods, or plastic can sit inside the condenser and continuously tick against the spinning fan blade. The sound often resembles crackling or rustling fire.

**What to do**
1. Turn thermostat OFF.  
2. Shut power at the outdoor disconnect or breaker.  
3. Remove the top grille (usually a few screws).  
4. Clear debris carefully.  
5. Reassemble before restoring power.

---

## 2) Failing Fan Motor Bearings
If the sound is constant while running and not just at startup, worn bearings can create a dry crackling or rough rotational noise.

**Signs**
- Slight vibration
- Fan speed fluctuates
- Unit still cools normally

This requires motor replacement. It will not fix itself.

---

## 3) Fan Blade Slightly Striking Shroud
If the unit shifted or screws loosened, the blade may lightly contact the housing. The sound can resemble rapid crackling.

Check for:
- Bent fan blade
- Loose top panel
- Unit not level

---

## 4) Frozen Coil Thawing (Less Likely Since It’s Ongoing)

If it has been happening for weeks continuously, this is less likely unless airflow is severely restricted. You would also notice weak airflow indoors.

---

## What This Is Probably Not
- Electrical arcing (that would be sharper and more aggressive sounding, and risky).
- Compressor failure (that’s more of a grinding or loud hum).

- Does it stop immediately when you turn the system off?

A continuous “wood-in-a-fire” crackling sound for weeks with no burning smell is almost always one of these:

---

## 1) Dry Fan Bearing (Very Common in Wall Units)

Inside a wall AC are two small blower fans (one indoor, one outdoor). When the motor bearings dry out, they make a constant crackling or dry rattling sound.

**Signs**
- Sound continues the entire time the unit runs  
- Stops immediately when you turn it OFF  
- Cooling still works  

This will not fix itself. The motor will eventually fail. In an apartment, this is a **maintenance issue for the landlord**, not you.

---

## 2) Debris Inside the Unit

Wall units collect:
- Dust clumps  
- Insects  
- Small debris  

If something is lightly contacting the blower wheel, it creates a constant crackling/ticking sound.

You can:
- Remove the front plastic cover
- Pull and clean the air filter
- Look for obvious debris

Do **not** disassemble deeper components in a rental.

---

## 3) Plastic Housing Expanding
Some wall units crackle continuously if the internal plastic housing is loose or vibrating. This is harmless but annoying.

You can test this by:
- Pressing gently on the front panel while it’s running  
- If the sound changes, it’s vibration

---

## What This Is Not
- Electrical arcing (that would sound sharper and more aggressive)
- Imminent fire (you would smell it)
- Refrigerant issue (that does not sound like crackling)

## Assistant

Water under a **wall/window AC unit** changes the diagnosis.

This is almost always a **drainage problem**, not a leak in the refrigerant system.

---

## What’s Happening

Window/wall AC units pull humidity from the air. That water collects in a drain pan and normally:

- Drips outside  
- Evaporates off the rear condenser fan  

If water is dripping inside and pooling below it, one of these is occurring:

---

## 1) Clogged Drain Hole (Most Likely)

Dirt, algae, or debris blocks the small rear drain opening. Water backs up and overflows indoors.

**What to check**
- Is the unit slightly tilted backward toward the outside? It should be.
- Look at the back (outside side) for clogged drain holes.
- If accessible, gently clear with a small zip tie or plastic stick.

---

## 2) Unit Not Tilted Backward

Wall/window units must lean slightly outward. If level or tilted inward, water drains into the room.

Even a small shift can cause this.

---

## 3) Frozen Coil Melting

If the earlier crackling was ice forming and now it’s melting, you’ll see significant dripping.

Signs:
- Weak airflow  
- Filter dirty  
- Unit running constantly  

Remove and clean the air filter immediately.

---

## Important: In a Rental (California)

If water is dripping inside:
- This is a landlord maintenance issue.
- Ongoing water intrusion can cause mold and property damage.
- You should notify management promptly in writing.

---

## Immediate Actions

1. Turn the unit OFF for now.
2. Clean the air filter.
3. Check tilt.
4. Check for visible drain blockage (if accessible safely).
