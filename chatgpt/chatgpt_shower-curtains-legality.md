La fitness does not have shower curtains in the showers is this legal

## Assistant

Short answer, Mr. White: **yes, it’s almost certainly legal** for LA Fitness to have showers with no curtains.

Here’s the reality under California / LA rules:

- **Local health-club rules (LA County)** say gyms have to provide *“adequate bathing, dressing, locker and toilet facilities”* for patrons, but they don’t say anything about curtains, doors, or partitions for each shower head. citeturn1search1  
- **LA City building code** sections that deal with shower/locker facilities are about *having* showers available (how many, when they’re required), not about requiring individual stalls or curtains. citeturn1search3  
- **Accessibility rules (ADA)** only clearly demand privacy features (like latches) for *single-user/unisex* bathing rooms. For multi-user, gender-segregated shower rooms, open/communal showers are allowed and still commonly built. citeturn0search12  

Where California *does* require “individual privacy in all toilet, bath, and shower areas” is in **special settings** like licensed residential care and health facilities (nursing homes, certain group homes, etc.), not commercial gyms. citeturn1search0  

Courts and lawyers generally treat **locker rooms as semi-private**: people have a reasonable expectation of privacy against cameras, peeping, or opposite-sex staff wandering through while you’re nude, but not a right to be completely visually shielded from others of the same sex in the locker/shower area. citeturn0search19  

So:

- **Simply not having shower curtains = not illegal** in California for a standard, single-sex or gender-identity-segregated gym locker room.  
- It *might* become a legal issue if, for example:
  - People in a public/common hallway can see directly into the showers;  
  - Opposite-sex staff are routinely walking through while members are naked;  
  - There are cameras or recording devices;  
  - Or there’s targeted harassment or discrimination (which could raise Unruh Civil Rights Act or harassment issues).

If what’s happening at your specific LA Fitness feels beyond just “open showers” (e.g., exposure to staff or the public, or creepy behavior), that’s when it’s worth:

1. Documenting what’s going on (dates, times, witnesses).  
2. Complaining in writing to the gym and asking them to change the setup or their practices.  
3. Talking to a California attorney or the California Civil Rights Department if it’s tied to harassment/discrimination.

But just **“no shower curtains” by itself is, in practice, legal** and pretty common in gyms.
