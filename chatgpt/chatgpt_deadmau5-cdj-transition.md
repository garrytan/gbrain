# Early Live Sets: Ableton and “Pressing Play”  
In his early career, Deadmau5 (Joel Zimmerman) was known for using **Ableton Live** as the backbone of his performances rather than traditional DJ decks. He would arrange his tracks in Ableton and trigger clips during shows, essentially pre-sequencing his sets. Deadmau5 himself admitted that his live shows were largely pre-planned in Ableton – joking that he sometimes just “roll[s] up with a laptop and a MIDI controller and ‘select’ tracks and hit a spacebar”【51†L121-L125】. In a 2006 forum post, he even quipped that an entire club set was done in Ableton, and that he would trigger some loops “so that it looked like I was a lil more ‘active’ up there on the stage”【49†L138-L144】. This candor about “pressing play” became infamous in 2012 when his **“We All Hit Play”** blog post stirred debate about how “live” EDM performances really are【51†L121-L125】. At the time, Deadmau5 proudly distanced himself from the traditional DJ label – noting *he had never actually operated a CDJ* and didn’t consider himself a DJ in the usual sense【4†L125-L133】. His shows were more like electronic concerts with synced visuals, where Ableton ensured everything ran on rails.  

# Transition to CDJs: First Forays into DJing  
Around the mid-2010s, fans began to notice Deadmau5 incorporating **Pioneer CDJs** and a mixer into his live set setup – a significant shift from his laptop-centric gigs. For someone who once claimed he “wasn’t a DJ” and avoided CDJs, this was a notable transition【4†L125-L133】. One early hint came in March 2014, when Deadmau5 stepped in last-minute to replace Avicii as Ultra Music Festival headliner. With little time to prepare an elaborate Ableton show, he likely used the festival’s standard CDJ setup to play his set (even cheekily trolling the crowd by mixing the children’s song “Old MacDonald” into Avicii’s hit *Levels*). By 2015, Deadmau5 was regularly performing more conventional DJ sets at club gigs and afterparties. For example, after headlining New York’s Governors Ball festival in 2015, he played an after-hours techno set at Verboten nightclub where he “went in” with a dark, driving selection【35†L78-L86】. He’d even tweeted beforehand about “rinse[ing] the…night with some techno,” and during the set he couldn’t resist slipping in a bit of Avicii’s “Levels” as a prank【35†L78-L86】【35†L84-L92】. This kind of spontaneous track drop was only possible now that he was mixing live on CDJs, rather than following a fixed Ableton script. It marked Deadmau5 embracing the art of DJing – beatmatching, live mixing, and on-the-fly improvisation – in a way he hadn’t early in his career.  

**Interviews and social media** around this time also captured Deadmau5 acknowledging the change. In April 2016, he live-streamed a two-hour session from his home studio, using a *hybrid rig* of **CDJ-2000s, a touch-screen controller, and Ableton** running together【33†L48-L56】. Dancing Astronaut reported that the stream showed him experimenting with new performance ideas and even making a few mistakes as he adjusted to the more hands-on workflow【33†L48-L56】. This candid session let fans see Joel practicing manual transitions and working through the learning curve of traditional mixing – something he had less need for in the “automated” Ableton days. By mid-2016, Deadmau5 openly talked about his CDJ use. He posted a photo of his setup featuring a pair of **Pioneer CDJ-2000NXS2** players (the then-flagship club decks) and a mixer, indicating he had fully equipped himself with industry-standard DJ gear【27†L1-L5】. Equipboard notes that by 2016 he was often seen performing with the CDJ-2000NXS2s and either a Pioneer DJM-900 Nexus mixer or Native Instruments controllers, depending on the show【45†L1-L9】. In short, around 2014–2016 Deadmau5 “transitioned” into doing many live sets on CDJs, especially for festival and club appearances that didn’t involve his big LED **Cube** stage. 

# Discussing the Change: “Look, Mom – No CDJs!”  
Deadmau5 has addressed this evolution in setup in a few public instances. Notably, in July 2016 he teased a return to his old live style (ditching CDJs) for a special performance with Eric Prydz. He took to Instagram to show off a new rig, captioning it: **“Yup. We’re doin a live show look mom, no CDJs!”**【11†L133-L138】. EDM Sauce called it a “bold move” and emphasized that Deadmau5 was *“turning away from his CDJs”* for that Tomorrowland b2b set【11†L128-L136】. The tongue-in-cheek “no CDJs” remark only makes sense because by 2016 he had been primarily using CDJs; fans understood this was a deliberate throwback to an Ableton-style live setup. In interviews around his **W:/2016Album/** release, Deadmau5 mentioned developing a new stage show (Cube 2.1) and implied he’d be focusing on a more intricate live production again【30†L139-L147】【30†L141-L144】. But outside of such special “live production” sets, he’s been candid that using CDJs has become a comfortable norm for him. For example, when asked about “faking” DJ sets, Deadmau5 explained on a 2016 live stream that at massive festival gigs (like EDC) he often **pre-programs the whole set in advance** to sync with visuals – essentially admitting it’s not mixed live【51†L91-L99】. This is a pragmatic choice: “They wouldn’t want to **** up,” he noted about big production festivals, so everything runs on a tight timeline【51†L91-L99】. However, for many other shows he now just DJs live. He’s no longer shy about calling himself a DJ in those contexts; in fact, he’s done traditional back-to-back DJ sets with the likes of Eric Prydz at events, which would be impractical without using CDJs or similar decks. 

# Why the Switch? Deadmau5’s Reasons  
**So why did Deadmau5 move from an Ableton-only approach to using CDJs?** While he hasn’t published a single manifesto on the subject, his comments and the nature of his gigs offer clear reasons. One big factor is **flexibility.** Using CDJs gave him freedom to improvise and adapt during a set in ways a pre-arranged Ableton set did not. “You can easily change your set on the fly to adapt to different audiences and their energy… This is where CDJs excel when compared to Ableton,” notes one DJ tech article【44†L41-L47】. Deadmau5 learned this first-hand. With Ableton, his shows were largely the same every night on a tour – the same intro, transitions, and ending, all time-coded to visuals. By contrast, once he switched to CDJ-based sets, he could respond to the crowd or his own whims. The 2015 Verboten gig proved he could decide in the moment to inject a nostalgic anthem or adjust the vibe, something *unthinkable* in a rigid Ableton playlist. “Most major festivals, you have to play a pre-recorded set…because they’re such a big deal…they wouldn’t want to mess up,” he told fans, explaining why he used to pre-plan so much【51†L91-L99】. In smaller shows, that pressure is lower, and CDJs let him *just wing it*. Deadmau5 also likely wanted to challenge himself. After years of essentially pressing play (and being criticized for it), learning to beatmatch and perform like a traditional DJ was a new skill to master. On Twitch streams he could be seen practicing transitions and even stumbling through the occasional trainwreck beatmatch【33†L48-L56】, showing he was actively honing that craft. 

Another reason is **practicality**. Hauling a custom Ableton rig (plus backup systems, controllers, interfaces, and a dedicated tech crew) for every show is expensive and complex. Deadmau5 has been frank about the immense cost and effort of his high-tech productions. By transitioning to USB sticks and CDJs for many bookings, he can fly in and use the standard club/festival setup that’s already there, which is far easier. This likely allowed him to say “yes” to more gigs (like one-off club nights or supporting slots) that wouldn’t justify the Cube’s logistics. It’s no coincidence that during the period he embraced CDJs, he also increased the number of casual appearances and afterparties he played. **Workflow** in preparation also shifts: instead of painstakingly arranging a set in Ableton Live with all his tracks broken into stems, he can prepare playlists in rekordbox (Pioneer’s music management software), set cue points, and practice mixing tracks into one another. Deadmau5’s song library is largely his own material, and he’s noted that in DJ mode he typically plays his own catalog (sometimes stretching back to old classics) rather than lots of others’ hits. Dillon Francis once joked about Deadmau5’s DJ sets: “Deadmau5 uses solely his own music in sets…all your music library is on the CDJs now”【28†L29-L34】. That reflects how Joel can load up a USB with dozens of his tracks, some fresh, some old, and mix them in whatever order he fancies that night, instead of sticking to a fixed sequence. 

# Impact on Live Performances & Preparation  
Deadmau5’s embrace of CDJs has influenced almost every aspect of his live shows – from the feel of the performance to how he readies himself beforehand. **On stage**, the difference is visible. In the Ableton days, Joel might be relatively static behind a laptop, occasionally tapping a MIDI controller or launching a clip, with the big LED mau5head and visuals doing much of the “performance.” Now, when using CDJs, he is more visibly engaged in classic DJ maneuvers – adjusting the mixer, looping or filtering tracks, and beatmatching by ear. There’s an energy to seeing him actually “mix” music live. Fans have noted that his CDJ sets bring a slightly rougher, more human element: transitions aren’t always perfectly beat-synced (he’s had a few slip-ups, which he’s the first to poke fun at), but that makes the show feel live and in-the-moment. Deadmau5 himself pointed out that one giveaway of a pre-recorded set is when “everything is **immaculately timed**” with the visuals – implying that when he’s *not* pre-recorded, the show might be a bit looser but more genuine【51†L98-L105】. Indeed, at events where he’s DJing on the fly, the lighting and visuals teams often switch to more generic effects or rely on software that follows the CDJs’ tempo info. It’s a trade-off: the spectacles may be a tad less synchronized than the Cube shows, but the music can breathe and take unexpected turns. 

**Preparation** for shows has likewise changed. With Ableton, Deadmau5 would spend weeks crafting a set: deciding an intro, editing his tracks into one long project file, programming lighting cues, and rehearsing the entire production with his team. Now, for a DJ set, he’ll prepare by updating USBs with his latest tracks, organizing playlists (perhaps by energy or theme), and practicing any particularly tricky transitions or mashups in advance. In interviews he’s mentioned that for huge festival sets – especially if tied to the Cube production – he will still prepare a lot of it in the studio to ensure technical perfection【51†L91-L99】. But for many tour dates, he’s comfortable just showing up with his music library and seeing where the night takes him. This hybrid approach means Deadmau5 can offer two types of live experiences: the **meticulously engineered “live” show** (e.g. his Cube 2.1 tour in 2017 or the collaborative live set with Eric Prydz featuring no CDJs【11†L130-L138】) versus the **freeform DJ set** (such as nightclub gigs, festival afterparties, or festival stage sets where he opts to spin tracks one by one). He seamlessly oscillates between these, which keeps his touring schedule dynamic. 

Deadmau5 has also spoken about how using CDJs has been “refreshing” in some ways. By 2014, after years of identical album tours, he was admittedly frustrated – both with aspects of the EDM industry and with the repetitive nature of his shows. Learning to DJ live injected new passion into his performances. It even opened the door for him to explore different genres under his alias *TESTPILOT* (for techno-focused DJ sets). In recent years he’s streamed niche genre DJ sessions (like drum & bass) from his studio, something that Ableton wouldn’t have easily facilitated【25†L47-L50】. Using CDJs and a mixer has essentially expanded Deadmau5’s versatility as a performer. As one dance music outlet summarized, by 2016 Deadmau5 had **“returned to the stage”** with a mix of new technology and was at his most “impressive and inventive,” whether deploying a full-stage custom setup or knocking out back-to-back **DJ sets**【30†L139-L147】. 

# Striking a Balance: Live Production vs. DJ Sets  
Today, Deadmau5 seems to have found a balance between the two approaches. He has not abandoned Ableton-driven shows – far from it. His highest-profile productions (like the Cube v3 in 2019 or special live streams) still rely on elaborate sequencing and live editing of songs. However, he now comfortably uses CDJs for the majority of festival appearances and club bookings. In a sense, he’s merged the mindsets: even when he DJs with CDJs, he brings a producer’s flair (sometimes feeding loops from Ableton or using drum machines alongside the decks), and when he does an Ableton “live” show, he might still freestyle elements or deviate from the script if he feels like it. This dual capability was on display at Tomorrowland 2016, where on one day Deadmau5 and Eric Prydz delivered a unique **analog live performance with no CDJs** (running computers and modular synths instead), and on another stage Deadmau5 could be seen doing a straight DJ set. As he cheekily wrote on Instagram then: *“look mom, no CDJs!”*【11†L133-L138】 – a wink to the fact that on most other days, yes, there **will** be CDJs. 

**In his own words**, Deadmau5 recognizes that whether you’re triggering clips in Ableton or beatmatching tracks on CDJs, the end goal is the same: to give the audience a great musical experience. “We all hit play… that’s about the most it seems you can do anyway,” he wrote, underscoring that electronic artists use whatever tools make sense for the show【51†L121-L125】. His transition from Ableton to CDJs wasn’t so much a repudiation of his old methods as it was an expansion of his toolbox. By adopting CDJs, Deadmau5 gained a new mode of expression and addressed some prior criticisms head-on. As a result, his live performances have become more varied and unpredictable. One night you might get a tightly synchronized audiovisual spectacle with every synth stab and strobe light timed to perfection; another night you might see Deadmau5 in “club mode,” mixing extended beats, taking requests (rarely, but it’s happened), and even smiling wryly when a mix doesn’t quite land on the beat. He’s noted that during pre-recorded sets, a DJ might just “twiddle filter knobs and clap” on stage【51†L99-L105】 – but now, when he’s mixing live, there’s genuine risk and skill involved, which seems to have reignited his enjoyment of performing. 

In summary, Deadmau5’s shift from an Ableton-only live setup to incorporating CDJs happened gradually over the mid-2010s, punctuated by events like his 2015 techno DJ sets and fully evident by 2016 when he spoke about “his CDJs” as a normal part of the rig【11†L128-L136】. He’s explained that big productions sometimes necessitate pre-planned Ableton sets for reliability, but embracing CDJs has allowed him to be more spontaneous and **DJ-like** in many shows【51†L91-L99】【35†L84-L92】. This transition has influenced everything from his pre-show prep (less programming, more crate-digging in his catalogue) to the feel of his concerts (more improvisation and interaction). As a result, Joel Zimmerman evolved from a self-described “non-DJ” into an artist equally comfortable rocking up to a club with a USB stick or unveiling a high-tech live show – whichever best fits the occasion. Or as one article aptly put it in 2016, *“Deadmau5 teases his plans to turn away from his CDJs and go with a live show setup”*【11†L128-L136】 – highlighting that these days, he can do either at the flip of a switch.
