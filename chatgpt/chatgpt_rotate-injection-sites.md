## Assistant

Yes — you should rotate injection sites, and rotating **legs** is a good idea.  

For tirzepatide (Truzepatide), recommended practice is:  
- Use the **same general body region** (abdomen, thigh, or upper arm) but change the exact spot each time to avoid irritation, bruising, or lipodystrophy.  
- If you prefer your thigh, you can inject into either leg — just pick a different area at least an inch away from the last injection site.  
- Many people alternate legs (e.g., left thigh one week, right thigh the next) to give each area time to recover.

## Assistant

For tirzepatide, “optimal” in the clinical sense means minimizing local tissue changes and ensuring consistent absorption.  
That points to **rotating legs** rather than sticking to one leg.  

Reasoning:  
- **Tissue recovery:** Even if you move the needle 1–2 inches from the last site, repeatedly using the same leg increases microtrauma and risk of localized fat atrophy or thickening over time. Alternating legs doubles the recovery interval for each site.  
- **Absorption consistency:** Repeated inflammation or small hematomas can slightly change absorption kinetics. Spreading injections across legs keeps each site’s tissue more uniform.  
- **Guideline alignment:** The rotation protocols used in insulin therapy (where subcutaneous delivery consistency matters) alternate both the *location within a limb* and *the limb itself*.  

So if you want the most clinically “perfect” routine:  
1. Alternate legs every injection.  
2. Within that leg, move at least 1 inch from the prior spot.  
3. Keep a mental (or physical) map so you’re not repeating the same point for several weeks.  

If you want, I can make you a **4-week thigh-only rotation map** so you’re hitting each site with maximal spacing. That would be the gold-standard approach.
