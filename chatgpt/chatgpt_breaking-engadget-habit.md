---

## Breaking the Habit
- **Block the site**: Use [Freedom](https://freedom.to/), [Cold Turkey](https://getcoldturkey.com/), or a free Chrome extension like *BlockSite* or *LeechBlock*. Block Engadget across all devices. That way even if you autopilot-type, you’ll hit a wall.  
- **Interrupt the muscle memory**: Unfavorite the site, clear it from autocomplete, and blacklist it in your browser’s DNS (e.g. map engadget.com to `127.0.0.1` in your hosts file).  
- **Replace the trigger**: When you feel the impulse to check Engadget, replace it with something quick and productive (e.g. open your GitHub issues, or a news feed that’s curated for developers).  

---

## Handling the FOMO
- **Custom digest (your idea)**: I can pull in tech news and filter out rage-bait, politics, and promo garbage. You’d just get relevant items: Apple features, OpenAI launches, Spotify changes, etc. That eliminates the need to “check.”  
- **Set a delivery schedule**: Instead of random dopamine hits, you’d get one clean update daily/weekly. This kills FOMO because you know you’re not missing anything important.  
- **Source diversification**: Rely on factual feeds like Apple’s newsroom, OpenAI’s blog, Product Hunt, or Techmeme instead of Engadget. They’re cleaner, less editorialized.

## Other Techniques
- **Commitment device**: Tell yourself “I don’t visit Engadget” (not “I can’t visit”). That reframing matters—cuts the inner debate.  
- **Mindful audit**: Keep a small sticky note near your desk: each time you catch yourself about to type Engadget, tally it. Seeing “12 times today” makes the habit obvious and easier to kill.  
- **Leverage RSS**: Subscribe to the official product feeds (Apple Developer News, OpenAI Updates, etc.) in an RSS app like Feedly. Then you don’t need media commentary at all.
