## Assistant

The Sonny Bono Copyright Term Extension Act, which took effect in 1998, not 1974, extended copyright terms in the U.S. For works published after 1923, the copyright term was extended to the life of the author plus 70 years for individual works, and for works for hire, anonymous or pseudonymous works, it is 95 years from publication or 120 years from creation, whichever is shorter.

"Mr. Sandman," published in 1954, is affected by these rules. Since it is not a work published before 1923, but rather a work for hire (typical for songs published by a music company), its copyright duration is 95 years from its year of publication due to the Act. Therefore, regardless of the Act's timing, "Mr. Sandman" is still scheduled to enter the public domain in 2050, not earlier due to any pre-1974 considerations.

For works made for hire, anonymous, or pseudonymous works, the term is 95 years from the year of first publication or 120 years from the year of creation, whichever expires first.

Since "Mr. Sandman" is considered a work for hire (published by a music company and not directly attributed to an author in the sense that copyright law considers for "life plus" terms), it falls under the 95 years from publication rule. "Mr. Sandman" is classified under the 95-year rule rather than a "life plus" rule because it is considered a work for hire, and the specific copyright terms that apply depend on the nature of the work and its publication details. The distinction is based on the type of work and how it was created:

1. **Works for Hire, Anonymous, or Pseudonymous Works**: For these categories, the U.S. Copyright Law specifies a term of 95 years from publication or 120 years from creation, whichever is shorter. This rule applies regardless of the life of the author. Since many songs and commercial music compositions are published by companies and are often considered works for hire (meaning the work is done by an employee within the scope of employment or has been specifically commissioned and agreed in writing to be a work for hire), they fall into this category.

2. **Works by Individual Authors**: For works created by individual authors, the copyright term is the author's life plus 70 years. This applies to works where the authorship is clear, and the work wasn't made for hire or doesn't fall into other specific categories that would extend or shorten the copyright term.

The distinction isn't about a choice between 95 years and a "life plus" timeframe but rather about the legal definition of the work's creation and publication. "Mr. Sandman," like many commercial songs, is treated under the copyright laws relevant to works for hire because of its publication status and how copyright ownership is typically assigned in the music industry. There's no "life plus 75 years" provision in current U.S. copyright law; it's "life plus 70 years" for individual works or 95 years from publication for works for hire.
