Short version, Mr. White:  
all of the “extras” are small adjuncts. Tirzepatide/semaglutide is doing 99% of the work; the glycine / carnitine / B12 are either:

- formulation helpers (make the peptide more stable / comfortable to inject), or  
- general-health supplements bolted on for theoretical benefit and marketing.

I’ll walk through each one and then give you some practical takeaways.

---

## 1. Glycine (what you’re on now)

**What it is**

- A simple amino acid involved in collagen, glutathione (antioxidant) and a bunch of metabolic pathways. Low circulating glycine is common in obesity and fatty liver disease.citeturn2search5turn2search22  

**Why it’s in these vials**

1. **Formulation role.** Glycine is a very common excipient in injectables: it buffers pH and can stabilize peptides, and sometimes makes injections sting less.
2. **“Bonus benefit” marketing.** Clinics claim glycine:
   - protects lean mass
   - improves recovery, sleep and inflammation  
  There *is* emerging human and animal data that glycine supplementation can improve some metabolic markers and may help liver health in severe obesity, but doses are usually grams per day by mouth, not the tiny amounts in a GLP-1 injection.citeturn2search14turn2search20turn2search26  

**Reality check**

- There are **no randomized trials** showing “tirzepatide + glycine” beats plain tirzepatide for weight loss or muscle retention.
- At typical injection doses glycine is very safe; the downside is mainly that you’re paying for something unproven.

For you: staying on tirzepatide + glycine is reasonable if you’re tolerating it and like it, but I wouldn’t expect a night-and-day difference versus pure tirzepatide.

---

## 2. L-carnitine (only offered with semaglutide in that menu)

**What it is**

- A nutrient that shuttles long-chain fatty acids into mitochondria so they can be burned for energy (the “carnitine shuttle”).citeturn1search2turn1search10  

**What the evidence says**

- Meta-analyses of randomized trials show **modest** effects: roughly ~1 kg extra weight loss and small improvements in lipids/glucose in overweight/obese or diabetic adults.citeturn2search1turn2search4turn2search21turn2search23  
- It’s not remotely in the same league as tirzepatide/semaglutide for weight loss.

**With GLP-1 drugs**

- There’s basically **no high-quality data** on “GLP-1 + carnitine” combos. It’s a rational-ish idea (“we’ll help you burn more fat while you eat less”), but it’s extrapolation.
- If you like L-carnitine, you can just keep taking it separately (oral or injection). You don’t need it mixed into the GLP-1 vial for it to work.

**Safety**

- Generally safe; main annoyances are nausea, diarrhea, and a fishy body odor in some people. Very high doses can be an issue in certain metabolic or kidney problems.

Why they don’t offer *tirzepatide* + L-carnitine is probably just: that pharmacy chose different SKUs, not a deep biological reason.

---

## 3. Vitamin B12 add-ons: methylcobalamin vs cyanocobalamin

You’re seeing:

- **Tirzepatide + methylcobalamin**  
- **Tirzepatide + cyanocobalamin**  
- (and similar combos with semaglutide)

**What B12 does**

- Cofactor for red blood cell production, nerve function, and DNA synthesis. Deficiency → anemia, neuropathy, fatigue.citeturn2search3turn2search9  

**Why stick B12 in a GLP-1 vial?**

- Some people on GLP-1 meds end up eating less animal protein, or they’re also on metformin, which *can* lower B12 over time. Rather than checking labs and supplementing properly, a lot of clinics just throw B12 in as a “more energy!” add-on.

**Methyl vs cyano**

- **Cyanocobalamin**
  - Synthetic, very stable, cheap.
  - Converted in the body to the active forms (methyl- and adenosyl-cobalamin).citeturn2search3turn2search9  
- **Methylcobalamin**
  - One of the active, “natural” forms.
  - May be retained a bit better and is often preferred if there’s kidney disease or concern about cyanide load, though for most healthy adults the difference is small.citeturn2search6turn2search9turn2search15  

**Big picture**

- For most people with normal kidneys, **either form works fine** for preventing/treating B12 deficiency.citeturn2search3turn2search6turn2search18  
- There is no good evidence that “tirzepatide + methylcobalamin” gives better weight loss than tirzepatide alone.
- B12 is water-soluble: if your levels are normal and you add more, you mostly just pee it out.

So the B12 combos are mainly:

- potentially useful if your B12 is low or low-normal  
- otherwise: convenient but mostly marketing.

## 4. Safety and regulation of compounded sema/tirz (important context)

Since you’re on compounded tirzepatide, this matters more than whether the add-ons are glycine vs B12.

- Compounded GLP-1 products are **not FDA-approved**. Potency, sterility, and exact contents can vary. The FDA has reported adverse events, dosing errors, and even fraudulent products.citeturn1search3turn1search12turn1search18turn1search22  
- FDA has said:
  - Shortage of **tirzepatide** is resolved (2024).citeturn1search8turn1news55  
  - Shortage of **semaglutide** is also considered resolved, and they’re moving to restrict compounded copies of Ozempic/Wegovy/Zepbound except in very specific situations.citeturn1news46turn1news51turn1news52  
  - Semaglutide *salt* forms (semaglutide sodium/acetate) are **not allowed** for compounding; those have been implicated in adverse events.citeturn1search17  

So the most important questions for you are:

1. Are they using **tirzepatide base**, not a random salt?  
2. Does the pharmacy provide **independent potency/sterility testing**?  
3. Is your prescriber monitoring labs and side-effects as if this were a brand-name GLP-1?

The glycine/B12 choice is secondary to those.
