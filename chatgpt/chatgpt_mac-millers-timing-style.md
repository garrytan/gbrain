## Assistant

Mac Miller’s music often feels “out of time” because of his stylistic choices, not because he couldn’t keep time. He leaned into **laid-back, behind-the-beat delivery** in both rapping and singing. That’s a jazz and soul influence—he’d intentionally place his voice slightly late against the drums, creating a looser, more human groove rather than a rigid, metronomic one.  

Other reasons his tracks can feel that way:  

- **Live instrumentation & swing**: Many of his later projects (especially *The Divine Feminine* and *Swimming*) feature live drums, bass, and keys with subtle swing or push-pull, which naturally creates that off-kilter feel.  
- **Drugged, dreamy vibe**: Thematically and sonically, his production leaned toward hazy textures, slower tempos, and woozy rhythms—reinforcing that unanchored sense.  
- **Influence from jazz & neo-soul**: Artists like Erykah Badu, D’Angelo, and Madlib inspired him; they all embrace grooves that float rather than lock tight.  

It’s deliberate looseness—a pocket that feels relaxed, vulnerable, and human.

## Assistant

That’s part of it too. Especially earlier in his career, Mac Miller often left takes a little raw — verses not perfectly quantized, vocals not tightly comped or pitch-corrected. It gave the music a “mixtape” authenticity and speed, letting him release more without obsessing over polish.  

Later on, even when he had access to top engineers, he still leaned into that looseness because it matched the aesthetic: unpolished, human, a little imperfect. So you’re right — a lot of that “out of time” feel isn’t because he couldn’t tighten it up, it’s because he either didn’t bother, or decided the imperfections felt more real.
