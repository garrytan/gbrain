## Assistant

Mr. White—

**Mostly yes, with one likely problem.**

- **30-day notice:** California’s Health Studio Services Act lets a gym require up to a **30-day termination notice**. Your 7/3 notice means the latest permissible end date is around **8/2/2025**. Email is an approved cancellation method if the contract lists it (the statute expressly allows email identifying the account). citeturn5view1turn7view2

- **Conditioning cancellation on a second payment cycle:** The gym can demand payment for dues owed **through the 30-day notice period**, and pursue collection if you don’t pay. But saying your **7/3 notice will be “denied” unless paid by 8/1 and you must submit a *new* 30-day notice** effectively exposes you to more than a 30-day notice requirement. That risks **noncompliance with Civil Code §1812.84(a)(1)** (“may require the consumer to give termination notice… **not to exceed 30 days**”). They should treat 7/3 as the notice date, end the contract after ~30 days, and separately collect what’s owed for that period. citeturn5view1

- **5-business-day unconditional cancellation:** If you signed less than **5 business days** before 7/3, you could have cancelled for a full refund. If not, this special right doesn’t apply. citeturn7view2

- **Annual/“facility” fee refund:** Outside the 5-day, relocation (≥25 miles), disability, or death scenarios, the statute doesn’t require refunding such fees; refunds in those special cases are **pro-rata**. A company “no refunds after 60 days” policy is generally enforceable unless it conflicts with those statutory rights or the contract’s own terms. citeturn7view2turn15view2turn15view3

**What to do**

3. If they refuse to honor 7/3 as effective notice, consider a **written demand letter** citing **Cal. Civ. Code §§1812.84 & 1812.85**, and, if needed, a **DCF/AG or small-claims** route. §1812.91 makes non-compliant contracts **void and unenforceable**. citeturn6view3turn7view2
