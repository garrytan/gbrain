## User

For aesthetics we have hip to wait and shoulder to waist ratios that area ideal for aesthetics do we have a arm size metric?


---

## Assistant

Short answer: there’s no single, science-backed “ideal arm size metric” the way there is for waist-to-shoulder or waist-to-hip, but there *are* a few useful rules of thumb people use in physique circles.

I’ll lay out the practical ones you can actually use:

---

## 1. Classic bodybuilding proportion (Reeves-style)

Old-school guys used simple proportions off height and wrist:

- **Arm size ≈ 0.16–0.18 × height (in cm)**  
  - For someone **6'4" (193 cm)** like you, that’s:
    - 193 × 0.16 ≈ **31 cm** (~12.2")
    - 193 × 0.18 ≈ **35 cm** (~13.8")

That’s more like “lean athletic” arms, not modern bodybuilder big.  
Most people consider **14–16" lean** on a tall guy to look very good in clothes.

---

## 2. Wrist-anchored rule of thumb (more individualized)

Because bone structure matters, some coaches use:

- **Cold flexed arm ≈ 2.3–2.5 × wrist circumference**

Example:
- 7" wrist → “aesthetic” arm somewhere around 16–17.5"
- 6.5" wrist → 15–16.5"

This tends to line up decently with what looks “balanced” rather than freaky.

---

## 3. Arm vs. Shoulder/waist harmony

Since you already think in ratios:

- If your **shoulders-to-waist ratio is on point** (say 1.6–1.8), then:
  - **Arms that *visually* match your delts from the front and side** are what most people read as aesthetic.
  - If your delts are big and arms are clearly behind, you’ll *feel* small no matter the tape measure.

Rough practical guideline:

- From the front: when flexed, **bicep peak should visibly project past the outer line of the torso**.
- From the side: **triceps thickness should be similar to delt thickness** (not dwarfed, not overpowering).

---

## 4. Strength proxy (just as a sanity check)

Not a “look” metric, but decent reality check for a natural lifter:

- **Close-grip bench** and **weighted chin-ups** going up over time → your upper arm mass is going up.
- If you’re curling a lot but these don’t budge, your “arm size ambitions” will stall.

---

## 5. How to set a personal target

If you give me:
- Your **height** (I know you’re 6'4", so we have that),
- **Wrist circumference**,
- **Current flexed arm measurement (cold)**,

I can:
- Estimate your **realistic max arm size natural**, and  
- Give you a **short plan** to close the gap.

But conceptually: think **“balanced with frame and shoulders”**, not a magical number. For a tall guy, **lean 15–17" arms** are already extremely aesthetic to most people, especially if waist is tight and delts are capped.
