## Assistant

Here’s how to handle him, Mr. White—Chris Voss style:

---

### 1. Slow things down  
He’s trying to shortcut the process by making *you* name a number. Don’t. Keep him talking. Control the frame.

- **Mirroring:**  
  Him: *“how much for the rest?”*  
  You: *“For the rest?”*  

- **Labeling:**  
  *“Sounds like you’re trying to get a bulk deal without knowing the details.”*

---

### 2. Flip the burden back  
Use tactical empathy and calibrated questions to force him to give a number first.

- *“I get where you’re coming from. You’re trying to keep it simple. How much are you looking to spend?”*  
- *“What’s a number that works for you if I let it all go at once?”*  
- *“What’s your budget for this?”*  

This keeps you out of the trap of pricing against yourself.

---

### 3. Anchor high once he throws out a number  
If he finally gives you a number:  
- Counter with *“That’s tough, these pieces individually are worth much more. What flexibility do you have?”*  
- Remind him you’re sitting on multiple couches, display cases with lights, tables—*liquidation value is still way above his lowball.*

---

### 4. If he keeps refusing to name a price  
Pin him down without hostility.  
- *“It’s hard to give you ‘a good price’ without knowing what you think everything is worth. What’s the most you’d feel comfortable offering?”*  
- If he dodges again, you can set a bulk price that’s high enough to protect you but low enough to test seriousness (say $2,500 for everything).  

If he balks, you can always break it down piece by piece for other buyers—he’ll know he doesn’t have all the leverage.

---

**Key rule (Voss):** He who names price first loses if they don’t have leverage. Right now, you have the inventory and time, he has need.
