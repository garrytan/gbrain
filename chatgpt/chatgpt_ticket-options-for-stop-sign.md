1. **Lack of Visibility**: You might argue that the stop sign wasn't clearly visible from a reasonable distance due to obstructions like foliage or parked vehicles, making it difficult for you to notice and stop.

2. **Necessity**: Some people argue that it was necessary to make a "rolling stop" rather than a complete stop to avoid immediate harm (e.g., an object falling from a truck ahead).

3. **Mistaken Identity**: If you believe you're wrongly ticketed and another vehicle was actually responsible, you might argue mistaken identity. Evidence like dash-cam footage could support this.

4. **Inadequate Signage**: You could argue that the stop sign doesn't meet state or municipal guidelines for size, height, or reflective material, thus making it legally inadequate.

5. **Ambiguity**: If the ticket is ambiguous or contains incorrect information (e.g., wrong location, date, or even stating you were only going 5 mph), you could argue that such errors bring the ticket's validity into question.

6. **Officer's Position**: If the ticketing officer was positioned in such a way that they couldn't adequately see whether you came to a complete stop, this might also be used in your defense.

Consult a legal advisor to evaluate the strength and applicability of these arguments for your specific case in Los Angeles, California.
