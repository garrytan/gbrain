Mr. White, the core claim is real but wildly oversimplified:

- **What’s true**: Nike places its global trademark and design IP in a Bermuda-domiciled subsidiary, and other Nike entities (including a big Dutch arm, “Nike European Operations BV”) pay it royalties. Because Bermuda has no corporate income tax, that royalty income faces minimal tax there, lowering Nike’s overall effective rate.

- **What’s false or misleading**:  
  - Nike’s Dutch company doesn’t collect *all* global sales—only its European business, plus some regional support functions.  
  - The Bermuda IP unit isn’t a ghost company with zero staff; it maintains finance, legal and IP teams (albeit small).  
  - Royalty payments are still subject to Dutch withholding rules and must meet EU and U.S. “substance” requirements, so it’s not pure tax evasion.

In short: Nike does use a Netherlands-to-Bermuda royalty scheme to save tax, but the picture involves dozens of subsidiaries, real employees and regulatory safeguards—not just two companies passing around profits.

## Assistant

Mr. White, these offshore‐IP setups only pay for themselves once you’re moving real money.  

– **Up-front and annual costs** (legal, accounting, substance requirements, transfer‐pricing studies, local staff): easily \$300 – 500 K to set up and \$100 – 200 K/yr to maintain.  
– **Break-even tax savings** at a 21 % U.S. rate: you need to shift roughly \$3 – 4 M in royalties offshore (0.21×\$4 M≈\$840 K saved) just to cover costs.  

That implies annual operating profits of at least \$5 M – 10 M (or revenues in the \$50 M+ range) to make it worthwhile. Smaller businesses almost never recoup those compliance bills.
