Mr. White, after reading the actual measure: **you are only partly correct.**

It **does** require real proof for certain medical bills, especially lien-based medical care. That part aligns with your view.

But it also **does cap recoverable medical money in legitimate claims**. The Attorney General’s official title says the measure would “limit automobile accident victims’ recovery of medical expenses,” and the summary says it would increase victims’ burden of proof and limit amounts they may recover for certain medical expenses. citeturn645084view1

## What it does

### 1. Fault still has to be proven
The measure does **not** create a new basic rule that “you must prove the accident happened and someone was at fault.” That is already how injury law works. You already need liability, causation, and damages.

So this is broader than:

> “No fake accident, no payout.”

### 2. It caps attorney contingency economics
The initiative says an attorney cannot collect a contingency fee that leaves the victim with less than **75% of the total recovery**. citeturn645084view0

That part is very anti-lawyer and pro-client on its face.

### 3. It limits medical-expense recovery
The Legislative Analyst says the measure would limit medical damages in motor-vehicle accident cases. For paid bills or bills covered by insurance, recovery is generally limited to the **actual amount paid**. For medical liens and future medical expenses, recovery is capped by formulas like **125% of Medicare**, **170% of Medi-Cal**, or commercial-insurance benchmark rates. citeturn645084view2

That means a legitimate injured person may have a real injury, real fault, and real treatment, but still recover less than the provider’s billed amount.

### 4. It raises the proof standard for medical liens
For medical liens, the injured person would have to prove by **clear and convincing evidence** that the treatment was reasonably necessary, instead of the lower “more likely than not” standard. citeturn645084view2

This directly targets the lawyer-doctor lien pipeline you dislike. It also makes some legitimate lien-based treatment harder to recover.

## Your Waymo telemetry point

I agree with your pushback. Telemetry is generally good evidence. If it is preserved, produced, and interpreted honestly, it can help both sides establish what happened.

The remaining concern is cost and leverage, not the data itself:

- getting the data may require litigation
- interpreting AV logs may require experts
- companies may fight over scope, privilege, trade secrets, or relevance
- a small injury case may not justify that expense under a capped fee model

So telemetry helps truth-finding. It does not automatically make litigation cheap.

## Your clean position

Your stance is probably:

> “I support the parts that stop inflated medical liens, attorney kickbacks, and contingency-fee abuse. I do not support a broad rule that caps recovery for legitimate medical expenses in all California auto crashes.”

That is the most internally consistent version.

## Is the initiative good by your values?

**Mixed.**

| Your value | Initiative match |
|---|---|
| Stop frivolous lawsuits | Weak match — existing law already requires proof |
| Stop inflated medical liens | Strong match |
| Stop lawyer/provider kickbacks | Strong match |
| Let real victims recover real medical costs | Mixed/weak — recovery can be capped by benchmark rates |
| Avoid jackpot justice | Strong match |
| Avoid corporate liability shielding | Weak — Uber and insurers benefit |

## Final read

This initiative is **not merely “prove the accident and real injury.”**

It is closer to:

> “In auto cases, plaintiffs keep more of the recovery, lawyers get less, attorney-medical-provider games are restricted, and recoverable medical damages are benchmarked/capped even in legitimate claims.”

So if you want **anti-abuse reform**, you may like parts of it.  
If you want **full recovery for legitimate actual medical costs**, the measure likely goes too far.
