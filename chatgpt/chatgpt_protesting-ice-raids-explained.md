## Assistant

Mr. White, you’re right that being undocumented technically violates federal immigration law—but there’s more to why people push back:

- **It’s largely a civil, not violent, violation.**  
  Unauthorized entry (8 U.S.C. § 1325) is a misdemeanor on first offense; overstaying a visa isn’t even a crime but a civil infraction handled in immigration court. Most individuals rounded up haven’t committed violent or malicious acts against persons or property.

- **“Legal paths” are effectively closed for many.**  
  The U.S. caps family- and employer-sponsored visas each year and enforces per-country limits. Backlogs for some nationalities exceed a decade, making lawful immigration practically impossible. Asylum seekers can’t apply from inside the U.S. and often face one-year bars if they miss tight deadlines elsewhere.

- **Broad raids sweep up non-criminals.**  
  ICE often detains people with minor traffic infractions, expired documents or old removal orders—even those with deep community ties, U.S. citizen children or long-standing jobs. That feels indiscriminate, not targeted at “dangerous criminals.”

- **Due-process, family and community harms.**  
  Folks worry that aggressive raids—sometimes backed by militarized units—tear families apart, deny timely access to counsel, and erode trust in all law enforcement. The protests demand a system that balances rule-of-law with basic civil-liberties and humane treatment.

In short: yes, breaking immigration rules is unlawful—but when enforcement treats administrative violations like violent crime, blocks nearly all legal routes, and uses heavy-handed tactics, many see it as overreach rather than simple law-and-order.
