## Major gaps & caveats

1. **Sampling bias**: Many of the surveys are among people who are already participating in LGBTQ identity forums, or responding to “LGBTQ automobile surveys” — these may over-represent people who care about brand symbolism or “brand as identity.”  

2. **Inferential weakness**: A claim like “lesbians are four times more likely to buy a Subaru” lacks published methodology (how “average consumer” is defined, controls for income/region/urban vs rural, etc.). Without controlling for those, the effect might be spurious (e.g. lesbians might be more common in certain geographies where Subarus are popular for other reasons).

3. **Confounding variables**: Socio-economic status, urban/rural residence, environmental values, preference for utility / outdoors activities — all of these correlate both with certain car preferences and with sexual orientation / identity community profiles. That makes it hard to isolate “sexual orientation” as the causal factor.

4. **Time & marketing influence**: Some of the correlation may be driven more by culture/identity/branding than by objective preference. If a group feels “this car brand reflects me,” that may increase likelihood of choosing it, but that’s not the same as showing a natural or inherent preference independent of marketing.  

---

## Conclusion

There *is* suggestive evidence — marketing case studies, community surveys, brand perception data — that lesbians are more likely than average to see Subarus favorably, or are more likely than some other groups to own them. But **no high-quality, large-scale, peer-reviewed study** I found confirms a statistically robust effect that lesbians drive Subarus significantly *above average*, controlling for all relevant variables.
