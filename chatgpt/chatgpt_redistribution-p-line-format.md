# Redistribution (p) Line Format

## User

i released a song on january 12, 2018. im now redirstrubiting the song to DSPs through a new distributor. i still own the full master. i did change the album art and previously it was on a 2 song product that included a remix now im putting it with jus the single song but using the same ISRC as the old one and we'll have the old one with matching isrc taken down. What should my (p) line read?

---

## Assistant

For your situation, the (p) line, which stands for the phonogram copyright notice, should accurately reflect the year of the original release of the recording, as well as the current year if the redistribution represents a significant reissue or remastering. Since you are redistributing an existing recording with a new album art and configuration (single song instead of a two-song product), but the actual recording is the same and retains its original ISRC, the (p) line should primarily acknowledge the original release year.

A typical (p) line format is “(p) [Year of First Publication] [Name of Copyright Holder].” If the redistribution is considered a new publication due to significant changes beyond just the artwork and pairing (such as remastering), you might also include the current year. However, in your case, where the recording itself hasn't changed, just the packaging and distribution method, the most critical aspect is to ensure the original release year is correctly cited.

Thus, your (p) line should read something like: “(p) 2018 [Your Name or Entity Holding the Rights].” 

This notation acknowledges your copyright from the time of the original release. There's no need to add the current year since the recording itself hasn't been altered, just the manner in which it's being presented and distributed. Make sure the name or entity listed is the rightful holder of the master recording rights. If you have a label or a production company that holds these rights, that entity's name should be included in the (p) line.

## User

---

## Assistant

If you have a label called The Bubblegum Factory to which you're licensing the song, but you still retain the copyright, the (p) line should reflect the label's involvement while maintaining the original release year. Since you own both the copyright and the label, you'll want to ensure that the (p) line clearly indicates the label as the current licensee or publisher, without obscuring the fact that the copyright is ultimately held by you.

In this scenario, the (p) line could read: “(p) 2018 The Bubblegum Factory.” This acknowledges that The Bubblegum Factory, your label, is the entity currently managing or licensing the recording, starting from the original release year. It implies that The Bubblegum Factory has the rights to distribute or publish the recording as of the noted year.

If you want to make it clear that you, as an individual, are the copyright owner and are licensing it to your label, you might consider a slightly more detailed notation, depending on the common practices in your jurisdiction and industry standards. However, typically, the (p) line focuses on the release or licensee entity and the year of first publication. 

For complete clarity, especially in contracts or licensing agreements, you would detail the arrangement between yourself and The Bubblegum Factory, indicating that while The Bubblegum Factory holds the licensing rights, you retain the copyright. This level of detail isn’t usually part of the (p) line but is crucial for legal agreements and copyright registrations.
