## Assistant

Yeah, walls are not the nice empty boxes we wish they were, Mr. White.

What’s probably happening:

- The **bottom hole** hit plain drywall cavity → plastic anchor goes in fine.  
- The **top hole** is landing right on something solid *just behind* the drywall:  
  - a drywall screw into the stud,  
  - a bit of metal stud,  
  - a tiny piece of blocking, or  
  - even a nail plate over a wire/pipe.

Those things can be only a fraction of an inch wide, so 1" higher can absolutely be different from the hole below. Your drill “grabbing” and refusing to go deeper is exactly what it feels like when you’re on a screw/metal or hard wood.

That’s why the top plug is sticking out: the hole stops shallow, so the anchor bottoms out early.

**What I’d do:**

1. **Stop trying to force the drill deeper** in that same spot—just in case it’s a plate protecting electrical/plumbing.  
2. **Pull that upper anchor out** with pliers.
3. Either:
   - **Move the top hole 1–2" to the left or right** (not straight up/down) and redrill your 1/4" pilot, *or*  
   - If you actually want to use the solid thing you hit, skip the plastic anchor and:
     - drill a small pilot (~1/8") and  
     - use a wood/metal screw directly into it, like you would into a stud.

4. Patch the bad hole later with a bit of spackle.

Short version: you’re almost certainly hitting some random hardware or framing behind the drywall. It’s normal, just don’t brute-force through it—move the hole or treat it like a stud.

## Assistant

You’re almost definitely hitting *something* solid right behind the drywall on that top hole, Mr. White.  

The wall isn’t just empty space + studs. Inside there can be:

- A **wood block / fire block** running horizontally between studs  
- The **edge of a stud** (your top hole could be just a bit closer to it than the bottom one)  
- A **metal protector plate** over electrical wires or a pipe  
- Occasionally, metal framing or corner bead

Even though the holes are one above the other, that’s enough distance for one to land in clear drywall and the other to catch the top of a block or plate.

When your bit hits that, it feels like the drill just refuses to go deeper, and the plastic anchor ends up standing proud of the wall like in your pic.

### What you should do

1. **Stop forcing it.**  
   If it’s a metal plate over wires or a pipe, you don’t want to chew through that.

2. **Pull that upper anchor back out.**  
   - Run a screw a few turns into the anchor.  
   - Grab the screw with pliers and pull/wiggle it out.

3. **Pick a new spot.**  
   - Ideally move **at least 1–2 inches sideways** and re-drill.  
   - Use a stud finder if you have one:  
     - If it shows a stud, skip the plastic anchor and just use a wood screw straight into the stud.  
     - If it shows nothing, drill your 1/4" hole again and the anchor should seat like the lower one.

4. **If you absolutely need that exact location:**  
   - Cut a small inspection hole around the problem spot (like 2"×2") with a utility knife/drywall saw and look inside.  
   - If it’s solid wood, great — mount directly into it.  
   - If you see wires/pipes/metal plate, patch the hole and shift your hardware.

Short version: nothing is “wrong” with the anchor; your top hole just landed on hidden structure or a protective plate. Don’t brute-force it—move the hole or open it up and see what’s there.
