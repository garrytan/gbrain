## Assistant

To achieve a film grain effect reminiscent of Victor Robertof's style in your portraits using Capture One, you'll want to focus on the Film Grain tool. While the exact settings can depend on the specific look you're aiming for and the characteristics of the original photo, here are some general guidelines to get started:

1. **Open the Film Grain tool:** Navigate to the Details tab in Capture One and find the Film Grain tool. If it's not visible, you might need to add it to your workspace via the "Add Tool" option.

2. **Select a Grain Type:** Capture One offers different grain types such as Fine, Silver Rich, Soft, etc. For a classic film look similar to Victor Robertof's photography, you might start with "Silver Rich" or "Fine" to emulate the detailed and subtle grain found in many types of film photography.

3. **Adjust the Grain Impact:** Begin with a moderate impact setting to see how the grain affects your image. The Impact slider controls the visibility of the grain. For portraits, a subtler effect often works best, so you might find settings around 20-40 to be a good starting point.

4. **Tweak the Granularity:** The Granularity slider adjusts the size of the grain particles. Smaller values will give you finer grain, which can be more suitable for achieving a look that's detailed yet not overly pronounced. For a Victor Robertof-inspired look, you may want to keep the granularity on the lower end.

5. **Experiment with Variability:** This setting adjusts how uniform the grain appears across your image. Increasing variability can make the grain appear more natural and less digital. 

6. **Preview and Adjust:** Use the before/after feature in Capture One to compare the edited version with the original. This can help you fine-tune the settings until you achieve the desired look.

Remember, the key to successfully emulating a film grain look is subtlety, especially with portraits. You want to enhance the photo without overwhelming the subject. It might also be helpful to look at some of Victor Robertof's work as a reference while you adjust your settings to better match his style.

Lastly, since each photo is unique, consider these settings as a starting point and adjust according to the specific lighting, texture, and mood of your portraits.
