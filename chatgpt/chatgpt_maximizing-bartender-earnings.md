- **Bottle Service & Big Spenders:** Venues with **bottle service** tables (common in Hollywood’s top clubs) create huge tabs that include automatic gratuities – often 18–20% on packages that can be $500-$1000+. While these tips mostly go to bottle service servers, bartenders may share a portion via tip pool【47†L21-L24】. Consistent regulars can turn even off-nights into decent money【31†L1353-L1361】, while tourists ensure the peaks are very high.

- **Service Charges & Tip Pooling:** **Automatic service fees** (common in Beverly Hills eateries and some WeHo spots) can boost baseline earnings, as every check has a built-in tip. This favors bars in hotels or fine-dining restaurants – bartenders don’t have to rely on goodwill alone. On the flip side, **tip pooling** policies can impact individual take-home pay. In some establishments, tips are split among the whole team (and sometimes beyond)【13†L333-L341】, which can flatten the extremes – great on slower nights (you still get a share) but limiting on nights where *you* personally might have earned more. Bartenders chasing the highest personal earnings often seek places with either minimal pooling or extremely high total tips.

- **Venue Reputation & Position:** A bar known for fast service or strong drinks might encourage more tipping (patrons appreciate quick service in a mobbed club). Also, where a bartender is stationed can matter – the **main bar in a nightclub** or the **pool bar at a hot rooftop** likely sees more orders (and tips) than a tucked-away service bar. Some bartenders hustle to get into the most lucrative “well” or shift at their workplace. Job postings in LA often mention “high-volume experience” for this reason【0†L15-L22】【36†L69-L77】 – employers know a seasoned high-volume bartender can handle the crowds that generate big tips.

As one Redditor succinctly advised an aspiring LA bartender: seek out *“the Venn diagram of massive volume and good tippers”*【28†L303-L309】. ### Factors to Weigh

1. **Short Slammed Shift vs. All-Day Slog:**  
   - **Keys/Zouk**-style clubs can be open for only 3–4 hours of actual service, letting you earn \$300–\$500 **quickly**—great hourly rate.  
   - Hotel bars/pool bars (**Skybar, Petit Ermitage, Edition**) often stretch 6–8 hours or more, with 2–3 hours of real “rush.” Overall daily tips can still be good, but the extra slow time dilutes your hourly average.

2. **Tip Pooling vs. Solo:**  
   - Many large clubs or corporate-run venues (like **Academy**, some H.wood Group spots like **Poppy**) pool tips. This can flatten extremes—steady on slow nights, but sometimes less on the absolute busiest nights.  
   - Smaller bars or single well bars (some sports bars, rooftop lounges with limited staff) might let you keep more individual tips if you’re solo.

3. **Clientele & Check Averages:**  
   - A wealthy or VIP crowd (Warwick, Poppy, private clubs) can push tip percentage up.  
   - A local sports bar or a short-slam night club can yield big volume at \$1–\$2 per drink but add up quickly.

4. **Reliability of Shifts:**  
   - **High-volume clubs** sometimes over-hire bartenders, rotating them so each has fewer shifts, or mostly staff for weekends only.  
   - A busy hotel rooftop might give you more regular, guaranteed hours—even if the rush is partly diluted.

5. **Location & Access:**  
   - If it’s **very close** (e.g., The Abbey, Rocko’s, Keys, Soho House), you might pick up last-minute shifts or fill-in opportunities, saving commute time and parking costs.

### Overall Takeaway

- **For max tips in the fewest hours**: new Hollywood/WeHo **nightclubs** (e.g. **Keys, Zouk, Warwick, Sound**) are your best bet, especially if they attract high-end or EDM crowds. Expect \$300–\$500 on a solid weekend, sometimes more.  
- **For a mix of decent tips + steadier environment**: **Skybar, Soho House, Poppy** can still net \$250–\$400 on good nights, but with longer shifts and potential tip pooling.  
- **For consistent but lower-volume**: **Rocko’s Tavern**, **The Den**, **Desert 5 Spot**, etc., might be more relaxed, but you’ll rarely see the huge multi-hundred-dollar nights that top clubs can bring.

Ultimately, if you prioritize **short shifts and high hourly** payoff, aim for the **3–4-hour nightclub** route—though it’s competitive, intense, and often limited to weekend nights. If you prefer more stable scheduling (and possibly more total hours per week), a hotel rooftop or members club might be more balanced, albeit with a lower peak hourly rate.
