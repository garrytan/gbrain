Mr. White,

“Trapped-water” explanations generally conflate two different phenomena:

1. **Post-workout swelling (the “pump”)**  
   - Resistance training causes an acute influx of blood, metabolites and fluid into muscle cells (“cell swelling”), which can stimulate anabolic signaling and contribute modestly to hypertrophy. citeturn2view0turn3search1  
   - This pump is real but transient—peaking during and immediately after a workout and subsiding over hours to days (often 7–11 days via inflammation‐related edema). citeturn4view0  

2. **Early “size gains” vs. true tissue growth**  
   - In novice trainees or when switching to a higher-carb or higher-calorie diet, you’ll often see 2–4 lb of size gain simply from glycogen repletion (each gram of glycogen holds ≈3 g water) and from muscle damage-induced edema—neither of which reflects new contractile protein. citeturn1view0  

3. **The “woosh” effect in fat loss**  
   - The idea that water remains “trapped” around fat cells during cutting and then suddenly “wooshes” away after a carb-load or high-water day is purely anecdotal. There are no peer-reviewed studies validating subcutaneous water dynamics in fat-loss plateaus—most accounts come from online forums, not clinical research. citeturn0search2  

**Bottom line:**  
- **Pump = cell swelling + transient fluid shifts** (a minor hypertrophic stimulus).  
- **Early bulk gains = glycogen + edema**, not new muscle.  
- **No solid evidence** supports a fat-loss “trapped water” and “woosh” mechanism beyond anecdote.

Hope that clears it up.
