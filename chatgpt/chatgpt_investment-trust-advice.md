Mr. White — that meme is **mostly misleading** (and missing big caveats).

## Fact check of the meme
1. **“Put it in a trust” doesn’t erase capital gains tax.**  
   - Moving stock into a **revocable living trust** changes basically **nothing** for income taxes (it’s still “you” for tax purposes).  
   - An **irrevocable trust** can change *who* pays tax, but it doesn’t make gains disappear. Trust tax brackets are often **worse** (they hit top rates at very low income).

2. **“Borrow against it, live tax-free” is not free.**  
   - Loans aren’t income, true, but you pay **interest**, and if the stock drops you can get **margin calls** / forced sale / liquidation risk.

3. **“Dies holding → kids inherit at $2M basis → IRS gets $0” is only sometimes true.**  
   - A **step-up in basis** at death generally happens for assets **included in the taxable estate**.  
   - If you move assets into structures that keep them **out** of the estate, you may **lose the step-up**.  
   - Even with a step-up, the estate could owe **estate tax** if large enough (federal threshold is high, but not infinite).  
   - California: **no special capital gains rate** (it taxes cap gains as ordinary income), but it generally follows the federal **step-up** concept at death.

Bottom line: the “trust + borrow = IRS gets $0” pitch is an **oversimplified rich-guy highlight reel**, not a reliable plan.

## Your mom’s situation (LA/CA): invested 2018, IPO in June, $100k position
Because it’s post-IPO, the key issues are usually: **lockup**, **taxes**, and **concentration risk**.

### What’s usually “best” if she has high conviction but you don’t want her to sell
A practical middle path most families use:

1. **Confirm the lockup + trading restrictions**
   - Many IPO shares can’t be sold for ~**180 days** (varies). If she’s an employee/insider, there may be blackout windows and rule 144 limitations.

2. **Plan taxes now (don’t guess)**
   - Since she bought in **2018**, any sale should be **long-term capital gains** federally.  
   - She’ll likely owe: **federal LTCG + NIIT (if applicable) + CA tax**.  
   - Have a CPA run scenarios for selling **0% / 10% / 25% / 50%** of the position after lockup to see after-tax outcomes and estimated payments.

3. **De-risk without fully exiting**
   - Common approach: sell **enough to (a) cover taxes + (b) recover original cost + (c) diversify a slice**, and keep the rest for conviction.
   - Example logic (not numbers): “Get principal back + remove ‘life-changing regret’ risk, keep meaningful upside.”

4. **Use risk controls once options exist**
   - After IPO (and once options are liquid), she can consider a **protective put** or a **collar** to cap downside while keeping upside. This can align with “I don’t want to sell” emotionally while still managing risk.

5. **Be very careful with “borrow against it”**
   - Securities-based lending can work for very conservative leverage, but it’s how people get wrecked in drawdowns. If she does it: small loan-to-value, cash reserves, and clear liquidation rules.

## The one “trust” idea that *can* be legitimately powerful (if she’s charitably inclined)
If she is already charitable, ask her CPA/attorney about:
- **Donating appreciated shares** (to a **DAF** or charity): potentially avoids capital gains on the donated portion and may get a deduction (limits apply).
- **Charitable Remainder Trust (CRT)**: can diversify without immediate tax recognition, but it’s complex and only fits certain goals.

A plain “put it in a trust” is **not** the magic.

- If it’s a small % and she can tolerate an 80% drawdown: **hold more**.  
- If she’d panic-sell in a crash: **pre-commit** now to a partial sale + downside protection plan.
