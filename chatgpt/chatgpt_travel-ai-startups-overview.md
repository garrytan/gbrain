Travel x AI: Startups Are Building Trust Platforms, Not Trip Planners
Mapping 20+ “inspiration” apps shows the real race is to earn micro-permissions
Daniel Kang
Jul 10, 2025


Source: By Author (Daniel Kang)
AI is transforming consumer travel—but most startups are solving for the easiest problems, not the most painful ones. Inspiration and itinerary tools aren’t the endgame. They’re the first impression—an entry point to earn trust.

My working hypothesis is this: the lasting moat in Travel × AI won’t be planning tools or clever UX. It will be platforms that empower Connectors and Specialists—people who can scale trust across communities. That trust is what bridges inspiration to booking, and eventually, to data-sharing.

In this piece, I focus on early-stage Consumer Travel AI startups, primarily Seed to Series A. Most of these companies haven’t hit full Product-Market Fit, but their product iterations act as free experiments for the rest of the industry. Watching how they build—and where they fail—is instructive.

Across the landscape, one core distinction emerged: interface. Most startups cluster into three types based on how they engage users:

Chatbot-first → LLM interfaces like ChatGPT

SaaS-first → Typical software input with AI enablement

Content-first → Social interfaces like Instagram reels or Tinder

In the next few pages, I’ll share an early map of these three clusters—how they operate, where they’re headed, and who might win.

1. Chatbot-First: Delight at the Top of Funnel
The earliest of the startups are concentrated here, providing a chatbot-like interface for inspiration and trip planning, along with visual tabs of maps and destinations to eventually book the trip.


Source: Screenshot of Mindtrip
Most players rely on LLM wrappers (foundational models), inventory APIs (flights, hotels, experiences), and some proprietary data. The key differentiator is probably going to be the distribution strategy.

In the last few years, VCs like Forerunner and SouthParkCommons have been betting on this space. There’s already been consolidation, including Layla acquiring Roam Around and Mindtrip acquiring Thatch.

Layla.AI: social-first travel bot with Instagram-powered acquisition. An interesting GTM strategy is their Instagram persona “@justasklayla” (425K followers) that allows early customers to DM for itineraries coupled with libraries of travel content before nudging them to the app. Their API integration is expansive with Booking, Skyscanner, GetYourGuide, Viator, and TripAdvisor, especially following their acquisition of Roam Around.

Funding: Raised a $3m seed round from investors including from M13 and co-founders of Booking and Skyscanner.

Founders: The CEO & Co-founder Saad Saeed previously co-founded Flink ($2.9bn instant grocery startup); Co-founders Jeremy Jauncey and Sardar Bali were executives at Beautiful Destinations, a large creator marketing agency in the travel industry.

Mindtrip: Inspiration to booking chatbot. What stood out was how much value it provides before signing up: itineraries, reviews, and “saving” key locations. In-app booking and group chat features once signed up was also useful. From a GTM perspective, they’ve expanded into content and community With their acquisition of Thatch, including creator affiliate programs and features to create itineraries by pasting a URL of travel content.

Funding: Raised seed and Series-A of $19m from top consumer investors like Forerunner and Costanoa.

Founders: Mindtrip has 12 co-founders all who worked at Ariba, and held senior positions including ex-VP Engineering at Google and Distinguished Engineer at Linkedin. The CEO-Cofounder is Andy Moss, a former VP at Ariba.

Others include: Coinvent AI (Airial), Guidegeek, Miso.com.

Other than the novelty factor of chat-based trip planning, the benefit of this interface was experiencing “delight” of reviews, itineraries, and value before introducing frictions points like signups and payments.

Mindtrip, especially had much higher rate limits for browsing users, likely possible with large VC funding to accommodate higher GPU-usage in exchange for higher absolute conversion of customers.

In contrast, Layla gave some value with their social content, but almost immediately required sign-ups, for their AI product, likely optimizing for conversion of high-intent users.

Running up against time
Novelty alone won’t be enough. Given that most emerging chatbot startups are borrowing technology (OpenAI, Gemini) and data/ inventory (Expedia, Booking, Skyscaner) via APIs, they’ll need to create unique, defensible UX and community, they risk being subsumed by horizontal tools like Perplexity and Gemini or legacy OTAs.

For example, OpenAI created partnerships with Booking, and will probably offer booking capabilities in the future. Legacy players like Expedia created chatbots with ChatGPT and features to paste IG reels for itineraries.

While the early capabilities were helpful, I’m unsure (especially as a consumer), if the problems being solved are painful enough to maintain relevance as foundational models improve and legacy partnerships are formalized. Without deep product and data differentiation fast, it’s uncertain if these companies could compete.

2. SaaS-first: Pragmatic and Profitable
Unlike chatbot-first startups that prioritize early delight and low friction, SaaS-first startups use AI to streamline existing workflows—trip generation, advisor enablement, or climate-focused logistics — on SaaS infrastructure for consistency of outcome and profitability.

The approach aligns with Brian Chesky’s stance at Airbnb, using AI to power backend operations, but notes it is not good enough to power user journeys, and focus on specific problems. That’s likely why they often seem to have more early traction that chatbot-led startups, and generally Series-A and above.


Source: Screenshot of Byway
Here are a few players:

Fora: Fora uses AI to expand the labor market (and trust market). It’s a new type of travel agency in the US that uses AI to enable anyone (including hobbyist) to make money as travel advisors (97% of them new to the industry). The consumer interface generally starts with a SaaS-style questionnaire, but Fora’s software helps new travel advisors create profiles and itineraries to inspire trips, and Fora’s AI software Sidekick to help advisors access data to support customer bookings.

Funding: Raised $73.5 million total, including a $40 million Series C led by Thrive Capital and Insight Partners. Previous funding included $13.5 million Series A co-led by Heartcore Capital and Forerunner.

Founders: Founded by Henley Vazquez (founder of a travel agency Passported), Evan Frank (former CEO of onefinestay, a luxury vacation rental company, sold to Accor for $170 million), and Jake Peters (co-founder of fintech startup PayPerks)

Byway: Optimizing niche logistics using AI. They build flight-free travel, offering slow, climate-friendly travel experiences via trains, buses, and ferries across Europe. In a short questionnaire format Byway can create an itinerary with AI. Most recently, they launched a chatbot with a data partnership with Seat61, a train route database. Roughly 60% of consumer book travel through ByWay’s Journey AI, and the other 40% from human concierge services.

Funding: Raised a £5 million ($6.3 million) seed round in 2023 led by Eka Ventures and Heartcore Capital. Earlier funding included a pre-seed round backed by Founders Factory.

Founders: CEO & Founder is Cat Jones, a former Managing Director at Founders Factory, focused on travel and media.

Others include: Luckytrip, Pilot, Journee, YGO Trips, Geovea, iPlan.ai, Wanderlog.

Overall, SaaS-first players had more friction (signups, questionnaire, apps) that chatbot players, before giving value to travellers. But they were often targeted real, monetizable pain points with greater precision from a variety of angles: Luckytrip, for example, auto-generated itineraries to “find your next trip in one tap.” Journee, booked out full-trips based on questionnaires and budgets.

But there are benefits to this model. Even early startups like Pilot and Wanderlog (though chatbot capabilities exist) seems to have intentionally opted in for SaaS-like interfaces for at least two reasons:

More deterministic: Developers have exact control of parameters and outcomes, which leads to precise, reliable, consistent outcomes for consumers. In contrast, the predictive nature of LLMs means varying outcomes and holds risks of hallucinations.

Lower variable cost: SaaS has a near zero marginal cost problem, and subscriptions tend to be the simpler, more profitable model (ARR). In contrast,

Every user query to an LLM-based chatbot incurs marginal cost due to API tokens on foundational models (OpenAI, Gemini) and vector databases (Pinecone, Qdrant). That means costs scale with revenue, which requires complex usage forecasts and rate limit pricing for profitability.


Source: a16z
However, both of these problems will likely disappear as model performance improves and inference costs fall. Aside from pure technological advancements, newer applications increasingly use sophisticated optimizations for cost and performance by dynamically adjusting model size, latency tolerance, and fine-tuning.

Is the Ceiling Capped?
The edge, in my view, is having identified a niche of problems that consumers care about and building data to truly delight customers. That advantage in niches probably holds in the short-term even against general-purpose models like ChatGPT or large OTAs with metasearch and agents.

However, the startup game is to monopolize a niche, and then expand. In the long run, as AI infra cost drops and UX standardizes, these players may have to rethink about the infrastructure from the ground up based on AI capability and user needs, rather than “adding” AI into their existing tech stack. Otherwise, the ceiling seems capped as large OTAs and LLM models also innovate in this space, solving broader, non-niche problems.

3. Content-first: Attention as the Moat
While most travel startups focus on product—better interfaces, workflows, or automation—content-first companies take a different route: Attention. Having built my own startup in the creator economy, I’m bullish on this thesis as well.

The bet seems to be that inspiration drives intent. While the data is mixed, some research claims up to 75% of travel inspiration starts with social content. You win by showing up where attention already lives—short-form video, creator networks, or embedded media inside apps people use daily.


Source: Screenshot of Steller
Product, infra, and AI exist downstream—to convert attention into action. It’s probably the bet that distribution is more defensible than features, and that whoever connects inspiration with transaction first, wins.

Here are a few players:

Steller: US storytelling travel platform uses short-form video content with booking functionality. Consumers can directly add places from videos into itineraries. Doubling down on content, Steller acquired Travel Mindset, an influencer marketing platform. Partnering with Viator and Booking.com for booking, Steller uses AI to fill "data gaps" layering accommodations, dining, and activities into recommendations with their creator data on top of public data. In a private chat with Steller (disclosing with permission), they also shared Birdie, an in-app chatbot to support itineraries.

Funding: Over $42 million raised including their Series-B, the thesis is to use content to surface local users for “human expertise” and AI to reduce workflow frictions.

Founders: Co-founded and led by Pete Bryant, previously a managing partner at Pioneer Ventures and Richard McAniff, former Corporate VP at Microsoft.

Unravel: UK/Singapore AI-powered video commerce platform dubbed the "TikTok of Travel" that transforms creator-driven travel content into booking experiences. Unravel partners with influential travel creators to curate authentic video content into transactions, now offering white-label services for businesses.

Funding: Raised $7 million Series A led by Nauta Capital, with participation from Active Partners, Slingshot Ventures, and notable investors including Olivier Bisserier (ex-CFO Booking).

Founders: Founded in 2020 by Vijay Anand (IBM), Abhimanyu Dasgupta (Deloitte), and Sunny Swetank (IBM).

Others include: Polarsteps, OfftheGrid.

While chatbot and SaaS-first startups begin with product, content-first startups begin with attention. Distribution is a moat. And in an AI-enabled world, owning the demand layer can become more valuable and defensible than owning commoditized models or API aggregations.

Even as generative AI reduces the cost of content creation—and even threatens to displace some creators—the advantage of controlling distribution likely grows stronger. Owning an engaged audience (or a network of creators with reach) compounds. If anyone can easily create content, that trust becomes more important.

That’s why almost every player, including incumbents, is racing toward this space—Mindtrip acquired Thatch; Layla and Guidegeek launched natively on Instagram. Even so, the attention economy is changing, with micro-creators and community-first trends rising ahead of traditional creators.

That said, while content-first platforms may dominate the top of the funnel, its ability to convert to bookings is still unproven. Beautiful content doesn’t always map to real pain points, and short-form video doesn’t always inspire trust in high-consideration purchases. It’s unclear whether they solve a painkiller problem for travellers.

The long-term bet could be that content-first players who quickly build infrastructure—personalization, smart routing, creator payouts—could beat product-first players trying to tack on community and trust afterward.

But without solving for conversion, trust, and booking confidence, content may remain just that: top of funnel with lots of noise.

The Winners Will be Platforms Empowering Connectors to Scale Trust
These are early days of Travel x AI, but I’ll share a few of my observations and prediction from doing this exercise.

Companies Are Solving for Trust
Early-stage consumer AI companies are congregating around inspiration and planning. I don’t think these are the most painful problems - but they are the most buildable. From both a consumer perspective (after visiting 45+ countries) and as a former investor, it’s clear that trip planning is not the endgame. It’s the entry point. Founders building 24/7 likely know this.

So why start here?

Startups can only target a limited problem set in small niches early on—ones that don’t require extensive data partnerships, regulatory hurdles, or platform lock-in. That’s the startup game → Find a niche, iterate rapidly, grow out.


Source: Oliver Wyman
That’s probably why we don’t see early-stage companies solving for known pain points like predictive price models, end-to-end booking, full-loyalty integrations, price comparisons, and multi-currency bank accounts despite customers, better suited for larger players like OTAs and unicorns like Hopper, Super.com, Yanolja

So it makes sense that the products target the easier problems solvable with good LLM wrappers, APIs, and a clean UI. But what are they solving for eventually?

Trust.

Inspiration and itinerary tools serve as lightweight ways to earn permission. They're the first impression. Despite heavy use of social media, surveys suggest still about half of travel inspirations comes from family and friends — people who we trust.


Source: McKinsey
My Hypothesis: Empowering Connectors and Specialists
If I had to bet on defensible moats in Travel × AI, I’d back platforms that empower Connectors and Specialists — tools that let individuals scale trust and deliver value within communities.

In The Tipping Point, Malcolm Gladwell draws on Stanley Milgram’s Small World Experiment to explain epidemic theory. He argue that not all degrees of separation are equal. Some people — Connectors — serve as epicenters of influence, holding an outsized number of weak ties. Others — Specialists — feed them niche, high-signal information.

Today, those roles map to creators, entrepreneurs, and locals:

The friend who knows all the spots

I’m not alone here, and this aligns with Forerunner’s broader thesis: backing companies that enable others (e.g., Shopify empowering merchants, Stan empowering creators) beyond the travel market.

Fora is a standout example. Instead of relying on AI-generated content or influencer campaigns, Fora equips individuals to act as travel advisors. These people aren’t entertainers — they are embedded in real social circles, trusted by friends and family.

Fora uses AI to amplify human trust at scale, enabling these advisors to reach thousands of micro-communities. Steller is heading in the same direction, building creator storefronts for monetization.

This is fundamentally different from entertainment trust (“I enjoy your content”) — travel demands execution trust (“I’ll spend thousands and risk my vacation on your rec”). And unlike one-off mercenary influencer deals, this model is sticky. Trust compounds.

In the long run, the real opportunity isn’t in planning or inspiration. It’s in scaling trust. Seen through this lens, these startups aren’t solving for trip complexity — they’re solving for confidence.

And if users trust you with their inspiration, maybe they’ll trust you with their wallet, and eventually, with their data: the one problem no startup has cracked.

Cracking the Bit by Bit Problem
How do you get all aspects of the consumer to make a truly frictionless, end-to-end experience? You eventually need the full stack of data. That includes:

Hard information: Credit card number, passport, global entry memberships, address, phone number, insurance files, loyalty numbers, visas, etc.

Soft information: Travel style, budgets, group dynamics, travel patterns, etc.

Upload happens bit by bit.

It’s not you know, that you plug your brain in one day. But you will talk to ChatGPT over the course of your life.

- Sam Altman

There’s no easy way to upload this (maybe syncing all my iPhone images?), and will need to happen bit by bit, one trip at a time. And no customer is going to give up their personal data unless they have trust. Oliver Wyman’s survey echoes these intuitions:


Source: Oliver Wyman
Companies that give delightful experiences for consumers over and over again, eventually building loyalty, and necessary data for every edge case they can build truly exceptional experiences. But I’m unsure if current startups using chatbots, SaaS, or content are the best interfaces to do so.

Learning phase of optimizing interfaces
Currently, all companies are broadly aligning to existing user behaviors:

Chatbot-first → LLM interfaces like ChatGPT

SaaS-first → Typical software input with AI enablement

Content-first → Social interfaces like Instagram reels or Tinder

While that makes from a product development perspective, I’m not sure if it is fully optimized and accounts for (1) future capabilities of AI and (2) customer pain points.

Taking YC’s lessons to “start with your own problems,” here’s a few quick ones that immediately come to mind of personal pain points.

Voice integration: Chatbots on all sorts of platforms (Slack, Whatsapp, Instagram) have been integrated, but voice still seems relatively unexplored. I talk much faster than I can type, and I can run through scenarios and collaboration in trip planning through conversations. One pain point of calling up Amex Concierge is that they lose all context of my previous inquiries, and I have to take time to verify my identity and share context again. Being able to converse and lock in bookings (with budgets and permission) on the spot could be helpful.

Personal agents as the middle layer: Related to the bit-by-bit problem, sharing personal information every time I use a new app (every airline app, hotel app, and OTAs) is annoying. For example, I had to download Turkish Airlines and Emirates app and input all information when I did my trips there. A middle layer of a personal agent with all my personal information like an EA should be able to share my information for services that I approve.

SEO to GEO: Search Engine Optimization (SEO) to Generative Engine Optimization (GEO) transition is not a new concept, with dozens of companies entering the space. Even as I’m writing this piece, one source over another is being prioritized from my GPT and Claude queries. Perhaps interfaces will be replaced by OTA inventories and partnership with ChatGPTs of the world. Perhaps startups enabling GEO for companies will emerge. Or maybe deep advertising wars on various general-purposes LLMs will emerge.


Source: a16z
These are, by no means, exhaustive, and in reality it may be a mixture of interfaces. While I’m sure exactly what interfaces will be optimal, I’m pretty confident that we haven’t tapped out experimenting with possible interfaces.

That’s one of the drivers as to why I’m sharing my ideas publicly.

Why Am I Writing This?
Travel x AI is still in the earliest days, and will evolve rapidly as AI starts adding real productivity into our economy and time. Inspiration and planning is the first real piece of my analysis of the 7 segments I’ve been examining, including innovations from incumbents and startups giving “peace of mind” to travellers.

The analysis is far from completely, and certainly not perfect. Even if I had considerable time, my bet is that we’d all have a better shot at guessing and shaping the future of travel with collaboration publicly than in siloes.

My intent is not to speculate or give prescriptions, but to start with my initial hypotheses and collaborate with operators, investors, and founders who are building the next generation of travel. I’d love to explore with all of you.

Please reach out if I’ve missed your company or if you want to collaborate!

If you found this insightful, please share the ideas so we come up with the best ones!

Mr. White, here’s the gist:

- **Core thesis:** The real moat in “Travel × AI” won’t be clever itinerary planners—it’ll be platforms that earn *trust* and let “connectors” (travel-obsessed friends, advisors, creators) scale that trust across micro-communities. Planning tools are just the foot in the door.

- **Three startup clusters:**
  1. **Chatbot-first** (e.g., Layla, Mindtrip) – LLM wrappers that wow at the inspiration stage but risk being commoditized by OTAs or general agents as model costs drop.
  2. **SaaS-first** (e.g., Fora, Byway) – AI bolted onto traditional software to streamline real workflows; more deterministic and profit-friendly but may hit a ceiling without re-architecting for future AI capabilities.
  3. **Content-first** (e.g., Steller, Unravel) – Lead with short-form video and creator networks; attention is the moat, but turning eyeballs into high-trust bookings is still unproven.

- **Why everyone starts with inspiration:** It’s buildable with APIs + LLMs and sidesteps heavy data or regulatory barriers. The goal is to collect “micro-permissions” trip by trip until users hand over the deep personal data needed for seamless, full-stack travel.

- **What wins long-term:** Platforms that empower niche experts and community connectors, compounding execution-level trust. Once you own that trust, you can monetize bookings and eventually control the user’s travel graph (wallet, passport, loyalty numbers, preferences).

- **Open fronts for innovation:** Voice-native agents, a personal-data middle layer that autofills every service, and the coming fight to optimize content for generative search (SEO → GEO).

Bottom line: Travel-AI startups that stop at “smart trip planner” will get squeezed. The ones that weaponize human trust—and use AI to scale it—have the durable edge.
