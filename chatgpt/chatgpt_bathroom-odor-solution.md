Guys and girls bathrooms tend to start to reek when we don't have shows for like a few days, four or five days. So if we do a show on Sunday and then we don't open again until Thursday, by the time you come around Thursday, the bathrooms smell. And so if you go through and you flush all the toilets and urinals and run all the sinks, it resolves it pretty quickly. ## Assistant

Mr. White — your diagnosis is right, with one small correction: it’s “P-traps,” not “pee traps.” When restrooms sit idle for a few days, the water in P-traps (including floor drains) evaporates and lets sewer gas leak in. Re-running water quickly restores the seal. citeturn4view0turn5view0

Here’s a tight SOP your rotating kitchen crew can follow during bathroom cleaning after ≥48 hours of downtime:

- Flush every toilet and urinal twice.  
- Run each sink (hot + cold) for 30–60 seconds.  
- Don’t forget floor drains: pour ~½–1 gallon of water into each. Optional: add 2–4 oz mineral oil to slow evaporation between shows.  
- Switch on the restroom exhaust fan for ~30 minutes.  
(Why this works: it re-primes traps so odors can’t pass.) citeturn2view0turn6view0

Extras (to reduce repeat issues):
- If you have trap primers on floor drains, make sure they work; they’re designed to keep seldom-used traps wet automatically. citeturn3view0  
- Mineral/vegetable oil in unused traps is an accepted method to slow trap seal evaporation; some codes even note it explicitly. citeturn2view0turn1search21

If the smell persists after re-priming traps, check common non-trap causes (bad toilet wax ring, blocked vent, cracked line) and call a plumber. citeturn7view0

- USC EHS: dried P-traps are the most common building odor source; monthly water top-offs prevent it. citeturn4view0
