## Assistant

**Linear onboarding** generally refers to an **onboarding flow that follows a straight, fixed sequence of steps** that every new user or employee moves through from start to finish. In product/UX terms it’s a **step-by-step guided process designed to get new users up and running quickly** by leading them through essential setup and learning tasks in order. citeturn0search20turn0search10

### What it means
• **Sequential steps:** Users complete a predefined set of actions one after another — e.g., sign up → verify email → set preferences → invite teammates — with little variation in path. citeturn0search15  
• **Goal-oriented:** The goal is to help users reach “activation” or initial value as fast as possible by exposing key features in a simple order. citeturn0search20  
• **Common in SaaS:** Many apps use linear onboarding for simplicity, especially early in the user journey. citeturn0search1

### Pros
- Clear and predictable for users.  
- Easy to design and measure.  
- Good for straightforward products or first-time users.

### Cons
- Can feel rigid or irrelevant to experienced users.  
- Treats all users the same, even if they have different goals.  
- Too many steps can increase drop-off. citeturn0search10

In contrast, **adaptive or conditional onboarding** changes based on user behavior, role, or needs rather than forcing everyone through the same sequence. citeturn0search10

If you meant **Linear (the product)** onboarding specifically, it’s a streamlined first-time user experience in the Linear app that walks new teams through setup with one input per step to quickly get their workspace ready. citeturn0search0
