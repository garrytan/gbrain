## Assistant

Your hypothesis is directionally correct, but the timeline is more nuanced.

The industry moved heavily toward real locations from roughly the late 1960s through the 2000s because of:
- lighter cameras
- faster film stocks
- Steadicam
- portable lighting
- sync sound improvements
- digital cinematography

But in the last ~10 years, the pendulum partially swung back toward stages because of:
- LED volumes / virtual production
- tax-incentivized mega stages
- streaming production scale
- VFX-heavy workflows
- weather/schedule control
- union and insurance costs

So the arc looks more like:

| Era | Dominant Approach |
|---|---|
| 1930s–1950s | Mostly stages/backlots |
| 1960s–2000s | Increasingly real locations |
| 2015–present | Hybrid: locations + controlled stages/LED volumes |

- move through crowds

## 10 years ago (2010s)

Probably peak location flexibility.

Key drivers:
- RED and ARRI Alexa digital cameras
- ISO performance exploded
- handheld gimbals
- drones
- wireless monitoring
- tiny crews

You suddenly could:
- shoot in cars
- shoot in natural light
- run-and-gun quickly
- avoid giant stage builds

## Important caveat

Interiors are still very often stages.

Even productions that feel “real location heavy” frequently do:
- exterior = real location
- interior = stage recreation

because stages give:
- removable walls
- ceiling rigging
- sound control
- lighting control
- repeatability

That has remained true for decades. citeturn0search9turn0search10

## Net answer

Your core thesis is broadly true:
- portable cameras and stabilization tech massively increased location shooting from the 1970s onward.

But:
- modern VFX and virtual production are partially reversing that trend for blockbuster filmmaking.

So the real pattern is:
1. Classic Hollywood = controlled stages
2. New Hollywood + digital revolution = locations
3. Modern franchise era = hybrid virtualized stages + selective locations
