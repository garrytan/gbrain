# Engadget Used to Make the Future Feel Playable. Now It Often Reads Like a Warning Label.

There was a version of Engadget that made the internet feel bigger.

For a generation of readers, it was a daily portal into the future: weird phones, translucent laptops, early tablets, improbable robots, game consoles, cameras, headphones, EVs, concept hardware, prototypes from trade shows, and devices nobody needed yet but everyone wanted to understand. The site had a basic posture toward technology: **look at this thing someone made**. That posture mattered. It treated invention as culturally important before the rest of the world did.

That is why the current version feels so deflating.

A quick scan of Engadget today still shows plenty of product coverage. There are headphone reviews, Acer gaming hardware, Android car-key explainers, Rivian launch news, Waymo robotaxi coverage, Intel handheld gaming chips, space stories, and consumer-electronics announcements. Engadget has not stopped covering technology. The shift is subtler. The highest-friction stories, especially around AI and tech power, often arrive with a tone of exhaustion, suspicion, or pre-loaded contempt. The site still reports on the future; it often sounds tired of it. citeturn339864view0turn711765view0

The AI coverage is the clearest case study. In Engadget’s current weekly AI archive, there are 11 AI stories. By headline-and-deck coding, most are framed around litigation, misuse, backlash, permission disputes, cultural degradation, or embarrassing failures. The list includes Perplexity being sued by CNN, Paramount using AI for an ugly Star Trek thumbnail, Stan Lee’s likeness being revived through AI, Pope Leo warning about AI risks, Ansel Adams permission issues, people recreating the voices of pilots killed in a crash, and Google struggling with definitions because of AI Overviews. The more constructive entries are there too: local AI chatbots on iPhone, DeepSeek cutting model prices, and Anthropic finding vulnerabilities. The ratio is the story. citeturn689083view0

This is not a demand for cheerleading. Tech journalism should scrutinize companies, founders, incentives, privacy failures, platform abuse, labor practices, data breaches, vaporware, scams, monopolies, and regulatory capture. A publication that treats every founder keynote as gospel is useless. The issue is posture. Skepticism is a tool. Cynicism is an editorial identity.

The difference shows up in verbs and adjectives. An AI feature becomes “slop.” A bad generated image becomes proof that AI is culturally corrosive. A chatbot failure becomes a referendum on the entire field. A founder’s moonshot becomes a “scheme.” A product decision becomes an occasion to dunk. That language may win clicks. It also trains the audience to approach the most consequential technology shift in decades as a blooper reel with lawsuits attached.

Elon Musk coverage illustrates the broader problem. Musk deserves hard coverage. Tesla, SpaceX, X, xAI, Neuralink, government contracts, securities disputes, labor disputes, and AI safety issues all deserve scrutiny. Recent Engadget coverage includes straightforward reporting on Musk’s SEC settlement over his Twitter stake. It also includes more loaded language around Musk’s lunar satellite-launch idea and skeptical framing around Tesla reallocating Model S/X production capacity toward Optimus robots. The factual basis may be real. The tonal center is the issue. citeturn818779view2turn818779view0turn818779view3

A better tech press would ask harder questions and more interesting ones.

What can local AI actually do on a phone today? What would it take for AI agents to become trustworthy enough for real work? Which humanoid-robot tasks are economically plausible this decade? What are the real bottlenecks in battery chemistry, inference cost, robot dexterity, autonomous vehicles, AR displays, chip packaging, satellite broadband, and synthetic media provenance? Which demos are fake? Which ugly prototypes are five years away from changing an industry? Which unpopular founders are still building important things? Which beloved companies are coasting?

That is the kind of curiosity that made old tech media valuable. It separated “this is flawed” from “this is irrelevant.” It understood that early products are often awkward because the future usually arrives in embarrassing form. The first MP3 players were clunky. Early smartphones were compromised. EVs were range-anxiety machines. VR was always “almost there.” AI image generation looked like a novelty until creative workflows changed. Local models looked weak until the economics of private inference started to matter.

The current Engadget still has flashes of that older instinct. Coverage of Intel’s Arc G-Series chips points toward a new generation of handheld gaming PCs. The Waymo Ojai story is about a purpose-built robotaxi. The Space section includes James Webb discoveries, lunar mission planning, and Psyche images from Mars. There is still curiosity on the page. It is often quieter than the scolding. citeturn711765view0turn689083view1

The tragedy is that technology has never been more worth covering with seriousness. AI is changing software. Robots are leaving labs. EVs are forcing car companies to rethink manufacturing. Satellites are becoming infrastructure. Wearables are drifting toward medical relevance. Chips are geopolitics. Cameras, phones, search, entertainment, education, and work are all being rebuilt at once.

A tech journalist entering the field right now has one of the best jobs in media. The beat is full of conflict, absurdity, ambition, fraud, genius, power, and invention. That combination should produce wonder and interrogation in equal measure. Too much of the current tone across tech media converts wonder into derision before the reader has a chance to understand the thing itself.

Engadget was once great because it made new technology feel inspectable. It invited readers to press their faces against the glass. The best version of the site did not need every gadget to be world-changing. It understood that a hinge, a screen, a chipset, a weird UI, a prototype robot, or a slightly better pair of headphones could still tell you where things were going.

That spirit is worth recovering.

The future deserves critics. It also deserves witnesses who still want to see what happens next.
