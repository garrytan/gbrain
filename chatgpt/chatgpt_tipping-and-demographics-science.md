Yes, Mr. White. There is **some reliable science**, mostly from hospitality economics, social psychology, and labor research. The cleanest takeaway is: **demographics can predict average tipping behavior, but weakly enough that they are poor predictors for any individual customer or server.**

The strongest general finding is that tipping is heavily shaped by **social norms and bill size**. One Cornell review notes that bill size is the single largest predictor of U.S. restaurant tip amounts, with an average R┬▓ around .70, meaning it explains far more variation in dollar tips than most other factors combined. Service quality matters, but less than people think: a meta-analysis of 8 published and 6 unpublished studies covering 2,645 dining parties at 21 restaurants found a positive relationship between service ratings and tips, though the relationship was weak and highly variable. ţłÇciteţłéturn941755view2ţłéturn238546view2ţłü

On **customer demographics**, the most studied variable is race/ethnicity in U.S. restaurants. Multiple studies by Michael Lynn and coauthors report that Black and Hispanic customers, on average, have left smaller percentage tips than White customers in certain samples. The better interpretation is norm exposure rather than character: LynnÔÇÖs 2014 study found that awareness of the 15ÔÇô20% tipping norm partially mediated BlackÔÇôWhite and HispanicÔÇôWhite differences, and a 2015 paper found perceived descriptive and injunctive tipping norms mediated racial/ethnic differences in hypothetical tipping. ţłÇciteţłéturn941755view0ţłéturn238546view0ţłü

On **income**, the evidence is more intuitive and fairly plausible: higher-income customers generally tip more often and/or tip larger amounts, especially in settings where tipping is voluntary or optional. A 2024 international restaurant study under service-inclusive pricing found that income and payment method were the strongest predictors of whether guests tipped, while bill size was the strongest predictor of tip amount. ţłÇciteţłéturn238546view5ţłü

On **gender and age**, the science is weaker and more mixed. Some studies find sex differences in tipping norms or behavior, and some find small effects. The effects are usually smaller than bill size, service context, social norms, payment method, and country/culture. One study on self-attributed tipping motives found that demographic differences in motives were small. ţłÇciteţłéturn941755view4ţłü

On **server demographics**, there is credible evidence of bias. A Yale Law Journal study of more than 1,000 New Haven taxi rides found that, after controls, African-American cab drivers were tipped about one-third less than White cab drivers, and African-American and Hispanic passengers tipped about half as much as White passengers in that sample. A restaurant study by Lynn, Sturman, and colleagues similarly found that Black servers were tipped less than White servers after controlling for perceived service quality and other variables. ţłÇciteţłéturn941755view3ţłéturn238546view1ţłü

There is also evidence that **appearance and gender presentation** can affect server earnings. One study found larger sales-adjusted tips for attractive female servers, better service providers, and high self-monitors, though those effects held only for evening tips, not lunch tips. That makes it suggestive, rather than a universal rule. ţłÇciteţłéturn238546view7ţłü

The reliability caveat is important: tipping data are messy. Many studies use surveys, hypothetical scenarios, single restaurants, or local samples. Real tip income is also poorly measured; a 2024 Census working paper found evidence consistent with systematic under-reporting of tip income. ţłÇciteţłéturn238546view4ţłü

**Bottom line:** there is real science behind demographic differences in tipping, especially around race/ethnicity, income, norms, and server race/appearance. The strongest practical conclusion is that tipping is driven more by **norms, bill size, payment design, culture, and context** than by demographics alone. Demographic averages exist, yet they are too noisy to justify predicting how a specific person will tip or treating customers differently.
