## Assistant

Mr. White, you saw a **triple trailer / LCV**: one tractor pulling **three short pup trailers**, usually around **28–28.5 ft each**. UPS/FedEx use them because parcels are often **volume-limited**, so three pups move a ton of cubic space with one driver.

How it works mechanically:

The tractor pulls the first trailer on the normal fifth wheel. Behind that, each extra trailer is connected with a **converter dolly**: a little axle-and-hitch unit with its own fifth wheel. So the setup is:

**tractor → trailer → dolly → trailer → dolly → trailer**

The air brakes and electrical lines are chained through the whole set. The rear trailers follow, but they have more sway and “crack-the-whip” risk, so the driver has to be smooth and avoid sudden lane changes.

How they get off the highway:

They usually **do not take all three into city streets**. They run on approved highways, then pull into a FedEx/UPS terminal, truck stop, toll-road plaza, or staging yard. There they “break the set”: drop trailer 3, drop trailer 2, move each pup individually with a tractor or yard truck. Backing up a full triple is basically avoided; if they need to maneuver tightly, they split it apart.

Legally, there are two categories:

**Two short trailers** — the classic double pups — are broadly allowed on the National Network. Federal rules protect 28 ft trailers, 28.5 ft grandfathered trailers, and prevent states from banning truck-tractor/semitrailer-trailer combinations on those routes. Nevada is one of the places where what you saw makes total sense. Federal rules list Nevada triples at **95 ft cargo-carrying length** and **129,000 lb max gross weight**, with routes tied to Nevada’s approved LCV routes. Nevada’s own LCV definition includes a truck-tractor with **two or three trailers**, over **70 ft**, with gross weight up to **129,000 lb**. citeturn686738view1turn522294view3
