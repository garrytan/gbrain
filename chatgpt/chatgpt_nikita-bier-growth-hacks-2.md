### 1. Build for **latent demand**  
- Bier emphasises identifying *what the user is really trying to do*, not just what you think they want. citeturn0search1turn0search6  
- He recommends: find a small but intense pain point, build around it, make adoption easy and repeatable. citeturn0search1turn0search4  
- Example: His app entity["mobile_app","TBH",0] targeted teenagers giving compliments — a simple function that met a latent social desire. citeturn0search6turn0search1  
**Action for you:** Ask “What are users doing repeatedly that they *hate* doing?” or “What are they doing happily that we can make easier or social?” Then build into that.

---

### 2. Saturate and reinforce your message  
- Bier says a user typically needs to see your message ~3 times before converting (at least in mobile/consumer apps) because trust and familiarity matter. citeturn0search1turn0search6  
- He used geofencing (“restricted access” in a region) to create urgency + word-of-mouth. For TBH: 5M users in 2 months with essentially $0 marketing. citeturn0search6turn0search1  
**Action:** Design your funnel so that the user sees your core value ~3-4 times in quick succession, via controlled channels. Use “invite friends” or localization to amplify.

---

### 3. Embed share/invite loops in-product  
- Growth isn’t just external marketing – the product itself must encourage sharing or social spread. Bier builds apps where users bring in other users. citeturn0search1turn0search6  
- He warns: If the viral loop is weak or requires too much friction, growth stalls. citeturn0search4  
**Action:** Make every new user’s first experience include an easy way to bring someone else in. Example: “Tag a friend” or “Share with your group”.

---

### 4. Reproducible testing process + eliminate confounders  
- Bier stresses that you should know *immediately* whether a test is working. If there’s “uncertainty,” your process isn’t clean. citeturn0search1  
- He suggests minimal variables per experiment, clear metrics, fast iteration.  
**Action:** Set up your analytics so you can run A/B tests quickly. Define success metrics (e.g., invite rate, retention after 7 days). Run small tests, pick winners, scale.

---

### 5. Leverage demographics / social graph characteristics  
- Bier notes: For teen-focused apps, the fact users *see each other everyday* improves virality dramatically. citeturn0search1turn0search6  
- Choose segmentation where network effects exist — e.g., friend groups in schools, workplaces, tight communities.  
**Action:** Identify a user segment with high density and frequent interaction, design features that exploit that (e.g., leaderboard vs friend group, gifting, shout-out).

---

### 6. Low friction onboarding and access constraints  
- The easier it is for a user to begin, the faster growth. Bier engineered minimal friction. citeturn0search6turn0search4  
- Also, sometimes he used “restricted access” (limited launch/go live in a geo) to create scarcity and FOMO. citeturn0search6  
**Action:** Remove unnecessary steps for sign-up. Possibly use “invite only” or “launch in one city first” to build story and momentum.

---

### 7. Metrics matter: retention > acquisition  
- Growth isn’t just about getting users; it's about keeping them. Bier emphasises that if users aren’t returning, the growth hack won’t hold. citeturn0search1  
**Action:** Track retention (day 1, day 7, day 30) and make sure your growth loops also enhance retention rather than just acquisition.

---

### 8. Be aware of platform/permission changes & ethics  
- One critique of Bier’s earlier work: apps that asked for full contact lists to drive growth faced backlash when platforms changed policies. citeturn0search5turn0search6  
**Action:** Growth tactics must be durable — consider privacy, permission constraints, platform policy changes. Build ethical and future-proof loops.
