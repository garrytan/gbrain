## Assistant

Here’s a quick Chris-Voss-style counter you can send back:

---

**“I totally get you want to move it fast. I can swing by today with cash. Would you be open to **$200** so we can both save time?”**  

*(If he pushes back, stay calm and mirror.)*  
- “Hmm… $250?”  
- “Seems like you’ve had a lot of interest—what’s the best you could do if I’m there in the next hour?”  

---

Key tactics:
- **Calibrated question** (“What’s the best you could do…”) forces him to justify his number.  
- **Anchoring low but reasonable** ($200 instead of his $300 or your $150) makes your real ceiling feel like a win.  
- **Time leverage** (“today with cash”) adds urgency.

Send it and let the silence work for you.

## Assistant

Got it—you’re the seller. Here’s a Chris Voss-style play to keep control and still pull the buyer up:

---

**1. Label & Mirror**  
“Sounds like you’re working with a tight budget?”  
*(Pause and let them talk. People reveal more when you label their position.)*

**2. Calibrated Question**  
“How can we get closer to the $300 I listed so it works for both of us?”  
*(Shifts the burden back to them to find a path up.)*

**3. Strategic Anchor**  
“I’ve already had interest near asking, so $150 isn’t something I can do. What’s the best number you can bring me today?”  
*(You’ve re-anchored around your price and invited a higher counter.)*

**4. Create Urgency**  
“I can hold it until this evening if you can meet me closer to my number, otherwise I’ll need to keep it available.”  

---

Stay calm, let silence hang after each question, and you’ll nudge them upward without ever sounding combative.

## Assistant

Here’s a Chris-Voss-style response you (or a bartender/server) can use when a guest complains about the cocktail prices at The Vault:

---

**1. Label their concern**  
“It sounds like you weren’t expecting the prices to be this high.”

*(Pause. Let them respond—silence is power.)*

**2. Mirror**  
“They feel higher than what you’ve seen elsewhere?”  
*(Repeat a key word/phrase they use. Keeps them talking and defuses tension.)*

**3. Calibrated question**  
“What would make a $16 cocktail feel worth it to you tonight?”  
*(Invites them to explain value gaps instead of just arguing about cost.)*

**4. Offer context without defending**  
“We use fresh-squeezed juices and premium spirits in every drink, and the show itself is included in the experience.”  
*(Facts, not excuses—presented after they’ve talked.)*

**5. Close with choice**  
“I want you to have a great night—would you like me to recommend something that gives the most bang for the buck, or would you like to see our two-for-one special after the show?”  

---

This keeps the vibe friendly, acknowledges the complaint, and steers the conversation toward options instead of a price fight—classic Voss “tactical empathy.”
