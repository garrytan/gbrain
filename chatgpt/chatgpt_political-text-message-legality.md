## Assistant

Mr. White, the annoying answer is: **some of this is legal, some may be illegal, and the “paid for by” issue is the strongest reporting angle if the texts lack attribution.**

## 1. Do Not Call list probably does not help

Political campaign calls/texts are generally treated differently from commercial telemarketing. The National Do Not Call Registry is aimed at telemarketing, and political outreach is generally outside that registry’s core ban. The FCC still regulates political robocalls/robotexts under TCPA rules, but **being on Do Not Call does not automatically make political texts illegal**. citeturn111561search0turn111561search21

## 2. Consent depends on whether the text is automated

For cell phones, political **robotexts** using an autodialer or automated texting system generally require prior express consent. Political messages are not exempt from the TCPA simply because they are political. citeturn111561search0

The campaigns are likely trying to fit into the **peer-to-peer/manual texting** bucket: a volunteer or paid texter clicks send one-by-one from software. That structure is often used because it may avoid the TCPA’s automated-dialer restrictions. Your instinct is right: the “volunteer hitting spacebar” model exists for exactly this reason.

The legal fight usually becomes factual:

| Question | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| Was a human actually sending each message? | Helps campaign argue it was manual/P2P. |
| Was the system generating or dialing numbers automatically? | Could trigger TCPA exposure. |
| Did you ever give consent to a party, petition, campaign, PAC, nonprofit, donation page, voter tool, etc.? | Campaign may claim consent or data sharing. |
| Did they keep texting after STOP? | Stronger complaint fact. |

## 3. “Reply STOP” does not prove you opted in

A STOP line is common compliance hygiene. It does **not** prove you consented. It also does **not** cure a prior unlawful automated text. It mainly gives them an opt-out mechanism.

Practically, reply **STOP** once. Then screenshot any later texts from the same sender/campaign/PAC/vendor. Continued texts after opt-out are better evidence.

## 4. “Paid for by” disclosure: likely required in California for mass-distribution campaign texts

For California state/local campaigns, the FPPC says campaign ads must identify who paid for or authorized the communication, and the basic disclosure is “Paid for by [committee name]” or “Ad paid for by [committee name].” citeturn687739view2

More specifically, the FPPC’s 2025 chart for candidate committees says **text messages sent using mass distribution technology** must include either:

- **“Paid for by”** followed by the candidate name and office sought, or
- **“With”** followed by the candidate name and office sought, with sender-identification rules.

It also says that for a same-day text exchange, the disclosure is required on the **first** message that supports or opposes a candidate or measure. citeturn104111view0

So if these are California candidate/ballot-measure texts using mass-distribution tech and they omit any sponsor/committee/candidate disclosure, that is a plausible FPPC complaint.

## 5. Federal campaign texts are murkier on disclaimers

For federal candidates/PACs, FEC rules require disclaimers on covered public communications, certain mass emails, websites, solicitations, and paid internet communications. The FEC defines public communications to include things like broadcast, newspaper, outdoor ads, mass mailings, phone banks, and paid internet/platform communications. citeturn687739view1

A plain SMS text is not as cleanly spelled out as California’s “text messages sent using mass distribution technology” rule. If the text solicits money, links to a donation page, is sent by a political committee, or is part of a paid platform campaign, there may still be FEC disclosure issues. For federal texts, report it to the FEC if there is no sponsor identification, but the California FPPC route is cleaner for CA state/local races.

## 6. Reporting steps

### Preserve evidence first

For each text, save:

- Screenshot showing the full message, phone number, date/time.
- Any link in the message.
- Whether it names a candidate, ballot measure, PAC, or committee.
- Whether it says “Paid for by,” “Ad paid for by,” “With [candidate],” etc.
- Your STOP reply and any later messages.

### Report unwanted/spam texts

1. **Forward the message to 7726** — that reports it to your carrier as spam. The FTC recommends forwarding unwanted texts to 7726/SPAM. citeturn687739view3  
2. **File an FCC complaint** under unwanted calls/texts. FCC complaints help enforcement and trend tracking. citeturn687739view4  
3. **Report to the FTC** at ReportFraud if the message looks deceptive, scammy, or phishing-related. citeturn687739view3  
4. **For California state/local campaigns:** file with the **California FPPC**, especially if there is no “Paid for by” disclosure. The FPPC regulates California campaign-ad disclosure rules. citeturn687739view2  
5. **For federal candidates/PACs:** file with the **FEC**, especially if the message solicits donations, advocates for/against a federal candidate, or hides who paid for it. citeturn687739view1  
6. **For LA city races:** also check the **Los Angeles City Ethics Commission**, since city campaign rules may apply.

## Practical read

Your strongest complaint is:

> “I received unsolicited political text messages using apparent mass-distribution technology. The message advocated for a candidate/measure and did not disclose who paid for or authorized it.”

Your TCPA claim is stronger if:

> “I never consented, the message appears automated, and they continued texting after I replied STOP.”

Your Do Not Call argument is weaker because political campaign outreach has broader exemptions than commercial telemarketing.
