Mr. White, here’s the concise, fact-focused explanation you’re looking for.

**“Perturbation” (as used by Dr. Mike Israetel) refers to the *stimulus disruption* that training imposes on the body — the degree to which training meaningfully disturbs homeostasis and forces adaptation.**

### What it indicates
In RP Strength terminology, **perturbation = quality of the hypertrophic stimulus**.  
A set with *high perturbation* is one that:

- Uses sufficient **load**  
- Is taken **close to failure** (high proximity to failure → high stimulus)  
- Involves **stable, consistent technique** so the target muscle is the one being overloaded  
- Produces visible **reps-in-reserve decline** across sets  
- Leads to **local muscle fatigue** rather than systemic sloppiness

**Low perturbation**, on the other hand, means the set didn’t challenge the muscle enough to meaningfully drive growth — often due to stopping too early, load too light, poor technique, or instability.

### Why it matters
Israetel uses perturbation to explain why:

- **Hard sets near failure** are the foundation of hypertrophy  
- **Progressive overload** must increase perturbation over time (more reps, weight, or closer-to-failure effort)  
- **Training volume** matters only if sets actually create meaningful perturbation; 10 weak sets aren’t better than 5 hard ones  
- **Junk volume** = sets with insufficient perturbation

### Simple rule of thumb  
A set has good perturbation if **you’re training within ~0–4 RIR with controlled technique** and the target muscle is clearly fatiguing.
