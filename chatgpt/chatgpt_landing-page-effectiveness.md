Mr. White, the data points in one direction:

John’s style can absolutely outperform cleaner, shorter pages in some contexts. It is not universally better. It tends to work best when the traffic is colder, the audience is more impulse- and promise-driven, the offer needs heavy persuasion, and the buyer is already acclimated to direct-response marketing. Cleaner, shorter pages tend to win when the audience is more product-aware, the brand is stronger, the product is easier to understand, and trust or long-term brand equity matters more than squeezing out maximum first-purchase conversion from cold traffic. citeturn264741search8turn216384search3turn216384search6

A few useful facts anchor this. Unbounce’s benchmark dataset, based on hundreds of millions of visits and tens of millions of conversions, puts the median landing-page conversion rate around 6.6% overall, with SaaS pages materially lower than some other categories. Unbounce also reported that more complex copy is hurting conversion more than it did a few years ago. Separately, Nielsen Norman Group’s long-running usability work found that people mostly scan pages, and that concise, scannable, objective writing improves measured usability versus dense promotional copy. citeturn264741search4turn264741search8turn216384search3turn216384search0

So the answer is not “long copy always wins.” The better rule is:

**Long copy wins when more persuasion is required.**  
That means cold traffic, expensive offers, weird/new offers, aspirational identity sales, or audiences trained by info-marketing funnels to expect long emotional setup, proof, bonuses, urgency, and repeated CTA exposure. In those cases, the page is doing the work of a salesperson. It is handling objections, creating desire, framing the price, and dragging a low-intent user toward action. That is why the old-school VSL / long-letter format still survives. citeturn140016search8turn216384search2

**Shorter copy wins when clarity is the main job.**  
If the user already wants “an AI assistant,” “team chat AI,” or “creator storefront,” then the page mainly needs to answer: what is it, what does it do, how much does it cost, and why should I trust it. Dense hype starts to feel like friction. For scanning users, white space, clean hierarchy, proof, product screenshots, and obvious pricing often help more than a wall of persuasion. citeturn216384search3turn216384search6turn264741search8

Why don’t more companies copy that style if it can convert?

Because conversion rate is not the only KPI.

A page can lift front-end conversion while lowering lead quality, increasing churn, increasing refunds, attracting the wrong customers, damaging brand perception, and making the company look cheap or manipulative. Conversion optimization literature keeps making this point: raw conversion rate by itself is an incomplete metric. The right question is contribution to durable revenue and good customers, not “did more people click buy today.” citeturn140016search0turn216384search8

There is also a customer-segmentation point here:

- Info-product buyers often tolerate, and sometimes expect, heavy persuasion.
- Startup, developer, and enterprise buyers often interpret the same signals as low trust, low product confidence, or scamminess.
- Creator-economy buyers are closer to direct-response culture than enterprise software buyers are.

That is why the same page can crush for one market and tank for another.

On the email side, the same rule applies. There is no universal result that “long emails convert better.” Email benchmarks are usually reported at the campaign level, not by copy length, and average email CTRs remain modest overall. The more defensible guidance is that long emails help when they need to sell an idea, tell a story, or overcome objections, while shorter emails usually work better for known brands, fast offers, and readers with existing intent. Fit matters more than length. citeturn264741search1turn140016search1turn140016search4

The simplest framework is:

**Use John-style direct response when**
- traffic is cold
- offer is emotionally charged or aspirational
- objections are numerous
- audience is creator/info-product adjacent
- you are willing to spend brand trust to raise immediate conversion

**Use OpenAI-style product-led pages when**
- traffic is warmer or branded
- product category is already understood
- trust and credibility matter more
- you want stronger long-term brand equity
- you need enterprise or mainstream appeal

My actual bet: for a broad software company, the winning setup is usually hybrid. Clean modern UX on top, concise product explanation first, then expandable depth below: proof, objections, FAQs, examples, pricing logic, customer results, maybe even founder-style narrative lower on the page. That gives scanners a fast path and lets persuadable users keep reading. It captures most of the upside of long copy without making the whole brand feel like an affiliate funnel.
