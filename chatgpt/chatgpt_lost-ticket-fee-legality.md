Mr. White — the legality of “lost ticket fees” (when you lose your parking-garage exit ticket and the garage charges you a fee) depends a lot on the nature of the garage (public vs. private), the posted terms, local law, and whether the fee was clearly disclosed. Here’s how the rule generally works in California / U.S. context.  

## ✅ When lost-ticket fees tend to be legal / enforceable  
- If you park in a **private garage or lot** (not a city/government lot), the owner has a contract with you when you enter: you’re agreeing to their terms (which often include paying a fee if you lose your ticket). - If the parking facility **clearly posts** the rate schedule (including “lost-ticket fee” or a “maximum daily rate if ticket lost”) at entrance or on your ticket, that transparency strengthens their contractual right to charge. Some local ordinances require a posted schedule of rates to be readable at the entrance. citeturn0search21turn0search16  
- In those cases, if you lose your ticket, the facility can demand the posted “lost-ticket” (or full-day) fee — and legally pursue it (or send to collections) if you don’t pay. citeturn0search16turn0search24  

## ⚠️ When lost-ticket fees might be contestable or weak legally  
- If it’s a **government-run or city parking facility** (vs. private), then fees/“fines” must follow statutory law; a garage can't arbitrarily impose a fee outside what law authorizes. Under California law, only authorized municipal processes can impose legally enforceable parking penalties/fines. citeturn0search7turn0search13turn0search9  
- If the garage did **not post the fee schedule or lost-ticket policy** at the entrance or otherwise clearly communicate it, you might argue there was no valid contract or notice — making the fee unenforceable. ## 🎯 What this means for you in Los Angeles  
- Many parking garages in LA are privately operated: if they properly post their rates and lost-ticket policy, the fee is likely valid (i.e. a contractual obligation).  
- If you believe the fee was not properly disclosed (no sign, unclear language, no printed policy with your ticket), you may have a credible argument to contest the fee — though success may depend on evidence (photos of signage, ticket stub, etc.).  
- Charges from private garages typically do **not** result in criminal fines or DMV actions like official parking citations do. citeturn0search16turn0search18
