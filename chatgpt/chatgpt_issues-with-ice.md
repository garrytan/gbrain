  2. **Non-criminal aliens** – non-citizens with no criminal record, whose only violation is *civil immigration status*.  

So when data show that “most arrests are of non-criminals,” it doesn’t mean ICE is grabbing citizens; it means they’re arresting people *solely* for being in the country without lawful status, not for committing any separate criminal act.  

**Bottom line:**  
- Illegal presence in the U.S. is *not* a criminal offense under federal law; it’s a *civil* violation.  
- ICE still has the authority to detain and remove those individuals, but critics argue that doing so en masse—especially when there’s no accompanying crime—diverts resources from serious offenders and raises proportionality concerns.

That’s a rational point, Mr. White — and you’re right that **entering or remaining in a country without permission** is indeed a *violation of law*.  

The nuance is **how U.S. law classifies that violation**:  

- **Illegal entry (crossing the border without inspection)** — *is* a **federal misdemeanor** under 8 U.S.C. § 1325(a).  
  - First offense: up to 6 months in jail.  
  - Subsequent entries: can become a felony under § 1326.  
- **Unlawful presence (overstaying a visa)** — *is not a crime* under U.S. law; it’s a **civil immigration violation** handled through administrative removal, not criminal court.  

So, under U.S. legal doctrine, someone can be **in the country unlawfully but not be a “criminal alien”** — because their case belongs to the civil immigration system, not the criminal one.  

Your France analogy fits morally and practically — any sovereign state can expel or detain unauthorized entrants. The controversy with ICE isn’t *whether* they can enforce immigration law (they clearly can); it’s *how broadly and aggressively* they do so inside the U.S. — for example, arresting long-term residents with families and no criminal record, or conducting workplace raids far from the border.  

In short:  
- **Legally:** unauthorized presence ≠ felony crime.  
- **Philosophically:** yes, they broke immigration law, and nations have every right to enforce it.  
- **Policy debate:** whether ICE’s enforcement methods are proportionate, targeted, and humane.

Fair point, Mr. White — your instinct (break the rule, get removed) is simple and understandable. Here’s the concise, factual picture of how the U.S. system actually handles that idea and why it’s more complicated than “kick ’em out” in practice:

1. **Removal is the primary remedy, but it’s civil**  
   - Most immigration violations are dealt with in civil *removal (deportation) proceedings*, not criminal courts. The end result you want — returning someone to their country — *is* the outcome ICE seeks in many cases.

2. **There are multiple enforcement paths**  
   - **Expedited removal / reinstatement** (fast removals at or near the border, or for people who re-entered after prior removal).  
   - **Formal removal proceedings** before an immigration judge (longer process, opportunity to apply for relief).  
   - **Criminal prosecution** only for a subset (e.g., illegal entry under 8 U.S.C. §1325, reentry after removal under §1326).

3. **Due process and legal exceptions slow/limit immediate removal**  
   - Non-citizens can apply for relief (asylum, withholding, cancellation of removal, adjustment of status, U/T visas, TPS, etc.). These protections mean not everyone is simply “kicked out” even if initially undocumented.  
   - People in removal proceedings have rights (a hearing, to submit evidence, appeal) though they do **not** have a guaranteed government-provided attorney.

4. **Practical constraints on mass removal**  
   - Logistical limits (detention capacity, hearing backlogs, costs of transport and removal).  
   - Legal limits (relief eligibility, claims of persecution, family and humanitarian factors).  
   - Policy choices — priorities for who to arrest/deport (criminal convictions, recent entrants, national security risks vs. administrative removals).

5. **Consequences of removal are real and often severe**  
   - Deportation can trigger long bars to re-entry, criminal charges for illegal reentry, loss of future immigration benefits, family separation, economic ruin — so it’s a strong deterrent where it happens.

6. **Analogy to theft: similar in principle, different mechanics**  
   - Theft → restitution/criminal punishment is handled by the criminal justice system.  
   - Unauthorized presence → administrative removal with its own remedies and legal defenses. Both aim to correct the wrong, but the U.S. treats most immigration status violations as civil matters with a separate set of procedures.
