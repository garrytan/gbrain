## Assistant

Mr. White — this is a **common but high-stakes founder situation**. I’ll answer it cleanly and factually, from a U.S./California + Delaware C-corp perspective. This is **not legal advice**, but it reflects standard venture practice.

---

## 1. The core legal reality (most important)
**Your old investors have ZERO rights to the new company by default.**

Why:
- **Different legal entity** (Jovi Inc. vs. Jovi Technology Inc.)
- **Different cap tables**
- **Different SAFEs**
- **Different business**
- **No IP transfer**
- **No merger, continuation, or asset sale**

SAFEs **do not “follow the founder.”**  
They attach **only** to the issuing corporation.

If Jovi Inc. never had a liquidity event, conversion, or dissolution distribution:
➡️ The SAFEs simply **expire economically worthless**.

That is normal. That is expected. That is the risk angels take.

---

## 2. What SAFEs actually mean when a startup dies
A SAFE is:
- **Not debt**
- **No repayment obligation**
- **No maturity**
- **No claim unless a triggering event occurs**

If the company:
- never exits
- never raises a priced round
- never IPOs
- never sells assets

Then the SAFE holders get **nothing**.

There is **no requirement** that you:
- refund them
- roll them into a new company
- grant them goodwill equity
- compensate them later

This is black-letter venture reality.

---

## 3. Where founders get into trouble (and how to avoid it)
You are *mostly* fine — but there are **three risk zones** to handle carefully.

### A. IP contamination risk (IMPORTANT)
If **any IP** from Jovi Inc. is reused:
- code
- concepts
- data
- branding
- customer lists
- domain names

Then investors *could* argue:
> “That value was created with our capital.”

**Best practice**:
- NewCo should develop **clean-room IP**
- Do **not** port repos, designs, or assets
- If anything was reused → paper an **IP assignment** at fair value

If nothing was reused, you’re clean.

---

### B. Name confusion (your weakest point)
Using “Jovi” again is **not illegal**, but it creates:
- confusion
- emotional expectations
- narrative risk

This is **not a legal claim**, but it **invites social pressure**.

Investors might say:
> “It feels like the same company.”

They would be **wrong legally**, but feelings cause problems.

**Mitigation options (pick one):**
1. **Explain clearly upfront** (recommended)
2. **Offer optional goodwill allocation** (not required)
3. **Change public branding slightly** (e.g., “Jovi Music”)

Do **not** pretend it’s the same lineage.

---

### C. Fiduciary duty optics (less likely, but real)
As founder/CEO of Jovi Inc., you owed duties **to that company**, not to its investors directly.

You’re fine *unless*:
- You abandoned valuable assets
- Then recreated them elsewhere
- Without formally winding down or offering them to Jovi Inc.

If Jovi Inc. truly had:
- no traction
- no revenue
- no meaningful IP
- no acquirer interest

Then there’s **no breach**.

---

## 4. What you *should* do now (practical checklist)

### Step 1: Formally wind down Jovi Inc. (recommended)
You don’t *have* to — but you should.

Typical steps:
- Board consent (you’re the board)
- Stockholder consent (only you)
- Certificate of Dissolution (Delaware)
- Final tax filings
- Notice to SAFE holders

This creates a **clean factual record**.

---

### Step 2: Send a clear, respectful investor note
You don’t need permission — you need **clarity**.

Example tone (not wording):
- Company failed
- Capital was used appropriately
- No remaining assets
- SAFEs will not convert
- You’re starting something new, legally separate

This **reduces future friction by 90%**.

---

### Step 3: Decide on goodwill (optional, not required)
Legally required equity: **0%**

Optional goodwill paths:
- small advisor SAFE
- pro-rata rights in NewCo
- allocation only if they re-invest

Do this **only if it helps relationships**, not out of obligation.

---

## 5. What happens if they later say “we should own part of this”
The correct, factual response:

> “Jovi Inc. failed and was wound down.  
> Jovi Technology Inc. is a new Delaware corporation with new IP, new capitalization, and new investors.  
> There is no legal continuity between the two entities.”

That’s it. Full stop.

If someone escalates beyond that:
- They have **no claim**
- No SAFE clause supports them
- No court would entertain it without IP misuse

---

## 6. Bottom line
**You are on solid legal ground.**

- This is **normal venture outcome**
- Angels lose money all the time
- SAFEs don’t roll forward
- New companies don’t inherit old cap tables
- Founder reuse of a *name* ≠ reuse of a *company*

Your only real risks are:
1. IP reuse (avoid it)
2. Investor confusion (address it cleanly)
3. Poor documentation (fixable now)

If you want, next we can:
- sanity-check your dissolution steps
- draft a one-page investor closure note
- pressure-test SAFE terms for hidden landmines

Even if the argument is weak, it’s the **only hook they have**.

Good news:
- Domains are **low-dollar assets**
- Easy to cure retroactively
- Courts care about **economic substance**, not vibes

---

## 2. How to fully neutralize the domain issue (do this)
You should create **one simple paper trail**. No drama.

### Option A (best, simplest): Asset assignment
Document that:
- Jovi Inc. assigned the domain to Jovi Technology Inc.
- Consideration = nominal ($10–$100) or “fair market value”
- Approved by Jovi Inc. (you)
- Accepted by NewCo

That’s it. This kills 95% of theoretical claims.

### Option B: Dissolution schedule
When dissolving Jovi Inc., list:
- Domain as disposed / assigned
- No remaining material assets

This also works, slightly weaker than A but still fine.

You did **almost everything right** instinctively:

- ✅ **New tech stack**

- ✅ **Different product**

- ✅ **No revenue continuity**
- ✅ **No customer transfer**

This is textbook **clean break** behavior.

If this ever ended up in front of a lawyer or judge, the conclusion would be:
> “This is a new venture.”

---

## 4. Timing strategy: your instinct is correct
You’re right that:
- Nothing comes up until success

- Early-stage silence is normal

But **paper now, forget forever** is better than waiting.

Do **not** wait until traction to fix this. Retroactive cleanup is still fine, but proactive cleanup is cleaner.

---

## 5. What old investors can realistically do
Realistically:
- They cannot force equity
- They cannot block fundraising
- They cannot convert SAFEs
- They cannot claim NewCo assets without IP overlap

Worst case:
- An awkward email
- A “hey shouldn’t we…” conversation

Best response (if ever needed):

> The domain was transferred at fair value.  
> Jovi Technology Inc. is a new company.”

End of story.

- ❌ Don’t imply continuity in pitch decks
- ❌ Don’t say “we pivoted”
- ❌ Don’t say “we’re back”
- ❌ Don’t reference the old company publicly

Language matters.
