## Assistant

Yes, **Mr. White — you still owe it.**

**Why (facts):**
- **Delaware franchise tax is based on existence, not activity.**
- If your corporation **existed at any time in the calendar year**, Delaware charges the **full annual franchise tax**.
- You incorporated **December 15, 2025**, so Delaware treats the company as active for **2025**, even if revenue and operations started in January 2026.
- Delaware **does not prorate** franchise tax for partial years.
- “No business activity” does **not** waive the tax.

**What you’re seeing:**
- $400 franchise tax (minimum for a Delaware C-Corp using the Authorized Shares Method)
- $50 annual report fee  
→ **$450 total** — correct and standard.

**Only ways this would not be owed:**
- The company was **never actually formed** (not your case), or  
- The company was **voided/cancelled retroactively** in 2025 (rare, formal process, usually not worth it).

**Bottom line:**  
You owe the $450. Pay it and move on — nothing is “wrong” with the filing.

No, **Mr. White — not for 2025.**

**Facts:**
- Your **2025 Delaware franchise tax is already at the statutory minimum** for a Delaware C-Corp.
- $400 franchise tax + $50 annual report fee = **$450**.
- There is **no proration**, **no inactivity reduction**, and **no waiver** for late-year incorporation.
- Changing methods (Authorized Shares vs. Assumed Par Value) **cannot reduce it below the $400 minimum**.

**What *can* help (going forward):**
- For **2026 and later**, you can often lower future taxes by:
  - Issuing **fewer shares** than authorized, and/or
  - Using the **Assumed Par Value Capital Method** (often drops taxes for startups with high authorized shares but low assets).
- If you **dissolve before Jan 1 of a future year**, you avoid the *next* year’s tax entirely.

**Bottom line:**  
You cannot reduce the $450 for 2025. Pay it, then optimize structure so you don’t overpay in 2026+.
