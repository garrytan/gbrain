## Assistant

Alright Mr. White — here’s the lowdown.  

When half-and-half gets **curdled in hot tea**, it’s almost always because of **acid and temperature**.  

### 🧪 Chemistry 101
Tea (especially black tea) contains **tannins** — natural polyphenols that make tea slightly **acidic**. That acidity messes with the milk proteins (mostly casein), causing them to clump. Add heat, and it accelerates the reaction.  

### ☕️ What’s Happening
- **Acid from the tea** destabilizes the proteins.
- **Heat** speeds up protein denaturation.
- The **fat in half-and-half** doesn’t cause it — it’s the **protein reacting to acid + heat**.
- If your half-and-half is **a bit old**, it’s more likely to curdle even faster because the pH drops as it ages.  

### 🧭 How to Prevent It
1. **Cool the tea slightly** — wait 30–60 seconds after steeping before adding cream.
2. **Add the half-and-half first**, then pour in tea slowly while stirring. This tempers it and reduces shock.
3. Use **fresher cream**.
4. If you’re using **citrus or flavored teas** (like Earl Grey), they’re more acidic — higher risk of curdling.
5. Try **whole milk** or **non-dairy creamers**; they’re less sensitive to acidity.

### 🚫 When It’s a Real Problem
- If it **smells sour** or **tastes off**, that’s spoiled half-and-half, not just curdling. Toss it.
- But if it’s just **tiny flakes or separation** in the cup and still tastes fine — that’s just chemistry, not spoilage.

So yeah, curdling in tea is **normal**, not dangerous — just ugly. Want me to explain why coffee doesn’t do it as much?
