**Short answer: Yes, you should exchange insurance information — but do not misrepresent anything.**

Here are the key points under California practice:

1. **You are required to exchange information**  
   California law requires drivers involved in an accident to provide:
   - Name and address  
   - Driver’s license number  
   - Vehicle registration  
   - **Insurance information (if available)**  
   This applies even when police are not called.

2. **Do not lie or imply coverage if it didn’t exist at the moment of impact**  
   Coverage depends on the **exact effective time** of the renewal, not when you paid.  
   - If the policy renewed **after** the accident, the insurer may deny coverage.
   - Giving the card is fine, **but do not state or imply the accident was covered** if you’re unsure.

3. **Notify your insurer immediately**  
   Call or report the accident now and:
   - Confirm the **policy effective timestamp**
   - Ask whether there is any **grace period or retroactive coverage** (sometimes exists, often doesn’t)

4. **Do not admit fault to the other party**  
   Stick to facts only. No apologies, no explanations about insurance timing.

5. **If the other party asks directly**  
   You can say:  
   > “This is my insurance information. My insurer is reviewing coverage based on timing.”

6. **If damage is over $1,000**  
   You are legally required to file an **SR-1 form** with the DMV within **10 days**, regardless of fault or coverage.

### Bottom line
- ✔ Exchange insurance info  
- ✔ Contact your insurer immediately  
- ✘ Do not lie or guess about coverage  
- ✘ Do not admit fault
