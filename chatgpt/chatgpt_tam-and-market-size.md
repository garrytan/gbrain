## Core framing

The cleanest version:

> “My read is that the core question in music tech is TAM. If you size the market as today’s DAW/plugin/producer-tool market, I agree it looks constrained. The reason I think this becomes venture-scale is that AI changes either the number of people creating music, the amount spent by rights holders, or both.”

## Best call script

Use something close to this:

> “Daniel mentioned you’ve been looking at music tech, and my guess is the real question is TAM. I actually think that’s the right question.”
>
> “My view is that music tech only becomes venture-scale if one of two things is true. First, AI expands the creator base by 10x or 100x. Suno is the early proof point there: over 100 million users, 2 million paid subscribers, and around $300 million in ARR. That suggests music creation is becoming a mass-market behavior.”
>
> “Second, even if creation gets noisy and fragmented, the value may concentrate around rights holders and recognizable IP. In that world, AI lets catalogs, likenesses, voices, and songs become programmable assets. Spotify/UMG’s paid AI remix product and Warner’s Suno deal are early signals that the major rights holders are moving toward monetized, licensed AI workflows.”
>
> “So the question is less ‘how big is the current music-tool market?’ and more ‘does AI turn music creation and catalog monetization into a much larger software market?’ If you believe either of those, I think this is a very interesting category. If both feel wrong, then I agree music tech probably looks too small.”

That last sentence is much better than:

> “If you don’t buy it, you should stop exploring music tech.”

The stronger investor version is:

> “If both of those assumptions feel wrong, then we probably won’t agree on the market.”

## The key move

Do **not** make the call about convincing them music tech is large. Make it about whether they believe the market is being **redefined**.

Your position should be:

> “I agree the old market is small. I disagree that we are selling into the old market.”

That is the core. TAM skeptics usually win if the debate stays inside the current category. You win by reframing the category.

## One cleaner version of your thesis

> “Jovie is a bet that music creation is moving from a professional workflow to a mass-market behavior, while music IP is moving from static catalog ownership to dynamic software-enabled monetization. Either trend creates a larger market than traditional music tech. If both happen, the market becomes substantially larger than people expect.”

That is the line I would build around.
