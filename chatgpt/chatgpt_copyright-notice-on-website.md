## Assistant

Mr. White — short answer: **yes, you should have a copyright notice**, but it’s not legally required for protection.

### 1. Is it legally required?
No. Under U.S. law (including California), copyright protection exists automatically once original content is created and fixed (text, images, code, etc.).

You **do not need** a copyright notice to own your content.

---

### 2. Why you should still include one
It helps with:

- Deterrence (signals ownership)
- Reducing “innocent infringement” defenses
- Professional credibility
- Clarifying ownership (especially for startups with contractors)

For a startup founder, it’s standard hygiene.

---

### 3. What to put on your site

Put this in the footer:

```
© 2026 [Your Company Name]. All rights reserved.
```

If it’s personal:

```
© 2026 Tim White. All rights reserved.
```

You do not need “All rights reserved,” but it’s fine to include.

---

### 4. When you need more than just a notice

You should also consider:

- **Terms of Service** (limits liability, governs disputes)
- **Privacy Policy** (legally required if collecting personal data — especially in California under CCPA/CPRA)
- **DMCA policy** (if users can upload content)

If your site collects emails, runs ads, uses analytics, or sells anything — you need a Privacy Policy at minimum.
