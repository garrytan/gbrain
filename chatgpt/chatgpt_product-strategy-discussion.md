## My read

The strongest underlying thesis is still:

> **AI makes creation easier. The scarce thing becomes attention, trust, audience ownership, and revenue.**

That is true for musicians, fitness creators, coaches, educators, comedians, podcasters, and indie media people. The reason music is still a good wedge is that the pain is especially sharp: artists constantly create “moments” but struggle to turn those moments into fans, merch, tickets, email/SMS, paid products, and repeatable revenue.

Current market signals support that. ## What I would do

### 1. Keep the external wedge music-focused

Do **not** dilute the public positioning yet.

Music gives you:
- a sharper ICP;
- clearer emotional urgency;
- obvious revenue paths: merch, tickets, drops, fan clubs, exclusive content;
- platform-specific data/integrations;
- cultural timing around AI, authenticity, artists, and ownership;
- a narrower market where you can sound like an insider.

“General creator tool” is a much harder initial position. It invites comparison to every AI content scheduler, link-in-bio tool, website builder, CRM, newsletter platform, Stripe wrapper, and social analytics tool.

The better framing is:

> **Jovie is the AI revenue agent for music creators. Internally, we are learning whether that becomes the AI revenue agent for creators generally.**

### 2. Run adjacent experiments without calling them pivots

I would run 2–3 small experiments in parallel, all using the same core product thesis:

| Experiment | ICP | Question |
|---|---:|---|
| Core music | independent artists / artist teams | Can we repeatedly turn a song/moment into fan/revenue actions? |
| Fitness creator | trainers / fitness influencers / coaches | Does the same “moment → product/community/revenue” loop work faster in fitness? |
| General creator | one narrow creator type, not “everyone” | Is there a broader pattern, or does the product become generic? |

The key: **do not build three products.** Build one engine and test three vertical wrappers.

For example, the fitness version should not be “a fitness app.” It should be:

> **An AI launch agent for fitness creators that turns a transformation, challenge, video, or training philosophy into a paid program, merch/drop, community funnel, landing page, email/SMS sequence, and social campaign.**

That tests the same company thesis.

### 3. Avoid AI music generation as the center

I would stay on the **growth, fan, monetization, and authenticity** side of music rather than the “generate songs” side.

AI music creation is becoming legally and platform-wise unstable. Deezer said AI-generated tracks reached about 44% of daily uploads while accounting for only 1–3% of streams, and it is removing AI-generated music from recommendations and demonetizing it. citeturn835786view3 Warner settled with Suno around licensed AI models, while the broader legal and licensing landscape remains active. citeturn835786view4

That makes the stronger Jovie angle:

> **Help real artists convert attention into owned fans and revenue.**

That is more durable than “make more music.”

## My decision rule

I would give the current direction a defined evidence window.

For the next cycle, measure:

| Metric | What it tells you |
|---|---|
| Activation | Do creators understand the product quickly? |
| First-value time | Can they get a useful campaign/output fast? |
| Willingness to pay | Is this a painkiller or novelty? |
| Repeat use | Does every song/moment create another use case? |
| Revenue tie-in | Can Jovie plausibly make or track money for them? |
| Manual pull | Are users asking you to do more for them? |
| Segment intensity | Which creator type has the sharpest pain? |

Then decide:

| Evidence | Move |
|---|---|
| Music creators activate, repeat, and pay | Double down on music. |
| Music creators like it but do not pay | Move upmarket: managers, indie labels, artist teams. |
| Fitness creators pay faster using the same engine | Add fitness as a second vertical test. |
| General creators only want generic content tools | Stay away from general positioning. |
| All verticals respond to “revenue agent” | Rebrand later around creator revenue, with music as proof. |

## My actual answer

**Keep going in music. Start experimenting outside music. Do not pivot yet.**

The likely winning path is:

> **Music wedge → creator revenue engine → selective vertical expansion.**

Fitness is worth testing because it has clearer productization than music: programs, challenges, coaching, memberships, supplements, merch, community, before/after stories. But it should be tested as **creator monetization infrastructure**, not as a standalone consumer fitness product.

The thing I would protect most is specificity. “Creator-focused” is attractive strategically, but weak tactically until you know which creator workflow has urgency, budget, and repeat behavior. Music still gives you that specificity.

## The crisp version

I would position Jovie like this internally:

> **We are building an AI agent that turns creator moments into owned audience and revenue. Music is the first wedge because the pain is acute, the culture is moving fast, and the monetization loop is visible.**

Publicly, I would keep it music-first.

Operationally, I would run fitness and one other creator vertical as cheap tests.

Strategically, I would only pivot after evidence shows another vertical has materially stronger activation, payment, or retention than music.
