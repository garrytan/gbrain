# 401k vs Roth IRA

## User

Yo dawg, my employer just signed me up for 401k. I should probably just opt out of the 3% deduction and automate a transfer into a self-directed Roth IRA and I would make more money, right?

---

## Assistant

Not necessarily, Mr. White. Let’s break this down concisely but accurately:  

---

### 1. **Employer 401(k)**
- The *3% deduction* often comes with an *employer match* (e.g., dollar-for-dollar up to 3%).  
- That’s a *100% guaranteed return* on your contribution — far superior to any normal market return.  
- Contributions also reduce your *taxable income*, which can improve take-home efficiency if you’re in a higher tax bracket.

**→ Opting out would forfeit that match — effectively losing free money.**

---

### 2. **Roth IRA (Self-Directed)**
- You invest *post-tax* money. Growth and withdrawals are tax-free in retirement.  
- You have full control over investment choices — including individual stocks, ETFs, REITs, etc.  
- 2025 contribution limit: **$7,000** (or $8,000 if 50+).  

**→ Excellent for flexibility and long-term tax-free compounding.**

---

### 3. **Best Strategy (Per Evidence-Based Finance)**
- **Step 1:** Contribute to your 401(k) at least up to the *employer match*.  
- **Step 2:** Max your Roth IRA annually (self-directed if you want control).  
- **Step 3:** If you still have money to invest, go back and increase your 401(k) beyond the match.  

This hierarchy is nearly universal among reputable financial planners because of the **guaranteed return of the match** and **tax advantages**.

---

### 4. **When Opting Out Might Make Sense**
Only consider skipping 401(k) if:
- There is **no employer match**,  
- The plan has **very high fees** or **poor investment options**,  
- You are **maxing a Roth IRA** and want more flexible access to your funds before retirement.  

---

**Verdict:**  
You’d make *less money* by opting out of a matched 401(k). Keep at least enough to get the full match, then automate Roth IRA contributions for flexibility and tax-free growth.

## User

When my employer lets me change my 401k to a Roth, can I, should I, and set percentage distributions for both? Should I do that with a Roth?

---

## Assistant

Good question, Mr. White — this is where it gets strategic. Let’s unpack it step by step:  

---

### **1. 401(k) vs Roth 401(k): What Changes**
Once your employer allows it, you can usually choose between:
- **Traditional 401(k):** Pre-tax contributions → taxed when withdrawn.  
- **Roth 401(k):** Post-tax contributions → tax-free growth and withdrawals.  

Both grow tax-deferred while invested; the main difference is *when* you pay taxes.

---

### **2. Should You Switch (or Split)?**
The right move depends on your current and future tax situation:

| Situation | Better Option | Reason |
|------------|----------------|--------|
| You expect to be in a **higher tax bracket** in retirement | **Roth 401(k)** | Pay taxes now while lower |
| You expect to be in a **lower tax bracket** later | **Traditional 401(k)** | Defer taxes until retirement |
| You want **tax diversification** | **Split both** | Hedge against future tax changes |

Most people (especially early-career or mid-career earners) benefit from **doing both** — e.g., **50/50 or 60/40 split** — because it balances flexibility and tax exposure.  

---

### **3. Can You Set Percentage Distributions?**
Yes — most plans let you set contributions like:
- 60% Roth 401(k)
- 40% Traditional 401(k)

These percentages apply to how your paycheck deductions are allocated.  
You can change this anytime during open enrollment (or often anytime if your plan allows).

---

### **4. Should You Also Have a Roth IRA?**
Yes — even if you do a Roth 401(k). Here’s why:
- Roth IRA has **broader investment choices** and **no required minimum distributions (RMDs)**.  
- Roth 401(k) does have RMDs unless you roll it into an IRA later.  
- Roth IRA gives you more **control and flexibility** in retirement.

---

### **5. Practical Setup (for most high-earning Californians)**
A good evidence-based structure (assuming you can afford it):
1. **Contribute to 401(k) up to the employer match.**  
2. **Contribute to Roth IRA (self-directed).**  
3. **Then increase Roth 401(k) share** if you want to favor tax-free growth.  

If you’re earning well and taxes are high now, you might **lean traditional** until retirement nears or your bracket stabilizes.

---

### **Summary:**
- ✅ Yes, you *can* split between Traditional and Roth 401(k).  
- ✅ You *should* if you want tax diversification.  
- 🧠 Use Roth 401(k) and Roth IRA for long-term tax-free growth.  
- ⚙️ Keep at least enough Traditional 401(k) to balance tax efficiency now.
