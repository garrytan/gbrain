# Auto-Tune Settings in Shellback & Max Martin’s Demo Vocals

## Retune Speed & Tuning Technique  
Shellback and Max Martin are known to tune demo vocals for a polished but natural sound. They often use Auto-Tune in **Automatic mode** with a **moderate (slower) Retune Speed**, rather than the extreme fast settings used for T-Pain/Cher effects【10†L138-L144】. In an interview, Shellback (Johan Schuster) described a simple approach: *“put it in auto-mode, retune speed set to fairly slow and [tracking] sensitivity set to quite fast (no more than 30)… then comp the vocals with Auto-Tune ON. Never use takes where you can actually **hear** Auto-Tune ‘working’.”*【10†L138-L144】 In practice, this means dialing back the retune speed so pitch correction isn’t obvious, and rejecting any vocal take that produces audible tuning artifacts. The goal is a tight, in-tune vocal that still feels human. Their camp applies pitch correction **subtly** – just enough to hit the “sweet spot” notes without drawing attention to the effect【4†L595-L600】. A Retune Speed around **20–30** is a common starting point for natural sounding pop vocals【42†L113-L121】, ensuring the tuning is gentle. By contrast, faster settings (close to 0) are rarely used in Shellback/Max Martin demos, since those would create an overt robotic effect that isn’t part of their signature sound. They instead keep correction mild, using the singer’s best takes and minor Auto-Tune help to deliver a clean, radio-ready demo performance.

## Humanize and Flex-Tune Settings  
**Humanize** and **Flex-Tune** are features in newer Auto-Tune versions (Auto-Tune 8/Pro and above) that help preserve a natural feel. While there aren’t many direct quotes of Shellback or Max Martin specifying these settings, we can infer their likely use. The **Humanize** knob slows down pitch correction on sustained notes【43†L1-L9】. This is useful if a song has long held notes or vibrato – it prevents those notes from sounding too flat or “robotic” when using a fast retune speed. In practice, a producer might start with Humanize at 0 while setting retune speed for the shorter notes, then raise the Humanize value until held notes sound smooth【43†L1-L9】. For modern pop vocals (which often hit clear, straight-tone notes without much vibrato), a high Humanize value may not be needed【43†L7-L15】. In fact, one guide notes that for many pop/R&B vocals with straight notes, you can leave Humanize at 0【43†L3-L9】. Shellback/Max Martin demos likely use **Humanize as needed** – if the singer sustains a note and it starts to sound artificially static, they’d increase Humanize to restore a more natural sustain. Conversely, if the vocal lines are short and percussive, Humanize might stay low or off to keep every note tightly in pitch.

**Flex-Tune** is another control (introduced in Auto-Tune 8) that **allows some natural pitch variation** by only applying correction as the singer’s pitch approaches the target note【42†L158-L167】. With Flex-Tune at 0, Auto-Tune will pull *every* note fully onto the scale pitch (the classic behavior)【42†L158-L164】. Turning Flex-Tune up lets the singer’s intonation glide a bit before snapping in – this preserves slides, scoops, and expressive nuance. For Shellback and Max Martin’s style, the use of Flex-Tune would depend on the vocal performance. If a demo singer is very close to pitch, a **moderate Flex-Tune (e.g. 50–60)** can be used to only correct notes that are noticeably off, avoiding over-tuning【42†L158-L167】. However, if they want the demo vocal locked *exactly* on each note (for that super-polished pop precision), they might keep Flex-Tune low or at 0, essentially the traditional Auto-Tune behavior. In sum, they prioritize a transparent correction – Humanize and Flex-Tune are likely adjusted to ensure the tuning doesn’t kill the vocal’s natural movement. When using modern Auto-Tune Pro on demos, one might see **Retune Speed moderately slow, Humanize engaged for any long notes, and Flex-Tune set mid-way** so that short falls or scoops into notes aren’t completely removed (maintaining some expressiveness).

## Key and Scale Considerations  
Proper **key and scale** settings are fundamental in Shellback and Max Martin’s vocal tuning approach. They always set Auto-Tune to the **song’s key and scale** (e.g. C Major, A Minor, etc.) so that the plugin knows which notes are musically correct. By defining the correct scale, Auto-Tune nudges any off-pitch singing to the nearest *intended* note, rather than a random chromatic note. This helps the demo vocals lock perfectly to the song’s chords and melody. In their polished pop productions, melodies are often diatonic (staying within the key), so an Auto-Tune scale can be set and left during recording. If a melody uses deliberate accidentals or blues notes outside the key, the producers may either allow those in the scale or bypass Auto-Tune for those phrases to preserve the intended notes. For the most part, though, Shellback and Martin’s songwriting stays in a clear key, so Auto-Tune’s job is simply to **keep the singer on those target notes**. Martin’s team has noted that they use tools like Auto-Tune or Melodyne subtly to ensure every important note hits the “sweet spot”【4†L595-L600】 – setting the correct scale is part of hitting that sweet spot. 

It’s also worth noting that on demo vocals (often sung by Max Martin himself or a session singer), they will choose a comfortable key for the vocalist. If the demo singer has limitations, the producers might adjust the key of the demo to suit them, then later have the artist sing in the final key. In those cases, Auto-Tune’s scale would be set accordingly for the demo, and possibly changed for the final artist recording. Nonetheless, the principle is the same: **tune to the appropriate musical scale**. This avoids any “wrong note” corrections. For example, if the song is in G major, they’ll set Auto-Tune to G major so a singer sliding slightly toward an F♯ or A is corrected properly, but a note like an out-of-key C♯ won’t be forced incorrectly (it would likely be left or handled manually if truly intended). Using the right key/scale ensures Auto-Tune works as a transparent intonation helper rather than an obvious effect.

## Additional Vocal Processing & Signature Sound  
Beyond pitch correction, Shellback and Max Martin rely on a chain of vocal processing to achieve their signature glossy pop sound. **EQ, compression, de-essing, and time-based effects** are used in combination to polish the tuned vocals:

- **Microphone & Preamp** – First, in tracking demos they often use high-end gear (Max Martin has been known to favor the Sony C-800G or Manley Reference mics with quality preamps【36†L13-L17】) to capture a clean, bright vocal. Starting with a great recording means less corrective processing later.  
- **EQ (Equalization)** – Cutting unwanted lows and mud, and boosting presence/highs for clarity is standard. They’ll typically high-pass the vocal (removing rumble) and add a gentle upper-mid boost to make the vocal crisp and forward. Sometimes a “telephone EQ” effect (drastic band-pass) is used creatively on a section (for example, in a verse) to create contrast【4†L595-L600】. Overall, the vocal is EQ’d to sit on top of a dense pop mix – clear consonants and airy brightness are hallmarks of their vocals. Mix engineers in Martin’s circle often use modern digital EQs (e.g. FabFilter Pro-Q) for precision cuts and analog-modeled EQs (like a UAD API 560 graphic EQ) for tone shaping【25†L7-L15】【25†L19-L27】.

- **Compression** – Pop vocals are heavily compressed to even out dynamics and keep them upfront. It’s common to see **1176-style compressors** in their vocal chain, sometimes in stages. For instance, mix engineers might hit a vocal with ~3 dB of gain reduction using an 1176 “Blackface” at 4:1, and even up to 10 dB on a faster “Blueface” model for an aggressive, in-your-face sound【37†L69-L77】【37†L70-L74】. The attack/release are tuned so the vocal stays energetic but smooth (often a medium-slow attack to let transients through, and a release timed to the vocal’s rhythm【37†L76-L84】). Shellback/Max Martin productions have that steady, polished vocal level — achieved by compression and also meticulous **clip-gain riding** or **automation** of vocal levels by their mix engineers. It’s reported that Serban Ghenea (who mixes many of their songs) even manually rides levels or uses Waves Vocal Rider to ensure every syllable sits right【25†L7-L15】. This tight dynamic control allows the vocal to be heard clearly even in a busy pop arrangement.

- **De-essing** – Since they boost vocal brightness, controlling sibilance is crucial. A de-esser (targeting ~5–8 kHz) is almost always in the chain to tame harsh “S” and “T” sounds【37†L81-L87】. For example, using a Waves Renaissance De-Esser around 5500 Hz taking off ~6 dB on peaks is a common setting【37†L81-L87】. This ensures the vocal can be bright without stabbing the listener’s ear on every “S”. 

- **Saturation & Enhancement** – Many Max Martin/Shellback vocals have a subtle *sheen* or grit that makes them sound alive. They sometimes apply mild **distortion or saturation** to enhance harmonics. In mixing “The Hills” (a track on which Max Martin was an exec producer), the engineer added SoundToys **Little Radiator** (analog heat) and **Decapitator** on the chorus vocal for some thickness and edge【25†L19-L27】. **Exciters** or subtle chorus effects (like MicroShift doubler) are also used to widen the vocal and add density【25†L19-L27】. These effects are used judiciously – you don’t obviously hear distortion, but the vocal has a rich, glossy character that pure EQ and compression alone wouldn’t achieve.

- **Reverb & Delay** – Rather than drenched reverb, their signature is often a **mix of short ambience and rhythmic delays** to give space while keeping the vocal forward. A favorite combination is a **plate or hall reverb** tucked behind the vocal plus a **delay** (echo) timed to the song. For instance, mixers for their productions often use **Valhalla VintageVerb** or **Altiverb** for a lush but quick-decaying reverb, and a **Waves H-Delay** for tempo-synced echoes【37†L93-L100】. A common pop trick is a **quarter-note or eighth-note delay** on the vocal, sometimes with high-end rolled off, so it provides a sense of space and repeat without cluttering new lyrics. This can be heard in many Max Martin-produced choruses where the tail of a vocal line repeats softly in time. Reverbs are used more sparingly – maybe a short plate for thickness and a longer reverb only on select big notes (for example, a held note at the end of a chorus might get a big reverb or delay throw for drama【4†L595-L600】). The result is a vocal that doesn’t feel dry, but also doesn’t wash out in reverb – it’s clear and intimate, with just enough ambiance to sound *expensive*. 

- **Vocal Alignment & Stacking** – Part of their “secret sauce” is how they stack vocals. Shellback and Max often layer **multiple vocal tracks** (doubles, harmonies, octaves) to amplify the chorus. They will record the same vocal line several times and *align and tune them perfectly* for a glossy unified sound. One engineer described the process: use Synchro Arts **Vocalign** to tighten the timing of backing vocal doubles, then copy the lead vocal’s Auto-Tune settings to all the backing layers so that every track is **exactly in tune** with the lead【17†L299-L307】. This way, when you pan and mix these doubles, the vocals sound huge and “3D” but without the loose, chorusy phase issues that normally come from layered vocals【17†L300-L307】. By tuning all harmony stacks identically, the chords or unisons they sing come out perfectly blended, contributing to that massive pop chorus effect. Additionally, if only one singer is available, they might alter formants/EQ on doubles to simulate a group of different voices【4†L586-L593】. All this is on top of the lead vocal, which remains the focal point, but the stacks provide a wide stereo spread and power. 

- **Creative Effects** – Finally, Shellback/Max Martin aren’t afraid to use creative effects on vocals for impact. We already mentioned telephone EQ for verses; they also automate filters, add **stutter effects**, or even a **vocoder** layer if it suits the song’s vibe. In some productions, you might hear a subtle **vocoder harmony or vocal synth** underlying the chorus (giving a slight robotic sheen supporting the real vocal). For example, a TikTok breakdown of their work on Taylor Swift’s “Delicate” notes that they “record your vocal and tune it, add a delay and some reverb, add some vocoder” as steps in their process【26†L9-L17】【26†L23-L27】. These moves are song-dependent – their core vocal sound is still a clean vocal, but enhanced with effects at just the right moments to create ear candy.

Overall, the combination of **meticulous tuning, clean compression/EQ, controlled effects, and layered production** is what gives Shellback and Max Martin’s vocals that unmistakably polished pop character. By the time a demo vocal has been tuned and processed through this chain, it already sounds like a record. Max Martin’s team is known to produce *“demo” vocals that could almost pass for final mixes* in terms of quality【22†L818-L825】 – and the above techniques are a big reason why.

## Evolving from Demo to Final Production  
In the workflow of these producers, demo vocals are often treated with the same care as final vocals, but there are some differences as the song progresses to final production. **During the demo phase**, speed and vibe are priorities. The team might rely on Auto-Tune’s automatic mode for quick results – slapping it on while the songwriter or demo singer performs, so they instantly get a polished sound. They monitor the vocal with Auto-Tune in real-time, ensuring the singer is delivering notes that tune well. This helps impress artists and label folks when pitching the song, as the demo sounds *incredibly* tight. Max Martin himself is a strong singer and has recorded many of his own song demos【22†L818-L825】, often heavily tuned so that his guide vocals sound radio-ready for the artist to reference. If an external demo singer is used, they’ll likewise tune and comp that performance to perfection before presenting the song.

When it comes to **final vocals** (recorded by the actual artist), the approach becomes even more refined. By this stage, the production and mix will involve top engineers (Serban Ghenea, John Hanes, etc.), and they may switch or augment the tuning method. It’s common that initial tuning is done in Auto-Tune for speed, but then any problem spots are fine-tuned with Celemony **Melodyne** or Auto-Tune’s Graphical Mode. In fact, forum discussions note that “Max uses Melodyne now” for detailed vocal editing【10†L209-L214】 – the logic being that Melodyne allows surgical adjustment of any notes that Auto-Tune (in auto mode) might have over-corrected or missed. The final vocal tuning might involve manually adjusting specific phrases, adjusting note transitions, or even dialing in a bit of natural vibrato that was flattened in the demo. The producers want the final vocals to retain the magic of the demo but with even fewer artifacts or flaws.

**Plugin versions and settings can evolve** from demo to final as well. For instance, a quick demo may use a low-latency plugin like **Auto-Tune EFX or Auto-Tune Artist** during tracking, which gives the singer realtime feedback. On final vocals, they might use the more full-featured **Auto-Tune Pro** in post-production or switch to Graph Mode for ultimate precision. Some engineers even deliberately use older Auto-Tune algorithms for their unique sound – one noted using *Auto-Tune EFX (an older version)* because “it pulls the vocal in a slightly different way than the newer versions,” adding that these days if you *don’t* tune a vocal a bit, “it almost does not sound normal” (even if applied very subtly)【25†L12-L17】. For iconic Max Martin productions of the past, many were likely tuned with **Antares Auto-Tune 5**, which is famous for its transparent yet effective sound; in fact, Auto-Tune Pro’s “Classic Mode” explicitly emulates the Auto-Tune 5 engine – the very sound heard on countless 2000s/2010s pop hits【48†L21-L25】. So in final mix, an engineer might engage Classic Mode to capture that familiar tuning character. 

Crucially, **the retune settings might be relaxed slightly in the final mix compared to the demo**. The demo might have been slightly over-tuned in spots (to ensure absolutely no off notes in the pitch reference given to the artist). When the artist records, hopefully they sing it better than the demo singer (having lived with the song). The tuning on the artist’s vocal can therefore be gentler, since the performance is stronger or more stylistically nuanced. The final production will aim to keep the vocal sounding as natural as possible *while still being perfectly in tune*. Shellback and Max Martin want listeners to focus on the hook and emotion, never the tuning. So any obvious Auto-Tune moments will be ironed out by automating the Retune Speed, using Humanize, or doing manual pitch edits on the final take. Backing vocals in the final are also tightly controlled; they will all be tuned to match the lead (just like in the demo), but sometimes the **formant or timbre is tweaked** to blend with the artist’s voice or to create a thicker stack without obviously sounding like clones.

In summary, the **demo vocal is essentially a blueprint** – tuned fairly strongly and processed to sound hit-ready – and the **final vocal is a polished monument**, where the same principles are applied with even more finesse. By the end, the listener just hears a seamlessly in-tune vocal with vibrant pop production around it, not aware of the behind-the-scenes retune speeds or plugin choices. As Max Martin’s team themselves put it, the tools like Auto-Tune or Melodyne are used only to ensure the vocal hits the right notes and shines, without ever stealing the spotlight【4†L595-L600】. The result is that characteristic Max Martin/Shellback vocal: effortlessly catchy, impeccably tuned, and emotionally impactful.
