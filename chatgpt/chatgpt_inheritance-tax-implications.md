## Assistant

Here’s the rundown, Mr. White:

### Step-Up in Basis
- When you **inherit stock (public or private, e.g., SpaceX)**, the tax basis usually “steps up” to the fair market value (FMV) at the date of death.  
- That means if your relative bought in at $50/share but it’s worth $400/share on the date you inherit, your basis becomes $400/share.

### Estate Taxes
- The **estate itself** may owe estate taxes if the total estate value exceeds the federal exemption (~$13.61M in 2024; California has no separate estate tax).  
- You, as beneficiary, generally don’t pay inheritance tax in California (there isn’t one), but the estate may.

### Income Taxes
- You **don’t owe capital gains tax** when you inherit.  
- You only owe tax when you **sell** the shares.  
- If you sell immediately at FMV, there’s little to no gain. If you hold and sell later at a higher value, you pay capital gains tax on the difference (long-term capital gains rate, regardless of holding period).

### Private Shares (like SpaceX)
- Liquidity is the catch. Private company stock isn’t freely tradeable. You may need to wait for a tender offer, secondary market sale, or IPO to realize cash.  
- Valuation for the “step-up” is based on a professional appraisal or recent 409A/secondary pricing.  
- The IRS can challenge valuations if they think it’s artificially low.

---

**Example:**  
- Uncle leaves you 1,000 SpaceX shares.  
- FMV at death: $100/share → basis = $100,000.  
- You sell in a future secondary offering for $200/share → $200,000 proceeds.  
- Taxable capital gain = $100,000 (long-term).
