## Assistant

Mr. White, in California this is legal because the tow charge is not treated like a normal voluntary purchase. It is a statutory consequence of parking on private property in violation of posted conditions. For a typical commercial lot, the property owner can have the car removed only if the lot complies with Vehicle Code § 22658, which usually means a sign at **all entrances** that is at least **17" x 22"**, with **1-inch lettering**, prohibits public parking, states vehicles will be removed at the owner’s expense, and lists the local traffic law-enforcement phone number plus the towing company name and phone number. A generic “customers only, violators will be towed” sign by itself may be missing required elements. citeturn780917view0

Your main instinct is correct on the policy issue: California does **not** require the entrance sign to state the exact dollar amount of the tow. The state handles that a different way. For a private-property tow, the charge is “excessive” if it exceeds the greater of the local law-enforcement contract rate for that jurisdiction or the tower’s CHP-approved rate. The storage facility also has to post a **Towing and Storage Fees and Access Notice**, give you a copy on request, and provide an **itemized invoice of actual charges before payment**. That invoice must spell out the hourly or per-item tow rate, daily storage rate, gate fee if any, the tow method, timing, and who ordered the tow. So the tow company cannot lawfully just invent any number it wants after the fact. citeturn883205view0turn819488view0

In Los Angeles, the 2025 Official Police Garage schedule shows a **standard-vehicle tow rate** of **$190** for the first hour when paid by non-credit means or **$195** by credit card, plus **$94/$96** for each additional half-hour, and **daily storage** of **$59/$60** for a standard vehicle. Private-property tow charges in LA generally cannot exceed the applicable LA law-enforcement rate or the tower’s CHP-approved rate. citeturn372985view2turn819488view0turn883205view0

California does give the vehicle owner some immediate protections. If you get back to the car **before it has been removed from the property and is in transit**, the tower has to **immediately and unconditionally release it**. If it is already hooked up but not yet gone, the tower can charge **no more than half** the regular tow rate. The regular tow rate can be charged only after the vehicle is actually removed and in transit. A private-property tow also has to be stored within **10 miles** of where it was removed, unless the towing company has prior written approval from the agency with primary jurisdiction. citeturn780917view0turn883205view0

On the damage issue, the statute is direct: the towing company is responsible for **damage during transit and subsequent storage**, and the vehicle owner may recover for damage caused by an intentional or negligent act in the removal or towing process. The owner also has the right, before paying, to get personal property out during normal business hours, inspect the vehicle, have the insurer inspect it, and pay by cash or valid bank credit card. citeturn780917view0turn819488view0

On paperwork, the tower usually cannot tow from private property without **written authorization** from the property owner or authorized agent who verifies the violation. That authorization has to identify the car, who authorized the tow, the grounds, and the relevant times. Before taking payment, the towing company must give the vehicle owner a **photocopy of that written authorization**. In certain “general authorization” cases such as fire-lane, hydrant, or blocked-entrance tows, the tower also has to take a **photo of the violation** and provide a copy when you claim the car. citeturn883205view0

The lien part comes from statute too. Civil Code § 3068.1 gives the tower a **possessory lien** for towing and storage once the vehicle is removed and in transit. For a vehicle valued at **$4,000 or less**, the lienholder must start DMV lien-sale procedures within **15 days**, send notice to the registered and legal owners and other interested parties, and the owner has **10 days** after mailing to oppose the sale. If the lien sale goes through, sale proceeds are applied to the towing and storage debt, and the owner can still be responsible for any remaining balance. citeturn177470view0turn405817view1turn405817view0turn819488view0

So the clean answer to your core question is this: California does **not** make the parking-lot sign disclose the exact tow price. The law instead relies on **statutory rate caps, yard-posted fee notices, and required itemized invoices after the tow**. That is why the system survives legally, even though it often feels coercive and one-sided in practice. citeturn780917view0turn883205view0turn819488view0

If you are dealing with an actual tow in LA, the fastest pressure points are: get the itemized invoice, get the written tow authorization, get the violation photo if applicable, photograph the damage, confirm the yard is within 10 miles unless there was agency approval, and compare the charges to the LA rate schedule. Specific violations can trigger civil liability against the tower or property owner under these statutes. citeturn883205view0turn819488view0turn372985view2

Mr. White, no. The **“first hour” is the tow job itself**, not an hour for you to come get the car. In Los Angeles, the current standard-vehicle tow rate is **$215 cash / $220 credit for the first hour or fraction thereof**, and then storage is a **separate daily charge** of **$66 cash / $68 credit per day** for a standard vehicle. citeturn317699view0

So for a normal passenger car in LA, a straightforward private-property tow is usually in this range:

- picked up within 24 hours: about **$281 to $288** total before any other lawful add-ons  
- about 2 storage days: about **$347 to $356** total before any other lawful add-ons

That is expensive. It is **not thousands of dollars in two days** for a standard car under the posted LA rate schedule. It can become very large if the vehicle is heavy-duty, needs special equipment, sits there for many more days, or you trigger lawful extra charges such as an after-hours release fee. citeturn930018view0turn615628view0

California law also gives you one important storage protection: if the vehicle is released **within 24 hours** after it reaches the storage yard, they can charge **only one day of storage**. Private-property tow/storage charges are excessive if they exceed the greater of the local law-enforcement rate or the tower’s CHP-approved rate. citeturn615628view0

There is also a separate **lien fee** issue. During the **first 72 hours of storage**, you have the right to retrieve the vehicle **without paying a lien fee**. That does **not** eliminate the tow charge or the daily storage charge. citeturn762323search0turn762323search1
