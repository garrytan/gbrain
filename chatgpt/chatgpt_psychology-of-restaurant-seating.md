## Assistant

Where people choose to sit in restaurants reveals quite a bit about social and psychological behavior. Here are the main dynamics:  

---

### **1. Territory and Control**
People tend to prefer seats that give them a sense of **control** over their environment.  
- **Back to the wall**: Provides safety and a full view of the room — a primal instinct rooted in threat avoidance.  
- **Facing entrances**: Common among people who are alert, dominant, or security-oriented.  
- **Corners and booths**: Feel private and enclosed, ideal for those who value personal space.

---

### **2. Social Hierarchy and Dominance**
Seating position reflects perceived status or assertiveness.  
- **Heads of tables** are subconsciously associated with authority or leadership.  
- In social groups, **dominant individuals** often sit in positions that command attention or control conversation flow (e.g., facing the most people).

---

### **3. Relationship Dynamics**
Couples and groups unconsciously communicate relationship quality through seating:  
- **Couples sitting side-by-side** often show intimacy or alliance.  
- **Across from each other** can suggest formality, tension, or a focus on conversation.  
- **Trios** usually form a “power triangle” — the person in the middle often mediates or controls the social balance.

---

### **4. Personality Traits**
- **Introverts** prefer edges, walls, or quieter corners to minimize social and sensory stimulation.  
- **Extroverts** gravitate toward central, open, or people-watching spots — they enjoy social visibility.  
- **Anxious personalities** choose seats with escape routes or line-of-sight to exits.

---

### **5. Environmental Influence**
Lighting, noise, and proximity to staff or other patrons affect choice:  
- People avoid high-traffic areas (near restrooms or kitchen doors).  
- Tables with **natural light** or **a view** tend to attract first — these evoke calm and pleasure.  
- Proximity to the bar can appeal to those seeking casual conversation or spontaneity.
