Yo, so my name is Tim White. If I join the Screen Actors Guild, what's the deal? Can my name still be Tim White in the credits?

---

## Assistant

Short answer: **maybe, but only if nobody else in SAG-AFTRA is already using “Tim White” (or something confusingly similar).**

Here’s how it works, Mr. White:

- **SAG-AFTRA doesn’t let two actors use the same professional name.**  
  If there’s already a *Tim White* (or *Timothy White* or *Tim Wight* etc.) registered, you won’t be allowed to use that exact name as your billing/credits name.

- **Your legal name and your professional name don’t have to match.**  
  On the contract and paperwork you can still be Timothy/Tim White, but the **on-screen credit name** has to be unique in the union’s records.

- **If “Tim White” is taken, you’d need a variation**, for example:
  - Tim J. White  
  - Timothy R. White  
  - T.R. White  
  - Tim James White  
  - Or even a completely different stage surname

- **This only matters once you actually join SAG-AFTRA or work under a SAG-AFTRA contract.**  
  Non-union/student work can list you however, but once you’re union, the name has to align with what’s registered.

If you’re serious about joining soon, the practical next step is:
- When you’re eligible, **ask SAG-AFTRA whether “Tim White” is available as a professional name**. If it is, you’re good. If it isn’t, we can brainstorm stage-name variants that still feel like *you*.

Yeah, the status *does* matter, but not in a simple “active vs inactive” way.

Here’s the real deal, Mr. White:

### 1. SAG-AFTRA protects names beyond just active members

SAG-AFTRA’s goal is that **no member uses a name that’s the same as, or confusingly similar to, any other member’s name**. citeturn0search3  

On top of that, there are “hold” rules depending on what happened to the other performer:

- **If a member dies** – their name is held and not reassigned for **3 years**.  
- **If a member changes their professional name** – their old name is also held for **3 years**.  
- **If a member honorably withdraws or owes dues / is in bad standing** – their name is held for **5 years**.  
- **If a member is expelled / kicked out** – their name becomes available immediately. citeturn0search7  

So: even if they’re not currently “active,” their name can still block you for a while, depending on their status.

### 2. You usually *can’t* claim the name if it’s in one of those “held” states

When you join, **they run a name check at the moment you pay your initiation fee**. If someone in the system (including those in a hold period) has that name or something too close, you’ll be told it’s not available and asked to use a variation. citeturn0search3turn0search9  

That’s why they ask you to bring multiple options.

### 3. The big exception: your *legal* name

SAG-AFTRA **strongly discourages** duplicate/confusing names, but they **can’t legally stop you from using your exact legal name** as your professional name. If you insist on using your legal name and it conflicts with someone else, they’ll have you sign an acknowledgment form saying you understand the risk of confusion. citeturn0search3turn0search10  

So for you:

- If **“Tim White” is your legal name**, you *can* push to use it, even if someone else has it, but:
  - You’ll likely have to sign that waiver.
  - You may deal with constant credit/IMDb/casting confusion.
- If **you’re flexible**, it’s usually smarter to pick a unique professional version:
  - Tim J. White  
  - Timothy A. White  
  - T.J. White  
  - Tim James White, etc.

### 4. What you can actually do now

You can’t really “reserve” the name in advance; SAG-AFTRA explicitly says availability is only final at the time you join and pay. citeturn0search3turn0search9  

Practical move:  
- Start using **one consistent professional form** now (e.g., “Tim J. White”) on your materials, *unless* you’re dead set on fighting for your exact legal name later.

If you tell me how attached you are to “Tim White” versus tweaks, I can help you brainstorm stage-name options that still feel like *you* but won’t be a nightmare in the union database.

## Assistant

Short version, Mr. White:  

- **You have a strong, explicit right to use “Tim White” if that’s your legal name.**  
- **Another member can’t “own” that name in SAG the way you’re imagining, and they can’t sell it like a title deed.**

I’ll break it down in plain English.

---

### 1. How “surefire” is it to use your exact legal name?

SAG-AFTRA’s own policy says:

- Their **objective** is that no two members use the same or confusingly similar professional name.citeturn0view0  
- BUT they **“cannot preclude a member from using their legal name as their professional name.”** When that happens, they make you sign an *Acknowledgement* form and they process it anyway.citeturn0view0turn0view1  

So for you:

- If your government ID literally says **“Tim White”** and you submit that:
  - **SAG can strongly discourage it.**
  - **They cannot outright block you from using it** as your professional name.
  - They’ll have you sign a form saying you understand there may be confusion, liability, etc.

Is it 100% guaranteed? Nothing involving humans is ever 100%, but **as far as SAG’s written policy goes, it’s about as solid as it gets**: if it’s your legal name and you’re willing to sign their Acknowledgement, they process it.

The real downside isn’t “they won’t let you,” it’s:

- You might be constantly mixed up with the other Tim White in casting databases, payroll, IMDb, residuals, etc.
- If the other Tim has any legal beef (trademark/branding), that’s a separate legal world outside SAG’s admin policy.

---

### 2. What can an existing member do with “their” SAG name?

SAG treats the “professional name” as **an administrative thing**, not property you own. Their rules:

- If someone **changes** their professional name, the old one is **held unassigned for 3 years**, then becomes available.citeturn0view1  
- If someone **dies**, their name is held 3 years.citeturn0view1  
- If someone becomes a **terminated former member**, the name is available **immediately**.  
- If they go on **Honorable Withdrawal** or **Suspended Payment Owing**, the name becomes available after **5 years**.citeturn0view1  

So, practically:

- A member **can** voluntarily change their professional name (via a written request), and once that’s approved, the old name eventually becomes available again under those timelines.citeturn0view0turn0view1  
- They **can’t** say “I want to keep this name forever and nobody else can ever use it,” beyond what SAG’s policy already provides.
- They **don’t** have an official, built-in “I grant Derek Jeter permission to also use this name” mechanism. SAG’s policy just isn’t written that way.

---

### 3. The “Derek Jeter” scenario you described

Say there’s already a SAG actor listed as **Derek Jeter**. Then the real MLB Derek Jeter decides to act and join SAG.

Real Derek has a few paths:

1. **Standard path (what SAG wants):**  
   - They tell him: “Name conflict, please pick alternate names.”  
   - He can choose *Derek M. Jeter*, *Derek Sanderson Jeter*, etc.

2. **Legal-name hammer:**
   - His legal name on his passport is Derek Jeter.  
   - Under SAG’s own FAQ, **they cannot preclude him from using his legal name** as his professional name; they’d have him sign the Acknowledgement form and process it.citeturn0view0turn0view1  
   - That could, in theory, leave **two Derek Jeters** on the books (which SAG hates, but they’ve explicitly admitted they can’t legally stop it).

3. **The “other Derek” voluntarily steps aside:**
   - Existing SAG Derek Jeter can **request a name change** (e.g., *Derek J. Jeter* or something totally different).  
   - Once the change is approved, his old name is held 3 years, then becomes available for reuse under the policy.citeturn0view1turn0view0  
   - SAG does not have a formal “hand off the name directly to this other guy tomorrow” process; it still runs through their timing and checks.

And the older Derek doesn’t get a formal “vote” on whether Real Derek can use his legal name. That’s governed by SAG’s policy + general law, not by his personal permission.

---

### 4. Can someone *sell* or *rent* their SAG name “rights”?

This is where it’s important to separate **SAG admin rules** from **actual legal rights**:

- SAG’s **Professional Name** slot is *not* a deed or a trademark; it’s an internal registration meant to minimize confusion.citeturn0view0turn0view1  
- There is **no official SAG mechanism** that says:
  > “Pay me $20k and I’ll transfer my professional name slot to you.”

Could two people do a private deal like:

> “I, Derek Jeter #1, promise I’ll change my SAG name and never use ‘Derek Jeter’ professionally again, in exchange for $20k”?

In reality:

- You could absolutely sign a private contract like that in the outside world.  
- SAG isn’t a party to the deal; they just see a normal **name-change request** from Derek #1, and later a **name-use request** from Derek #2.
- Whether that contract is enforceable, smart, or creates other legal issues is a **lawyer question**, not a SAG policy question.

Also, even if you did that deal:

- Derek #1 changing his name doesn’t give Derek #2 **exclusive** worldwide rights to “Derek Jeter.”  
- Trademark/publicity rights are a whole different area of law, not controlled by SAG’s internal name list.

So: **you can’t really “sell your SAG name” in the clean, official way you’re imagining.** You can only:

- Change your own SAG professional name (freeing your old one under their timing rules), and
- Make whatever private promises/contracts you want outside of SAG (at your own risk, with your own lawyer).
