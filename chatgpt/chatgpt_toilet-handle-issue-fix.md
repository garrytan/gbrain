## Assistant

The issue you're describing is likely due to a faulty flapper, chain, or handle mechanism in your toilet tank. Here's a step-by-step guide to help you fix it:

1. **Turn off the Water Supply**: Locate the water shut-off valve behind your toilet and turn it off. This will prevent water from flowing into the tank while you work on it.

2. **Inspect the Handle and Chain**:
    - Remove the lid from the toilet tank.
    - Check the handle to ensure it moves freely and isn't corroded or loose.
    - Look at the chain that connects the handle to the flapper. It should have a slight slack when the flapper is closed. If the chain is too tight or too loose, adjust it by reattaching it to a different hole in the handle lever.

3. **Check the Flapper**:
    - The flapper is the rubber piece at the bottom of the tank that lifts when you flush and seals the flush valve afterward.
    - Ensure the flapper is properly aligned and seated. If it's worn out or warped, it may not create a proper seal, causing the water to continuously flow or refill intermittently.
    - If the flapper looks damaged or worn, replace it. Flappers are inexpensive and available at most hardware stores. Make sure to get the correct size and type for your toilet model.

4. **Inspect the Flush Valve**:
    - While the tank is empty, check the flush valve seat for any mineral deposits or debris. Clean it if necessary.

5. **Test the Float and Fill Valve**:
    - Check the float mechanism. If it's set too high, the tank will overfill and water will continuously flow into the overflow tube. Adjust the float to the correct water level as indicated by the manufacturer.
    - Inspect the fill valve for any debris or malfunction. If it's not working correctly, consider replacing it.

6. **Reassemble and Test**:
    - Reattach the chain to the handle and the flapper.
    - Turn the water supply back on and allow the tank to fill.
    - Flush the toilet a few times to ensure everything is working smoothly and no water is leaking into the bowl when the tank is full.

If you follow these steps and the problem persists, it may be necessary to replace the entire flush mechanism, including the fill valve and flapper assembly. This is also relatively straightforward and can be done with a universal toilet repair kit.
