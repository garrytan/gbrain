Here’s the **smartest, lowest-friction way** for a pre-seed startup to buy a **MacBook Pro** and stay clean for taxes, accounting, and future diligence.

---

## 1. Buy it **through the company**, not personally
- Pay with the **company card** or company bank account.
- Title it as a **company asset** from day one.
- Avoid reimbursements if possible—they’re messy and raise questions later.

---

## 2. Use **entity["organization","Apple","technology company"] Business / Apple Store for Business**
- Even without a seed round, you qualify.
- Benefits:
  - Business invoice with company name
  - Easier asset tracking
  - Cleaner depreciation records
- You can still buy online; no sales rep required.

---

## 3. **Do NOT finance** (unless cash is truly tight)
**Pay upfront if you can.** Reasons:
- Hardware financing complicates books
- Creates small liabilities that don’t impress future investors
- No real ROI benefit at your stage

Exception: If cash runway is fragile, short-term 0% financing is acceptable—but still second-best.

---

## 4. Expense + depreciate correctly (this matters)
- Treat the MBP as a **capital asset**, not a simple expense.
- Typical treatment:
  - Depreciate over **3–5 years**
  - Or use **Section 179** to expense it immediately (very common for startups)
- Your CPA will usually prefer **Section 179** early on to simplify books.

---

## 5. Skip AppleCare+ unless this is mission-critical hardware
Facts:
- Apple hardware failure rates are low.
- AppleCare+ is usually **not cost-optimal** unless:
  - This is the *only* dev machine
  - Downtime would materially hurt the company
If you do buy it, expense AppleCare separately.

---

## 6. Avoid refurbished **unless cash matters**
- Apple Certified Refurbished is fine technically
- But:
  - Looks marginally worse in diligence
  - Small savings relative to total burn
For a founder machine, buy **new** unless you’re optimizing every dollar.

---

## TL;DR (Best Practice)
**Company card → Apple Business Store → pay upfront → capitalize + Section 179 → no financing**

## The correct setup (clean + scalable)

### 1. Create a **company Apple ID** (not your personal one)
Use:
- `it@yourcompany.com` or `ops@yourcompany.com`
- Company-controlled email + MFA tied to the company, not you personally

Purpose:
- Ownership of hardware
- AppleCare, invoices, and receipts
- Access continuity if founders/employees change

### 2. Enroll in **entity["organization","Apple Business Manager","device management platform"]** (free)
This is the real win.

What it gives you:
- Centralized device ownership (Apple recognizes the Mac as company property)
- Ability to assign devices to users without sharing Apple IDs
- Clean handoff if someone leaves
- Future-proofing for MDM (Jamf, Kandji, etc.)

You do **not** need:
- A seed round
- An IT team
- Multiple employees

Just a legal entity + domain email.

## What **not** to do
- ❌ Don’t tie Macs to your personal Apple ID
- ❌ Don’t share one Apple ID across people

- **Company Apple ID** → purchasing + ownership
- **Your personal Apple ID** → iCloud, Messages, FaceTime, App Store apps on your user account

Apple fully supports this split. Zero downside.
