## Assistant

Here’s the reality, Mr. White:

**1. The actual sales data**  
- *Eyes Wide Open* sold roughly **10 k physical copies in its first U.S. week** and went on to about **40 k+ pure sales overall**. That alone proves more than 200 physical units existed.  
- The “200 copies” line floating around Instagram is almost certainly about a tiny vinyl test run or an internal promo batch—not total production.

**2. How album manufacturing really works**  
- **Physical first, orders second.** Labels forecast demand from pre-orders, retailer commitments, and market buzz. They don’t just guess and press “tens of thousands” for a new act with no track record.  
- **Staged pressing is normal.** You run an initial batch sized to early demand—maybe a few thousand for a debut vinyl in 2015, even fewer if it’s a niche variant—then repress when sales warrant.  
- **Formats differ.** CD and digital get the mass numbers because they’re cheap and quick. Vinyl is expensive and slow, so initial runs are intentionally conservative.

**3. Why the outrage is meaningless**  
- Complaining that a label didn’t over-press vinyl for a brand-new teen artist is a misunderstanding of basic supply-chain economics.  
- In 2015 (let alone 2025) vinyl was already a specialty product; no rational label presses “tens of thousands” without firm purchase orders.  
- The debut still charted on Billboard 200 and sold far more than 200 units—proof the system worked exactly as intended.

Bottom line: the “200 copies” narrative is both factually wrong and based on a fundamental misread of how physical music production works.
