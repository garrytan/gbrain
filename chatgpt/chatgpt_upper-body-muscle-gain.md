# Upper body muscle gain

## User

According to science and recommendations of Mike isratel and Menno hensleman, if I can put on 6lbs of muscle in a year say, due to my limit of genetic potential,, does that mean if I only train upper body I can build bigger arms or do I just put on less lbs of muscle. Assuming I stack volume up on upper body vs split it between upper and lower? Am I able to double upper body muscle? 

---

## Assistant

Short answer:  
You **can** bias more growth to your upper body by putting almost all productive volume there, but you **can’t “double” your upper-body gains** vs a normal, well-designed full-body plan. You’ll just shift where the muscle is added, with diminishing returns, and total yearly muscle gain probably won’t go up – it may even go **down**.

Let’s break it down in practical terms.

---

### 1. Is “6 lbs per year” a hard, global cap?

No. That “6 lbs of muscle per year” is:

- An **estimate** at your current training status  
- Based on a **normal, whole-body program**, decent nutrition, etc.  
- Not a fixed “budget” you must split 50/50 between upper and lower.

Your genetic potential is more like a **lifetime ceiling** for how big each muscle can get.  
How much of that you realize is determined per muscle: **no stimulus = no growth there**.

So if you stop training legs:

- Legs will **maintain or slowly lose** muscle.
- Upper body will continue to grow if you train it hard and recover properly.
- Total net muscle gained (scale weight from lean mass) might still end up around that 6 lbs, maybe a bit less or more – but that number is not a precise “law”.

---

### 2. What happens if you dump all your volume into upper body?

From an RP / Isratel-esque framework:

- Each muscle has:
  - **MEV** (minimum effective volume)
  - **MAV** (maximum adaptive volume – where most growth happens)
  - **MRV** (maximum recoverable volume)

You get the **best ROI** between MEV–MAV. Past that, you enter:

- **Diminishing returns**, then  
- **“Junk volume”** – lots of work, tiny or no extra growth, more fatigue.

If you:

- Take upper-body muscles to their **MAV/MRV**, and  
- Lower body to basically **no training** or just maintenance,

…you’ll **increase the fraction** of your yearly gains that go to upper body, but you **won’t linearly scale growth** just by stacking more sets.

Example idea (not exact numbers, just the logic):

- Balanced training:
  - Chest: 12–16 hard sets/week → X growth
  - Back: 12–16 sets → X
  - Quads/hams/glutes: 12–16 sets → X
- Upper-only focus:
  - Chest: 20–24 sets
  - Back: 20–24 sets
  - Arms/delts: heavily pushed too  
  - Legs: 0–6 sets (maintenance or nothing)

Result:  
- Upper might grow **more than before**, but not **2× more** just because you doubled sets.  
- Much of that extra volume will be **low ROI**.

---

### 3. Can you “double upper-body muscle” vs a normal split?

Realistically: **No.**

Reasons:

1. **Local genetic ceiling per muscle**  
   Biceps, triceps, delts, etc. each have their own upper limit. Training them harder can get you closer to that ceiling **faster**, but not **past** it.

2. **Diminishing returns to volume**  
   Going from 10 → 15 sets/week can noticeably increase growth.  
   Going from 20 → 30 sets/week often just **beats you up**.

3. **Systemic recovery limits**  
   There’s a **whole-body MRV** too: nervous system, connective tissue, sleep, etc. You can’t just infinitely pile upper-body work without that capping adaptation.

So compared with a smart full-body program:

- You might get **somewhat larger arms/chest/delts** at the same time point if you really emphasize them.
- But **not** like: “I was going to put on 3 lbs of upper-body muscle, now it’s 6 lbs.” That’s fantasy land.

---

### 4. What *does* change if you skip legs?

If you stop lower-body hypertrophy work:

- **Upper body**
  - Can be pushed to the **upper end** of its productive volume range.
  - Might end up **a bit bigger** vs if you split volume evenly.
- **Lower body**
  - Either stays similar with a tiny bit of maintenance work (like 3–6 hard sets per week), or  
  - Slowly **shrinks** if you truly do nothing.
- **Total body muscle**
  - Could easily be **similar or lower** than in a balanced program.  
  - The “extra” upper-body size doesn’t fully replace the lost potential lower-body gains.

This is exactly why some **men’s physique** style guys under-train legs: they want a certain look, **not** maximal total muscle mass.

---

### 5. Straight answers to your specific questions

> If I can put on 6 lbs of muscle in a year, does only training upper mean I can build bigger arms, or do I just put on less muscle total?

- You can **bias more of that gain** to upper body.
- You’ll likely have **slightly bigger arms/upper body** than if you split effort evenly.
- But total **lbs of muscle** gained might be **similar or lower**, not higher.

> If I stack volume on upper vs splitting it, am I able to double upper-body muscle?

- **No.** You can’t just double upper-body muscle by reallocating volume.
- You’ll get **some extra** upper-body growth, but far from 2×.

---

### 6. Practical takeaway for you

If your goal is **max jacked upper body** and you don’t care about legs:

- Push upper-body muscles toward the high end of **productive volume**:
  - Rough ballpark: ~15–25 hard sets/week per muscle group (chest, back, delts, arms), with good exercise selection and proximity to failure.
- Keep legs at:
  - **Maintenance** volume (3–6 hard sets/week) if you want to keep what you have, or  
  - **Minimal** if you truly don’t care and accept some leg loss.

Just don’t expect:

- A magical doubling of upper-body gains; expect **incremental advantages** and **aesthetic redistribution**, not a new physics.

## User

But if I’m eating in a surplus of a half point per week. Those pounds can be directed entirely to say my arms vs split between my arms and legs?

---

## Assistant

Good question to zoom in on, Mr. White.

**Short answer:**  
No—you can’t “route” all of that 0.5 lb/week *scale weight* into your arms. Training decides **where** muscle grows; the surplus just **allows** it. Once your arms are growing as fast as they can respond to training, extra calories just become **fat or other tissue**, not extra arm muscle.

---

### 1. What the surplus actually does

~0.5 lb/week gain ≈ ~250 kcal/day surplus.

That extra energy gets partitioned into:

- Muscle (everywhere that’s being trained and can still adapt)  
- Glycogen + water  
- Fat  
- Other lean tissues (organs, etc.)

You do **not** have a selector switch that says:  
> “Put all of it into biceps and triceps.”

Your body allocates based on:

- Local stimulus (what you’re training)  
- Hormones, genetics, recovery  
- How close each muscle is to its current ceiling  

---

### 2. What happens if you only train upper / arms?

If you:

- Train arms (and upper body) hard,  
- Do **little or nothing** for legs,  
- Eat a consistent ~0.5 lb/week surplus,

Then:

- **Leg muscles**:  
  - Won’t grow much, may even shrink slowly if totally untrained.
- **Arms/upper**:  
  - Get *more* of the muscle growth allocation *relative to legs*, because they’re the only ones being signaled.
- **Total weight gain**:  
  - Still around 0.5 lb/week, but only a **fraction** of that is arm muscle.  
  - The rest is fat + other tissues.

So yes, you’re **biasing** where the *muscle* shows up (upper > lower),  
but you are **not** turning that entire 0.5 lb/week into arm meat.

---

### 3. The key cap: local muscle protein synthesis

Each muscle has a **max rate** it can realistically grow at, even with:

- Optimal surplus  
- Plenty of protein  
- Great training

Once your arms are close to that max rate:

- Extra calories **do not** make them grow proportionally more.  
- They mostly just increase **fat gain** or go to other processes.

That’s very much in line with how Isratel / Menno talk about it:

- Surplus = **permissive**, not infinitely **anabolic**.  
- **Training + genetics** set the growth rate; once that’s saturated, more food = more **fat**, not more muscle.

---

### 4. So to answer your exact question

> Those pounds can be directed entirely to say my arms vs split between my arms and legs?

- **No**, not as scale weight.  
- You can:
  - Make **most of your muscle growth** happen in the upper body by only training it.
  - Minimize leg growth by not training legs.
- But you **cannot** make the entire 0.5 lb/week gain become arm muscle. Only a **small slice** of that weekly gain will be arm muscle; the rest is fat and other tissue.
