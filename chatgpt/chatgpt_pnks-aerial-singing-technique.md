Mr. White, there’s very little formal research on “standing-on-the‐abs” vocal drills—most of it is anecdotal from elite performers. In essence, loading the abdominal wall forces the diaphragm and transverse abdominis to work harder just to phonate, but it isn’t a safe or optimal way to build “breath support” long‐term. From a strength‐science perspective (per RP Strength and Dr. Mike Israetel), you need three principles for durable core engagement under load:  

1. **Progressive Overload & Specificity:**  
   • You want to build the muscles that stabilize the ribcage while singing—namely, the diaphragm, transverse abdominis, pelvic floor, and obliques. citeturn0search2turn0search6  
   • Instead of a person standing on your gut, use graduated resistance: e.g., sing sustained notes while bracing into a light sandbag press against the lower ribs (start at 5–10 lb and ramp up over weeks). This follows RP Strength’s model of progressively overloading a muscle group.

2. **Core‐Stabilization Drills With Phonation:**  
   • Dr. Mike Israetel emphasizes co‐contraction exercises: brace your core as if preparing for a heavy squat, then phonate on a comfortable pitch. Hold a plank (or a light-loaded Pallof press) while doing breath‐control exercises—lip trills or straw phonation—so you’re learning to stabilize under true axial tension.  
   • This trains intra‐abdominal pressure without risking bruises or rib trauma.

3. **Breathing‐Resistance Devices & True Diaphragmatic Work:**  
   • Devices like the PowerLung or Breather let you work the inspiratory muscles against calibrated resistance. You can do 2–3× per week, 30–60 breaths per set, building up to moderate resistance. That’s far more precise (and safer) than random external weight on your torso.

In short, the “person standing on her abs” approach is a flashy gimmick with questionable safety and no real advantage over targeted, progressive core‐and‐breath‐strengthening. A more effective protocol is:  

- **Diaphragm‐specific resistance** (PowerLung/Breather, 2–3×/week)  
- **Core‐brace phonation drills** (plank + lip trill or straw on exhale, 3–5 sets of 20–30 sec)  
- **Sandbag/rib‐press phonation** (5–10 lb starting weight, 2–3 sets of 10–15 sec phonation)  

That respects both RP Strength’s principles and Dr. Mike’s emphasis on stability under load—without risking injury from someone stomping on your midsection.
