## Assistant

Mr. White, assuming this is ordinary mechanical / nonspecific low back pain: **yes, train your spinal erectors, but do it as graded capacity work rather than chasing lower-back hypertrophy as the main cure.**

The science-based target is **tolerance to movement and loading**: walking, normal activity, progressive resistance training, hip-hinge competence, trunk endurance, sleep/recovery, and avoiding repeated flare-triggering volume. Chronic low back pain responds best to exercise-based care; a Cochrane review found exercise improved chronic low-back pain versus no treatment/usual care/placebo, with pain about **15 points better on a 0–100 scale** at around three months. citeturn906695view2

## First: rule out “don’t mess around” symptoms

Get medical evaluation promptly if you have **new bowel/bladder dysfunction, saddle numbness, progressive leg weakness, fever, unexplained weight loss, history of cancer, major trauma, immunosuppression, or severe unrelenting night/rest pain**. Guidelines emphasize screening for cancer, infection, trauma, inflammatory disease, and cauda equina-type neurologic signs. citeturn457097search2turn457097search15

Routine imaging is usually low-value without red flags or a result that would change management; NICE specifically advises against routine imaging in nonspecialist settings and encourages people to continue normal activity. citeturn317176view1

## Stronger spinal erectors?

**Probably useful, rarely the whole answer.**

Spinal erectors can be undertrained, deconditioned, or intolerant to fatigue. Strengthening them can help. The evidence does support resistance training for chronic nonspecific low back pain, but the newest reviews suggest externally loaded resistance training is **safe and feasible** while often producing similar outcomes to unloaded exercise; that points toward gradual exposure, consistency, and appropriate dosing as the key drivers. citeturn317176view5

A useful way to think about it:

**Weak/tolerant issue:** your back aches from standing, walking, basic hinging, or light daily tasks.  
→ Build endurance and progressive loading.

**Overload/recovery issue:** pain follows deadlifts, rows, good mornings, heavy squats, or high-volume posterior-chain days.  
→ Reduce direct spinal loading and rebuild.

**Technique/bracing issue:** pain spikes when you lose position under load.  
→ Train bracing and hinge patterning. RP’s bracing framing is directionally useful here: the trunk, including abs, obliques, and erectors, is the force-transfer structure for lifting. citeturn811065view2

## Lower-back hypertrophy?

**Eventually, yes. As treatment priority, no.**

Bigger erectors can be valuable for lifting and physique. Menno Henselmans notes the erectors are mainly trained through squats, deadlifts, and dedicated spinal-extension work. citeturn811065view1 But if you are currently in pain, chasing a lower-back pump, soreness, or failure is a bad first objective.

Use an RP-style “minimum effective dose” mindset: start with low volume, good technique, and steady progression. RP’s own hypertrophy guidance treats volume landmarks as starting points, and notes that less-trained or less-tolerant lifters should use lower volumes and steady progression. citeturn811065view0

## Practical plan

### During a flare: 3–10 days

Keep moving, but pull back from heavy spinal loading.

Do:

- **Walk** 10–20 minutes, 1–3 times per day.
- Use **heat** if it helps.
- Keep normal daily activity, scaled down.
- Avoid bed rest except brief rest periods.
- Pause heavy deadlifts, good mornings, heavy bent-over rows, max-effort squats, and loaded spinal flexion.

The ACP guideline recommends non-drug options first for acute/subacute low back pain, including superficial heat, massage, acupuncture, or spinal manipulation; for chronic low back pain, it recommends starting with non-drug treatments such as exercise, motor-control exercise, yoga, tai chi, mindfulness-based stress reduction, CBT, or spinal manipulation. citeturn317176view0

### Rebuild phase: 4–8 weeks

Train the low back **2–3 times/week**, submaximally.

Use this pain rule: **0–3/10 discomfort is acceptable if it does not worsen later that day or the next morning.** If pain increases for more than 24 hours, cut load/ROM/sets by 30–50%.

Example template:

**Day A**

- Bird dog: 2–3 sets of 6–10/side, slow
- Side plank: 2–3 sets of 20–45 sec/side
- Hip thrust or glute bridge: 2–4 sets of 8–15
- Light 45-degree back extension: 2–3 sets of 10–15, RPE 5–7

**Day B**

- Dead bug or Pallof press: 2–3 sets
- Romanian deadlift: 2–3 sets of 6–10, RPE 5–7
- Chest-supported row: 2–4 sets of 8–15
- Farmer carry: 2–4 short carries, braced

**Day C, optional**

- Walking, sled push, split squat, hamstring curl, or reverse hyper if tolerated.

Progression: add a little range, reps, load, or one set only when symptoms are stable for two sessions.

### Long-term “bulletproofing”

Aim for:

- **Walking:** at least 3–5 sessions/week. A 2024 Lancet WalkBack trial of 701 adults found a progressive walking + education program reduced activity-limiting low-back-pain recurrence; median time to recurrence was **208 days vs. 112 days** in controls. citeturn906695view1
- **Direct erector work:** 4–8 hard-ish sets/week at first.
- **Heavy hinge exposure:** 1–2 times/week once symptoms are stable.
- **No failure work for the low back** until you have had several pain-stable weeks.
- **Deload** if low-back fatigue accumulates across squats, deadlifts, rows, RDLs, and back extensions.

## What to avoid wasting time on

Lumbar braces, belts as pain treatment, traction, passive treatment by itself, and routine imaging usually have weak value for nonspecific low back pain. WHO recommends education, exercise, some manual therapies, psychological therapies, and NSAIDs where appropriate for chronic primary low back pain, and advises against routine lumbar braces/supports, traction, and opioids for most people. citeturn906695view4

## Verdict

**Train spinal erectors?** Yes, gradually.  
**Train lower-back hypertrophy?** Later, after symptoms are stable.  
**Main objective:** build load tolerance, trunk endurance, hinge mechanics, walking capacity, and recovery consistency.  
**Best first move:** two weeks of walking + low-volume trunk/hinge work, then progressively reintroduce heavier hinges.
