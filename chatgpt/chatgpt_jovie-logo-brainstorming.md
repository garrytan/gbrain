## Assistant

Mr. White,

The right Jovie logo feels like **compounding momentum**. It should read as a system, a cadence, and a release engine. Your current flywheel instinct is the right foundation.

Here are the strongest directions.

## 1. Release Wheel
A **solid black circle** with one **clean white channel** carved through it. The cut has a slight clockwise sweep, so it feels like the wheel is already moving. The lower end of the cut can finish with a subtle hook that hints at a **J**.

This is the strongest route. It says:
- flywheel
- weekly motion
- release cycle
- operational system

At favicon size it still reads. On merch, watermarks, and video end cards it stays premium.

## 2. Orbit J
A thick circular form that almost closes, with a precise gap and a vertical or curved inner cut that makes a hidden **J**. It feels a little more elegant and a little less forceful than the Release Wheel.

This is strong if you want Jovie to feel more cinematic and refined.

## 3. Cascade Mark
Three simple forms stepping upward on a diagonal. Think of three bold arcs or blocks that feel like:
song → lyric video → acoustic version → live version

This directly expresses the upward wave you described. It is conceptually good. It is less iconic than the wheel unless it gets reduced to a very simple silhouette.

## 4. Open Packet
A square or rounded-square form with one panel opening outward. It feels like a release package unfolding into formats, channels, and fan touchpoints.

This is a good product-company logo. It is more “system software” and less “momentum.”

## 5. Monogram Loop
A custom **J** built from one continuous stroke that almost closes into a circle. It can feel premium and ownable if drawn well.

This is useful as a secondary route. It works best when the J still feels like motion, not just typography.

## The best answer
I would push hardest on **Release Wheel + subtle J**.

The clearest brief is:

**A bold circular flywheel with one decisive negative-space cut that implies motion, release cadence, and a hidden J.**

That gives you:
- an icon that stands on its own
- a clean black/white default
- a mark that animates naturally
- a symbol that matches the product story exactly

## What it should look like
Picture a **solid black disc**.  
A **white curved slash/channel** enters around the upper-left or upper-right and exits lower on the form.  
The cut is asymmetric. That asymmetry creates energy.  
The exit of the cut resolves into a tiny bend or terminal that quietly suggests the letter **J**.

That is enough. One shape. One cut. One memorable move.

## Design rules
Use these constraints:

1. **Filled shape over outline.** Filled marks hold better at small sizes and feel more premium.
2. **One distinctive cut.** Multiple lines make it generic.
3. **Slight asymmetry.** Perfect symmetry kills the feeling of motion.
4. **No explicit music symbols.** Keep it abstract and ownable.
5. **Pass the 16px test.** It has to survive as an app icon, favicon, and watermark.
6. **Color belongs around the mark, not inside it.** The logo stays black or white. Campaign color can live in backgrounds, rings, frames, motion, or surrounding UI.

## What to avoid
Avoid notes, equalizer bars, vinyl grooves, smiley cues, sparkles, and busy monograms. Those push it toward “creator app” or “music startup.” Jovie should feel like the operating system behind sustained releases.

## Wordmark direction
For the wordmark, I would keep it restrained and custom:
- likely **jovie** in lowercase
- a custom **j**
- a **v** angle or terminal that echoes the cut in the icon
- generous spacing
- clean, high-contrast geometry with one human curve

The icon does the heavy lifting. The wordmark should support it.

## The practical next move
Sketch **9 black-and-white versions** of the Release Wheel across three variables:
- curved cut / angled cut / hybrid cut
- obvious J / subtle J / no visible J
- perfect circle / slightly optical circle / heavier bottom weight

One of those will likely emerge as the brand.

The direction I would bet on is this:

**Jovie = a stable wheel with a moving cut.**  
That captures the whole thesis: keep creating, keep shipping, keep compounding.
