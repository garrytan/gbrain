## Assistant

Mr. White, use this as the call script.

## Call strategy

Assuming this is **AI2 Incubator / AI House**, tailor the call around this: they care about **applied AI**, **domain expertise**, **technical commercialization**, **GTM**, **customer pilots**, and **fundraising readiness**. Their published model says they work with founders on AI architecture, model development, UX/UI, product strategy, customer pilots, GTM, hiring, storytelling, and fundraising; remote incubation is supported; published funding is up to **$600K SAFE at a $10M cap**, with early solo-founder allocations starting at **$100K**, plus **7% common stock**. citeturn545203view1turn545203view2

Your frame:

> **Jovie is the AI manager for music artists. I spent 15 years doing this manually. Now I’m turning those workflows into software.**

Do **not** over-explain smart links. Smart links are the wedge. The real company is the **agentic business layer for music catalogs**.

---

# 1. Opening

### You

> Thanks for taking the time. Daniel thought this would be a strong fit because AI House seems focused on applied AI companies with real domain complexity, and that’s exactly what Jovie is.
>
> My goal today is simple: show you why this should exist now, why I’m the right founder to build it, and figure out whether AI House is the right partner to help us go from prototype to repeatable revenue.

### Then ask

> Before I jump in, what would make this a useful call for you? Are you mostly trying to understand the market, the product, the AI architecture, founder fit, or fundraising fit?

---

# 2. If they say: “Just give us the pitch.”

### You

> Jovie is the AI manager for music artists.
>
> Music creation is becoming one-click. Distribution is already one-click. But turning a catalog into revenue is still a mess. Artists have releases, fans, merch, shows, content, playlists, collaborators, distributors, and platforms — but no operating system that connects all of it.
>
> Jovie imports an artist’s catalog, builds their profile and smart links, captures fans, watches for release and audience signals, and then turns those signals into actions: fan alerts, merch drops, campaign tasks, reports, playlist-pitch drafts, distributor updates, and eventually collaboration and sync outreach.
>
> I’ve spent 15 years doing this manually in music — 90M+ streams, releases with Armada and Black Hole, licensing through Universal, and real catalog growth work. The bet is that the next layer of music infrastructure is an AI operator that helps artists make money from the catalog they already have.

Then stop.

Let them react.

---

# 3. If they seem interested

### Them

> That’s interesting. How does it work?

### You

> The loop is simple:
>
> **Catalog context → opportunity detection → generated action → human approval → execution → revenue or audience signal → memory.**
>
> Today, the product can ingest catalog data, build artist profiles, create smart links, support fan capture, and start turning releases into workflows. The first monetizable workflows are fan alerts, release campaigns, merch drops, and automated reporting.
>
> The product becomes more valuable as it learns the artist’s catalog, audience, releases, geography, collaborators, and business history.

### Then bridge to AI House

> This is why AI House is interesting to me. The hard part is not making a chatbot for artists. The hard part is turning messy creative-business context into reliable agentic execution.

---

# 4. If they ask: “Why now?”

### You

> Three reasons.
>
> First, creation and distribution have been solved. Suno and similar tools make creation easier. TuneCore, DistroKid, and others made distribution easier. The bottleneck moved to monetization and audience operations.
>
> Second, the volume of music and creators is exploding, which makes manual artist management impossible for the long tail.
>
> Third, AI agents are finally good enough to handle the repetitive business workflows around releases: generate the plan, draft the copy, build the assets, update the artist profile, notify fans, write the report, and remember what worked.
>
> So the timing is right: artists have more content, more tools, more platforms, and more chaos. Jovie turns that chaos into a repeatable operating loop.

---

# 5. If they ask: “Is music a big enough market?”

### You

> I’d separate the wedge from the long-term market.
>
> The wedge is independent artists, especially creators with catalogs and frequent releases — DJs, producers, and artist teams. That’s where I have unfair insight and access.
>
> The larger market is music IP operations: managers, labels, distributors, catalog owners, and eventually broader cultural IP businesses.
>
> Recorded music alone reached **$31.7B globally in 2025**, growing **6.4% year over year**, and that excludes a lot of the activity Jovie can touch: merch, live, sync, creator campaigns, fan monetization, and services around releases. citeturn762784search0turn762784news20
>
> So I’m starting with artists because that’s the sharpest wedge. The product expands upward to teams that manage many artists and catalogs.

---

# 6. If they ask: “Aren’t artists broke?”

### You

> Many are. That’s why the wedge has to be free profile creation, smart links, catalog import, and audience capture.
>
> The paid users are artists and teams already spending money on tools, marketing, design, SMS, link-in-bio products, distribution add-ons, freelancers, and managers.
>
> The bigger revenue opportunity is teams: managers, labels, distributors, and artist-services companies managing many catalogs. The artist wedge gives us the data layer and product surface. The economic buyer can move upmarket.

---

# 7. If they ask: “What’s the actual product today?”

### You

> Today Jovie has the early primitives:
>
> - Spotify/catalog ingest
> - artist profiles
> - smart links
> - fan capture
> - release pages
> - early AI workflow generation
> - early campaign/reporting infrastructure
>
> The next product milestone is to make one revenue loop undeniable:
>
> **import catalog → detect opportunity → generate campaign or merch drop → publish → notify fans → measure results → report back.**
>
> That’s what I want funding and AI House support to accelerate.

---

# 8. If they ask: “What’s the AI?”

### You

> The AI layer has three jobs.
>
> First, understand the artist: catalog, releases, fan signals, geography, audience, brand, and history.
>
> Second, identify opportunities: a release moment, a merch angle, a fan alert, a playlist pitch, a distributor report, a collaboration lead.
>
> Third, execute the workflow: generate the assets, draft the message, create the page, recommend timing, and remember the outcome.
>
> The important part is the closed loop. Jovie should learn which actions drive clicks, fan captures, purchases, replies, and revenue for each artist.

### If they push harder

> I’m early, so I’m not pretending the architecture is done. The current opportunity is to harden the memory, evaluation, workflow orchestration, and tool execution layer. That’s one reason AI House is attractive: this is an applied AI systems problem inside a messy real-world domain.

---

# 9. If they ask: “Why are you the founder?”

### You

> I have founder-market fit.
>
> I’ve spent 15 years inside music doing the work Jovie is automating. I’ve driven 90M+ streams, released with Armada and Black Hole, licensed through Universal, worked across creator marketing and catalog growth, and manually built the kinds of workflows most artists never systemize.
>
> I also know the emotional reality of artists. They don’t wake up wanting a dashboard. They want more fans, more money, more momentum, and less admin.
>
> Jovie is built from that insight.

---

# 10. If they ask: “Why hasn’t this existed already?”

### You

> The old market was too fragmented and too manual.
>
> Artists used separate tools for links, email, SMS, distribution, merch, analytics, content, and ads. None of those tools had persistent context across the artist’s catalog and career.
>
> AI changes that because the product can finally reason across messy context and generate useful work from it.
>
> The missing layer is memory plus execution. That’s Jovie.

---

# 11. If they ask: “Who are competitors?”

### You

> There are three categories.
>
> **Smart links:** Linkfire, Feature.fm, Hypeddit-type tools.  
> **Music marketing platforms:** SymphonyOS and adjacent campaign tools.  
> **Artist-services / management workflows:** managers, freelancers, agencies, label services.
>
> Jovie starts with some familiar surfaces, like profiles and smart links, because artists understand them. The differentiated product is the AI manager layer that detects opportunities and executes revenue workflows around the catalog.

### If they say “couldn’t they add AI?”

> Yes, existing tools can add AI features.
>
> The question is whether they have the persistent artist context, workflow system, and incentive to become the operating layer around the catalog. Jovie is built around that from day one.

---

# 12. If they ask: “What’s traction?”

### You

> We’re early and pre-scale.
>
> Current traction is:
>
> - live product
> - founder dogfooding
> - one active dogfood artist
> - four organic external artists from product exposure
> - zero paid acquisition
> - $25K angel check committed
> - 180K people in my own founder/artist audience
>
> I’m intentionally gating onboarding until the product is stable enough that the first artist experience is strong.
>
> The next milestone is first repeatable paid adoption and a clear path to roughly $250K ARR.

---

# 13. If they ask: “What are you raising?”

### You

> The standalone bridge plan is **$500K on a $5M post-money SAFE**, with **$25K already committed**.
>
> For AI House specifically, I understand your published structure is different — up to $600K on a SAFE at a $10M cap, with early solo-founder allocations starting around $100K and 7% common stock. I’m treating this as a fit conversation around the program and partnership, not just a check. citeturn545203view1turn545203view2

### If they ask use of funds

> The use of funds is very focused:
>
> 1. Harden the core product loop.
> 2. Improve AI workflow reliability.
> 3. Convert organic artists into paying users.
> 4. Build the first repeatable GTM motion.
> 5. Get to a seed-fundable story around revenue, retention, and workflow execution.

---

# 14. If they ask: “Why AI House?”

### You

> AI House seems unusually aligned with what Jovie needs.
>
> I don’t just need generic accelerator advice. I need help turning a high-context, messy industry workflow into a reliable applied AI system.
>
> The useful areas are:
>
> - AI architecture and agent evaluation
> - product strategy
> - GTM and pricing
> - customer/design partner access
> - fundraising readiness
> - technical and strategic pressure from people who understand applied AI
>
> That maps directly to what AI2 says it helps with: AI architecture, model development, UX/UI, product strategy, customer pilots, GTM, storytelling, and fundraising. citeturn545203view1

---

# 15. If they say: “This feels like a creator tool.”

### You

> That’s the risk, and I’m intentionally designing against it.
>
> The creator-tool trap is selling subscriptions to broke individual creators with high churn and low urgency.
>
> Jovie starts with artists because that’s where the data and workflow starts. The durable business is the operating layer for people and companies managing music IP: artists, managers, labels, distributors, and catalog owners.
>
> The more catalog, fan, campaign, and revenue data Jovie owns, the more valuable the system becomes.

---

# 16. If they say: “This sounds like a services business.”

### You

> The market currently solves this with services. That’s exactly why software can win.
>
> Managers, freelancers, labels, and agencies already do this manually: plan the release, create assets, update links, write fans, pitch playlists, report results, sell merch.
>
> Jovie productizes that work.
>
> The first version can be high-touch because I want to learn the workflow deeply. The company only works if the repeated workflows become software.

---

# 17. If they say: “Where does revenue come from?”

### You

> Three layers.
>
> First: SaaS for artists and teams. Free profile, paid automation.
>
> Second: usage or transaction fees around high-value workflows like fan alerts, merch, and commerce.
>
> Third: team/label/distributor plans for managing multiple artists and catalogs.
>
> I’m still testing pricing. I’m more confident in the value wedge than the exact packaging. The near-term goal is to prove that Jovie can create measurable revenue or fan growth for artists.

---

# 18. If they ask: “What would prove this works?”

### You

> The first proof point is simple:
>
> Artists import a catalog, Jovie identifies an opportunity, Jovie ships a workflow, and the artist gets measurable output: fan captures, clicks, merch sales, replies, or revenue.
>
> After that, I’d track:
>
> - activation rate from catalog import to published profile
> - fan capture rate
> - workflow completion rate
> - revenue per artist
> - repeat workflow usage
> - artist/team conversion to paid
> - retention by catalog size and release frequency

---

# 19. If they ask: “What’s the seed story?”

### You

> The seed story is:
>
> Jovie proves that music artists and teams will let an AI agent operate the business layer around their catalog.
>
> If we can show that artists import catalogs, activate profiles, capture fans, run repeat workflows, and pay because the system drives measurable outcomes, then this becomes the AI operating layer for music IP.
>
> The seed round funds expansion from individual artists into teams, labels, distributors, and catalog owners.

---

# 20. If they are skeptical or quiet

### You

> The simplest way to evaluate this is: do you believe the business side of creative work becomes agentic?
>
> If yes, music is one of the best starting points because it has repeated launches, fragmented tools, emotional users, messy data, clear revenue moments, and a founder with deep domain access.
>
> That’s the core bet.

Then ask:

> Which part feels least proven to you right now — market, product, AI depth, GTM, or founder fit?

---

# 21. If they like it

### Them

> This is interesting. We should keep talking.

### You

> Great. The next best step would be a product and technical deep dive.
>
> I can show the current product, the catalog import flow, the artist profile, and the roadmap for the first revenue workflow.
>
> Who else should be in that conversation?

Then close:

> What would you need to believe after that call to move forward?

---

# 22. If they are lukewarm

### Them

> Keep us posted.

### You

> Makes sense. To make the follow-up useful, what milestone would change your level of interest?
>
> Is it first paid artists, stronger demo, more technical architecture, clearer GTM, or a specific kind of customer signal?

If they give an answer:

> That’s helpful. I’ll use that as the bar and follow up with exactly that evidence.

---

# 23. If they pass

### Them

> It’s too early / not a fit.

### You

> Understood. Before we wrap, I’d love the blunt version: is the issue stage, market, AI depth, founder fit, or something else?
>
> And is there one person you think would understand this market better than most?

---

# 24. Questions you should ask them

Use 3–5 of these, depending on time.

> What do you need to see at this stage to get conviction?

> How do you evaluate domain-expert founders building applied AI companies?

> For AI House, what separates a strong applied AI company from a thin AI wrapper?

> Where do you think Jovie is weakest right now: AI depth, GTM, market, product, or business model?

> If you were helping me for the next 90 days, what would you push me to prove first?

> Does AI House usually help more with technical architecture, GTM, customer pilots, or fundraising at this stage?

> Who in your network has thought deeply about music, creator tools, or vertical AI workflows?

---

# 25. Lines to memorize

> **Jovie is the AI manager for music artists.**

> **The product does not make music. It makes the business around music happen.**

> **Smart links are the wedge. The company is the AI operating layer around the catalog.**

> **Artists don’t want dashboards. They want more fans, more money, more momentum, and less admin.**

> **I spent 15 years doing this manually. Jovie turns that playbook into software.**

> **The loop is catalog context, opportunity detection, execution, measurement, memory.**

> **The wedge is independent artists. The expansion is teams managing music IP.**

---

# 26. Things to avoid saying

| Avoid | Say instead |
|---|---|
| “It’s like Linkfire with AI.” | “Smart links are the wedge. Jovie is the AI manager around the catalog.” |
| “Artists are broke.” | “The free wedge serves artists; the paid product expands to serious artists, teams, managers, labels, and distributors.” |
| “We’re pre-traction.” | “We’re early, with a live product, organic artists, founder dogfooding, and a clear first revenue loop.” |
| “AI OS for music rights owners.” | Use only after they understand the simple version. Start with “AI manager for music artists.” |
| Long feature lists | Keep returning to the loop: context → opportunity → action → result → memory. |

---

# 27. Best closing script

> I think Jovie is early in exactly the way AI House can help: strong founder-market fit, real product, messy applied AI problem, clear wedge, and a big expansion path if the first workflow works.
>
> The next thing I’d like to do is show you the product and get your read on whether this is the kind of company AI House wants to help build.
>
> What should the next step be?
