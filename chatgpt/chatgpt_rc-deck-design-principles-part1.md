Mr. White, the world-class Jovie RC deck should look like a **YC-style fundraising deck that is aggressively plain, huge, readable, and proof-oriented**.

Michael Seibel’s core frame is: be clear and concise, explain what you do in simple language, cover what you do / team / traction / unique insight / market size / ask, and make the slides boring enough that the investor listens to you instead of studying the design. citeturn607928view1turn607928view0

Jovie’s official docs currently describe it as “the all-in-one platform for musicians to manage their career, connect with fans, and grow their audience,” with features around profiles, releases, smart links, audience tools, chat/AI, tips, and analytics. citeturn884748view0 The deck should compress that into a much simpler investor sentence:

# Jovie is the AI manager for music artists.

That is the headline. Everything else proves it.

## Design rules

Use **10–12 slides max**. White or black background. One font. No decorative design system. No tiny screenshots. No paragraphs.

Each slide gets:

**1 big headline**  
**1 proof point or visual**  
**1 short supporting line max**

Recommended type sizes:

| Element | Size |
|---|---:|
| Slide headline | 52–72 pt |
| Supporting line | 30–40 pt |
| Metric | 72–110 pt |
| Screenshot labels | 24–32 pt |
| Footer/source text | 12–16 pt |

The screenshot principle is exactly right: a product shot on the cover proves, instantly, that **this is software**, **it exists**, and **it is music-related**. That removes cognitive risk before the investor hears the pitch.

## The deck

### 1. Cover

**Purpose:** Make the company impossible to misunderstand in 5 seconds.

**Layout:**  
Left side: text.  
Right side: large product shot, ideally showing an artist profile, audio player, fan/chat interaction, or AI manager interface.

**Copy:**

**Meet Jovie**  
**The AI manager for music artists**

Subline:  
**Artists use Jovie to grow fans, manage releases, and run their career.**

No logo-only intro. No abstract mission. No “future of music” language.

---

### 2. What Jovie does

**Purpose:** Give the investor the clean product model.

**Copy:**

**Jovie helps artists do the work a manager would do.**

Then three plain words or phrases:

**Plan releases**  
**Talk to fans**  
**Grow revenue**

This slide should feel almost too simple. Seibel’s YC guidance is to start with what the company does in the simplest possible language and avoid making the idea sound overly impressive. citeturn607928view1

---

### 3. Specific example

**Purpose:** Make the product concrete.

**Copy:**

**A 50k-listener artist releases a single.**

Jovie helps them:

**Build the release page**  
**Message fans**  
**Track what worked**

Use a real example if you have one. Even better:

**“Artist X used Jovie to launch Y and got Z fan actions in N days.”**

This is the Airbnb-style “specific example” slide. It turns “AI manager” from concept into a workflow.

---

### 4. Product proof

**Purpose:** Show the product exists and what it actually does.

**Layout:**  
One large screenshot. Two callouts max.

**Headline:**

**The artist runs their career from one place.**

Callouts:

**AI chat with fans**  
**Releases, links, tips, analytics**

Jovie’s docs already support the feature cluster: profiles, releases/smart links, audience tools, chat/AI, tips, and analytics. citeturn884748view0

Do not show a dense dashboard. Show the most “manager-like” moment. The investor should see: “Oh, the artist asks Jovie to do something, and Jovie helps run the business.”

---

### 5. Traction / progress

**Purpose:** Prove speed and momentum.

Seibel’s traction guidance is that traction needs a time box because investors are judging how much you accomplished relative to how long you’ve been working on it. citeturn607928view0

**Headline options:**

**Built and launched in X weeks**  
or  
**Artists are already using Jovie**

Then use the three best numbers only:

**X artists onboarded**  
**Y fan conversations**  
**$Z tips / revenue / transactions**

The exact numbers matter less than the relationship between **speed** and **progress**. A weak example:

“500 artists signed up”

A stronger example:

“500 artists signed up in 21 days”

A world-class version:

**500 artists joined in 21 days**  
**38% connected Spotify**  
**$12k fan revenue processed**

Use whatever your strongest real metrics are. If traction is meaningfully strong, this should be slide 2, right after the cover.

---

### 6. Unique insight

**Purpose:** Teach the investor something they did not already believe.

**Headline:**

**Artists do not need another dashboard. They need someone to do the work.**

Supporting line:

**Music tools tell artists what happened. Jovie helps them decide what to do next.**

This is the most important strategic slide. The insight is: artists are becoming solo operators of increasingly complex businesses. They need management capacity before they can afford management.

Alternative sharper version:

**The next generation of artists will be managed by software before they are managed by people.**

This is probably the best “big idea” line in the deck.

---

### 7. Why now

**Purpose:** Explain why this becomes a venture-scale company now.

**Headline:**

**Millions of artists are now small businesses.**

Supporting proof points:

**Distribution is self-serve**  
**Fan channels are fragmented**  
**AI can finally operate the workflow**

The global recorded music market reached **$31.7B in 2025**, growing 6.4%, and IFPI says paid streaming continues to drive industry growth. citeturn663821search1 That is useful context, but avoid making the market slide only about recorded music revenue. Jovie’s market is artist operations software, manager replacement, fan monetization, and independent artist infrastructure.

---

### 8. Market

**Purpose:** Show venture scale with bottom-up math.

Seibel specifically recommends bottom-up market sizing: number of customers multiplied by revenue per customer per year. citeturn607928view0

**Headline:**

**Jovie can become the operating system for independent artists.**

Use a simple equation:

**N artists × $X/year = $Y market**

Example structure:

**1M serious independent artists**  
**× $300/year**  
**= $300M annual software market**

Then optional expansion:

**+ fan commerce**  
**+ tips**  
**+ services**  
**+ data / financial tools**

The slide should show your math, not a giant music-industry TAM report.

---

### 9. Business model

**Purpose:** Show how Jovie captures value.

**Headline:**

**Jovie makes money when artists grow.**

Pick one primary model. Do not list five monetization ideas.

Best likely version:

**Subscription per artist**  
**+ take rate on fan revenue**

Example:

**$19–$99/month per artist**  
**5–10% of tips, drops, or fan commerce**

If today’s actual model is different, use the real model. The key is that the investor should understand how revenue scales without needing an explanation.

---

### 10. Go-to-market

**Purpose:** Show how Jovie acquires artists cheaply.

**Headline:**

**Every artist profile is distribution.**

Three mechanisms max:

**Claim your artist profile**  
**Share release links**  
**Fans discover Jovie through artists**

This is stronger than “we’ll run ads” because it implies product-led growth. If the product has public artist pages, smart links, fan chat, or tipping pages, those are acquisition surfaces.

Best version with numbers:

**X public artist pages**  
**Y monthly profile visits**  
**Z% convert to artist signups**

---

### 11. Team

**Purpose:** Show founder-market fit and ability to ship.

**Headline:**

**Built by artists and software operators.**

For you, the strongest version is probably:

**Tim White — artist + founder**  
**90M+ streams / releases across major labels**  
**Building the tool he needed as an artist**

Then add technical/product proof for whoever builds. Seibel’s team guidance is to state what each person does and list specific accomplishments, not long biographies. citeturn607928view0

This slide should feel like: “This founder understands the artist problem from inside the machine and can build software.”

---

### 12. Ask

**Purpose:** Ask directly and tie the money to milestones.

Seibel’s ask guidance is direct: say how much you are raising and what milestone the money gets you to; focus on revenue or usage milestones rather than hiring plans. citeturn607928view0

**Headline:**

**Raising $X to make Jovie the AI manager for 100k artists.**

Then:

**Raise:** $X  
**Runway:** 18–24 months  
**Milestone:** X artists / $Y ARR / $Z fan revenue

Bad ask:

“Use of funds: engineering, marketing, operations”

Better ask:

**$1.5M gets us to:**  
**25k artists**  
**$500k ARR**  
**$2M fan revenue processed**

Investors care what the money produces.

## The best slide order

Use this order if traction is already meaningful:

1. Cover: Meet Jovie  
2. Traction  
3. What Jovie does  
4. Specific example  
5. Product proof  
6. Unique insight  
7. Why now  
8. Market  
9. Business model  
10. Go-to-market  
11. Team  
12. Ask

Use this order if traction is still early:

1. Cover: Meet Jovie  
2. What Jovie does  
3. Product proof  
4. Specific example  
5. Unique insight  
6. Progress / build speed  
7. Why now  
8. Market  
9. Business model  
10. Go-to-market  
11. Team  
12. Ask

Seibel’s guidance allows flexibility in the middle of the deck: put the most impressive material early, because every couple of minutes must earn the next couple of minutes. citeturn607928view0

Mr. White, this is the stronger company:

# Jovie is the AI manager that finds and fixes the business gaps in an artist’s career.

The old framing sounds like:

**profiles · releases · fans · links · tips · analytics**

That reads like a music SaaS dashboard.

The better framing is:

**Jovie audits an artist, finds the highest-leverage missing piece, and executes the fix.**

That is a tech company. The product is the agentic execution layer for artists.

## The core pitch

The deck should revolve around this sentence:

**Artists are now businesses, and almost none of them have operators. Jovie gives every artist an AI manager that can identify what is missing, create the asset, launch it, message fans, collect money, and learn from the result.**

That is much bigger than “artist tools.”

The manager metaphor works because a real manager does four things:

1. **Understands the artist**
2. **Finds opportunities**
3. **Coordinates execution**
4. **Turns outcomes into the next move**

Jovie should be pitched as software that performs that loop.

## The right category

I would avoid leading with “music management software.”

Use one of these:

**Jovie is the AI manager for music artists.**

or sharper:

**Jovie turns every artist into an AI-managed business.**

or most venture-scale:

**Jovie is building the autonomous management layer for the music industry.**

The phrase “AI manager” is still the simplest. The deeper explanation is:

**Jovie performs the work an artist’s first manager, marketer, merch operator, fan CRM, and release coordinator would normally do.**

**What job did Jovie do for the artist?**

**Jovie has profiles, links, fans, tips, analytics, and merch**

Instead of:

**Jovie has fan messaging**

**Jovie has analytics**

**first audit**

**No merch**  
**No fan capture**  
**Weak bio**  
**No current offer**  
**No release plan**  
**No direct fan channel**  
**No collaborator pipeline**

For the RC deck, only one of these needs to fully work.

The strongest one is:

**No merch → create merch → launch store → message fans → collect money.**

---

### 3. Design generation

Each concept should include:

**mockup**  
**title**  
**short rationale**  
**price suggestion**

Example:

**“Tour-black tee — based on your cover art and darker visual identity. Suggested price: $35.”**

Artist can say:

**“Use this one.”**  
**“Make it simpler.”**  
**“Generate three more.”**

This makes the product feel alive.

---

### 4. Storefront creation

After approval, Jovie creates the product page automatically.

Needs:

**product image**  
**description**  
**price**  
**size variants**  
**checkout**  
**fulfillment path**  
**artist profile placement**

The artist should never have to “set up a store.” Jovie should say:

**“I added this to your profile.”**

---

### 5. Fan activation

Jovie drafts the message.

Channels can start simple:

**SMS**  
**email**  
**profile announcement**

The message should be artist-specific.

Example:

**“Your fans who saved your last release are the best audience for this drop. I drafted a message for them.”**

The artist approves.

Jovie sends.

---

### 6. Payment and payout

Choose the cleanest architecture early.

The product can say:

**“You made $X. Connect Stripe to receive payouts.”**

Behind the scenes, this needs a clear decision:

**Jovie as merchant of record**  
or

This matters for chargebacks, taxes, refunds, fulfillment, and artist payout timing. The deck does not need to explain this. The product needs to avoid ambiguity here.

Start with:

**white tee**  
**hoodie**  
**poster**

The magic is the agent workflow, not unlimited SKUs.

Example:

This completes the manager loop.

**smart links**  
**tips**  
**analytics**  
**profiles**  
**fan chat**
