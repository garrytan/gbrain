## User

As someone who has a relatively untrained chest, particularly upper chest, looking to get volume in on that and specialize it in this mesocycle, and I'm trying to pick the exercise. What do we think about the Smith machine incline bench press versus a regular incline bench press? Keeping in mind we're going after hypertrophy and trying to get the best SFR specifically for the upper pecs and nothing else. Don't really care about auxiliary. We're just trying to hit it with as much volume as possible.

## Assistant

Mr. White — for *upper-pec hypertrophy with the best stimulus-to-fatigue ratio (SFR)*, I’d generally pick **Smith machine incline press** over a free-weight incline press for most people, *especially* if your upper chest is relatively untrained and you’re planning to push volume hard this mesocycle.

### Why Smith often wins for SFR (upper pec bias)
- **Stability is “free”**: less effort spent balancing = more of your effort goes into producing force through the pecs. That usually means **closer-to-failure sets with less systemic fatigue** and less session-to-session technique variance.
- **Easier to standardize the groove**: you can lock in the elbow path and bar path that best loads your clavicular pec fibers (upper pec) and just repeat it across lots of sets.
- **Less shoulder/low-back “tax”** at high volumes: the stabilizers and bracing demands are usually lower than free-weight incline. That’s exactly what you want when “auxiliary doesn’t matter.”

### When the regular incline bench can be better
Free-weight incline can be great, but for your specific goal it tends to lose on SFR unless:
- you’re already very skilled at it and it’s extremely stable for you,
- you can keep your execution identical under fatigue,
- and it doesn’t light up front delts/shoulders more than pecs for you.

If any of those aren’t true, **fatigue rises faster than stimulus** when you start stacking volume.

### How to make the Smith incline hit upper pecs hardest
If you choose Smith, these cues usually help bias upper pecs and keep delts from stealing it:
- **Incline angle:** *low-to-moderate* (about **15–30°**). Higher inclines trend more anterior delt for many lifters.
- **Bar path / touch point:** lower on the chest than you think (upper chest/low clavicle area), with forearms roughly vertical at the bottom.
- **Elbow angle:** moderate flare (not pinned, not full flare) — think elbows ~45–70° from torso depending on comfort.
- **ROM:** full stretch without shoulder discomfort; controlled eccentric; pause lightly if it helps keep tension on pecs.
- **Straps optional:** if grip/forearms fatigue first, straps can further improve SFR.

### Practical recommendation for your mesocycle
- **Primary movement:** Smith incline press (your “volume workhorse”).
- **Execution target:** sets of ~6–12 (or 8–15) taken to **0–2 RIR** most of the time.
- **Why:** it’ll let you accumulate a *lot* of hard sets on the upper pec with less “wasted” fatigue than the free-weight version.

### Quick self-check to decide in 2 sessions
Run both for 2 sessions each with the same set/rep/RIR scheme and ask:
1) Which one gives a better **upper-pec pump and soreness** (not mandatory, but useful when untrained)?
2) Which one keeps performance **more stable across sets**?
3) Which one produces less **shoulder/front-delt takeover** and joint irritation?

Whichever wins those is your best SFR choice — and in practice, that’s very often the **Smith**.
