Mr. White, in California, the left-turning driver usually carries the bigger legal risk in that hypothetical.

Vehicle Code section 21801 says a driver turning left must yield to oncoming traffic that is close enough to be a hazard and must keep yielding until the turn can be made with reasonable safety. The California civil jury instruction on that rule says the left-turn driver must make sure no oncoming vehicle is close enough to be a hazard before crossing. citeturn468767view0turn382081view0

So if you turn left across an oncoming car that then goes straight, you are usually at least partly at fault, even if that driver had a left blinker on. A blinker shows intent. It does not transfer the right-of-way. California DMV guidance also says an unprotected left turn must be made only after yielding to oncoming traffic and only when it is safe. citeturn725100view3turn725100view2

That said, the other driver can also be at fault. California requires an appropriate signal when a movement may affect another vehicle, and a turn signal must be given continuously during the last 100 feet before the turn. If the other driver signaled left, induced you to turn, then drove straight and caused the crash, that signal evidence can support negligence against that driver. A traffic-statute violation that causes the crash can create a presumption of negligence under Evidence Code section 669. citeturn239103view0turn468767view1turn956940view0

Bottom line: you are probably not automatically 100% clear just because the other guy lied with his blinker. In California, fault would likely be apportioned. The left-turning driver often starts out with primary fault, but the straight-going driver who falsely signaled can take a significant share if witnesses, dashcam, intersection video, or other evidence shows you reasonably relied on that signal. In California, if you are making the left, you still have to yield to oncoming traffic until the turn can be made with reasonable safety. The statute does not give you a safe harbor because the other driver had a blinker on, paused in the intersection, or the cars behind you were honking.
