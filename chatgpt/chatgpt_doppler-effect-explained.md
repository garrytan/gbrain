**Doppler** refers to concepts based on the **Doppler effect** — the change in the frequency of waves when the source and/or the observer are moving relative to each other. citeturn0search4turn0search5

**1) Doppler Effect (Physics)**  
The **Doppler effect** describes how the frequency (or pitch) of waves — such as sound or light — changes depending on motion. If a source moves **toward** you, waves are compressed and the frequency you detect is **higher**; if it moves **away**, waves stretch and the frequency is **lower**. citeturn0search4  
Example: an ambulance siren sounds higher-pitched as it approaches and lower as it recedes. citeturn0search4

**2) Doppler in Medicine (Ultrasound)**  
In medical imaging, **Doppler ultrasound** uses the Doppler effect to measure **blood flow**. Sound waves bounce off moving blood cells; changes in the reflected frequency tell you the **speed and direction** of blood flow. Doppler ultrasounds help detect blockages, narrowed vessels, clots, and assess circulation. citeturn0search3

**3) Other Uses**  
- **Radar:** Doppler radar measures velocity (e.g., weather systems). citeturn0search27  
- **Astronomy:** Shift in light frequency reveals motion of stars/galaxies. citeturn0search8  
- **Navigation & Sensors:** Doppler effects help calculate speed or movement in various technologies. citeturn0search27

In short, *Doppler* typically means something involving **frequency changes due to relative motion** — especially waves like sound or light — and includes technologies like Doppler ultrasound that exploit this phenomenon. citeturn0search5
