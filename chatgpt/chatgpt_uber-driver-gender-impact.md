### Key points:

- **Yes, there are fewer women drivers.** In the U.S., only about 19–27% of Uber drivers are women (varies by source).
- **Yes, men likely take more solo rides.** Most Uber users skew male, especially late night and weekend solo rides.
- **Yes, predatory riders could exploit this.** If male passengers can select a woman driver (e.g. by toggling preferences, canceling until matched), it increases surface area for harassment.

### Net effect:

- **If the system lets *riders* opt for women drivers**, harassment risk **goes up**. You're giving harassers a targeting tool.
- **If the system only lets *women riders* opt into *women drivers*** (opt-in, rider-only), **harassment goes down**, *if* supply meets demand.
- **But** since there are far fewer women drivers, in practice most female riders will still get male drivers, **limiting safety gains** unless Uber recruits more women drivers.

### Summary:

If this system allows men to select women drivers in any way, it increases harassment risk. If it's women-rider-only and opt-in, it's probably a marginal net benefit—but limited by low supply. The risk is in the implementation details.
