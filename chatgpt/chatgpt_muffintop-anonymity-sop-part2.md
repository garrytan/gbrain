
# SOP: Maintaining Anonymity in the MUFFINTOP Music Project

**Overview:**  
This Standard Operating Procedure outlines how to protect the real identity behind **MUFFINTOP** at every stage of the project’s growth. It covers legal and practical steps to ensure anonymity is preserved in contracts, music releases, live shows, online presence, merchandising, and media interactions. Each section below provides clear guidelines and actionable measures, drawing on successful tactics used by anonymous artists (e.g. Daft Punk, Marshmello) and industry best practices. Adhering to these procedures will help guarantee that MUFFINTOP’s true identity is never revealed publicly.

## 1. Identity Protection

- **Use a Pseudonym for All Credits and Communications:** Always operate under the stage name **“MUFFINTOP”** in public-facing contexts. In songwriting credits, album liner notes, social media, and press releases, use only the pseudonym (or a designated alias) – never your legal name. This applies to project credits on streaming services, music videos, and any published materials. For example, Taylor Swift wrote a song under the pseudonym “Nils Sjöberg” to conceal her involvement【14†L85-L93】. Emulate this by ensuring *all* creative contributions by you are credited to *MUFFINTOP* or another alias, not your real name. Maintain separate email addresses and phone contacts under the MUFFINTOP name for all industry communications.

- **Establish a Legal Entity to Shield Your Name:** Form an LLC (or similar corporate entity) to serve as the legal identity behind MUFFINTOP. An LLC can effectively “be” the artist for all contracts and financial dealings, keeping your personal name off agreements【34†L587-L591】. Ideally, form the LLC in a jurisdiction that allows anonymity for owners (e.g. Delaware, Nevada, New Mexico, Wyoming)【4†L83-L91】. Use a registered agent or attorney as the official contact so that public business records list the agent instead of you【4†L79-L87】. This way, anyone searching corporate filings won’t find your name. **Tip:** When filing the LLC, explicitly request anonymity and use a *nominee manager* if available (a third party who is listed in formation documents in place of the real owner). All music industry contracts (with labels, distributors, vendors, etc.) should then be signed by the LLC or an authorized agent of the LLC, not by you personally. This creates a legal buffer protecting your identity【34†L587-L591】.

- **Use Pseudonyms in Personal Writing Credits:** If you contribute to songwriting or production, consider registering a writer pseudonym with your performing rights organization (PRO) or publisher. Many artists and writers do this – e.g. Harry Styles used the alias “Mick Greenberg” for songwriting credits【14†L113-L121】. Work with your publisher or admin (Kobalt KOSIGN, etc.) to ensure that any public databases list your alias or LLC as the rights holder, not your birth name. This prevents curious fans or journalists from finding your name via copyright records or PRO searches. **Example:** Marshmello’s real name appeared in a royalty registry for one of his songs, effectively outing his identity【22†L57-L61】. Avoid this by having all registrations done under the MUFFINTOP entity or pen name.

- **Enforce NDAs with Everyone Who Knows:** Every individual who is brought “inside” (e.g. collaborators, co-writers, session musicians, managers, lawyers, stylists, video directors, live crew, etc.) must sign a strict Non-Disclosure Agreement before any work begins. The NDA should specifically cover nondisclosure of your real name, personal details, and any unmasked appearances. It should define heavy consequences (legal and financial) for breaching confidentiality【11†L348-L352】. In practice, NDAs are extremely common in the industry to protect sensitive info【11†L348-L352】 – make this a standard operating rule for MUFFINTOP. Even friends or family involved should sign NDAs or at least be firmly briefed on secrecy. No one should discuss or hint at MUFFINTOP’s identity to outsiders or on social media. When collaborating remotely, use code names in files and conversations (for instance, refer to yourself as “MT” or another alias in shared documents).

- **Limit the Inner Circle and Compartmentalize Knowledge:** Keep the number of people who know your true identity to the absolute minimum necessary. For those who do know, emphasize the importance of secrecy not just legally but also in casual conversation. Even well-meaning associates can slip up. (One anonymous artist’s mother excitedly shared his new music on Facebook and tagged his real name, accidentally exposing his alter ego【7†L209-L217】 – a cautionary tale that even family must be on board with the confidentiality policy.) As MUFFINTOP grows, maintain a need-to-know principle: for example, touring crew might know you only as the character and not by real name. Use separate “circles” – a core group (manager, lawyer) who know the identity, and an outer circle (session players, etc.) who might work with MUFFINTOP but know only the persona. The fewer people with knowledge, the fewer points of potential leak.

- **Trademark and Intellectual Property Protection (Anonymously):** Secure trademarks for the stage name “MUFFINTOP” and any logos or distinctive symbols through your LLC or a specialized trust. Registering your name as a trademark prevents others from exploiting it【4†L139-L147】. To preserve anonymity, file the trademark under the LLC’s name or have your attorney listed as the correspondent. This way, the public USPTO records won’t display your personal info. Protecting the brand early ensures you won’t face someone else stealing the name and potentially forcing you to reveal yourself to fight it【4†L139-L147】. All copyrights for music, artwork, etc. should likewise be registered in the name of the LLC or via an anonymous registration option (U.S. Copyright Office allows pseudonymous registrations where the public record can list “Author: pseudonymous”). Keeping IP registrations anonymized will guard against researchers tying filings back to you【1†L9-L17】.

## 2. Music Distribution & Publishing

- **Distribute Music Under the Stage Name Only:** When releasing music, use distribution platforms in a way that shields your identity. Platforms like **AWAL** (for distribution) and **Kobalt’s KOSIGN** (for publishing administration) can accommodate stage-name releases – ensure all public-facing metadata (artist name, songwriter credits, etc.) is the pseudonym. **Setup:** Create your AWAL account using the MUFFINTOP LLC as the account owner. Provide any required legal documents (tax ID, bank info) from the LLC. This way, royalty payments and contracts are tied to the company name. The distributor should list *MUFFINTOP* as the artist on streaming services and nowhere include your real name. Many independent artists release music without tying it to their legal identities【7†L189-L197】, and distributors are used to this. Explicitly confirm with AWAL reps that only your stage name will appear on credits and stores (they may have you fill artist metadata accordingly). Maintain a separate **EIN** and bank account (from your LLC) for the distributor’s payment info, so that even internal payment records use the company’s name【4†L103-L111】.

- **Publishing and Song Registration via Alias:** For songwriting and publishing, use your pseudonym or an entity name in all registrations. If you’re using Kobalt’s KOSIGN platform (or a similar publishing admin service), apply and register songs under the MUFFINTOP name. If KOSIGN requires identifying the actual songwriter to collect from PROs, coordinate with their team on how to keep that info confidential. One approach is to register an alternate writer name with your PRO. For example, Taylor Swift’s team was able to register “Nils Sjöberg” as a songwriter on a hit song【14†L85-L93】. You can similarly create a writer profile under a pseudonym, so that databases like ASCAP or BMI don’t display your legal name publicly. **Important:** When splitting royalties or credits with co-writers/producers, ensure your portion is attributed to the pseudonym or LLC. Use your LLC as the publishing designee for your share (e.g., *Muffintop Music LLC* as the publisher). This prevents collaborators from accidentally registering your real name in their split sheets or on PRO registrations. Always double-check song metadata (credits in Spotify, Apple Music, etc.) to verify that only the MUFFINTOP alias and/or company appear. A single metadata slip (like a real name in songwriter credits) can tip off journalists, as happened with Marshmello【22†L57-L61】, so diligence here is critical.

- **Control Metadata and Digital File Info:** Before uploading any music or sending out files, scrub all metadata for identifying info. Ensure that audio files (WAV, MP3 metadata) don’t contain your name in the “Created by” or file properties. Use the stage name or leave fields blank. Similarly, when registering ISRC codes, list the owner as the LLC or stage name. If using a distributor like AWAL or DistroKid, the “songwriter” or “composer” fields might request real names for rights purposes; in such cases, provide either the pseudonym or the name of a trusted business partner (or entity) who can act on your behalf. It’s legal to release music pseudonymously【1†L9-L17】, so insist that any publishing credits reflect that. Keep a close eye on how your information is displayed on public platforms: for instance, if an artist profile or Spotify bio is auto-generated, make sure it doesn’t pull any personal data (some platforms have biography sections – supply a crafted bio for MUFFINTOP that contains no personal backstory beyond the persona).

- **Use Administrative Layers:** To further distance your name, consider using a music publishing administrator (like Songtrust or Kobalt) and a distributor who are reputable and understand your anonymity requirements. Communicate clearly with them: *no personal name in any press or marketing.* If signing up for AWAL (which sometimes requires an application), you might have to provide real info for verification – do so confidentially, and then ask that all outward-facing documentation uses the stage name. It can help to have a lawyer or manager be the point of contact with these services, reinforcing that communications go through the LLC or alias. Additionally, register a **business manager** or **accountant** to handle royalty accounting under the company name. That way, if any royalty statements or credits are published (e.g., Billboard or PR announcements of songwriting credits), they will list the company or pseudonym. 

- **Monitor Royalty and Rights Databases:** Keep track of databases like PRO repertoires, MLC (Mechanical Licensing Collective) entries, or credits on sites like AllMusic. These often become public. If you see your real name appear, work quickly with your publisher to amend it or at least hide it. For example, if a song is registered with BMI under your real name, see if BMI allows adding an AKA. Some PROs list an IPI number that could be cross-referenced to you; mitigate this by not sharing your personal IPI publicly and perhaps using a different pseudonymous writer account if feasible. The goal is that a fan googling MUFFINTOP or combing through song credits finds no obvious link to your legal identity.

## 3. Live Performances & Public Appearances

- **Helmet On, Always:** The defining element of MUFFINTOP’s branding is the helmet (assuming this is akin to Daft Punk’s helmets or Marshmello’s bucket). **The rule is: the helmet stays on during *all* public-facing moments.** From the minute you arrive at any venue or public space where you might be recognized, ensure your face is covered. Daft Punk famously have **never** appeared in public without their robot disguises since 2001【37†L282-L290】. Emulate that discipline. This means wearing the helmet (or an alternate face covering) during soundchecks, rehearsals on stage, and even brief interactions where venue staff or early attendees might be present. If the helmet is uncomfortable for long periods, have a secondary lightweight mask or balaclava that obscures your face for times you can’t wear the full helmet. Only remove the helmet in completely secure, private areas (e.g., your locked dressing room with no cameras). If you need handlers to help with the helmet (for example, to guide you when visibility is low), choose one or two trusted crew members who are NDA-bound and trained to do this discreetly out of sight.

- **Secure Travel Logistics:** Plan all travel with anonymity in mind. When flying to gigs or traveling to appearances, use aliases for reservations whenever possible. For flights, you must use your legal name with the airline for ID purposes, but you can often ask the airline or promoter to keep your name off public passenger lists (for private charters, manifest details can be kept confidential). If budget allows, use VIP airport services (private security screening, escorts) to avoid being seen in security lines. If that’s not feasible, consider wearing a plain disguise during transit – e.g., a hat, face mask, and sunglasses – to make your appearance generic. Since your face isn’t known to the public yet, you have an advantage; maintain that by not drawing attention. Keep the signature helmet in a carry-on case (never risk it in checked luggage). At the destination, have a trusted person meet you to transfer you to a private area where you can put on the MUFFINTOP helmet before any public-facing event. **Hotels:** Never book hotel rooms under your real name when traveling for the project. Have your tour manager or an alias handle the booking. High-profile artists often check in under fake names – adopt this practice early. Instruct hotel staff (discreetly) that you require privacy; for instance, ask that no personal name be listed on the hotel phone or room registry, and disable any public listing of your room (so fans or press can’t call your room if they suspect you’re there). Whenever possible, go through service entrances or use staff elevators at venues and hotels to avoid public lobbies when unmasked.

- **Backstage and Venue Protocols:** Coordinate with venues and promoters ahead of time about your anonymity requirements. Include in your performance rider a clause about privacy – e.g., *“Artist requires that all individuals with access to backstage or dressing room areas be pre-approved by artist management. No photography or videography of the artist without helmet is allowed. Venue staff must not attempt to identify or address the artist by anything other than the provided stage name.”* Post signage on dressing room doors such as “RESTRICTED: Access by Tour Manager Only” to keep random staff or opening acts from wandering in. During soundcheck, if possible, limit attendance to only essential crew. Alternatively, do a closed soundcheck (no venue staff or guests in the room) to keep your identity concealed. At front-of-house (FOH), ensure that any video cameras that might show the stage (for IMAG screens or recordings) are only activated during the actual show when you have the helmet on. If you need to test those cameras in advance, do so *with the helmet on*. Instruct the FOH engineer and lighting crew that the helmet is an integral part of the performance persona – lights should be designed such that you’re not suddenly exposed (for instance, avoid any effect that could inadvertently make a helmet transparent or show your face silhouette).

- **Stage Entrance/Exit:** Develop a routine for getting on and off stage without revealing yourself. Many masked artists have a team assist them right until they go on stage. For example, you might have a hood or alternate mask on while walking to side-stage, then don the MUFFINTOP helmet seconds before going on. Kill the house lights during entrance and exit if possible, so no one glimpses anything in those transitions. After performing, return immediately to the private backstage area before removing any part of the costume. Consider using a small pop-up tent or partition backstage as a “helmet off zone” if a private room isn’t adjacent to the stage. Only your day-to-day manager or one security person should be present when you remove the helmet and cool down. Have spare masks/helmets in case one needs to be removed publicly (for instance, if an emergency occurs on stage, you can swap to a backup helmet or head covering rather than fully unmask).

- **Transportation and Crew Discretion:** Train your touring crew and drivers on anonymity procedures. When being transported (by car, tour bus), the rule should be that you put on the helmet or a full face covering before stepping out of the vehicle at venues or public stops. Drivers should drop you off in secure areas out of view when possible. Crew members should never refer to you by your real name in public or over radios – use a code name if needed when communicating (“The package is on the move” instead of “[RealName] is coming out”). Small details help: for instance, if catering is set up backstage, have someone else prepare a plate for you so you don’t have to roam common areas unmasked. If meet-and-greets or fan interactions are part of the plan, do them **in character** only – full helmet on, and preferably with a manager present to handle any tricky questions. Remember that fans might try to catch you off-guard (e.g., calling out a random name to see if you react), so stay in character at all times in these settings. Daft Punk exemplified this by **abiding in total silence or speaking via vocoders in public appearances** – at the 2014 Grammys they let Pharrell and others speak while they nodded silently in helmets【37†L268-L276】. You can employ similar tactics: use body language or prepared voice modulator clips instead of speaking normally, to avoid giving away your natural voice or accent.

- **Avoiding Accidental Leaks:** Plan for common leak scenarios: for example, someone backstage tries to take a quick photo of you without the helmet. To counter this, institute a **“no phones” policy** in certain areas. You can require that all crew and visitors put phones in lock pouches (like Yondr pouches) when in the sensitive zones. If that’s not feasible, at least put up signs that photography of the artist is prohibited. Hire a security person who isn’t afraid to politely stop anyone pulling out a camera near you. Also be mindful of *your own* behavior in public: do not mention your hometown, age, or other personal details in casual chats that could give clues. If journalists or curious industry folks are around, have a cover story for basic questions (e.g., if asked “Where are you from?” the persona might have a vague fictional origin story). The idea is to give no kernel of personal info that could later be pieced together.

- **International Considerations:** If MUFFINTOP will perform internationally, work closely with immigration lawyers and promoters to maintain secrecy. Visas and work permits will necessarily have your real name and passport info, but you can ask that promoters and festival organizers keep that information tightly held. Often, the artist name is all that’s advertised; ensure that any visa-related correspondence doesn’t accidentally get sent to someone who might leak it. For example, if a festival is announcing artists, make sure they refer only to MUFFINTOP. When traveling through customs and immigration, you’ll have to present your passport (with real name and occupation). One tactic is to request a more private processing if you ever travel with the helmet (some artists have been known to travel with their stage costumes; Marshmello once arrived at an airport wearing the helmet to play into the persona【22†L43-L51】, though he likely had to remove it for security). Use your judgment – you might not want to actually wear it through immigration, but you can carry it and if asked, explain it’s part of a performance character without giving your name. Often officers won’t connect dots unless you’re extremely famous, but as MUFFINTOP grows, consider using a business visa that lists a company name rather than a well-known personal name.

## 4. Digital Presence

- **Anonymous Social Media Accounts:** Create fresh social media profiles for MUFFINTOP on all relevant platforms (Instagram, Twitter/X, TikTok, Facebook, etc.) using the stage name exclusively. Do not set these accounts up with any personal email or phone number – use the official MUFFINTOP email (e.g., **info@muffintop.com**) and a secondary phone number that cannot be traced to you (you can use a VoIP number or an app like Google Voice for verification texts). The profiles’ bio information should be in-character and contain no personal details or hints (for instance, no mention of hometown, age, or prior projects). **Operational Security:** Access these accounts only through devices or browser profiles dedicated to MUFFINTOP. This prevents any accidental cross-posting from personal accounts and avoids social platforms correlating your identities via cookies or saved logins. Use a VPN when logging in, especially if managing accounts from a location associated with you – this adds a layer of IP address anonymity, making it harder for platform insiders (or advanced fans) to guess your location or identity. All interactions (posts, comments, DMs) should be done *in the voice of MUFFINTOP*, not your personal voice. If your natural writing style is distinctive, be mindful to adopt a slightly different tone or use a social media manager to craft posts.

- **Website and Domain Privacy:** If you set up an official website (e.g., **muffintopmusic.com**), register the domain through your LLC and use domain privacy services. Domain registration records (WHOIS) can inadvertently expose the owner’s name and address. To prevent this, use a registrar that offers WHOIS privacy or list the LLC and a P.O. Box or your lawyer’s address as the contact【4†L113-L121】. For example, Twisby Records advises using your LLC’s info when buying a domain to keep your identity off public records【4†L113-L121】. Host the website on an account under the LLC name as well. Use secure, privacy-focused web analytics (or none at all) to avoid leaks of admin info. If you use Google Analytics or similar, register it under the MUFFINTOP Google account (not your personal Google account) so that no personal name is visible in analytics dashboards or shared reports.

- **Secure Communications:** All official emails should come from an address like *contact@muffintop.com* or a similar alias. Set up this email through a provider that doesn’t expose account owner details. Google Workspace or ProtonMail using the custom domain are good options (Google Workspace allows multiple aliases under one account, which can be convenient【4†L129-L137】). Use strong, unique passwords and enable 2-factor authentication on all MUFFINTOP accounts (email, social media, website CMS, etc.). For 2FA, use app-based authenticators or hardware keys registered to the alias email – avoid SMS 2FA to your personal phone. This not only secures the accounts from hackers (who could dox you) but also prevents any login recovery from revealing your identity (e.g., you don’t want a password reset email going to your personal address).

- **Ghost Management of Social Media:** Consider delegating day-to-day social media posting to a trusted third party (social media manager or PR agency) who will act as *ghost posters*. They can log in as MUFFINTOP to publish content and reply to fans according to your guidelines. This creates an extra buffer: you are not directly interacting, reducing the chance of slipping up (like using your normal colloquialisms or posting a personal photo by mistake). If you do this, ensure the manager is under NDA and ideally doesn’t even know your real identity – they only need to know the persona’s content style. Provide them with prepared materials (photos of the helmeted character, graphics, etc.) and messaging. If fans or industry people DM the accounts, have either an automated response or the social manager respond in-character. **Never use personal accounts or IPs to manage official pages.** For example, don’t make the MUFFINTOP Facebook page tied to your personal Facebook profile – instead, create a separate business manager account under the LLC.

- **Content Strategy to Preserve Anonymity:** Plan your content in a way that feeds the mystique instead of undermining it. Post photos where you are always in the helmet or otherwise unidentifiable (gloves can cover unique tattoos or scars, for instance). Avoid live streams or spontaneous videos that could catch you off-guard. If you do livestream, consider using filters, an animated avatar, or only streaming performances where you’re fully in gear. Some artists use voice changers in live Q&A sessions to maintain anonymity – for instance, you could do a “robot voice” if it fits your branding, or have a moderator read your typed answers aloud. Keep personal life completely out of the digital presence: no behind-the-scenes of you out of character. If you want to share studio footage or similar, ensure you’re still masked or only hands/instruments are visible. Also, manage time zones and locations in posts carefully. Don’t post from your home at a time that indicates where you are (fans can deduce location from things like time of posts, background landmarks, or even the language of an auto-generated caption). Using scheduling tools for social media can help – you can queue posts to go live at specific times without you needing to physically be online (and those tools often mask the posting source).

- **Untraceable Infrastructure:** Use privacy-friendly services for any analytics or marketing. For instance, if you have a mailing list for fans, use an email service provider under the LLC and do not include any real address in the email footer (CAN-SPAM laws require a mailing address; use a P.O. Box or your lawyer’s office address, not your home). If you run ads on Facebook/Instagram or Google, create business ad accounts through the MUFFINTOP pages – do not run them via a personal ad account which could display your name to collaborators. Similarly, if you sell music or merch on Bandcamp or other platforms, set up the profile under the band name and use the company info for payout settings. All payments from fans (for merch, digital downloads, etc.) should route to the company’s PayPal/Stripe/bank, again keeping your name out of receipts or transaction records that could leak. 

- **Monitoring and Hygiene:** Regularly audit MUFFINTOP’s digital footprint. Google the stage name and see what information comes up – if any article or site erroneously links to your real identity, have your team reach out to get it removed or edited (especially if it’s something under your control, like a profile page). Keep an eye on Wikipedia or fan forums once you gain notoriety; these are places where curious people might speculate about identity. You might choose to ignore speculation (which is fine), but ensure no one has concrete info. If you find a piece of personal info in the wild, that’s a sign to tighten that area of operations. For instance, if someone finds a connection via a copyright registration, then double down on pseudonymous registrations going forward. Use alerts (Google Alerts on your real name and on MUFFINTOP) to catch any mentions quickly. Finally, maintain discipline: no matter how tempting, **do not use MUFFINTOP accounts to vent personal feelings or comment on unrelated matters in a way that would make people suspect there’s a “real person” behind the mask**. Always stay “on brand” as an enigmatic persona.

## 5. Merchandising

- **Anonymous Online Storefront:** Set up merchandise sales in a way that fans buying products never see your personal details. The easiest method is to use print-on-demand and fulfillment services under the MUFFINTOP brand. Platforms like Printful, Teespring, or **Teepublic** allow you to create and sell merch (t-shirts, hoodies, etc.) using their fulfillment; you can operate these under a business name so that your real name and address aren’t attached to shipments【42†L9-L17】【42†L25-L33】. For example, when creating an account, use *Muffintop Merch LLC* with the LLC’s EIN for tax forms. Ensure the “ship from” address on packages is a generic warehouse or the name of the print provider, not your home or personal address. If you prefer to handle inventory yourself for higher quality or signed items, use a third-party fulfillment center. You can manufacture items and have them stored and shipped by a fulfillment service (or a trusted friend’s business), so you’re not personally going to the post office (which could risk someone catching your name). Many artists at scale partner with merch companies that take care of distribution; from the start, choose such partners and have them sign NDAs regarding any of your information they learn.

- **Payment Processing and Store Admin:** Use an e-commerce platform like Shopify (or Bandcamp for music and merch) with anonymous registration. The public-facing store should list *Muffintop Official Merchandise* as the seller. In Shopify settings, you can usually provide a business address for contact – use a P.O. Box or your attorney’s office rather than anything that could lead back to you. For payment gateways (Stripe, PayPal), set them up under the LLC’s bank account and tax ID. This way, customer receipts and credit card statements will show a charge from “Muffintop Merch” or similar, not a person’s name. Double-check the checkout process by doing test orders: see if any auto-generated emails or invoices inadvertently contain personal info. If so, adjust the settings or templates. For instance, ensure the customer service email is a MUFFINTOP address and the signature is just the project name (no individual’s name).

- **Fulfillment Privacy:** If you ship any items directly (like special bundles or limited editions you handle), set up a mailing solution that hides your origin address. Use a return address that is an alias – e.g., “MTP Productions, 123 Example Street, Los Angeles” (some address that isn’t directly tied to you; it could be a packing center or a rented mailbox). Better yet, use a fulfillment partner who can ship on your behalf from their address. If you must ship yourself, invest in a PO Box or mailbox at UPS Store to use as return address. Never put your real name on return labels; use the company name. Also, consider customs forms for international shipping – these sometimes require a name and signature. Use a generic first name (or the company name) for sender, or have a team member be the sender of record.

- **Drop-Shipping and Scalability:** As demand grows, plan to transition all merch operations to scalable, anonymous systems. Drop-shipping (where a supplier sends goods directly to the buyer) is useful for items like printed shirts, accessories, etc. For example, you upload your designs, and when orders come in, the print-on-demand service prints and sends them. They will typically label the package with your brand name and their fulfillment address. Verify with any service you use that they *don’t include invoices* with personal details in the package. You might even test-order your own merch to see what the packaging looks like. If something has to be included by law (like a return form), see if it can list the LLC. Additionally, if you integrate a storefront on your website, use plugins that allow blind shipping. Some artists create a completely separate merch company entity to handle all products – if you choose to do this, keep that company’s info separate too (but it can still point back to the same central LLC if someone really dug, so a single LLC might suffice if properly shielded).

- **Merchandise Design and Branding:** When designing merch, be mindful not to inadvertently include identifying easter eggs. Keep designs focused on the MUFFINTOP logo, helmet imagery, or stylized text. Avoid using any symbol or number that has personal significance (e.g., don’t hide your birthdate or initials in a design, as hardcore fans might over-analyze it). Keep the mystery alive in the merch as well – you can even play into it (e.g., a shirt that says “I am MUFFINTOP” inviting fans to share the identity). If you do photoshoots for merch, do them in costume only. For instance, if you need product photos of you wearing the merch, ensure you’re wearing the helmet/gear in those photos. All visual marketing materials for the merch should continue the anonymous aesthetic.

- **Customer Service Practices:** Set up a customer support email (something like *merch@muffintop.com*) or use the e-commerce platform’s messaging system for any inquiries. If you’re personally responding to customer emails, do so under a generic alias (sign off as “Muffintop Support Team” or have your manager handle it). Do not provide personal help via your own email or phone. If someone asks a question like “Who is the real Muffintop?” in a cheeky email, ignore it or have a canned on-brand response (“The Muffintop is in all of us :)”). Never confirm or deny any identity rumors in any customer interaction. 

- **Financial Hygiene:** Keep the finances of merch sales separate from your personal finances. All revenue should go to the business account. Pay any collaborators or vendors from that account. Not only does this help for taxes, it prevents scenarios where, say, a refund check or PayPal payment accidentally shows your personal name. It also means any public financial filings (if any) relate to the company, not you. As the merch operation grows, you might hire help (for design, customer service, etc.) – bring them under NDA and instruct them on anonymity: e.g., if they reply to a customer, they should never say, “I’ll ask the artist” using your actual name.

## 6. Contracts and Legal Matters

- **Sign Everything Through an Entity or Representative:** In all contracts – whether with record labels, distributors, publishers, venues, or service providers – avoid signing in your personal capacity. Instead, have **MUFFINTOP’s LLC** (or a legal trust) be the signatory. You can be listed as “John Doe, Manager of Muffintop LLC” in the signature block if absolutely needed, but if you can, even delegate the signing to an attorney or business manager. For instance, your lawyer can sign an agreement on behalf of the LLC as “Authorized Signatory.” The contract’s party should be the LLC (e.g., “Muffintop LLC d/b/a [doing business as] MUFFINTOP”). This way, if the contract ever becomes public or is registered somewhere, it doesn’t blatantly show your name. Using a corporate entity is a common tactic to maintain privacy and limit liability【34†L587-L591】. Ensure your personal name is only present on internal documents (like the LLC’s operating agreement and maybe confidential tax forms), not on the face of deals.

- **Embed Confidentiality in Contracts:** Add clauses in all major contracts to reinforce anonymity. For example, if you have a management contract or booking agent contract, include a provision that the manager/agent will not divulge the client’s identity without written permission. For label or distribution deals, you can request a clause that the company shall not use your personal name in any publicity or disclosures. While industry folks might learn your name for legal purposes, having a contractual confidentiality clause gives you recourse if they ever leak it. It also signals how serious you are about privacy. Non-disclosure obligations can sometimes be built into the main contract or exist as separate NDAs attached. Work with your entertainment lawyer to draft language that specifically addresses identity protection (not just unreleased music or trade secrets, but the artist’s real identity as confidential information).

- **Taxation and Payments:** Obtain an **EIN (Employer Identification Number)** for your LLC【4†L98-L106】 and use that for all tax documents and payments. Anyone paying you (for a show, a royalty, etc.) should issue checks or wires to the LLC, not to your name. Provide a Form W-9 of the LLC when required (in the US) – the W-9 will show the LLC’s name and EIN, thus keeping your SSN and personal name off the paperwork. Similarly, when registering with SoundExchange, PROs, or royalty agencies, use the company’s details where possible. Keep personal and company finances separate. This has the added benefit of simplifying anonymity: if you never commingle funds, you’ll never have to, say, deposit a MUFFINTOP check in your personal bank (where a bank teller might recognize a stage name and ask questions). Instead, you’ll deposit to the business account. From the business account, you can pay yourself a salary or distribution, which is a private internal transaction. If you pay collaborators or contractors, pay them from the company as well. All of this means any Form 1099s or tax records that are public (some states publish certain business info) won’t list your personal name, just the LLC.

- **Legal Address and Contact Details:** Use your lawyer’s office or a registered agent address on contracts. Contracts often include notice addresses for each party – do not put your home address. Create a “business office” address for MUFFINTOP (this could be a P.O. Box or a mail forwarding service if you don’t want to use your lawyer’s address). That address goes on contracts, invoices, and letterheads. The phone number, likewise, can be a forwarding number or your manager’s number. This way, if a contract is shared or ends up in someone’s files, it doesn’t contain personal contact info that could leak. 

- **Visas and International Legalities:** Plan ahead for international travel by structuring your applications carefully. If you need an O-1 or P-1 visa (for a U.S. performance) or visas elsewhere, you’ll have to submit personal documents to governments, but your team can request that petition approvals or announcements use only the stage name. For example, a U.S. O-1 visa might be titled “For the beneficiary: MUFFINTOP (real name redacted)” in press – though typically those aren’t public, sometimes the petitioning employer (like a label) might announce it. Make sure they don’t. For travel, carry a letter from your company explaining the character if needed – some artists have had customs question why they have a weird helmet. A letter on company letterhead stating “The bearer is an artist who performs under a mask for privacy; please inspect in private if necessary” can help if you ever need to remove the helmet for a security check away from the public.

- **Handling Pressing Legal Situations:** If, in a worst-case scenario, you face a lawsuit or legal action, work through counsel to file documents under seal or via pseudonym if possible. Sometimes artists get sued (or sue someone) and if that happens, court documents could name you. Your attorney can file motions to use a pseudonym or simply always refer to you as “MUFFINTOP (legal name on file with court)”. This is more of an extreme measure, but it’s good to have discussed with your lawyer so they know to protect your identity in any legal filings. Also, ensure that any **trademark or copyright disputes** (should they arise) are handled by attorneys so you don’t have to personally appear or give statements that might expose you.

- **Scaling Up the Team with Confidentiality:** As MUFFINTOP grows, you will likely hire more people – a touring crew, social media managers, possibly a publicist, etc. Make it standard practice that **every contract or job offer includes an NDA** about your identity. This even includes short-term hires like a music video crew or session musicians. Your production or management company can have a boilerplate NDA for anyone coming on set or into the studio. It might feel formal, but it’s a normal part of business (and people are used to it with high-profile projects). By setting this expectation, you create a culture of secrecy. For example, those working with the masked band Ghost had no idea who the singer was for years【34†L559-L567】 – that kind of secrecy is achievable with careful compartmentalization. If an employee breaches it, have clear grounds in their contract for termination and legal action. This will deter loose lips. 

- **Work with Professionals Experienced in Anonymity:** Whenever possible, choose lawyers, managers, or consultants who have worked with anonymous artists or are known for discretion. You might ask around (quietly) for industry people who have handled projects like Deadmau5, Marshmello, Sia, or Daft Punk. They’ll be familiar with the pitfalls (for instance, they’d know how to register a song so that the PRO doesn’t accidentally reveal the writer’s name to the public, etc.). If you can’t find someone with that background, at least thoroughly brief your team on these SOPs. Make sure your lawyer, manager, and accountant each know that protecting the identity is a top priority and part of their job. They should feel comfortable reminding others (e.g., telling a promoter “Do NOT put the artist’s real name on the itinerary sheet that goes out to 100 staff”). All it takes is one well-meaning person CC’ing the wrong email or printing the wrong name on a pass to create a leak, so your core team must be vigilant on your behalf.

## 7. PR & Media Strategy

- **Carefully Controlled Interviews:** As the project gains attention, media outlets may request interviews. Plan to do these **without exposing face or voice**. Options include email interviews (you answer questions in writing via your publicist) or interviews where you appear *in full character*. Daft Punk, for example, gave interviews on rare occasions but **always in their robot helmets** and often with pre-recorded vocal effects or through intermediaries【37†L268-L276】. You can similarly do press appearances wearing the MUFFINTOP helmet and outfit – if it’s a video interview, insist on keeping the helmet on throughout. You might distort your voice or speak minimally. Another tactic is to send a **spokesperson** or act through an avatar. For instance, you could do a radio interview where the host reads your written answers on-air, or have your manager speak on your behalf (as long as they don’t slip up and say “I” or your name). If the persona allows, you could create an AI-generated voice and use that to respond in real-time with a voice modulator (practice this to avoid technical issues). The key is to never directly connect your natural voice or face with the MUFFINTOP persona in media. Sia, as a reference point, often performed with her back to the audience and had others speak for her in interviews when she was emphasizing privacy – you can use similar misdirection.

- **Set Boundaries with Press and Stick to Them:** Through your publicist or PR team, establish ground rules for any media engagement. Common guidelines could be: *no personal questions*, *no photography beyond the approved masked photos*, *the interviewer may not ask to remove the helmet*, etc. Most reputable journalists will honor these if agreed in advance. It can even be part of the interview agreement that if they violate the terms, the interview can be terminated. Have a bit of fun with it – some journalists actually enjoy the mystique. (Daft Punk once did a photo shoot where the journalists were given their own masks to wear, leaning into the concept.) By defining boundaries, you avoid awkward on-the-spot refusals. Also, prepare some in-character backstory or quips to deflect identity questions. For example, if asked “Why do you hide your identity?” you might respond with a witty line like Marshmello did (“I don’t take off my helmet because I don’t want or need fame... The helmet makes me Marshmello and also makes you Marshmello”【22†L63-L72】) – i.e., keep it about the music or the audience, not about you.

- **Leverage Mystery as Marketing:** Instead of seeing anonymity as a hurdle, treat it as part of MUFFINTOP’s brand strategy. Craft PR narratives that emphasize the creative reasons for anonymity: e.g., “MUFFINTOP wants the focus on the music, not the individual,” or “the helmet represents the collective nature of the project” – anything that steers conversation away from the person and towards an artistic statement. This not only helps justify your secrecy to the press but can create intrigue that benefits marketing. Many successful acts (like Marshmello and Daft Punk) have used mystery to their advantage, generating buzz as fans speculate【22†L45-L53】. You can encourage this in harmless ways (tease content that plays into the lore, etc.) but ensure not to accidentally give real clues. If you do any PR stunts (for example, Marshmello humorously “revealed” himself as Tiësto once as a prank【22†L45-L53】), plan them carefully so they don’t backfire. Decoy tactics can be fun – having someone else in a helmet appear publicly while you’re known to be elsewhere, for instance – but these should be executed with strict secrecy within your team.

- **Press Releases and Credits:** When issuing press releases (for new music, tours, etc.), write them to omit any real name. Use your publicist’s contact for press inquiries (not you). In the boilerplate, describe MUFFINTOP in third person as an entity or concept. If you have notable accomplishments under your real name (say you were a producer or writer before), resist the temptation to mention them. Any link like “MUFFINTOP is actually an award-winning producer in disguise” will send journalists digging. It’s better to craft a fresh narrative for MUFFINTOP’s origins that doesn’t invite identity guesses. Also coordinate with any collaborators: if you feature on someone else’s song or have a guest on yours, make sure they and their teams know not to mention your identity. They should refer to you as MUFFINTOP in any interviews about the collab. A quick friendly NDA or written understanding can be used when doing features to cover this.

- **Public Appearances and Awards:** If you attend industry events or award shows, do so in full disguise. Plan logistics to avoid slip-ups (similar to live performances). For example, arrive in a private car directly to the red carpet with helmet on, so there’s no moment where paparazzi catch you unmasked. If you accept an award, you can emulate Daft Punk’s approach: remain silent or use prepared audio, let a collaborator do the speaking, or keep it extremely short in a disguised voice【37†L268-L276】. These moments can actually enhance the legend of the project if done right (people still talk about Daft Punk’s cool, silent acceptance in robot helmets【37†L268-L276】). If networking at events, you may choose to step away to a private area to remove the helmet for one-on-one conversations with key industry people – but only do this if necessary and with people you trust (and ideally who are under some NDA or professional code to not expose you). Otherwise, network in costume; it might be odd, but plenty of industry folks have interacted with masked artists professionally without knowing their identity, and it’s become somewhat normal.

- **Crisis Management (if a Leak Occurs):** Despite best efforts, be prepared with a plan if your identity ever leaks or is speculated upon by a major source. The plan can be: neither confirm nor deny. Often the best strategy is to continue the stance of not addressing it. For instance, when publications eventually pointed to Marshmello being Chris Comstock, Marshmello’s response was simply focusing on the positive reasoning for the helmet【22†L63-L71】. You could issue a generic statement via your publicist: “Muffintop’s identity is not important – we only care about the music and the experience.” Do not give in and reveal because of pressure; plenty of artists have had rumors swirling and still stick to the persona. Over time, true fans and media will respect the boundary if you consistently enforce it. However, internally, investigate how the leak happened (was it a credit on an album? a disgruntled ex-collaborator? a hacked email?). Shore up that gap to prevent further damage. And let your close team know how to respond – they should all be on the same page (“we don’t comment on identity rumors” is a simple line to use).

- **Use Decoys and Misdirection Sparingly:** If the project concept allows, you might sometimes use decoys (e.g., hiring an actor to play “Muffintop” for an interview or appearance while you control remotely). Some bands have done this to throw off press. If you go this route, plan it very carefully and with legal guidance (the person should sign a strict NDA and possibly be on payroll to ensure loyalty). This is an advanced tactic and can backfire if the decoy doesn’t act correctly, so weigh the risks. Often, simply limiting appearances is easier than managing decoys. An alternative misdirection is to give the media fun talking points that distract from identity – for example, create a fictional story or say “Muffintop could be anyone: it could be one person or a collective of producers.” Let them chase a red herring rather than the truth.

- **Consistent Persona in Media:** Finally, maintain consistency in how the MUFFINTOP character is portrayed. If your persona has a certain style (mysterious, humorous, etc.), make sure all public statements align. This consistency helps prevent slip-ups. You won’t accidentally mention something personal if you’re sticking to a well-defined character script. Develop a short list of “approved facts” about MUFFINTOP you can share (e.g., genre, influences, the meaning of the name if any) and deflect everything else. Train your team – if a promoter or MC is introducing you, have them use a pre-written intro that doesn’t include any personal info. If a biography is needed for a festival program, supply one that is deliberately vague or fictional in terms of identity. By controlling the narrative tightly, you leave little room for others to fill in blanks (which is when guesses or leaks occur).
