## Assistant

In general, **police officers do have to pay for public parking meters**, unless their vehicle is clearly used in an emergency capacity or displays an official exemption (like a special placard). Here's how it breaks down:

---

### 🚓 Police vs. Parking Meters

#### 1. Typical Meter Parking (Non-Emergency Vehicles)  
- Parking meters are enforced by **LADOT traffic officers**, not just LAPD.  
- Ordinary police vehicles parked at a meter must usually pay, unless a valid exemption applies citeturn0search6.

#### 2. Exemptions  
Some jurisdictions allow exemptions when:  
- The vehicle is clearly **on-duty** for police or fire operations, or  
- The meter or sign explicitly exempts certain official vehicles citeturn0search9turn0search11.  

In California, disabled-placard holders are exempt, and in some cities, police vehicles on active duty with proper placards can also be exempt.

---

### In Los Angeles (Your Location)  
- **LADOT’s FAQ** clarifies that if a parking meter accepts either coins or card payment and you are within enforcement hours, you must pay—unless it’s officially broken, or you're parked under a clearly marked emergency exemption citeturn0search6.  
- There’s *no blanket exemption* for police cruisers just because they're police cruisers—they’re expected to pay like any other vehicle unless there’s signage indicating otherwise or it’s directly related to an emergency call.

---

### Key Takeaways for Mr. White:
- **Yes**, cops need to pay parking meters in LA—unless they’ve got:
  - A **valid, on-duty placard**,
  - Are responding to an **emergency**, or
  - Signs/meter permit **official/exempt parking**.

- Simply being in a police vehicle doesn’t waive the parking meter requirement.

---

**Bottom line:** LAPD officers must pay for meters during regular duty. Only clearly marked emergency situations or official exemptions qualify for free parking. Without those, meter payment is required.

If you spot a police vehicle not paying, it’s likely a different situation than a regular parked cruiser—could be an emergency, official business, or legitimate exemption.
