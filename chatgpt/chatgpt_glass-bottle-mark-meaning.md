If you're trying to determine whether the bottle was repurposed, here are a few things you can look for:

1. **Label Residue:** Check for any remnants of labels or adhesive on the bottle. Alcohol bottles typically have labels that, if removed, might leave some residue. If there's no label but signs of one being removed, it could suggest repurposing.

2. **Cap or Closure:** Examine the type of closure the bottle has. If it has a screw cap or cork, these are typically associated with alcohol bottles. If the closure looks different from what you'd expect for alcohol (like a different type of stopper or cap), it might have been repurposed.

3. **Internal Staining or Scent:** Sometimes bottles that held alcohol retain a scent or staining from the original contents. If the bottle has a strong alcohol smell or visible staining, it was likely used for alcohol originally. If the inside looks especially clean or has a different scent, it might have been cleaned and repurposed.

4. **Wear and Tear:** Look for signs of wear that might suggest the bottle has been used for something else. Scratches, unusual markings, or a buildup of residue that doesn't match typical alcohol use could indicate repurposing.

5. **Content Type:** If the bottle is filled with a substance different from what you'd expect for alcohol (e.g., oils, sauces, or homemade brews), it was likely repurposed.

6. **Different Closure/Seal:** If the bottle has been sealed in a way that doesn’t align with typical alcohol packaging (like a rubber stopper instead of a cork or screw cap), it might have been repurposed.

If none of these signs are present, and the bottle looks typical of an alcohol bottle, it may still be in its original state. However, if there are indications like a different type of closure, contents, or signs of label removal, it’s possible the bottle was repurposed for another use.
