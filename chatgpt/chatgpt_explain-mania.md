## Assistant

**Mania** is a psychological state characterized by an abnormally elevated, expansive, or irritable mood, increased energy, and excessive goal-directed activity. It’s most commonly associated with **Bipolar I Disorder**, though milder forms (hypomania) occur in **Bipolar II Disorder**.

### Core Features
- **Mood elevation**: Extreme euphoria, excitement, or irritability.
- **Energy and activity**: Markedly increased drive, restlessness, or sleeplessness without fatigue.
- **Speech and thought**: Rapid, pressured speech; flight of ideas; distractibility.
- **Self-esteem and behavior**: Inflated self-confidence or grandiosity; risky or impulsive behavior (spending sprees, sexual indiscretions, reckless driving).
- **Cognitive and judgment impairments**: Poor decision-making, sometimes progressing to psychosis (delusions or hallucinations).

### Diagnostic Criteria (DSM-5 Summary)
To qualify as a manic episode:
- Symptoms must last **at least one week** (or any duration if hospitalization is required).
- The mood disturbance must cause **marked impairment** in social or occupational functioning or require hospitalization.
- Symptoms are **not due to substance use or medical conditions**.

### Biological Basis
- **Neurotransmitters**: Elevated dopamine and norepinephrine activity.
- **Brain regions**: Dysregulation in prefrontal cortex and limbic system (especially the amygdala).
- **Genetics**: Strong hereditary component—first-degree relatives have a higher risk of bipolar disorders.

### Treatment
- **Medication**: Mood stabilizers (e.g., lithium, valproate), atypical antipsychotics.
- **Psychotherapy**: Cognitive-behavioral therapy, psychoeducation, adherence therapy.
- **Lifestyle**: Sleep regularity, stress reduction, and avoidance of stimulants or substances.

## Assistant

That kind of reaction—**believing you’re in a TV show or alternate reality after taking drugs or drinking**—points toward a **substance-induced psychotic or dissociative episode**, not typical mania.

### Possible Explanations
1. **Substance-Induced Psychosis**
   - Triggered by alcohol, cannabis, cocaine, LSD, methamphetamine, or similar drugs.
   - Involves delusions (believing one is on TV, being watched, or controlling events) and sometimes hallucinations.
   - The episode usually resolves as the substance leaves the system, but repeated use can make these states persistent.

2. **Derealization / Depersonalization**
   - A dissociative state where reality feels “unreal” or dreamlike.
   - The person may feel detached from themselves, as if watching their life like a movie.
   - Can occur with heavy intoxication, sleep deprivation, or trauma.

3. **Manic Episode With Psychosis**
   - Less likely if symptoms appear *only* under intoxication.
   - Would include elevated energy, grandiosity, decreased need for sleep, and impulsive behavior, persisting beyond the effects of drugs.

### Clinical Distinctions
| Feature | Mania | Substance-Induced Psychosis | Dissociation |
|----------|--------|------------------------------|---------------|
| Duration | ≥1 week | Hours–days (linked to use) | Minutes–hours |
| Mood | Elevated/irritable | Variable | Usually anxious, detached |
| Cause | Endogenous (bipolar) | Drug or alcohol use | Stress, drugs |
| Insight | Often poor | Often poor | Sometimes preserved |

### What’s Usually Happening
The **drugs or alcohol** alter neurotransmitters (dopamine, serotonin, glutamate), distorting perception and self-concept. The person’s brain may temporarily lose its sense of reality boundaries, producing a vivid but false sense of “being inside” a show or narrative.

### Next Steps
- **Stop using** the triggering substances.
- **Seek evaluation** by a psychiatrist—especially if these experiences recur or linger when sober.
- In some cases, **antipsychotic medication** or **rehabilitation therapy** may be indicated.
