## The core risk pattern

The danger comes from four things stacking together:

| Factor | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| **Tiny wheels** | Potholes, street plates, uneven pavement, curbs, and debris are much more dangerous than on bikes. |
| **Standing posture** | Falls tend to throw the rider forward onto face, wrists, shoulders, head. |
| **No helmet** | Head/face trauma becomes much more likely to matter. |
| **Wrong operating space** | Sidewalks endanger pedestrians; streets expose riders to cars; bike lanes are safest when protected. |

## Sidewalks

In California, sidewalk riding is generally illegal for motorized scooters except briefly to enter or leave adjacent property. California law also caps motorized scooters at **15 mph**, prohibits passengers, requires a license/permit, and requires helmets for riders under 18. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn577977search1Ņłü

That aligns with the safety logic: sidewalks are bad for pedestrians, and streets are bad for scooter riders unless there is proper bike infrastructure.

## Bottom line

**They are meaningfully risky.** The best LA data suggests e-scooter injury rates are in the same ballpark as motorcycles on a per-trip basis, though motorcycle injuries are generally more severe/fatal. The big difference is that scooters feel casual, cheap, and frictionless, so people ride them like toys while exposed to traffic like vehicles.

For personal safety, the sane rule is:

**Protected bike lane + helmet + sober + no passengers + no sidewalk + no night chaos = acceptable risk.**

**No helmet, car traffic, bad pavement, sidewalk weaving, or drunk/night riding = dumb risk.**

## Your friendŌĆÖs crash is the classic e-scooter failure mode

What you described is exactly why they are sketchy in cities:

- **Small front wheel hits pothole**
- Front wheel deflects sharply
- Handlebar turns instantly
- Rider has almost no recovery window

A bike has bigger wheels, a longer wheelbase, more gyroscopic stability, and the rider is seated. That also explains why a helmet only solves part of the problem. A helmet reduces head-injury risk. It does not protect your face, teeth, wrists, clavicle, knees, tibia, fibula, ankle, or hip.

## Why cities struggle to enforce it

The issue is practical enforcement:

- Police generally prioritize cars, violent crime, DUIs, and major collisions.
