Potentially considering joining a VC firm as an entrepreneur in residence, a creator partnerships person, or a community person. I’d love to host a podcast on venture and tech or help with content creation or something. I want to be in a role to get decent income and carry. But I have limited resume

## Assistant

Mr. White, here’s the blunt breakdown for getting into a VC firm in one of those roles given your current position and goals:  

---

**1. Roles you’re eyeing & reality check**  
- **Entrepreneur in Residence (EIR)** → Usually goes to founders with prior exits or strong VC relationships. Without a big startup win or strong tech resume, this is a long shot *unless* you bring a unique angle (e.g., deep creator economy network, brand building chops).  
- **Creator Partnerships / Community** → Much easier entry point. If you can show you can grow an audience, run events, or drive inbound deal flow from non-obvious networks, you can get in without a traditional founder track record.  
- **Content / Podcast Host** → Totally viable if you can pitch a high-quality, differentiated concept that also funnels deal flow and brand lift to the firm.  

---

**2. Path to “decent income + carry”**  
- **Base salary:** Non-investing VC roles (community, marketing, ops) usually pay $80–150k depending on firm size/location.  
- **Carry:** Rare in non-investing roles unless you negotiate a slice and can prove you directly contribute to deals that close. More realistic at smaller, scrappier firms where everyone wears multiple hats.  
- **Leverage:** You’ll need to position yourself as *deal flow engine + media amplifier* so they can justify giving you carry.  

---

**3. How to compensate for limited resume**  
- Build a **public footprint** now: Launch a mini podcast, newsletter, or video series on creator economy + tech + venture. Even 3–5 sharp episodes with credible guests makes you look “plugged in.”  
- Show **community-building proof**: Run a few high-value dinners, panels, or networking events that attract founders/investors. Document it.  
- Highlight **unique access**: If you can get in front of founders/influencers before they hit TechCrunch, that’s leverage you can sell to a VC firm.  
- Package as **deal flow + brand content hybrid**: “I’ll grow your audience *and* your pipeline.”  

---

**4. Pitch framing to a VC**  
- Don’t pitch yourself as “hire me, I’ll figure it out.” Pitch as:  
  > “I can create a high-quality podcast/content platform that puts [Firm] at the center of the conversation in [target sectors]. I can tap into non-obvious founder and creator networks for early deal flow, run events to deepen relationships, and give you a brand edge that brings in deals before competitors see them.”  

---

## Assistant

That’s actually the smarter long-game play, Mr. White.  

If you **start your own podcast first**, you flip the dynamic:  

---

**1. You own the audience**  
- You’re not tied to one firm’s brand.  
- You can feature *multiple* investors, founders, and creators → turns every guest into a warm relationship.  
- You control distribution and sponsorship deals.  

---

**2. You create leverage with VCs**  
- If the show gets traction, firms will *want* to sponsor or get you in-house because you’ve already proven you can attract their target audience.  
- You can make them compete for sponsorship rights → higher rates, better terms.  

---

**3. You can still get to carry indirectly**  
- If a firm likes you enough, they might bring you in part-time for brand/community in addition to sponsoring the show. That’s when you negotiate carry.  
- You can also *personally* invest in early guests’ startups if you get access early.  

---

**4. Execution shortcut**  
- Launch with a **minimum viable season** (4–6 episodes).  
- Record *fast* (Zoom or in-person with simple gear), focus on **smart, founder-focused conversations** that bigger shows aren’t covering.  
- Build a **social clip strategy** so every guest promotes it.  

---

**Playbook:**  
- **Step 1:** Pick a niche that’s VC/creator/tech crossover (e.g., “Where Creators Become Founders” or “Before the Term Sheet”).  
- **Step 2:** Line up first 4 guests *before* launch.  
- **Step 3:** Release episodes weekly, promote on LinkedIn, Twitter/X, TikTok.  
- **Step 4:** By episode 5, start *quietly* letting VC firms know you’re open to sponsorship or collaboration.  

---

If you do it right, you won’t be asking VCs for a job — they’ll be asking how to get on *your* platform.
