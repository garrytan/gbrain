### 1. Your current hero:  
**“Your fans are waiting.”**  
Subhead: *Connect Spotify. Your profile is live in under a minute.*

### Brutally honest take:

## 🚫 “Try Jovie today.”
Weak. Generic. Replaceable with any SaaS on earth.

YC partners hate generic verbs like *try*, *discover*, *unlock*, *empower*. It signals you don’t have a sharp point of view. It also shifts focus from the user to the company.

Good hero copy makes the user feel something immediate and specific.  
This feels like a landing page template.

---

## ⚠️ Lowercasing “your fans are waiting”
Visually softer. Less punch.

You’re moving from:
> **Your fans are waiting.**

To:
> your fans are waiting

That loses authority. Hero copy should feel declarative and confident. The capitalized version reads like a headline. The lowercase version reads like body text.

---

## 🤔 “Claim handle”
This is the most interesting change.

It introduces:
- Scarcity
- Ownership
- Urgency
- Platform vibes (Twitter/IG-era mechanics)

That’s good.

But it raises a strategic question:

Are you:
1. A Spotify growth tool?
2. Or a fan identity / profile platform?

If the product’s real wedge is “claim your fan handle before someone else,” then that should be the headline — not the CTA.

Right now it feels bolted on.

---

# YC Lens: What’s Missing?

YC advice would likely be:

1. What *problem* are you solving?
2. Who exactly is this for?
3. What changes in their life in 60 seconds?

“Your fans are waiting” is emotionally compelling but abstract.

Better versions might lean into something more concrete like:

- Turn Spotify listeners into real fans.
- Own your fan identity.
- Make your Spotify profile work harder.
- Convert streams into community.

Or if handle scarcity is the wedge:

- Claim your fan handle before someone else does.
- Your fan identity. Secured in 60 seconds.

---

# The Real Strategic Question

What’s the core action?

Right now your strongest specific line is:
> Connect Spotify. Your profile is live in under a minute.

That’s extremely good because:
- It’s friction-aware.
- It implies ease.
- It reduces uncertainty.

If you remove that and replace it with “Try Jovie today,” you’re downgrading clarity.

---

# My Direct Recommendation

If you want to evolve it, do this instead:

Headline:
> Your fans are waiting.

Subhead:
> Claim your fan handle. Connect Spotify. Go live in 60 seconds.

CTA:
> Claim your handle

That:
- Keeps the emotional hook.
- Introduces scarcity.
- Maintains clarity.
- Preserves speed.

---

# Bottom Line

Your proposed change is directionally interesting but currently weaker than what you have.

It makes the page more generic and less sharp.

If you’re going to pivot toward “Claim handle” as the wedge, then commit fully and restructure the hierarchy around ownership and urgency — not “Try Jovie.”

If you want, I can rewrite the entire hero section like a YC Demo Day slide.

Good. That changes the evaluation.

Footer CTAs are different. They’re not for persuasion — they’re for catching people who are already convinced.

From a YC perspective:

### “Try Jovie today.”
Still weak. It’s generic and adds no new motivation. If someone scrolled to the footer, they’ve already processed the value prop. Repeating a bland SaaS CTA wastes the slot.

### “Your fans are waiting”
Stronger. It reinforces the emotional hook. That’s good for a footer — reminder energy works.

### “Claim handle”
This is the best part. It’s specific and action-oriented. Way better than “Get started” or “Try.”

---

## What YC Would Push You On

Footer CTA should:
1. Reinforce the core promise.
2. Remove friction.
3. Push a single clear action.

It shouldn’t introduce a new concept unless that concept is your wedge.

If “handle” is central to the product, then yes — lean into it.

If it’s not core, it will feel like a random mechanic introduced too late.

---
