
Mr. White, the disconnect is economics.

The dangerous version is:

**“We sell $300/month software to artists.”**

The stronger version is:

**“Jovie replaces creative-business labor. Artists are the first operating surface. Managers, labels, agencies, and media companies are the economic buyers.”**

The company should price like an operator, not like a creator tool.

# The core answer

Jovie should become:

## **The AI operator for cultural businesses, starting with music artists.**

The universal loop is:

**Detect signal → identify opportunity → create asset → launch action → measure result → improve the next action**

For an artist, that looks like:

**No merch → designs generated → storefront launched → fans messaged → revenue collected**

For Disney, Netflix, a sports team, a label, or a media brand, it looks like:

**Viral moment detected → brand-safe creative generated → approval routed → post/campaign launched → performance measured**

Same underlying product. Different buyer. Different price point.

# Your pricing concern is correct

A $299/month artist plan equals **$3,588/year**. Spotify says the **100,000th-highest-earning artist generated more than $7,300 in Spotify royalties in 2025**, and more than a third of artists generating $10,000+ were DIY or began DIY. That means $299/month can make sense for a narrow professional artist segment, but it is too expensive as the default mass-market artist motion. citeturn466012view1

So the deck should never imply:

**“Millions of artists will all pay us $299/month.”**

The credible version is:

**“Artists use it. Businesses pay for it.”**

# The business model that makes sense

## 1. Free / low-cost artist wedge

This is acquisition, data, proof, and distribution.

Call it something like:

**Jovie Street**  
or  
**Jovie Starter**

For the busker example, the product should let you walk up and create:

**QR profile**  
**tip link**  
**SMS capture**  
**instant merch preorder**  
**song request / fan signup page**

Pricing:

**Free to start**  
**payment processing passed through**  
**small transaction fee or fulfillment margin**  
**upgrade when they need Jovie to operate for them**

This creates the magic demo without depending on broke artists for revenue.

The busking plan is actually powerful because it proves:

**Jovie can create monetization from zero infrastructure in minutes.**

That is a deck-worthy proof point.

## 3. Roster plan

This is probably the best first serious business model.

Buyer:

**managers**  
**indie labels**  
**music marketing agencies**  
**distributors**  
**artist services companies**

Pricing:

**$2,500–$10,000/month per roster**  
or  
**base fee + per active artist**

Example:

**$3,000/month for 20 artists**  
**+$100/month per additional active artist**  
**+ usage for generated campaigns / merch / SMS / creative**

Why this works:

A manager or label already pays humans to do repetitive creative operations. Jovie lets one person approve and launch work across a roster.

The product becomes:

**“Run 50 artists with the operating capacity of a 10-person team.”**

That is much more believable than asking 50 individual artists to each pay $299.

Buyer:

**entertainment studios**

This is the same product loop as the artist merch example.

The reason this is attractive: enterprise buyers have budget, urgent need, and existing labor spend. The risk is sales cycle, approval complexity, and brand safety. So it should be an expansion path or parallel pilot, not the first thing that makes the whole company dependent on Disney saying yes.

# Avoid the label / distributor trap

Jovie should not take rights, masters, publishing, catalog revenue, or broad creator income.

The safe model:

**Subscription for operator capacity**  
**usage fees for actions**  
**performance fee only on money Jovie directly creates**

Examples of acceptable performance fees:

**10% of merch profit Jovie launches**  
**5% of fan-commerce revenue Jovie generates**  
**campaign success fee on approved business outcomes**

Patreon’s current public pricing is a useful reference point: it charges creators **10% of income earned on Patreon plus payment processing and other fees**, which shows the creator platform world already normalizes revenue share. Jovie should use rev share carefully because the bigger positioning is labor replacement, not creator platform tollbooth. citeturn466012view2

The Jovie line should be:

**“We don’t take artist rights. We operate revenue workflows.”**

# Why generic commerce is a bad category

If Jovie becomes “AI storefront for artists,” it gets commoditized fast.

Shopify is already pushing AI commerce, agentic storefronts, Sidekick, workflow automation, checkout, payments, shipping, and agent tools. citeturn466012view3

So Jovie’s defensibility should be:

**artist and cultural context**  
**opportunity detection**  
**action selection**  
**approval workflows**  
**closed-loop measurement**  
**learning what works across creative businesses**

The storefront is infrastructure. The operator is the product.

# The better company shape

There are three possible companies here.

## Option A — Artist SaaS

**Pitch:** AI manager for artists  
**Buyer:** individual artists  
**Pricing:** $29–$299/month  
**Problem:** many users lack budget  
**Verdict:** good wedge, weak standalone economics

## Option B — Roster operator

**Pitch:** AI operator for managers, labels, and artist-service teams  
**Buyer:** businesses managing many artists  
**Pricing:** $2.5k–$10k/month  
**Problem:** requires product depth and trust  
**Verdict:** best near-term business model

## Option C — Cultural response engine

**Pitch:** AI operator for brands and media companies  
**Buyer:** enterprise marketing/social teams  
**Pricing:** $10k–$50k/month  
**Problem:** enterprise sales, approvals, brand safety  
**Verdict:** biggest expansion market

My recommendation:

# Build Option B, demo with Option A, architect toward Option C.

That gives you speed, authenticity, and venture-scale economics.

# What to build

Build the product as a general **operator engine**, then expose the artist version first.

## 1. Managed entity record

This is the core object.

For an artist:

**catalog**  
**visual identity**  
**fan channels**  
**audience data**  
**commerce status**  
**release history**  
**goals**  
**past actions**  
**results**

For a brand:

**brand rules**  
**IP permissions**  
**audience segments**  
**channels**  
**approval policy**  
**campaign history**  
**performance data**

Same architecture.

## 2. Signal engine

Jovie needs to detect things worth acting on.

Artist signals:

**no merch**  
**new release**  
**show announced**  
**fan list growing**  
**song gaining traction**  
**collaborator overlap**  
**no direct fan channel**

Brand/media signals:

**viral moment**  
**trend spike**  
**competitor post**  
**audience conversation**  
**news event**  
**sports/music/pop culture moment**

## 3. Opportunity cards

Every recommendation should become an executable card.

Format:

**What I found**  
**Why it matters**  
**What I made**  
**Approve / revise / launch**

Example:

**You have no merch. I made three designs based on your latest cover art. Pick one and I’ll launch the store.**

This is the interface.

## 4. Execution workflows

Start with four workflows.

### Workflow 1: Merch launch

**detect gap**  
**generate designs**  
**mock up product**  
**create product page**  
**add to profile**  
**draft fan broadcast**  
**handle checkout**  
**route fulfillment**  
**report result**

### Workflow 2: Fan capture

**create QR/profile**  
**capture SMS/email**  
**tag fan source**  
**suggest next message**

This is where the busker demo fits.

### Workflow 3: Collab outreach

**find compatible artists**  
**explain fit**  
**draft pitch**  
**track replies**

### Workflow 4: Cultural moment response

**detect moment**  
**generate brand-safe creative**  
**check rules**  
**route approval**  
**publish or schedule**  
**measure**

Workflow 4 can be rough internally at first. It shows the platform expands beyond music.

## 5. Approval and safety layer

This matters for both artists and enterprise.

Jovie should support:

**human approval before publishing**  
**brand voice rules**  
**IP restrictions**  
**blocked topics**  
**legal/compliance review**  
**audit history**

For the Disney-style example, the enterprise version needs an owned-IP mode. Disney can use Disney IP. Other brands need a mode that avoids protected characters, likenesses, and copyrighted assets.

## 6. Measurement loop

Every action needs a result.

Track:

**revenue created**  
**fan signups**  
**clicks**  
**conversion rate**  
**approval rate**  
**time saved**  
**human edits required**  
**cost per action**  
**gross margin per workflow**

These are the metrics that prove Jovie is replacing labor.
