# The MySpace Problem

MySpace lost for an obvious reason that took a long time to become obvious.

It let users customize everything.

At the time, this felt generous. You could change fonts, colors, layouts. You could add music that auto-played. You could turn your profile into a small, chaotic expression of yourself. Facebook did the opposite. You couldn’t customize anything. Same layout. Same colors. Same structure for everyone.

Only one of those companies is still around.

This wasn’t an accident.

Customization feels empowering, but it hides a cost. It quietly transfers responsibility from the product to the user. Once you let users decide how things should look and behave, you’re asking them to do design work. Worse, you’re asking them to do product work. Most people are not good at that. And even the ones who are can’t compete with a team whose only job is to obsess over it.

That’s the MySpace problem.

It didn’t just apply to social networks. It keeps reappearing wherever creators are told that expression and effectiveness are the same thing.

They’re not.

A musician’s job is not to design pages. A writer doesn’t design the bookstore. They write the book. The book is the art. Everything around it exists to get out of the way and help it sell.

This is why Amazon product pages all look basically identical. They’re not colorful, expressive landing pages. They’re restrained. Predictable. Almost boring. That’s intentional. The page isn’t there to be admired. It’s there to help you decide and buy. Uniformity builds trust. Constraint creates momentum.

When you look at products that win at scale, they all do this. They say no to customization because they understand something subtle: design is not taste, it’s iteration.

Great products are built by teams that run hundreds of experiments you never see. They argue about button placement. They test one-pixel shifts. They obsess over border radius, contrast, hierarchy. They QA on every screen size. They make sure it works on the newest iPhone and an ancient Android. Then they do it again.

Even if a musician spent all their time on this, they would still lose. Not because they aren’t smart or creative, but because they’re competing against a system.

And they shouldn’t be competing at all.

Creators are supposed to create.

This is where link-in-bio tools started to repeat the MySpace mistake.

The link-in-bio is the modern profile page. It’s where attention goes after discovery. But instead of treating it like infrastructure, most tools treat it like a canvas. Unlimited links. Unlimited colors. Fonts, backgrounds, layouts. Every page different. Two Linktrees look like two entirely different products.

That’s framed as a feature. It’s not.

What it really creates is the paradox of choice. No hierarchy. No clear next step. Just a vertical pile of options competing for attention. The result is predictable: fewer clicks, less action, more admiration.

Pretty pages don’t convert.

This is where, when I started thinking about how to build Joby, the answer felt obvious.

The link isn’t the art. The music is.

Joby isn’t a place for self-expression. It’s a piece of infrastructure designed to move attention in one direction. We made it opinionated on purpose. Fewer choices. Clear calls to action. Design decisions centralized and improved over time instead of reinvented by every artist.

That’s not restrictive. It’s leverage.

The mistake MySpace made was assuming users wanted freedom more than results. The mistake Linktree makes is assuming creators want expression more than outcomes.

They don’t.

They want people to listen. To follow. To buy tickets. To care.

The best products don’t ask users what they want to customize. They decide what works and remove the rest. Constraint isn’t the opposite of creativity. It’s what lets creativity compound.

Say no to designing pages.  
Say yes to making work.

That’s how things scale.

# The MySpace Problem

I was born in 1991. By the time I was in middle school, every one of my friends had a MySpace profile.

It was most people’s first real exposure to social networking. For artists especially, it mattered. Bands broke on MySpace. Songs spread there before “going viral” was even a phrase. For a while, it felt like the center of the internet.

Early MySpace profiles were relatively simple. But MySpace didn’t lock things down. You could inject HTML and CSS. People started hacking layouts. Then themes. Then full custom pages. By the mid-2000s, profiles were blinking, auto-playing, unreadable messes.

Around the same time, Facebook did the opposite. It removed choice. One layout. One feed. No profile hacking. No music players. No glitter GIFs. It felt boring, but it worked.

That pattern keeps repeating.

In the early 2000s, customization was everywhere. AIM let you customize fonts, colors, sounds, away messages. PCs had ports for everything. Android let you tweak nearly every aspect of the interface. The assumption was that more control was always better.

Twenty years later, technology is dramatically more advanced—and yet users are allowed to customize far less.

That’s not an accident.

Apple is the clearest example. Early PCs shipped with an absurd number of ports. Apple reduced them. Then reduced them again. People complained every time. And yet Apple’s market share and influence grew.

Android lets you customize almost everything. iOS doesn’t. And yet a far higher percentage of iPhones run the latest version of iOS compared to Android devices running the latest version of Android. Fewer models, fewer forks, fewer choices—faster updates, fewer bugs, tighter control.

Fragmentation feels empowering at the edges, but it slows everything down at the center.

Tony Fadell understood this early. Before Nest, before the iPhone, he worked on the iPod. At the time, MP3 players were covered in buttons. Each feature had a control. They were technically powerful and practically unusable.

The iPod reduced everything to a wheel.

That wasn’t a loss of functionality. It was an abstraction. The interface got out of the way so the music could come forward. The other players disappeared. The iPod didn’t.

The same thing happened with phones. Google experimented with modular hardware. The idea was elegant: swap cameras, batteries, components. In practice, it fragmented the product, slowed iteration, and made quality control harder. The project died.

What these examples have in common is restraint.

Great products don’t give users more choices. They absorb complexity themselves so users don’t have to.

This matters even more for creators.

A musician’s job is not to design pages. An author doesn’t design the bookstore. They design the book. The work is the art. Everything around it is infrastructure.

That’s framed as freedom. What it actually creates is confusion.

When there’s no hierarchy, nothing stands out. When everything is customizable, nothing is optimized. You get admiration instead of action. Pretty pages that don’t convert.

This is the modern version of the MySpace problem.

Less expression in the wrapper. More power behind the work.

The mistake MySpace made wasn’t generosity. It was confusion about where creativity belongs.

Creativity belongs in the work.

Everything else should get out of the way.

The products that win at scale all make this trade. Facebook over MySpace. iPod over button forests. iPhone over modular phones. Spotify over personal landing pages.

The mistake isn’t giving users freedom. It’s giving them responsibility they shouldn’t have.

Creativity belongs in the work.

Everything else should get out of the way.
