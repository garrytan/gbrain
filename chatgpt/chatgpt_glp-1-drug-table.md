**GLP-1 vs. GLP-3:**

### GLP-1 (Glucagon-Like Peptide-1)
- **Role:** GLP-1 is a hormone primarily involved in the regulation of blood glucose levels. It is secreted by the intestines in response to food intake. Its main functions include stimulating insulin release from the pancreas (which helps lower blood sugar levels), inhibiting glucagon release (which would otherwise increase blood sugar), and slowing gastric emptying, which helps you feel full longer.
- **Clinical Use:** GLP-1 receptor agonists (like semaglutide and liraglutide) are medications that mimic the action of GLP-1. They are widely used in the treatment of Type 2 diabetes and, more recently, for weight management due to their effects on appetite and satiety.

### GLP-3 (Glucagon-Like Peptide-3)
- **Role:** GLP-3 is much less studied and understood compared to GLP-1. There is very limited information available about GLP-3, as it does not play a significant role in clinical medicine, unlike GLP-1. It may be a term used in early-stage research or in specific experimental contexts, but it is not a well-recognized or characterized hormone in current medical practice.
  
In summary, GLP-1 is a well-known and extensively studied hormone with established roles in managing diabetes and weight loss. GLP-3, on the other hand, is not commonly referenced in the medical literature, and there is little information about it in clinical use. Therefore, GLP-1 is the focus of current treatments and research in metabolic disorders.
