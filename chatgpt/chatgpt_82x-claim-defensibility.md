Mr. White, as shown, I would call it **weakly defensible at best**.

The main problem is that the headline says **“8.2x more fans”** while the footnote says **“8.2x more streaming momentum vs the industry average.”** Those are materially different claims. Under FTC guidance, advertisers have to substantiate the objective claim consumers reasonably take away from the ad, judged from the ad’s overall impression, and a disclosure cannot fix a headline if it contradicts the headline’s meaning. citeturn663049view0turn663049view1turn938699view0

The placement is also risky. The asterisk explanation appears in small text on a later screen, after the bold claim and CTA. FTC guidance says qualifying information has to be **clear and conspicuous**, and the test is whether consumers actually notice and understand it in context, especially on mobile. Simply placing the disclosure somewhere in the flow is not enough. citeturn663049view0turn938699view0

There is also a substantiation problem. “Fans,” “streaming momentum,” and “industry average” are all undefined here. An objective comparative performance claim needs support before the ad runs, and if the ad reasonably suggests more than one meaning, the advertiser needs support for each reasonable interpretation. citeturn663049view0turn663049view1turn663049view2

California law is unfavorable to this style of claim if it tends to mislead. Section 17500 makes it unlawful to disseminate internet advertising that is untrue or misleading and known, or that should be known with reasonable care, to be untrue or misleading. citeturn901208view0

My bottom line: **they may have data that supports some narrower statement, but this exact presentation is exposed.** The clean, more defensible version would be something like: **“Based on our 2025 internal analysis, Pro artists averaged 8.2x higher streaming momentum than [defined comparison group].”** Then they should define the metric, sample, period, and comparator right next to the claim.

A second issue exists with **“Top artists choose un:hurd”**. If that is meant literally, it reads like an objective market or endorsement claim and would also need substantiation. citeturn938699view0
