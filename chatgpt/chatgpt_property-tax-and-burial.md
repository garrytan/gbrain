Is it true that if I put a body in my backyard, I don't have to pay property tax?

---

## Assistant

In short: **No, simply burying a body in your backyard doesn’t exempt your property from taxes.** Here's why:

---

### 🏡 1. **Cemetery exemptions are very strict**

- In **California**, to get a cemetery exemption under Prop. 13, a property must be used **exclusively** for human burials or cemetery care—and typically have **six or more** graves or be legally established as a cemetery citeturn0search0.  
- A **single burial** at your home does **not** qualify. Property with fewer graves is still taxed as normal citeturn0search7turn0search12.

---

### 🌵 2. **State-specific rules vary—none favor “one-off” graves**

- In **Texas**, you can get an exemption if the **entire property** is used solely for burials, and **not for profit**. One grave on a residential lot doesn’t cut it citeturn0search1.
- Many states (including California) follow similar logic: exemptions apply only to **dedicated cemeteries**, not mixed-use land with one grave citeturn0search1turn0search2.

---

### 📜 3. **Why a backyard grave won’t stop tax liabilities**

- A **single burial** doesn’t convert your home into a legally recognized cemetery.  
- This means **property taxes still apply**, and the county can still assess, bill, and potentially place a lien or foreclose if taxes aren’t paid citeturn0search7turn0search12.

---

### ✅ Summary for California (Los Angeles, Mr. White)

- Burying a body in your backyard won’t make your land tax-exempt.
- To qualify, you'd need to convert the land into a **proper, exclusive cemetery**—with legal establishment, multiple graves, zoning approval, etc.
- That’s not a path most homeowners take just to dodge taxes.
