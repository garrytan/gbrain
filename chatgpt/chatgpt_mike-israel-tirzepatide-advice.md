# Training Frequency in Cutting: Dr. Mike Israetel’s Perspective

**Maintaining Muscle on a Cut:** Dr. Mike Israetel emphasizes that the *primary goal during a fat loss phase is muscle retention*. This means your resistance training should continue to provide enough stimulus to keep the muscle you built while bulking【28†L9-L16】【28†L29-L34】. The good news is you don’t need as much volume or frequency as you did to gain muscle – **maintenance volume** is considerably lower. In fact, research compiled by Dr. Israetel suggests you can maintain muscle with roughly one-third of the volume it took to build it【15†L162-L167】. For example, if you normally did 15 sets for a muscle group in a week, about 5 quality sets can suffice to maintain your size【15†L162-L167】. The key is that those sets are still challenging (close to failure or heavy) to send a strong “keep this muscle” signal【28†L31-L34】.

**6 Days vs 3 Days – Is Lower Frequency OK?** According to Dr. Israetel’s principles, *reducing training frequency* from 6 sessions per week to 3 per week during a cut is absolutely acceptable **if** you can still hit each muscle with sufficient weekly volume and intensity. Evidence shows that hypertrophy can be equally preserved as long as total work per week is similar, regardless of how you split it【17†L29-L36】. Dr. Israetel generally recommends training each major muscle at least twice weekly for growth, but maintenance can be achieved with lower frequencies when volume is adjusted accordingly【15†L162-L167】. For instance, instead of 6 shorter workouts, you might do 3 longer full-body (or upper/lower) sessions. What’s important is that your **weekly sets per muscle** remain at or above maintenance needs and you’re lifting heavy enough. He cautions never to *reduce training intensity* during a cut – you want to keep lifting as heavy (or near failure) as you safely can to preserve strength and muscle【28†L29-L34】. In practice, this means your 3-day program should still have you pushing hard on key lifts (squats, presses, pulls, etc.) even if you’re not in the gym as often.

**Volume and Recovery Trade-offs:** One reason dropping to 3 sessions per week can work well is that a calorie deficit limits your recovery capacity. Dr. Israetel notes that your maximum recoverable volume (**MRV**) is lower when cutting than when massing【43†L185-L193】. In his own contest prep, he reduces lifting volume compared to his bulking phase – a rule of thumb being ~4-6 fewer sets per bodypart per week than he could handle while bulking【43†L185-L193】. By training 3 days a week, you naturally moderate total volume and allow more rest days, which can prevent accumulated fatigue. High frequencies (5-6 days) with bulking-level volume in a deficit might push you into excessive fatigue, risking performance drops or muscle loss. Dr. Mike warns that when systemic fatigue is too high, catabolic processes can overtake anabolic ones, stalling progress or even causing muscle loss【31†L89-L97】. **Fewer sessions** can thus be a strategic move to manage fatigue, as long as those sessions are high-quality. He preaches “quality over quantity” when life stress or diet fatigue is elevated – better to do a bit less training but do it well, rather than crush yourself with junk volume【15†L101-L107】.

**Cognitive and Emotional Fatigue:** Beyond the physical recovery aspect, dropping from 6 to 3 workouts can *free up mental bandwidth*, which is a legitimate consideration during a cut. Dieting (especially hard cuts) is psychologically taxing – Dr. Israetel has shared that constant hunger is the **hardest part** of cutting for him, creating enormous stress over time【45†L239-L248】. If cutting back gym days helps you feel less overwhelmed and lets you focus on dialing in nutrition, sleep, and stress management, it can be well worth it. Remember, a 6-day bodybuilding split is not magic – it’s just one way to distribute volume. A 3-day plan can be equally effective for maintenance if programmed right. In fact, Dr. Mike often recommends “maintenance phases” where you intentionally train at minimum levels while prioritizing recovery or other goals【15†L101-L107】. Think of a cut as a quasi-maintenance phase for strength: you’re holding onto muscle, not trying to PR every week. As long as each muscle gets a stimulus at least once a week (ideally twice) and you maintain your strength levels, you won’t shrink. One evidence-based guide suggests **2-3 full-body strength sessions per week** is sufficient to preserve muscle during a moderate deficit【37†L247-L253】 – which aligns well with your proposed 3x/week schedule. Many people find they can keep their strength steady on such a routine, which is a great sign that muscle is being retained.

## Muscle Retention via High-Quality Hypertrophy Training

**Preserve Strength, Preserve Muscle:** A big part of “high-quality” training in a cut is maintaining your relative strength and effort in the gym. Dr. Israetel stresses not to let your training become easy just because you’re eating less – you should **train as hard as you can while dieting**【28†L29-L34】. In practice, this means using the same weights or rep ranges you did at the end of your bulk (if not trying to gradually progress them). If you can keep most of your lifting numbers close to pre-cut levels, that’s strong evidence you’re keeping your muscle. It’s normal that towards the end of a long cut you might back off big weights *slightly* to avoid injury (e.g. higher reps, a bit lighter loads when extremely lean)【28†L25-L33】, but the effort remains high. Dr. Mike’s advice: **don’t deliberately reduce intensity** – challenge yourself and aim to **beat reps or load** where possible, just as you would in a gain phase【28†L25-L34】. This approach signals your body to hold onto muscle fibers because they’re still needed for heavy work.

**Volume Matters – But More Isn’t Always Better:** To retain muscle, you do need enough volume (number of hard sets), but doing *extra* volume won’t necessarily help and can hurt if it causes excessive fatigue. The goal is to hit that “maintenance volume” sweet spot. As noted, maintenance volume is relatively low for trained muscles – approximately one-third of the volume used to grow【15†L162-L167】 – especially if intensity is maintained. Some coaches (e.g. Lyle McDonald) even suggest intermediate lifters might maintain on ~2/3 of their usual volume in a cut, while Dr. Mike’s experience is that ~**half to one-third** volume can suffice if done properly【15†L162-L167】【43†L185-L193】. That means if you were doing 20 sets/week for back on a bulk spread over 6 days, you could potentially drop to ~7-10 sets for back on your cut. Those could be split across 2 of your 3 weekly sessions (say 5 sets on Monday, 5 on Friday for back) and likely maintain size, given adequate effort. Lowering volume also helps ensure you can keep pushing heavy weights without burning out. In Dr. Mike’s own cutting routine write-up, he started with “moderate volume” training (~14 sets per bodypart/week) and only increased to ~18 sets later to keep progress going, noting he was careful not to exceed his reduced MRV on a deficit【43†L174-L183】【43†L185-L193】. The takeaway: **do enough, but not more than you can recover from**. Three days of lifting with ~10-15 hard sets per muscle per week (just an example) can absolutely maintain or even build muscle if you are below your genetic max – whereas six days of all-out training in a deficit might run you into the ground.

**Frequency vs. Volume Distribution:** Hypertrophy science finds that training frequency mainly helps you distribute volume; frequency itself (e.g. 3 days vs 6 days) is not an independent driver of muscle gain or retention when weekly volume is the same【17†L29-L36】. Dr. Israetel echoes this in his recommendations: you can train a muscle 2-4 times a week for growth, but doing so is chiefly about spreading sets out so you’re not doing 20 sets in one session【17†L39-L46】. If you condense your volume into fewer sessions, you may actually get *better* pumps or stimulus in a given workout (because of more exercises/sets in one go), but you’ll also accumulate more fatigue in that session. Higher frequency means each workout is smaller and perhaps you can lift slightly heavier each time, but you’re in the gym more days. There’s a personal adherence factor here: some people **enjoy** going to the gym almost daily; others get drained trying to juggle that with work, diet, life, etc. Dr. Mike recognizes this trade-off and encourages lifters to adjust frequency according to recovery and lifestyle. If hitting the gym 6 days is ruining your motivation or leaving you exhausted during a cut, scaling back to 3 or 4 days can improve your *consistency* and effort in those sessions. As long as you train each muscle at least ~1-2 times a week with sufficient volume, you will retain nearly all your muscle during a cut【37†L247-L253】. In fact, one **RP Strength** guide for fat loss explicitly advises **“at least two to three strength sessions per week”** as a baseline to keep metabolism up and muscle on【37†L247-L253】. That aligns perfectly with your plan of 3 sessions, giving confidence that it’s enough to preserve hypertrophy.

## GLP-1 Agonists (Tirzepatide) and Training Frequency Considerations

**Dr. Mike’s Take on GLP-1 for Fat Loss:** Dr. Israetel has spoken very favorably about GLP-1 receptor agonist drugs like semaglutide (Ozempic/Wegovy) and tirzepatide (Mounjaro) for weight loss. He rejects the notion that using such medication is “cheating” – noting that humans have always used tools to improve their lives, and this is no different【58†L1-L4】. The major benefit of these drugs is **massive appetite suppression**, which helps people sustain a calorie deficit with much less willpower. In Mike’s words, these medications can almost be considered a “health elixir” for those who are otherwise healthy enough to use them【20†L5-L13】. By curbing extreme hunger, GLP-1 agonists allow dieters to *free up willpower for other aspects of life* (career, family, or indeed, training quality) instead of constantly battling food cravings【58†L1-L4】. Dr. Mike experienced this first-hand: after a bodybuilding show, he started Ozempic and described the effect on his ravenous hunger as **“miraculous”**【50†L133-L141】. He found it dramatically reduced the suffering of dieting, to the point he could sleep normally and feel human again during a fat loss phase【50†L157-L165】【50†L162-L170】. This improvement in sleep and reduction in diet stress can indirectly help muscle retention – *sleep is a powerful factor for holding onto muscle mass* (poor sleep elevates muscle-wasting hormones), so anything that improves sleep during a cut is beneficial【50†L160-L168】.

**Muscle Loss and “Ozempic Butt”:** A common concern with GLP-1 weight loss drugs is that people might lose muscle along with fat. Dr. Israetel has addressed this, and he’s *skeptical that the drugs inherently cause muscle loss* beyond the fact that any weight loss can cause some lean tissue loss if you’re not careful【24†L215-L223】. He points out that if someone simply eats less (because the drug kills their appetite) and does **no resistance training**, they will indeed shed muscle – essentially crash-dieting with no muscle stimulus【51†L224-L232】. In that scenario, you might get the dreaded *“Ozempic ass”*, a term he quotes describing folks who diet away their glutes and end up with a flat butt【51†L224-L232】. But this is not a foregone conclusion of the drug – it’s the result of dieting without lifting. Dr. Mike’s stance is that **concurrent resistance training and high protein** can allow people on GLP-1 meds to retain muscle just fine【34†L169-L177】【24†L224-L232】. This is echoed by other experts and research: a 2022 clinical trial on tirzepatide (a GLP-1 agonist) found the ratio of fat lost to lean mass lost was similar to traditional diet interventions【33†L291-L299】. In other words, people don’t lose *more* muscle on GLP-1 meds than they would from any comparable amount of weight loss – and with proper training, they can minimize muscle loss. The Renaissance Periodization team strongly advises anyone using these drugs to **keep lifting and eat plenty of protein** (≥120 g/day) to safeguard muscle mass【34†L169-L177】. So, whether or not you use Mounjaro, the formula for muscle retention remains: **lift weights and eat protein**.

**Recovery and Training Adjustments on GLP-1:** If you are using a GLP-1 agonist during your cut, there aren’t huge changes to training frequency recommendations – you should still aim to train at least a couple of times per week. However, some practical considerations: these medications can cause gastrointestinal side effects (nausea, slower gastric emptying), so you might time your workouts and meals so you’re not working out on a very full stomach. You may find your overall energy is okay (since many users still have normal blood sugar and energy levels, just less hunger), but you must consciously **get enough nutrients**. With a blunted appetite, it’s easy to under-eat protein or carbs which could impair workout performance or recovery. Prioritize protein intake to hit that ~0.8–1 gram per pound of body weight if possible, as Dr. Mike recommends for anyone dieting【27†L114-L121】. In terms of fatigue, GLP-1 agonists can actually make the diet feel *easier* – less mental fatigue from hunger. Dr. Mike notes that extreme hunger is usually the limiting factor that makes people feel miserable and sleep deprived on a cut【50†L157-L165】【50†L162-L170】. By reducing that, the drug can leave you with **more energy to train** or at least not feeling drained by constant cravings. Some users report slightly reduced exercise capacity anecdotally, but others feel fine; there’s no strong evidence that GLP-1 meds inherently reduce muscular performance. In fact, by helping you lose weight, your relative strength (strength-to-bodyweight) might improve, and your joints experience less stress, which could make training feel smoother. As Mike alludes, these drugs don’t help you lose *more* fat than diet alone, but they make the process much more comfortable【50†L157-L165】【50†L168-L172】 – so you can adhere to your plan better. **Adherence** is ultimately the biggest factor in success: a 3x/week program you can stick to (thanks to manageable hunger and recovery) will beat a 6x/week plan that you burn out on.

**Bottom line:** Dr. Israetel is *supportive* of leveraging tools like tirzepatide to aid fat loss, as long as they’re combined with sound training. He has publicly said it’s “a huge revolution” for physique athletes because it mitigates the suffering of cutting【50†L168-L172】. Just remember that the drug itself doesn’t build or spare muscle – your training does. So, whether you train 3 days or 6 days, on or off medication, the principles of muscle retention are the same: hit your muscles with sufficient stimulus regularly, manage fatigue, and fuel/recover well. If using a GLP-1 frees up enough mental and physical energy that you can crush 3 solid workouts a week and stay in a caloric deficit, then it’s doing its job. Dr. Mike would far rather you diet with less hunger (and stress) and slightly lower training frequency, *than grind yourself into the ground* with over-training and willpower alone. As he puts it, relying on willpower for every aspect of weight loss is unnecessary when modern pharmacology can shoulder some of that load【27†L106-L114】. Freeing up that willpower means you can invest more effort into making those 3 training sessions truly high-quality – focusing on good form, progressive overload, and perhaps enjoying the process more, instead of feeling like an exhausted slave to a 6-day routine.

## Key Takeaways for Cutting Phase Training

- **Maintain Muscle with Less Volume:** You do **not** need to train 6x/week to preserve muscle during a cut. Muscle maintenance volume is much lower than growth volume (about one-third, in many cases), so you can reduce training frequency/volume and still keep your gains【15†L162-L167】. For example, dropping from 6 sessions to 3 sessions per week is fine – as long as those sessions collectively hit at least your maintenance number of hard sets for each muscle. Keep intensity high to compensate for fewer total sets【28†L31-L34】. 

- **Keep Lifting Heavy (Don’t “Coast”):** The stimulus to keep muscle is primarily heavy weight or high effort. Dr. Mike Israetel advises not to drop your lifting intensity when cutting【28†L31-L34】. Try to **maintain your strength** on big lifts and push near failure on your sets. This preserves the mechanical tension on muscle fibers that tells your body to hold onto them. If you maintain (or even slightly improve) your performance from your bulk, you can be confident you’re preserving muscle.

- **Adjust Volume to Recovery:** In a calorie deficit, your recovery ability is lower, so trying to do the same volume *and* frequency as your bulk can cause excessive fatigue【43†L185-L193】. It’s usually wise to do a bit less lifting volume (e.g.  ~20-30% fewer sets) than you did while massing【43†L185-L193】. Fewer weekly sessions naturally helps in this regard. Dr. Israetel notes that if life stress or diet fatigue is high, prioritizing **quality over quantity** is crucial【15†L101-L107】. Three focused workouts where you’re fresh will beat six mediocre, drained workouts. Pay attention to fatigue; if you’re feeling run-down, it’s better to back off volume or frequency rather than dig yourself into a hole【31†L85-L93】.

- **Hit Each Muscle 1-2x per Week:** With a 3-day routine, be sure your program’s structure still gives each muscle group at least one sufficiently large stimulus per week (ideally two). **Training each muscle twice a week** is a common recommendation for hypertrophy【17†L19-L27】, and it works for maintenance too, though some advanced lifters can maintain with even 1x/week if the session is intense enough. A full-body split three times a week or an upper/lower split with an extra day of overlap can ensure no muscle is neglected. Evidence indicates that total weekly sets are what count – whether you do them in 3 workouts or 6 – so choose the split that lets you perform best and stick to your diet【17†L29-L36】.

- **Monitor Strength and Muscle Signs:** Use performance as a gauge. If you’re losing a lot of reps or weight on the bar week to week, you might be cutting too hard or not providing enough stimulus. Ideally, you’ll **hold steady or even gain strength** in some lifts during a cut (particularly if you were well-trained, you might at least maintain). If you notice big drops, consider adding a bit more volume or carbs, or slowing the rate of weight loss. Slight strength loss can happen when very lean, but generally staying strong means muscle is safe.

- **High Protein & Recovery:** Ensure you keep protein intake high (Dr. Mike recommends ~1 g/lb body weight or at least 0.6–0.8 g/lb as a minimum【27†L114-L121】) to support muscle retention. Protein plus resistance training synergize to prevent muscle loss. Also prioritize sleep and stress management – these factors are often overlooked but can tip the balance in favor of muscle preservation. Dieting is a stress on the body; mitigating other stress (sleep, relaxation) helps recovery. Israetel specifically managed his meal timing in his cut to improve sleep, because being well-rested aids both fat loss and muscle retention【45†L239-L248】【45†L255-L264】.

- **GLP-1 Agonists Can Help Adherence:** Medications like **tirzepatide (Mounjaro)** or semaglutide (Ozempic) can make a cut much more tolerable by crushing hunger【50†L157-L165】. Dr. Mike Israetel considers these drugs a huge benefit for physique athletes cutting, mainly because they reduce the “mental anguish” of dieting and improve sleep by curbing late-night hunger【50†L160-L168】【50†L168-L172】. This can indirectly improve your training quality (you’re less distracted by hunger or fatigue). If using such a drug, take advantage of the freed-up *willpower* to put more focus into your training and recovery routines【58†L1-L4】.

- **Still Need to Train on GLP-1:** Fat-loss meds are **not a free ride** to slack off in the gym. You must continue robust resistance training to ensure the weight you lose is *fat* and not muscle【34†L169-L177】. As Dr. Israetel humorously warns, if you just eat way less on Ozempic and don’t lift, you’ll shrink your muscle too – the “Ozempic ass” phenomenon of losing muscle in your glutes, for example【51†L224-L232】. So, whether you feel super energetic or a bit sluggish on the drug, stick to your 3-day lifting plan. Two to three sessions a week of heavy lifting are non-negotiable to signal muscle retention【37†L247-L253】. The drug itself only helps control appetite; *you* are responsible for providing the growth/maintenance stimulus through training.

- **Listen to Your Body:** Lastly, remember that everyone’s response is individual. Some lifters thrive on 6-day programs even while cutting (usually those with higher work capacity or perhaps on performance enhancers or maintenance calories), but many do better scaling back. If dropping to 3 days keeps you progressing or maintaining and feeling less burned out, it’s likely the right call. You can always add a fourth day if you find 3 isn’t enough, but in most cases, three well-designed full-body or upper/lower sessions will **absolutely preserve hypertrophy** during a cut【37†L247-L253】. Stay consistent with your training, use the diet or medication to drive fat loss, and you’ll hit your goal of fat loss *with* muscle mass intact.
