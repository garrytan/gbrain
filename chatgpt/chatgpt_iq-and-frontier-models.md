## Assistant

Mr. White, yes, **IQ-equivalent numbers have been assigned to frontier models**, but they are **unofficial and weakly comparable** to human IQ.

Official labs generally publish **task benchmarks**, not IQ scores. OpenAI’s GPT-5.4 release reports things like GDPval, SWE-Bench Pro, OSWorld-Verified, Toolathlon, and BrowseComp; Google reports Gemini 3.1 Pro on reasoning/problem-solving benchmarks like ARC-AGI-2; Anthropic reports Claude Opus 4.6 across agentic coding, computer use, tool use, search, finance, and long-context tasks. Those are performance benchmarks, not psychometric IQ tests. citeturn201685search0turn538199view3turn538199view1

The best answer is: **we do not know the “real IQ” of current frontier models.** We have **IQ-style test scores**. TrackingAI runs AI models on the public Mensa Norway test and an “offline” test it describes as privately made and outside public training data; public reporting says GPT-5.4 Pro recently reached about **150** on the Mensa Norway-style public benchmark. Alan D. Thompson has also published a much more aggressive **195+ IQ-equivalent** estimate for 2025 frontier models. Treat both as “scores under a specific testing protocol,” rather than a model’s actual IQ. citeturn858634view0turn986615view1turn858634view2

Why the caveat matters: IQ tests are normed on humans, while LLMs are stochastic, prompt-sensitive, and trained on huge web corpora. Current LLM psychometrics lacks standardized test design and administration, and public benchmarks can suffer from training-data contamination, which can inflate scores. citeturn506603view2turn506603view1

**Practical interpretation:** on certain IQ-like pattern/reasoning tests, current frontier models are scoring in roughly the **130–150+ human-tail range**. Claims above that, especially **195+**, are extrapolations and should be treated as signaling “very strong on selected cognitive tasks,” rather than “this system has a human-like mind with IQ 195.”

### Human IQ spectrum

Modern IQ scales are usually centered at **100**, with a standard deviation of **15**. Roughly 68% of people fall from 85–115, 95% from 70–130, and 99.7% from 55–145. citeturn970741search2

Using the current world population of about **8.3 billion** as a rough base: citeturn752015search0

| IQ threshold | Approx. percentile | Approx. rarity | Approx. people above it globally |
|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| 85 | 16th percentile | bottom-normal boundary | ~7.0B above |
| 100 | 50th percentile | average | ~4.15B above |
| 115 | 84th percentile | top 16% | ~1.32B above |
| 130 | 97.7th percentile | ~1 in 44 | ~189M above |
| 145 | 99.87th percentile | ~1 in 741 | ~11.2M above |
| 150 | 99.96th percentile | ~1 in 2,331 | ~3.6M above |
| 160 | 99.997th percentile | ~1 in 31,600 | ~263k above |
| 175 | 99.99997th percentile | ~1 in 3.5M | ~2,400 above |
| 190 | ~99.9999999th percentile | ~1 in 1B | ~8 above |
| 195 | ~99.99999999th percentile | ~1 in 8.3B | ~1 above |

Above roughly **160**, the numbers become mostly statistical extrapolation. Most real IQ tests have ceilings and wider error bars at the extremes. So “IQ 150” is a meaningful high-tail comparison; “IQ 190+” is usually a model-based estimate rather than a cleanly measured human score.
