## Assistant

Mr. White, I’m not an attorney and this isn’t formal legal advice, but here are some general guidelines many creators follow to minimize takedowns when reacting to TV shows on Twitch:

1. **Keep it Transformative**  
   - Fair use in the U.S. hinges on whether your content is “transformative.” Provide substantial commentary, analysis, and critique. Pausing the show frequently and discussing what’s happening helps show that you’re not just rebroadcasting it.

2. **Limit How Much You Show**  
   - Avoid displaying long, unaltered segments. If you show extended clips without interruption, rightsholders are more likely to flag it. Short clips combined with frequent commentary is safer.

3. **Use Watch Party Features (If Available)**  
   - Some platforms (like Amazon Prime Video) allow Twitch watch parties. In those cases, viewers need access to the show themselves, and Twitch syncs your commentary. This can sidestep copyright issues if supported for the show you want to watch.

4. **Check the Rights First**  
   - Some companies allow (or even encourage) reaction content, while others are more aggressive about takedowns. Look for official statements from the show’s owner or see if they have a content usage policy.

5. **Use Overlays or Visual Alterations**  
   - Many creators shrink the show’s footage, add overlays, or blur it slightly. This alone won’t guarantee protection, but it makes it clearer you’re producing transformative content rather than simply re-streaming.

6. **Mute or Replace Audio When Not Commenting**  
   - Audio triggers a lot of automated takedowns. If you lower or periodically mute the TV show’s audio, it’s less likely for automated systems to match it.

In short, the more you transform, critique, and make your own commentary central—rather than simply airing the original show—the safer you’ll be from takedowns. If you’re concerned about specific legal nuances, it’s best to consult a lawyer familiar with entertainment and copyright law in California.
