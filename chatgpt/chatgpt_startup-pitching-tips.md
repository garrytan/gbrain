## Core principle
A pitch is a **clear explanation of what you do, who it’s for, and why it matters now**. Investors evaluate clarity and conviction more than polish.

## Seibel’s structure for a strong pitch

### 1. Problem (specific and real)
State a concrete pain point affecting a defined group.
- Avoid broad statements like “communication is broken”
- Example: “Small e-commerce brands lose 20% of revenue due to abandoned carts”

### 2. Solution (simple and obvious)
Explain your product in one sentence.
- If it takes more than 1–2 sentences, it’s not clear enough
- Show how it directly solves the problem

### 3. Why now
Explain timing.
- Market shift, tech change, regulation, or behavior change
- Investors want inevitability, not just possibility

### 4. Traction (proof)
This is where credibility comes from.
- Revenue, growth rate, users, retention
- Even small numbers matter if growth is strong

### 5. Market (realistic, not inflated)
- Define a reachable market first
- Avoid “$100B TAM” without a path

### 6. Business model
- How you make money
- Keep it simple and believable

### 7. Team
- Why you specifically can win
- Relevant experience > generic credentials

---

## Delivery rules Seibel emphasizes

- **Clarity over cleverness**  
  If a smart person can’t understand you quickly, the pitch fails.

- **Speed matters**  
  You should explain your startup in ~30–60 seconds.

- **No jargon**  
  Avoid buzzwords like “AI-powered platform” unless necessary and specific.

- **Be concrete**  
  Numbers, examples, and specifics beat abstractions.

- **Practice relentlessly**  
  A great pitch sounds natural but is heavily rehearsed.

---

## Example (tight version)

> “We help Shopify stores recover abandoned carts using personalized SMS. Brands typically lose 20% of revenue from drop-off. Our tool automates targeted follow-ups and has increased recovered revenue by 15% across 50 stores. We charge a monthly SaaS fee plus usage. E-commerce brands are shifting to direct-to-consumer, and SMS is outperforming email right now.”

---

## Common mistakes he calls out

- Starting with vision instead of problem  
- Overcomplicating the product  
- Hiding weak traction behind storytelling  
- Pitching features instead of outcomes  
- Sounding uncertain or rambling

## Seibel’s exact framing advice (from that video)

He repeatedly pushes founders toward this structure:

> **“We help [customer] do [thing] by [product], and it results in [measurable benefit].”**

Example in his style:
- “We help freelancers get paid faster by automating invoicing, increasing on-time payments by 40%.”

---

## What he criticizes (with contrast)

Weak version:
> “We’re a next-generation AI-powered engagement platform for SMBs”

Strong version:
> “We help small businesses get more repeat customers using automated text messages”

The difference is **zero ambiguity**.

---

## His key rule from that video

**If a smart 12-year-old can’t understand your pitch, it’s too complicated.**

That standard forces:
- no buzzwords  
- no abstraction  
- no multi-step explanations  

---

## Practical takeaway

A strong Seibel-style pitch can be reduced to:

1 sentence:
- What you do  
- Who it’s for  
- Why it matters  

Then expand only if asked.
