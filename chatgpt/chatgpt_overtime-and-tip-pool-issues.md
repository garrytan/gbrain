## 1. What might be illegal here

From what you said, I’m hearing at least two big red flags for a Los Angeles, CA worker:

### A. Unpaid overtime (last year)

In California (non-exempt employees):

- Overtime = **over 8 hours in a day** or **over 40 in a week**, plus extra rules for 7th day in a row, double time, etc.  
- Being “salary” or having a fancy title (like “manager”) **does not automatically make you exempt**. What matters is what you *actually do* at work and how much you earn.

So if:
- You were working more than 8 hours/day or 40/week,  
- And they didn’t pay you overtime last year,  
- And you’re really non-exempt (most bar/restaurant workers are),

then you may be owed **back overtime plus penalties and interest**.

California statute of limitations on unpaid wages/overtime is generally **3 years**, sometimes extended to **4 years** under certain claims, so last year is still very much in play.

---

### B. Tip pool (“tit-pull”) issues

It sounds like you’re talking about a **tip pool** at a bar/restaurant.

California’s basic rule:

- Tips belong to the employees, not the employer.
- The employer (and their “agents”) **can’t keep any part of the tips**.
- Tip pools are allowed, but **only among employees who are part of the chain of service** (servers, bartenders, bussers, etc.).
- Using a fake “manager” title or sticking actual managers into the tip pool so they effectively skim tips can be a problem, especially if that “manager” is acting on behalf of the company (hiring, firing, disciplining, setting schedules, etc.).

If you were:
- In a tip pool that was structured weirdly, or  
- Denied your fair share, or  
- Managers/owners were taking cuts they shouldn’t,

that can turn into claims for **unlawfully taken tips and related penalties**.

## 2. How to actually get this investigated (California / LA)

You basically have two main paths, and you can use both:

### Option 1 – File a wage claim with the CA Labor Commissioner (DLSE)

This is the “official government investigation” route.

What it does:
- You file a **wage claim** for unpaid overtime, unpaid wages, and possibly unlawful tip practices.
- The DLSE can:
  - Investigate,
  - Hold a conference/hearing,
  - Order the employer to pay back wages, interest, and some penalties if they find violations.

What you should gather before/while filing:

- Pay stubs (all of last year + this year)  
- Any time records you have (schedules, text messages, time-clock screenshots, photos of posted schedules, etc.)  
- Your job title(s) and what you actually did at work  
- Approximate hours per day / days per week you worked  
- Anything showing how the tip pool worked (written policy, messages, who was in it, how percentages were split)

Where to file (conceptually, not linking directly in case it changes):
- Look up **“California Labor Commissioner wage claim (DLSE Form 1)”**.  
- You can usually file **online, by mail, or in person**.  
- Choose categories like **unpaid overtime**, **unpaid wages**, and mention **tip pool / tips not properly distributed** in the explanation.

What to say in the description (rough idea you can adapt):
> I worked as [your role] at [business name] in Los Angeles, CA from [start date] to [end date or “present”]. During 2024 they did not pay overtime despite me regularly working more than 8 hours per day and/or 40 hours per week. In 2025 they started paying overtime, which suggests they knew the prior practice was wrong. I was also required to participate in a tip pool where [describe who was in it, especially if managers/owners got a share or it was unfair]. I believe I am owed unpaid overtime, possible penalties, and possibly improperly taken tips.

You **don’t** have to calculate everything perfectly beforehand. Give your best honest estimate; the DLSE will work with your info and employer’s records.
