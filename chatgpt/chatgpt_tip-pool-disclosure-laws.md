## Assistant

Mr. White — short answer: **California law doesn’t require employers to disclose the exact math/“formula” of a tip-pool to employees.** You can mandate a pool and choose the split, but you must follow the rules below.

# California & Los Angeles rules (employee-side)
- **Tip pools are lawful** if they’re *fair/reasonable* and exclude owners/managers; participants can include folks in the “chain of service” (servers, bartenders, food runners, some BOH). citeturn4search0turn4search4turn4search2  
- **Employers/agents may not keep any part of tips** and must pay credit-card tips in full (no processing-fee deductions). citeturn3search3turn5search2  
- **No statewide duty to reveal the pool’s formula** (e.g., points/percentages) to employees. If a business took the **federal tip credit** (rare/impermissible in CA), it **must notify employees of any required tip-pool contribution amount**—but CA doesn’t allow a tip credit, so this federal notice rule generally doesn’t apply here. citeturn0search12  
- **Pay stubs**: CA’s itemized wage-statement statute lists required items (gross/net, hours, rates, deductions) but **does not require showing the tip-pool math**. (Many employers still list tip lines for tax reporting.) citeturn5search0

# Customer-facing disclosure (service charges)
- As of **July 1, 2024**, CA’s “honest pricing” law (SB 478) — **as amended by SB 1524** — requires restaurants/bars that use mandatory fees to **clearly and conspicuously disclose the fee and its purpose on menus/price displays** (i.e., no hidden or drip pricing). This is disclosure to **customers**, not employees. citeturn1search0turn1search2turn1search3turn1search6  
- **LA City hotels**: any “service charge” must be **paid entirely to the hotel workers who performed the service** (and not to supervisors/managers), and payouts must match how the charge is described to customers. citeturn1search1

# Practical takeaways
- You can set a mandatory pool and choose the distribution, but keep it **reasonable**, **exclude managers**, **pay out on time**, and **document the policy** (who’s in, how it’s split), even though the formula itself isn’t legally required to be disclosed. The core legal risks come from **who shares** and **whether any “house cut” occurs**, not from failing to publish the math. citeturn4search0turn4search4
