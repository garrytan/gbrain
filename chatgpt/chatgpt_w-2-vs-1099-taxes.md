## Assistant

Got it, Mr. White — here’s how this works in your situation:  

---

### 💼 1. What changed between 1099 and W-2
When you were paid on a **1099**, you were considered **self-employed**.  
That means:
- You were responsible for both **income tax** and **self-employment tax** (15.3% for Social Security + Medicare).
- You could **deduct business expenses** and show losses to reduce taxable income.
- No taxes were withheld during the year — you handled them at tax time.

Now that you’re on a **W-2**:
- You’re legally treated as an **employee**.
- The employer must **withhold taxes** from each paycheck: federal income tax, California state tax, Social Security, and Medicare.
- You **can’t deduct** most work expenses anymore (unless you itemize and qualify under narrow rules).
- The 15.3% self-employment tax is split — you pay half (7.65%), and your employer pays half.

---

### 💰 2. Why your check shows 30% withheld
That’s a fairly common percentage for California service workers with fluctuating tips and no dependents on file.  
Your **W-4** determines the federal withholding, and your **DE-4** (California equivalent) sets your state withholding.  
If you didn’t specify dependents or additional allowances, the system defaults to higher withholding to avoid underpayment.

---

### 📅 3. What happens at tax time
If 30% is being withheld and your total yearly income isn’t extremely high, then **yes — you’ll likely get a refund**.  
Essentially:
- If they withhold **more** than your actual tax liability, you get the **difference refunded**.  
- If they withhold **less**, you’ll owe the IRS and California FTB.

Because you previously offset your 1099 income with losses, you might see that you’re **paying more in taxes now** — but that’s because:
- You can’t claim as many deductions as an employee.
- You’re no longer able to “zero out” your taxable income with unrelated business losses.

---

### ⚙️ 4. What you can do now
To reduce over-withholding:
1. Ask your employer for a **W-4** and **DE-4** form.
2. On those, you can:
   - Claim 1 or more **dependents** if you qualify.
   - Add “Exempt” **only if** you had no tax liability last year **and** expect none this year (rare case).
3. Use the **IRS withholding estimator** (on irs.gov) to fine-tune how much is taken out.

---

### 🧾 5. Quick example
Let’s say you earn $1,800/week × 52 weeks = ~$93,600 gross income.  
- As a **1099 contractor**, you could deduct expenses, but you also paid full self-employment tax (15.3%).  
- As a **W-2 employee**, you lose some deductions, but you only pay half that tax — and the rest comes out in regular withholding.  

So even though the paychecks *feel* smaller, your total year-end tax may not actually be much higher — and you might get a **big refund** if withholding stays at 30%.
