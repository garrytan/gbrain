I got a friend who owns a bar called St. Felix on Coanga Boulevard in Hollywood, and there's another bar next to it called Tribute, and there's like three or four other bars. Oval Room is one of them. There's a bunch of others, right? And my idea is for John to have a punch card that has St. Felix and then the other like five or six bars on Coanga Boulevard on that block, all on the punch card. And people come to St. Felix, get the punch card, get their first stamp on the punch card when they buy a drink at St. Felix, and then walk down the street to each one of the bars and get a punch card. When their card is completely punched, they come back to St. Felix and get a free shot. That's my idea, and I think that it would drive more sales to St. Felix and that everyone who saw them at all the other bars would come in, and I don't think that any other bars have a problem with it because we'd be driving traffic to those bars too. Tell me if this would work and if this is legal.

## Assistant

Mr. White — the idea **can work from a marketing standpoint**, but **as written it has real legal risk in California**. Here’s the clean breakdown.

---

## 1. Will it work commercially?
**Yes, conceptually.**  
What you’re proposing is essentially a **bar crawl incentive**. Similar promotions already exist and they do:

- Increase **foot traffic**
- Encourage **multiple stops**
- Anchor the experience around one “home base” bar (entity["local_business","St. Felix","Hollywood, Los Angeles, CA, US"])

From a pure sales/behavior angle, it’s sound.

---

## 2. Is it legal in California?
**Not as described.** The main issue is **California ABC (Alcoholic Beverage Control) law**.

### 🚫 The legal problems

### A. “Free alcohol” as a prize
California **generally prohibits giving away free alcoholic drinks** as part of a promotion that:
- Requires a **purchase**
- Encourages **consumption at multiple locations**
- Or functions as a **contest or inducement**

Your model =  
Buy drinks → collect stamps → receive **free shot**  
That’s a textbook **illegal inducement** under ABC rules.

---

### B. Tied-house & cross-promotion issues
If St. Felix is:
- Paying for cards
- Coordinating rewards
- Or acting as the “reward issuer”

…then ABC may view this as **improper cross-promotion** between separately licensed premises, especially if **ownership differs**.

Even if all bars agree, **consent does not override ABC restrictions**.

---

### C. Encouraging overconsumption
A crawl that **explicitly rewards visiting multiple bars** can be interpreted as promoting excessive drinking — something ABC scrutinizes hard.

---

## 3. How to make it legal (important)
You **can salvage this idea** with a few key changes.

### ✅ Legal-safe alternatives

**Option 1: Non-alcoholic reward**
- Completed card = **free merch**, food item, or discounted cover
- Example: T-shirt, hat, cocktail napkin set, or $5 off next visit

**Option 2: Discount, not free alcohol**
- “$5 off one drink” (not free)
- Discount must be **clearly limited** and **single-use**

**Option 3: St. Felix-only participation**
- Other bars are **optional stops** (no purchase required)
- Only St. Felix stamps count toward the reward  
  (This weakens the crawl, but lowers risk)

**Option 4: Third-party organizer**
- A neutral entity (not any bar) runs the crawl
- Bars opt in
- Rewards are **non-alcoholic**
This is how many legal bar crawls operate.

---

## 4. Practical reality
Bars do promotions like this **all the time**, and many technically violate ABC rules. Enforcement is:
- Complaint-driven
- Spotty
- But **painful if triggered** (fines, license risk)

If St. Felix is valuable to your friend, **don’t gamble the license** for a free-shot promo.

---

## Bottom line
- **Marketing idea:** Solid  
- **Legal as written:** ❌ No  
- **Fixable:** ✅ Yes, with non-alcohol or discount-based rewards
