Mr. White, the goal is extremely clear:

**Build a majority-controlled, default-alive company that reliably pays you $150k/year while preserving geographic freedom and no boss.**

That means the real KPI is probably **owner-controlled durable cash flow**, rather than growth for its own sake.

That clarifies what matters.

## 1. Translate the life goal into a business target

$150k/year salary is **$12,500/month** before taxes.

To safely support that, the company likely needs something closer to:

| Item | Monthly amount |
|---|---:|
| Founder salary | $12.5k |
| Payroll/tax/benefit load | ~$1k–$2k |
| Company operating costs | variable |
| Emergency/profit buffer | ~$5k–$10k |
| Minimum healthy company cash flow | **~$20k–$30k/month** |

So the real target may be:

> **Get Jovie to $25k/month in reliable net cash flow while you maintain majority control.**

That is much more concrete than “grow the company.”

## 2. Make revenue the default task

Your natural tendency is to drift toward comfortable work. That is normal. The fix is to remove discretion.

Every day should start with a forced question:

> **What is the highest-probability action I can take today that creates revenue, retains revenue, or shortens the path to revenue?**

Then do that before anything else.

The categories are simple:

1. **Sell** — outbound, demos, follow-ups, closing.
2. **Retain** — fix things that keep paying users active.
3. **Convert** — improve activation, onboarding, pricing, checkout, sales materials.
4. **Collect signal** — talk to users/prospects to find what makes them buy.
5. **Build only when tied to revenue** — features that unblock a sale, improve retention, or increase conversion.

Everything else is secondary.

## 3. Use a “KPI-first backlog”

Your backlog should have columns like this:

| Task | KPI moved | Expected impact | Confidence | Time cost | Score |
|---|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|

Score it roughly as:

> **Impact × Confidence ÷ Time Cost**

Then sort by score.

This prevents you from picking tasks because they are fun, elegant, technically interesting, or emotionally safe.

A good rule:

> If a task does not name the KPI it moves, it does not enter the weekly priority list.

## 4. Separate “operator work” from “builder work”

Founder brains often prefer building because building feels productive and avoids rejection. Revenue work has more emotional friction.

So split work into two modes:

**Operator mode:**  
Sales calls, outbound, follow-ups, customer interviews, pricing, retention, collections, onboarding.

**Builder mode:**  
Product, engineering, automation, systems, design, docs.

For your current goal, operator mode should dominate until revenue is stable.

A reasonable cadence:

| Time block | Focus |
|---|---|
| First 2–4 hours/day | Revenue work only |
| Later in day | Product/build work |
| End of day | Review KPI movement |

The key is doing uncomfortable revenue work before your brain gets to earn dopamine from comfortable work.

## 5. Give AI agents the boring, direct, measurable work

You are exactly right that agents can help because they do not have your avoidance patterns. The trick is to give them tasks with measurable outcomes.

Bad agent instruction:

> “Research ways to grow revenue.”

Better:

> “Find 50 companies matching this ICP, identify the right buyer, enrich contact info, draft a personalized outbound message, and rank them by likelihood to buy.”

Even better:

> “Every weekday, produce 25 qualified leads, 25 personalized first-touch emails, 10 follow-up emails for stale prospects, and a report showing replies, booked calls, and objections.”

Use agents for:

| Agent role | Output |
|---|---|
| Lead research agent | Qualified prospects |
| Outbound drafting agent | Personalized email/DM drafts |
| Follow-up agent | Re-engagement messages |
| CRM hygiene agent | Clean pipeline and next actions |
| Customer insight agent | Summaries of objections, feature requests, churn risks |
| Pricing/revenue agent | Analyze pricing, expansion, upsell opportunities |
| Competitor agent | Track positioning, pricing, and feature changes |

Give each agent a KPI. No vague “helpfulness.”

## 6. Create a weekly revenue operating system

Every Monday, decide:

**This week’s revenue KPI:**  
Example: “Book 10 qualified demos,” “Close $3k MRR,” “Reactivate 5 accounts,” “Get 20 buyers to complete onboarding.”

**This week’s top constraint:**  
Examples: lead volume, conversion rate, unclear ICP, weak onboarding, no urgency, pricing friction.

**This week’s top 3 bets:**  
Only three. More creates fake productivity.

Every Friday, review:

| Question | Answer |
|---|---|
| Did revenue go up? | Yes/no |
| Did pipeline go up? | Yes/no |
| What created actual movement? | Keep doing it |
| What felt productive but did nothing? | Kill or delegate |
| What was avoided? | Schedule first next week |

## 7. Use personal stakes as the filter

Your goal is emotionally powerful because it is specific:

You want freedom, control, stability, a better apartment, a dishwasher, no cockroaches, walkable gym, car insurance handled, and enough money to support a calm life with your girlfriend.

That should be written at the top of your operating doc.

Something like:

> **The mission is not prestige. The mission is freedom. A default-alive, majority-controlled company paying me $150k/year gives me control over my time, location, relationships, and lifestyle. Revenue is the path.**

Then when a task feels fun, ask:

> **Does this get me closer to the dishwasher apartment?**

That sounds funny, but it is useful. It converts abstract startup ambition into something concrete.

## 8. Install an anti-avoidance rule

Use this rule:

> **The task I least want to do is probably the one closest to revenue.**

That will often mean:
- follow up with a prospect,
- ask for money,
- ask why someone did not buy,
- raise pricing,
- make the sales page clearer,
- send outbound,
- call a customer,
- remove a feature that does not matter,
- look directly at weak metrics.

Make the uncomfortable thing the first work block of the day.

## 9. Track only a few numbers

For your current objective, I would track:

| Metric | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| Monthly recurring revenue or monthly gross revenue | Core progress |
| Net cash flow | Determines default-alive status |
| Pipeline value | Future revenue |
| Qualified demos/calls booked | Sales momentum |
| Conversion rate | Sales/product fit |
| Retention/churn | Durability |
| Founder salary coverage ratio | Progress toward your life goal |

Founder salary coverage ratio:

> **Current monthly distributable cash flow ÷ $12,500**

Examples:
- $2,500/month = 20%
- $6,250/month = 50%
- $12,500/month = 100%
- $25,000/month = stable with buffer

That number should be visible every day.

## 10. Make the company serve the life

The clean strategic frame:

> You are building a freedom business with startup upside.

That means you should prioritize:
- high-margin revenue,
- recurring revenue,
- low headcount,
- automation,
- majority control,

- simple operations,
- customers who pay reliably,
- products that do not require constant custom labor.

Avoid strategies that create dependency on:
- constant fundraising,
- large teams,

- investors controlling direction,
- a business model that requires you to live in meetings forever.
