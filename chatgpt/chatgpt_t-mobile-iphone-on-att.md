Mr. White — **yes, it is legal** for T-Mobile to **refuse to unlock a phone that is still being financed**, even if you plan to keep paying and keep the line active.

### ✔ Federal rules (U.S.)
- The FCC requires carriers to **unlock phones once they are fully paid off**, upon request.
- The FCC **does NOT require** carriers to unlock a device **before it is paid off**.
- Carriers are allowed to tie unlocking to completion of the financial obligation.

🔎 Source concept: FCC/CTIA consumer unlocking guidelines — carriers must unlock **after financial obligations are met**, but **they are not obligated to unlock early**.

### ✔ California law (Los Angeles)
- California does **not prohibit** a carrier from locking a financed device.
- The phone is considered **collateral for the loan/installment agreement**, so the lock is legally enforceable until payment is complete.

### ⚠ Why this is legally allowed
- A financed phone is **not technically fully owned by you** until the installment contract is completed.
- The carrier locking the phone is legally similar to holding a lien until the debt is satisfied.
