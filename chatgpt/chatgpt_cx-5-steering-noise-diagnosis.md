## Assistant

Mr. White, slow-speed squeaks on full-lock turns usually come from one of these:

- **Power-steering belt slippage**  
  - Worn or glazed serpentine belt (or weak tensioner) can “hold” a high-pitched note when the PS pump hits full pressure.  
  - **Severity:** Can lead to belt snap and sudden loss of assist—check fluid level and belt condition **ASAP**.

- **Power-steering pump whining**  
  - A worn pump bearing or internal vane wear will grow louder over time.  
  - **Severity:** Moderate; schedule a pump inspection soon to avoid leaks or total failure.

- **Steering-rack or subframe bushings**  
  - Rubber bushings can rub or deform under lateral load, especially when you let off the brake and the chassis shifts.  
  - **Severity:** Low-medium; noisy but won’t kill you—replace when they start cracking or if play appears in steering.

- **Control-arm / sway-bar bushings**  
  - Dry or hardened bushings squeak under chassis roll at slow speeds.  
  - **Severity:** Low; purely annoying until they tear, then you’ll feel clunks.

- **Strut-mount bearings**  
  - The top of the strut assembly can creak if the bearing wears, particularly at turn-in.  
  - **Severity:** Low; replacement only when noise persists or ride quality degrades.

- **(Unlikely) CV-joint noise**  
  - Inner joints at full lock sometimes groan, but they usually click under load—probably not your culprit here.  
  - **Severity:** If it were CV, you’d hear it on drive-away as well; still, watch for grease leaks or torn boots.

**Next steps:**  
1. **Check PS fluid level** (low fluid = belt slip).  
2. **Visually inspect the serpentine belt and tensioner** for glazing or cracks.  
3. If belt & fluid OK, have a tech grease or replace suspect bushings & mounts on your timeline—steering assist issues are the only ones that demand immediate attention.

## Assistant

Correct, Mr. White—you can DIY this or have your local shop (Intermechanic) handle it. Here’s how to check on your 2016 CX-5:

1. **Warm up & level**  
   • Drive a few minutes so fluid’s at operating temperature.  
   • Park on a flat surface, engine idling or turned off per Mazda’s spec (your owner’s manual will confirm).  

2. **Locate the reservoir**  
   • Under the hood, on the passenger side firewall, you’ll see a small translucent plastic tank with a yellow cap marked “POWER STEERING.”  

3. **Check level & condition**  
   • With the engine off (if that’s Mazda’s procedure), remove the cap/dipstick, wipe it clean, reinsert fully, then pull it out again.  
   • Fluid should sit between the “MIN” and “MAX” marks.  
   • Good fluid is clear to light amber. If it’s dark brown/black or has sludge, plan a flush.  

4. **Top off if low**  
   • Use **Mazda Genuine ATF M-V** (or equivalent Dexron-VI spec).  
   • Add slowly—overfilling can aerate the fluid.  

5. **Inspect for leaks**  
   • Look at hoses, fittings, and around the pump/rack for wet spots. Low level + no loose cap often means a leak.  

If it’s low or dark, top it off and re-check after a test drive. If it drops again or remains noisy, schedule a pump/belt inspection.
