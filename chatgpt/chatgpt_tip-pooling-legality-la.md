**1) Who’s in the pool**

Mr. White — short answer:

**“Averaging the week’s tips” is not expressly illegal in California,** but it **can become unlawful** if it pays people who **didn’t serve those customers** (i.e., outside the “chain of service”) or if **managers/supervisors** are included. California’s rule is that **gratuities are the sole property of the employees to whom they were left**, tip pools must be **fair/reasonable**, and **managers/supervisors may not share**—how you split must track the actual service provided. citeturn2search0turn2search2turn2search1

What that means for a weekly-average method:
- If a single **weekly pool** causes cross-subsidy between different **shows/shifts** (people get tips from patrons they never served), it **cuts against §351’s ownership rule** and DLSE’s “fair/reasonable” distribution guidance. The **safer/standard** approach is **per-shift (per-show) pools**, with only the staff who worked that show (and any documented prep reasonably allocated to that show). citeturn2search2  
- **Managers/supervisors** (anyone with hiring/firing/scheduling authority) **cannot** be in the pool under CA law—weekly or otherwise. citeturn2search1turn2search6  
- L.A. City/County don’t add extra pooling rules for non-hotels; their special **service-charge** rules apply to **hotels** only. citeturn6search0

**What to ask for to verify legality (fast):**

2) **Per-show payout reports**: show tips collected, participants, points × hours, and math. (FYI: starting **Jan 1, 2026**, California is adding **record-keeping/enforcement** teeth around gratuities—so clean documentation is becoming mandatory.) citeturn2search20

Bottom line: **Weekly averaging is only “legal” if it still pays the actual service team for the patrons who tipped and excludes managers.** In practice, a **per-show pool** is the compliant, defensible way to avoid §351 problems. citeturn2search0turn2search2
