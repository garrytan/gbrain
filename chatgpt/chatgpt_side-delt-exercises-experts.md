# Best Side Delt Exercises for Hypertrophy – Expert Recommendations

Developing the **lateral deltoids (side delts)** is key to achieving wide, “3D” shoulders. Below we compile top exercise picks and tips from three renowned hypertrophy experts – **Dr. Mike Israetel**, **Jared Feather**, and **Menno Henselmans** – focusing on lateral raise variations and their rationale. Each emphasizes slightly different nuances in biomechanics, resistance profiles, range of motion, and stimulus-to-fatigue ratio (SFR). Use these insights to optimize your shoulder training.

## Dr. Mike Israetel (Renaissance Periodization) – Top Side Delt Movements and Tips

- **Go-To Exercises:** Dr. Israetel often recommends *lateral raise variations* (cable or dumbbell) and *upright rows* as primary side delt builders. Cable lateral raises – especially the single-arm **behind-the-back cable lateral raise** – provide constant tension on the side delt through the full range, including the stretched bottom position【45†L77-L85】 (a key advantage over dumbbells, which have virtually no tension at the very bottom【30†L69-L77】). Dumbbell lateral raises are still effective for mid-to-top range tension, and machine lateral raise stations can be useful for stability and high-rep work. Upright rows (using a barbell, EZ-bar, or dumbbells) allow heavier loading to overload the side delts and traps, though form needs to be monitored closely.

- **Stimulus-to-Fatigue Ratio:** Side delt isolation exercises tend to have a **high SFR**, meaning you get a strong muscle stimulus with relatively low fatigue. Dr. Israetel notes that upright pressing movements often hammer the front delts, whereas lateral raises directly target the side delts with minimal systemic stress【42†L220-L228】【20†L33-L39】. Because lateral raises don’t heavily tax your overall recovery, you can do more total sets and train the side delts more frequently. In contrast, upright rows (a compound movement) generate more fatigue – they involve the traps, biceps, and lower back stabilizers – so they typically require longer rest between sets and careful monitoring of shoulder comfort. For example, **seated machine lateral raises** involve virtually no assisting muscles, so you could get away with very short rest periods, whereas heavy **barbell upright rows** might need ~2 minutes of rest due to greater cardiovascular and systemic fatigue【8†L25-L33】. Overall, Israetel considers lateral raises one of the highest-SFR exercises for shoulders, making them a staple for volume work.

- **Optimal Range of Motion:** When performing lateral delt exercises, more **range of motion** isn’t always better. Dr. Israetel advises raising your arms approximately to shoulder level (roughly 90° abduction) or a bit higher **only if it’s pain-free**【34†L228-L236】. Lifting higher can recruit more traps and potentially impinge the shoulder joint, so “as high as you can with **no pain**” is the rule【34†L228-L236】. In fact, he notes the **bottom half of the lateral raise** provides the most hypertrophic benefit to the side delts, since that’s where the muscle is under stretch and generating high tension【34†L230-L238】. Practically, this means you don’t need to swing the weights all the way up every rep – maintaining tension in the lower-to-mid portion can be highly effective. (Israetel even popularized a *“Super ROM lateral raise”* variation involving raising the arms above horizontal for a greater top contraction, but he acknowledges that a deep stretch is **more important** than an extreme top ROM for growth【30†L81-L87】.) For upright rows, he similarly suggests pulling until upper arms are about parallel to the floor and not much higher, to avoid shoulder strain. 

- **Form Cues:** To maximize side delt engagement, Israetel and the RP team emphasize a **controlled, strict form** on lateral raises. Keep your arms **mostly straight** (a slight elbow bend is fine but keep it consistent) and initiate the movement by lifting your elbows up and out, rather than swinging with your torso【16†L25-L33】. A classic cue is *“lead with the elbows”* – this helps ensure the side delt is doing the work of abducting the arm. Avoid generating momentum with your legs or lower back; the rep should be smooth on the way up and down. However, don’t overthink trying to “isolate” the delts to the point of tensing up – **it’s okay if the traps assist at the top end of the movement** as long as the side delts are the prime movers【20†L49-L57】. Israetel jokes that if you try too hard to not use your traps (shrugging, etc.), you’ll overly complicate the lift【20†L49-L57】. Instead, focus on feeling the side delts burn and let the upper traps stabilize naturally. Another RP tip is to consider a **thumbless (false) grip** on dumbbell or cable laterals to reduce gripping and forearm involvement – this can help some lifters better target the delts by not squeezing the handles too hard.

- **Reps and Loading:** Mike Israetel advocates training the side delts primarily in **moderate to high rep ranges**. Very heavy, low-rep work on lateral raises is neither safe nor effective – *“anything under 10 reps is some combination of dangerous and absurd for laterals”*【33†L13-L16】. Instead, he finds the **10–20 rep range** to be a sweet spot for most side delt work, offering the best trade-off between muscle stimulus, joint safety, and mind-muscle connection【34†L245-L254】. He also encourages including some even higher-rep sets (20–30 rep range) for side delts, as they often respond well to lighter loading and longer time under tension【34†L255-L263】. Lower reps (5–10) can be used occasionally (for example, on upright rows or machine presses) to provide variety and progressive overload, but they shouldn’t dominate your program for side delts【34†L255-L263】. If you do use heavy upright rows, keep the form strict and stay in a safe ROM. One way Israetel structures training is by **varying rep ranges across the week**: e.g., heavy 6–10 rep upright rows on one day, moderate 10–15 rep laterals on another, and high 20–25 rep laterals or cable raises on another【35†L282-L290】【35†L292-L300】. This hits different muscle fibers and resistance profiles while managing fatigue.

- **Volume & Frequency:** The side delts tend to have a **high recovery capacity**, so Dr. Israetel often programs them with high volume and frequency. Renaissance Periodization’s guidelines suggest that many individuals can start around ~6 sets per week (minimum for growth) and work up to ~20+ hard sets per week for side delts as needed【12†L7-L9】. In fact, advanced trainees specializing on delts may handle upwards of 30 weekly sets for side delts if spread over multiple sessions【12†L7-L9】. To accommodate this volume, training side delts **3–6 times per week** is common in Israetel’s approach【10†L185-L193】. He has noted that side delts recover **so quickly** that you could train them almost every session if you wanted, as long as you don’t exceed your recovery capacity in a single workout. For example, RP might have you perform some side delt work on **most days** in a push/pull split – since the muscle doesn’t get very sore or beaten up, it’s ready to go again after a day or two of rest【26†L1-L8】【26†L27-L30】. This high frequency strategy, combined with incremental increases in set volume (adding a set week to week), can yield rapid growth in the lateral delts. Just be mindful of overall shoulder fatigue and any joint warnings; if an exercise starts causing discomfort or stalls in progress, rotate to a different variation (RP suggests keeping 1–2 key side delt exercises in a mesocycle and swapping out if they get stale or irritating【10†L180-L188】【10†L201-L209】).

## Jared Feather (Renaissance Periodization) – Emphasize Frequency and Quality Reps

- **High-Frequency Focus:** Jared Feather – co-author with Dr. Israetel and an IFBB pro bodybuilder – is known for training his lateral delts with **high frequency**. He often hits side delts **3 to 4 times per week**, believing that smaller muscle groups recover fast and can be trained more often for maximal growth【26†L27-L30】. In his own routine, Jared might work lateral delts every other day, spreading the volume across sessions. This frequency ensures a constant growth stimulus and allows you to accumulate lots of weekly sets without trashing the delts in any one workout. (He’s noted that even 4x/week is usually well tolerated as long as soreness has subsided – “small muscles like delts and biceps can easily be hit 3–4 times a week” in his experience【26†L1-L8】.)

- **Exercise Selection:** Feather’s favorite side delt exercises mirror the RP list: he relies on **lateral raises** and **upright row variations**. Importantly, he varies the implement and intensity between sessions to avoid wear-and-tear and to train the delts through different strength curves. For example, across a week of training he might do: 
  - Day 1: **Heavy Barbell Upright Rows** (5–10 rep range) – a compound move to overload side delts and traps with a heavy weight【35†L282-L290】.  
  - Day 2: **Moderate Dumbbell Lateral Raises** (10–15 reps) – focusing on strict form and a deep burn in the side delts【35†L284-L292】.  
  - Day 3: **Moderate Dumbbell Upright Rows** (10–15 reps) – a slightly different angle than barbell, gentler on wrists/shoulders, still hitting side delts【35†L288-L296】.  
  - Day 4: **High-Rep Cable Laterals or “Thumbs-Down” Laterals** (20+ reps) – using a cable for constant tension or an internally rotated grip to finish the week with a pump-focused isolation【35†L292-L300】.  

  This is just one example, but it illustrates Jared’s approach: mix heavy and light, free weight and cable, to thoroughly fatigue the side delts without overstressing the joints【10†L186-L194】. He’s also a fan of the **leaning cable lateral raise** (holding a stable object with the off-hand and leaning away to increase the bottom stretch) as well as the **machine lateral raise** for high reps, especially when fatigue is high and you want to isolate the muscle safely.

- **Quality of Execution:** In terms of form, Jared Feather underscores the same key points as Israetel. He performs lateral raises with **strict technique** to ensure the delts are doing the work. One of his tips is to use a **thumbless grip** on lateral raises【23†L23-L26】. By not wrapping the thumb (for instance, holding a dumbbell more in the palm/fingers), you can take tension off the forearm flexors – this reduces the tendency to “pull” with your hands and helps you lift by *abducting the shoulder*. It’s a small tweak that some lifters find helpful for better mind-muscle connection. Jared also keeps a **slight bend in his elbows** and raises his upper arms to about shoulder height, very similar to the RP guidelines discussed above. He avoids any excessive swinging; if he wants to overload beyond strict form, instead of cheating, he’ll add techniques like **partials** (e.g. doing extra half-reps in the bottom range when he can no longer get to full height) or **drop sets** to extend a set – these intensification methods increase stimulus while keeping the movement targeted to the delts. 

- **Programming and Volume:** As a competitive bodybuilder, Jared often prioritizes side delts in his workouts. He sometimes even trains delts **before** heavy presses on upper body days to ensure they’re fresh and receiving maximum tension (not pre-fatigued by chest/triceps work). He’s noted that pre-exhausting side delts usually doesn’t hurt his pressing strength much, but **postponing delt isolation until after pressing can mean the delts are already tired and get less stimulus** – so he likes to flip the order when shoulder growth is a focus (this strategy is context-dependent, of course). Jared typically does around **3–5 sets per session** for side delts, especially when training them ~4x a week, totaling ~12–20 sets weekly. He advocates adjusting volume based on recovery: e.g., if you’re barely getting sore and still progressing, you can add a set here and there; if you start noticing joint aches or lingering fatigue, back off slightly. Since the side delts don’t produce big DOMS for most people, a key gauge of recovery is performance – as long as you’re able to hit your reps/weights in each session, the frequency and volume are manageable. Like Israetel, Jared finds side delts respond well to **high reps and short rests** at times, so he’ll use techniques like **myo-reps or rest-pause sets** on machine laterals or cable laterals to thoroughly exhaust the muscle without a huge time cost. The overarching theme is that lateral delts can take a beating and bounce back quickly, so Jared leverages that by training them often and with enough volume to force growth (while watching for any signs of overreaching).

## Menno Henselmans – Optimizing Lateral Raises for Maximum Side Delt Stimulus

- **Prioritize Lateral Raises (with a Lean):** Menno Henselmans favors lateral raise variations as the *best* movements for isolating the lateral delts. He often recommends performing them with slight technique adjustments to maximize tension on the target muscle. One of his top picks is the **“lean-in” cable lateral raise**, where you stand holding a cable (or dumbbell) and **lean your torso slightly toward the cable stack**. This position lets your arm start behind the body, maintaining tension on the side delt even at the bottom of the rep (when the delt is fully stretched). Menno stresses that training muscles at long lengths (i.e. under stretch) produces superior hypertrophy, and this lateral raise variant provides exactly that: *“there’s still tension in the bottom position”*, which can enhance stretch-mediated muscle growth【13†L109-L117】【13†L117-L125】. By contrast, a conventional dumbbell lateral raise done standing upright gives you a moment of zero tension at the bottom (weights directly under shoulder), which is a missed opportunity for growth. Menno’s goal with the lean or cable variation is to **keep constant tension** through the full range and especially to challenge the delt in the stretched position.

- **Body Posture and Fiber Alignment:** A unique cue Menno Henselmans emphasizes is to **bend forward (hinge at the hips) slightly when doing lateral raises**【42†L243-L251】【39†L1098-L1106】. The reason: leaning the torso forward shifts the exercise more toward the true lateral fibers of the deltoid. If you perform lateral raises perfectly upright (or with your arms a bit to the front, as many people unintentionally do), you end up recruiting a lot of anterior delt. Menno cites research showing that even during a “side” raise, the front deltoid can contribute **around 75%** of the force if your form isn’t optimized【42†L251-L258】. To counter this, he instructs trainees to *“really focus on those lateral fibers”* by tilting forward and **moving the arms directly out to the sides**, not in front of the body【39†L1098-L1106】【40†L1129-L1137】. You should feel your side and even rear-deltoid region doing the work, rather than the front of your shoulder. An easy way to check: in a mirror, the dumbbells (or cables) should travel in a plane roughly even with your shoulder joint or slightly behind, not out in front of you. Menno often says **“shoulder abduction”** (arms out to sides) is the movement we want – if your elbows drift forward, you’re mixing in shoulder flexion (front delt action) which we want to minimize for side delt isolation.

- **Hand and Arm Position:** Henselmans suggests a couple of tweaks in arm position to further enhance side delt activation. First, he recommends keeping the arms fairly straight or only a gentle elbow bend. Many lifters bend their elbows a lot during lateral raises (sometimes to 90°), essentially turning it into a sort of “chicken wing” lateral. Menno notes that a big elbow bend **shortens the lever arm** and can shift emphasis away from the delts (while also usually causing the dumbbell to track forward)【39†L1090-L1099】【39†L1098-L1106】. Instead, a longer arm (just a soft bend) makes the side delt work harder through a larger radius. Second, he often has clients perform raises with a **“thumbs-down” or internally rotated grip** (imagine pouring water out of a jug at the top of the rep). By internally rotating the shoulder slightly, you potentially reduce assistance from the front delt and put the side delt in a more direct line of pull. He advises not to overdo this (extreme internal rotation can impinge the shoulder), but a slight tilt such that your thumb points downward at the top can help some people feel their side delts more【40†L1132-L1140】. If you try the thumbs-down raise, keep the raise to about shoulder height and make sure it feels comfortable on your shoulder joint【40†L1132-L1140】. Menno sometimes calls this the “**thumbs-down lateral raise**,” akin to the old Arnold cue of “pour the pitcher” – useful as long as it’s pain-free. 

- **Use Full Range and Stretch Reflex:** Menno is a big proponent of using a **full range of motion** *and* leveraging the stretch at the bottom of exercises. For lateral delts, that means he encourages lowering the weight down until the delt is fully stretched **but not relaxing at the bottom**. In practice, you should **not let the dumbbells touch your sides or go slack between reps**. Maintain tension and then explode into the next rep. In fact, Henselmans explains that using a bit of speed on the bottom turnaround can be beneficial: a quicker eccentric and a brief **stretch reflex** can amplify the tension on the muscle at its longest length【13†L117-L125】. He points out that if you always slow down and pause at the bottom, you lose the stretch reflex and reduce the forces right where the muscle is maximally lengthened【13†L109-L117】【13†L117-L125】. By contrast, lowering the weight a bit faster (under control) and then **“catching” it and reversing direction** creates a powerful stimulus – the muscle has to decelerate the weight and re-accelerate it, which spikes tension. So Menno’s advice: *don’t bounce uncontrollably*, but do **use a fluid, continuous motion** on lateral raises. Feel a slight stretch at the bottom and immediately go into the next rep – this keeps the side delt under tension and potentially increases hypertrophy signaling.

- **Machines and Presses:** Henselmans is somewhat **skeptical of machines** for side delts. He argues that the shoulder is a very mobile joint and fixed-path machines (whether a lateral raise machine or certain shoulder press machines) might force a groove that isn’t ideal for everyone【42†L235-L243】. Many shoulder machines also seat you upright, which conflicts with his cue to lean forward. If a machine’s pads/handles don’t line up with your body, you could end up training a hybrid movement (e.g., a machine shoulder press that turns into more of an incline press, missing the side delts【2†L93-L101】). Thus, Menno generally prefers **free weights and cables** that allow you to adjust your body position. He notes that an overhead press performed with a **wide grip and elbows flared** can hit the side delts fairly well【42†L231-L239】【40†L1169-L1177】 – for instance, a behind-the-neck press or a very high incline dumbbell press will involve more lateral delt than a narrow-grip press. However, he usually programs presses primarily for overall shoulder development (and because front delts and traps will assist), while **lateral raises remain the cornerstone** for isolating the lateral deltoid. In short, Menno’s approach is: use dumbbells or cables to *abduct the shoulder joint directly against resistance*, and avoid any apparatus that limits that motion or brings in excessive front delt. The only machine he’s noted using for delts occasionally is a reverse pec deck or cable setup for rear delts – but for side delts, he sticks to variations of raises.

- **Volume and Frequency:** In Menno Henselmans’ programming, the **lateral delts get a lot of love**. He often says to **avoid front delt exercises entirely** (like front raises) because front delts are already hit hard in all pressing movements【42†L221-L229】. That frees up more of your “shoulder workout” to allocate to side and rear delts. Menno mentions that many people’s front delts are *overdeveloped* simply due to bench presses, overhead presses, etc., while their side delts lag from lack of direct work【42†L227-L235】【42†L229-L237】. To remedy this, he programs **multiple lateral raise variations and rear delt movements** in a given routine. For example, a push/pull split might include lateral raises on push days and some side delt isolation on pull or leg days as well (since they recover fast). While Menno doesn’t often publicize exact set numbers, he implies doing *“a lot of lateral raises”* to bring up your shoulders【42†L229-L237】. It’s not uncommon in his programs to see 4–6 sets of lateral raises in a session, possibly split between two variations (e.g., 3 sets leaning cable laterals + 3 sets dumbbell laterals). Combined with another day of shoulder work, the weekly set count for side delts can easily reach the mid-teens or higher. He also favors training delts with moderate frequency – **at least 2x per week** and often more. Since he often employs an upper/lower or full-body training structure, side delts might get stimulus every 48 hours or so. This aligns with his view (similar to Israetel’s) that the side delt recovers quickly and rarely gets **sore** enough to require long breaks. Essentially, Menno’s volume strategy is to almost **overload the side delts with attention**: he programs *“almost all of the deltoid work focused on the lateral and rear deltoids”*, letting the front delts grow on autopilot【42†L255-L263】【42†L257-L265】. This specialization approach ensures the limiting factor in shoulder appearance (usually side delt width) gets the most stimulus. Just as with the other experts, Menno will adjust volume up or down based on recovery – but the take-home message is that most people likely need to do **more side delt work** (and relatively less front delt work) than they currently do, to achieve that round capped-shoulder look.

## Comparison of Recommendations

Despite some differences in approach, **Israetel, Feather, and Henselmans all agree on core principles** for side delt hypertrophy:

- **Lateral Raises are King:** All three experts prioritize some form of lateral raise as the top side delt exercise. They downplay or outright avoid front delt isolation, channeling most of their effort into side delt movements【42†L221-L229】【20†L33-L39】. Whether it’s a dumbbell lateral raise, cable lateral raise, or machine lateral, this exercise (and its variants) is the common cornerstone for building wider shoulders.

- **Emphasize the Stretched Position:** A recurring theme is maximizing tension where the lateral delt is stretched (arm down by your side). Israetel notes the bottom half of the lateral raise is most growth-promoting【34†L228-L236】, and Henselmans specifically tweaks form (leaning in/forward) to ensure continuous tension at the bottom【13†L117-L125】. All suggest **not relaxing at the bottom** of a rep – maintain tension and use a full range of motion that keeps the muscle working.

- **Upright Rows – Useful but With Caveats:** Dr. Mike and Jared Feather often include upright rows as a heavy compound complement to raises【10†L186-L194】. They allow more load on the delts but also involve traps and can stress the joints if done carelessly. Menno is more cautious, preferring pure abduction exercises; he notes many people shrug during upright rows or use a grip that turns it into a trap exercise【18†L9-L17】. All would agree that if upright rows are used, one should **use a shoulder-width or wider grip, raise elbows only to shoulder height, and avoid any painful range**【18†L29-L33】【34†L228-L236】. When done correctly, upright rows can effectively target side delts (and Menno acknowledges behind-the-neck or elbows-out presses hit side delts similarly【42†L231-L239】), but lateral raises remain the more isolated approach.

- **High Frequency & Volume:** There’s consensus that side delts can handle a lot of volume and frequent training. Feather and Israetel explicitly train them 3–6 days a week with dozens of weekly sets spread out【26†L27-L30】【35†L284-L292】. Henselmans likewise favors putting **most of your shoulder volume** into side delts across multiple sessions【42†L255-L263】. In practical terms, you might see side delts directly worked on two upper body days and perhaps even as “mini sessions” at the end of other workouts. All stress monitoring recovery, but since side delts recover quickly (often no significant soreness), the limiting factor is usually cumulative fatigue – which they mitigate by rotating exercises, rep ranges, and keeping quality high.

- **Form Agreement:** The experts agree on performing lateral raises with **good form and mind-muscle connection**. They all instruct to lift with the shoulder (not heaving with legs/back), keep the motion primarily in the shoulder joint (minimal elbow motion except a fixed slight bend), and avoid any posture that overly brings in the front delt. Menno is most strict about leaning forward and not letting the front delt work【42†L243-L251】, whereas Mike and Jared are a bit more relaxed about slight cheat or trap involvement as long as the side delt is being overloaded【20†L49-L57】. But none of them advocate swinging heavy weights up – all prefer using an appropriate load that allows the side delts to truly fatigue. Small tweaks like grip (thumbless grip, internal rotation) are individual preferences they’ve mentioned to enhance focus on the delts【23†L23-L26】【40†L1132-L1140】.

In summary, **Dr. Israetel and Jared Feather** (of RP) bring a methodical, high-volume approach: combining cable or dumbbell laterals (for high SFR) with occasional heavy upright rows, and ramping up frequency to get those “capped” delts. **Menno Henselmans** similarly doubles down on lateral raises, fine-tuning the biomechanics (torso angle, arm path) to truly isolate the lateral head, and favors constant tension techniques to maximize hypertrophy. By applying the shared principles from these experts – *focus on lateral raises, emphasize the stretch, train the muscle often with enough volume, and execute with proper form* – you’ll be following an evidence-based path to bigger, broader side delts.
