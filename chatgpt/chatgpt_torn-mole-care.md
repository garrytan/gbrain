Raise mole got caught on something is half hanging off. what to do

---

## Assistant

If a raised mole is partially torn or hanging off, here’s what you should do:

### **Immediate steps:**
1. **Do not pull it off.** This can increase bleeding and infection risk.
2. **Clean the area** gently with soap and water.
3. **Apply pressure** with sterile gauze if it's bleeding.
4. **Cover it** with a clean bandage.
5. **Avoid touching or irritating it further.**

### **Next steps:**
- **See a dermatologist or urgent care ASAP.** Torn moles need to be evaluated to rule out:
  - Infection risk
  - Incomplete removal
  - Atypical or malignant features

A doctor may:
- Remove the rest cleanly
- Send it for biopsy (especially if it’s new, changed, or looks irregular)
- Prescribe antibiotics if infected

### **Don’t:**
- Use scissors or razors at home
- Apply alcohol or hydrogen peroxide aggressively (can slow healing)

If it's after-hours and not bleeding much, bandage it and call for an appointment first thing.

What will a doctor do

---

## Assistant

Here’s what a doctor will typically do when you come in with a torn or half-hanging mole:

### 1. **Examine the Mole**
- They’ll inspect size, color, symmetry, and border to assess for signs of skin cancer.
- They may ask if it’s changed recently or if you’ve had issues with it before.

### 2. **Clean and Numb the Area**
- Antiseptic is used to clean the site.
- A local anesthetic (like lidocaine) will be injected to numb it.

### 3. **Remove the Mole**
- **If it’s hanging:** They’ll shave or excise the remaining tissue.
- **If it’s suspicious:** They’ll remove the whole mole with a margin of healthy tissue.

### 4. **Send it for Biopsy**
- If the mole looks atypical or you’ve never had it checked, they’ll send it to a lab for pathology.

### 5. **Bandage and Aftercare**
- You’ll get wound care instructions (usually antibiotic ointment and bandages for a few days).
- Sutures may be used if it’s a full excision with stitches.

### 6. **Follow-Up**
- If pathology shows anything abnormal (e.g., dysplasia, melanoma), they’ll contact you for next steps.

Quick, clean, and often painless once numb.

## User

If they remove it will it scar

---

## Assistant

Yes, it will likely scar — but the **size and visibility** of the scar depend on:

### **1. Type of Removal**
- **Shave removal:** Minimal scarring, usually flat and lighter or darker than surrounding skin.
- **Excision with stitches:** Higher chance of a visible line-shaped scar.

### **2. Location**
- Areas with thin or mobile skin (like chest, back, shoulders) tend to scar more.
- Areas with good blood flow (like the face) often heal better.

### **3. Aftercare**
- Keep it clean and moist (e.g., with petroleum jelly).
- Avoid sun exposure — UV darkens scars.
- Use silicone sheets or scar gel if it’s a concern.

### **Bottom line:**
Yes, a scar is almost certain, but **most are small and fade well** over time, especially with proper care. A dermatologist will aim to make it as minimal as possible.

## User

So should i have a plastic surgeon remove it

---

## Assistant

If the mole is in a **high-visibility area** (face, neck, etc.) and you're **concerned about scarring**, yes — a plastic surgeon is a smart choice. They specialize in:
- Minimizing scarring
- Aligning incisions with natural skin lines
- Using advanced closure techniques

### Otherwise:
For most body locations (like back, arms, legs), a **dermatologist** can remove it effectively, and scarring is usually minor.

### Best approach:
- **See a dermatologist first.** If the mole is suspicious, they’ll prioritize biopsy over cosmetics.
- **If it’s benign and you care about appearance**, ask for a plastic surgeon referral.

Don’t delay treatment just for cosmetic reasons if the mole is damaged or bleeding. Get it looked at quickly.

## Assistant

In **Los Angeles**, whether you need a referral depends on your insurance:

### **If you have PPO insurance:**
- **No referral needed.** You can book directly with a plastic surgeon or dermatologist.

### **If you have HMO insurance:**
- **Yes, you’ll need a referral** from your primary care physician (PCP) to see a specialist.

### **If you’re paying cash:**
- **No referral required** — you can go directly to anyone.

If it’s urgent or bleeding, you can also go to **urgent care**, and they may refer you to a dermatologist or surgeon on the spot.
