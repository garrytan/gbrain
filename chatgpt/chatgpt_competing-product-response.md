## Assistant

Mr. White, the clean move is this:

Treat them as a competitor, not a collaborator.

You are not shooting yourself in the foot by looking at a public product. That is normal market research. The risk starts when you move from “customer evaluating a product” into “peer founder sharing roadmap, strategy, and detailed feedback.”

So the immediate decision is:

**Best default:** politely decline the free access and keep it warm.  
That preserves the relationship, avoids weirdness, and keeps your head clear.

Send this:

“Appreciate it. It looks well done. I’m pretty heads down right now, so I’m probably not the right person to test it thoughtfully, but wishing you guys well with it.”

If you want to leave the door open:

“Appreciate it. It looks well done. I’m pretty heads down right now, so I’m probably not the right person to test it thoughtfully, but wishing you guys well with it. If timing opens up later I’ll reach back out.”

If you actually want the market intel, do it with boundaries:

“Thanks, I appreciate that. I may take a look from a user perspective. Just flagging that I’m heads down building in this space, so I’m probably not the best person for detailed product feedback.”

Rules if you do check it out:
- Use it like any normal customer.
- Do not share your roadmap.
- Do not give strategic feedback.
- Do not join calls about how they built it.
- Do not accept any NDA or special access.
- Do not use their private materials.

On the co-founder rabbit hole: ignore it. This interaction is nowhere near enough to justify thinking about that.

The fastest action is the polite decline. That is the least mentally expensive move and keeps your position clean.
