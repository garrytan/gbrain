# Origin of term 86

## User

Where is the term 86 originate

---

## Assistant

The exact origin isn’t nailed down, but “86” has a few strong theories from U.S. restaurant and bar slang in the early 1900s:

- **Prohibition–era bars:** Bartenders would yell “86 it” when a liquor ran out or when they needed a troublesome customer removed.  
- **Chumley’s, NYC:** The speakeasy’s back door was at 86 Bedford Street. Staff supposedly told patrons to “86” (use the 86 Bedford exit) when police raided.  
- **Diner lingo:** Soda-jerks and short-order cooks used numbers for menu items; 86 meant “out of it” or “don’t serve.”  

By the 1930s it meant “cut off, cancel, or remove,” which is why restaurants today say “86 the salmon” when an item is sold out.

## User

But two of those don’t explain where it came from

---

## Assistant

Correct—most “origin stories” are guesses layered on top of each other.  
Here’s what’s solid and what’s not:

**What actually has evidence**  
- **Diner/short-order slang**: 1920s–30s soda-jerk manuals list “86” as “out of” or “don’t serve.” This shows up in print decades before the speakeasy tales, making it the only firmly documented root.

**What’s likely folklore**  
- **Chumley’s 86 Bedford Street**: First mentioned long after the fact. No contemporary records tie the number to that door during Prohibition.  
- **Prohibition bartenders**: Same problem—no period sources using 86 this way.

So the clean answer: *86* comes from early-20th-century diner and soda-fountain shorthand for “no longer available,” and later got applied to kicking out customers or canceling things. The speakeasy stories are colorful but unsupported.
