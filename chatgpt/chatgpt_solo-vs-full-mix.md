## Assistant

Yes. A few related concepts:

- **Incubation effect** — the strongest match for what you're describing. You work on a problem, stop thinking about it, and the solution appears later while doing something else. This is well-documented in creativity research.

- **Unconscious thought theory** — the idea that some processing continues outside conscious awareness after you stop actively working on a problem.

- **The Zeigarnik effect** — unfinished tasks stay active in your mind, which can contribute to insights surfacing later. Not exactly the same thing, but related.

- **Paradoxical intention** — when trying hard to make something happen actually makes it less likely (commonly discussed in anxiety and sleep psychology).

- **Ironic process theory** — often called the "white bear problem." Trying not to think about something can make you think about it more. Closely related to why forcing an insight often prevents it.

For your tweet, I'd probably reference **the incubation effect**:

> Psychologists call it the incubation effect. You work on a problem, walk away, and your brain keeps processing it in the background. The insight shows up later—usually in the shower, on a walk, or halfway through a workout.

That's essentially the scientific version of your "toggle between solo and full mix" analogy.
