# Worst case scenario parking

## User

If you ran the gate, tailgated a car out of the parking garage, and your bill for the parking was $1, what theoretically is the worst that could happen? Like, what, did they just sue you? Like, they send you a bill, you refuse to pay, they sue you for it, and then the court denies the lawsuit because you can't sue someone for under $20?

---

## Assistant

Short version: yes, what you did *can* technically be a crime / civil violation even if the fee is $1, and no, there is **no “under $20 you can’t sue” rule.**

Let’s break it down in real-world terms.

---

## 1. What laws could this touch?

You’re in California (Los Angeles), so:

### Criminal side (theoretical worst case)

Depending on the DA / city prosecutor and the facts, they could try to frame it as:

- **Theft of services / petty theft** – you intentionally avoided paying a required fee.
- **Trespass** – entering / remaining in a controlled area without following the conditions of entry (payment).
- **Vandalism / property damage** – if you actually broke or damaged the gate, now you’re in a totally different world, because the damage can be **hundreds or thousands** of dollars even if the parking fee was $1.

For a $1 avoided fee with no damage, it’s very unlikely anyone is going to push criminal charges. But “theoretical worst case” is:

- Police are called.
- You’re cited or arrested for a misdemeanor (like petty theft).
- You deal with court, a record, fines, possibly paying restitution and extra penalties.

That’s the *legal* max, not the *probable* outcome.

---

## 2. Civil side: suing you

There is **no rule** that you can’t be sued for under $20. That’s just wrong.

- In **California small claims court**, there’s a **maximum**, not a meaningful minimum.
- People absolutely sue over small amounts; it’s just usually not worth the time and filing fee, so they don’t bother for a dollar.

The garage operator could, in theory:

- **Send you a bill** (original fee + admin fees).
- **Send you to collections** if they have your info (plate + DMV lookups, or account data).
- **Ban you from the property** and trespass you next time.
- **Sue you**, even if it’s stupid over $1, especially if they’re really going after you for gate damage, repeated offenses, or a contractual penalty clause (some garages have posted “$X penalty for tailgating / lost ticket,” etc.).

Courts don’t throw it out just because the amount is small. They might be annoyed, but it’s not “under $20, case dismissed.”

---

## 3. Practical “worst realistic case”

Remove the Hollywood drama and think practical:

If there’s **no damage** and this is a **one-off**:

- Most likely:  
  - They do nothing, or  
  - They send a bill / nasty letter if they can identify you (plate cameras are common).
- A bit more aggressive:
  - They tack on “administrative penalties” and send to collections.
  - They ban your plate from future entry; gates don’t open for your car.
- Very aggressive plus bad luck:
  - They file a small claims case, especially if they claim more than $1 (penalties, admin costs, whatever).
  - You waste time in court and possibly owe a small amount + costs.

If there **was damage to the gate**, *that* is the real nightmare:

- Repair costs easily in the **hundreds to thousands**.
- They can sue you for that full amount.
- Their insurer might pay and then come after you (“subrogation”).
- Now a prosecutor actually cares: thousands in damage + intentional act looks a lot more like vandalism + theft of services.

---

## 4. Federal vs state confusion

You’re probably thinking of:

- **Federal civil cases** (like diversity jurisdiction), which usually require at least **$75,000 in controversy**.
- That has **nothing to do** with:
  - Local parking,
  - State small claims courts,
  - Whether a private operator can sue you for a tiny amount.

Your parking dispute would be in **state court**, not federal, so the federal thresholds don’t save you.

---

## 5. Real answer to “what’s the actual risk here?”

For a $1 underpayment, one-off, no damage:

- Realistically: embarrassment, maybe a bill, maybe being banned or hassled next time.
- Legally possible: civil claim, collections, technically even a misdemeanor case.
- But the **“you can’t sue for under $20” idea is pure myth.**

If you’re worried about this specific incident and they can identify you, the most adult, low-drama move is:  
pay what you owe if they ask, don’t argue over a dollar, and don’t repeat the behavior.

I’m not your lawyer, and this isn’t legal advice, but that’s the honest outline of the “worst case” versus what usually happens.
