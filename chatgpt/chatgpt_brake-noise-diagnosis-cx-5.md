---

### 1. **ABS Activation (Most Likely)**
- When you brake hard, especially downhill, your **ABS (anti-lock braking system)** kicks in.  
- The ABS modulator pulses the brake pressure rapidly (you’d feel it in the pedal as vibration).  
- That pulsing can cause a **light metallic chatter or clinking** from the calipers or brake lines.  
- The sound matches your description: quick, high-pitched, metallic, not a thump, only under hard braking.

---

### 2. **Brake Pad Hardware / Anti-Rattle Clips**
- If the **anti-rattle clips** or pad retaining hardware are slightly loose or worn, pads can vibrate under high braking load.  
- That vibration produces a metallic “clink-clink-clink” sound, especially downhill when more weight shifts forward.  
- Usually light and higher-pitched—exactly as you described.

---

### 3. **Heat Shield or Exhaust Shield**
- A thin **metal heat shield** around the exhaust or catalytic converter can rattle when the car vibrates hard.  
- Hard braking downhill shifts and jolts things—if a shield is slightly loose, it can buzz or “t-t-t-t” briefly.

---

### 4. **Suspension / Strut Mounts**
- Less likely since your sound is so high-frequency, but worn **strut mounts or sway bar links** can clink under sudden load transfer.  
- These usually make a deeper, more “clunk” sound though.

---

✅ **Most Probable Diagnosis**: ABS engaging + slight brake hardware vibration.  
If you mostly hear it **only on hard braking downhill** and it’s light/metallic, it’s almost certainly ABS normal noise amplified by pad movement.  

---

### What You Can Do
1. **Confirm ABS:** Next time it happens, check if the brake pedal is vibrating rapidly. If yes → ABS, normal.  
2. **Inspect Brake Hardware:** Have a shop check anti-rattle clips and pad fitment. Cheap/easy fix if loose.  
3. **Check Heat Shields:** Tap them with a wrench; if they buzz, re-secure with hose clamps or new bolts.

## Assistant

Perfect description — that helps narrow it down a lot. Since you’re **not feeling it in the pedal** and it sounds **light, metallic, and free-floating**, we can pretty safely set ABS aside. What you’re describing matches a few common CX-5 (and similar Mazda/Toyota platforms) culprits:

---

## 🔎 Likely Causes

### 1. **Brake Pad Movement in the Caliper**
- Pads are held by thin **anti-rattle clips/shims**.  
- If those clips wear or the pad backing plate is slightly loose, the pad can “float” forward under braking and clink when it hits the caliper stop.  
- Because pads are very light, the noise is **high-frequency, metallic, and free-sounding** — exactly like what you describe.  
- Most noticeable when you **first load them going downhill** (more pad shift than flat road braking).  

---

### 2. **Loose Heat Shield or Exhaust Shield**
- The CX-5 has several thin tin shields around the exhaust/catalytic converter.  
- They’re notorious for vibrating or clinking when the car jerks to a stop.  
- The sound is **very tinny, high-pitched, and “light”**, often only a split-second rattle.  
- From inside the car, it can sound like it’s coming from the middle or front underside.

---

### 3. **Suspension Hardware (Sway Bar Links / Strut Top)**
- If a sway bar end link or strut mount is slightly worn, braking transfers weight forward and can cause a “tick-tick” metallic sound.  
- Less likely given your description (those usually sound heavier), but still worth a look.  

---

### 4. **Brake Caliper Slide Pins**
- If the **guide pins** aren’t greased, the caliper can move slightly and let the pad chatter.  
- Again, that produces a **light metallic clink** when the brakes grab.

---

## ✅ How to Pinpoint
1. **Low-speed test:** In an empty lot, roll slowly downhill and brake lightly. If you hear the same “t-t-t,” it’s likely **brake pads shifting**.  
2. **Heat shield tap test:** With the car cool and parked, knock the thin metal shields under the car with your hand/wrench. If one rattles, that’s your guy.  
3. **Visual check:** Look at brake pad clips/retainers. If any look bent, rusty, or missing, that’s almost certainly it.  

---

## 🔧 Fixes
- **Brake hardware kit** (new shims/retainers): cheap, usually $15–30 per axle.  
- **Heat shield re-secure**: a mechanic can use simple hose clamps or bolts to tighten.  
- If you’re getting brakes serviced soon, ask them to check pad fitment and caliper pins.
