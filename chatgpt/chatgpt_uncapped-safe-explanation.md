## Assistant

In startup finance, an **“uncapped SAFE”** is a **Simple Agreement for Future Equity (SAFE)** that **has no valuation cap** — meaning there’s *no maximum valuation* set for when the investor’s SAFE converts into equity in a future priced round.citeturn0search0turn0search3

**Key points:**  
- SAFEs are contracts where an investor gives money now in exchange for *future equity* when the company raises a priced round or has a liquidity event.citeturn0search40  
- A **valuation cap** normally limits the conversion price, ensuring early investors get more shares if the next round’s valuation is high.citeturn0search3  
- An **uncapped SAFE** **lacks that cap**, so the SAFE converts at the *actual valuation* in the next priced round (possibly with a discount if included).citeturn0search1

**Implications:**  
- **For founders:** less negotiation friction and potentially *less dilution* if the company grows quickly because you never gave investors a floor on the price they convert at.citeturn0search15  
- **For investors:** higher risk and usually less upside protection — if your startup’s valuation jumps, uncapped SAFEs can lead to relatively *small equity stakes* for the amount invested.citeturn0search2

**Market and risk reality:**  
- Uncapped SAFEs are *rare* in traditional VC contexts because sophisticated investors want protection (caps or discounts).citeturn0search4  
- They *can* be used strategically for quick closes or bridge funding, but can be tough to sell to investors focused on downside protection.citeturn0search22

In short: **uncapped SAFE = SAFE with no valuation ceiling**, shifting valuation risk to the investor and offering simplicity to founders.citeturn0search0
