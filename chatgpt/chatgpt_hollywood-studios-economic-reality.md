# Impact of AI and Virtual Production on Filmmaking

Advances in AI-driven content creation and virtual production technology are starting to transform how movies and TV shows are made. Tools like OpenAI’s **Sora** and Adobe’s **Firefly** are early examples of generative AI that could reduce the need for certain practical filmmaking elements. **Sora** is a cutting-edge text-to-video model that can **create realistic video scenes purely from text instructions**, generating clips up to about a minute long that adhere to a user’s prompt【29†L107-L115】【29†L123-L131】. In demos, Sora has produced everything from a neon-lit city street to wooly mammoths in a snowy meadow based solely on text descriptions. Adobe’s **Firefly Video Model**, released in beta in 2024, takes a slightly different approach – it’s designed to assist filmmakers in post-production. Rather than generating an entire scene from scratch, Firefly can **generate short 5-second HD clips or effects to fill gaps in footage or add elements (like changing a sky or adding weather effects)**【27†L117-L125】. For instance, a filmmaker could ask Firefly to create a few extra seconds of establishing shot or add digital rain to a scene, seamlessly integrating it with real footage. Adobe has positioned Firefly as a tool within its Premiere Pro editing software to **“enhance or fix existing scenes,”** rather than replace the camera entirely【27†L117-L125】. The fact that these AI models exist and are improving highlights a future where **some content (backgrounds, crowd scenes, minor shots) can be generated algorithmically** instead of being filmed traditionally.

Meanwhile, **virtual production** – the use of LED walls and game-engine technology to create live digital backdrops (pioneered by *The Mandalorian*’s “Volume” stage) – is blurring the line between physical and digital filmmaking. Virtual production allows filmmakers to shoot actors on a soundstage that is surrounded by massive high-resolution screens projecting 3D environments. This technology replaces the need to travel to on-location sets or build extensive practical sets for every scene. It also enables real-time adjustments to scenery and lighting. Initially, some criticized virtual production for yielding a somewhat “digital” look, but as techniques improve, it’s becoming a reliable part of the toolkit. Creatives are learning when to use virtual sets versus practical ones to avoid a one-size-fits-all aesthetic【28†L163-L172】【28†L181-L189】. Crucially, virtual production **shrinks the need for large physical sets and locations**, yet it **increases the need for soundstage space equipped with these LED walls**. Many new stages being built (including those in development in Vegas, NJ, etc.) are expected to be outfitted for virtual production work. 

Generative AI further turbocharges virtual production. We’re quickly approaching a point where **filmmakers can conjure entire 3D environments or modify virtual sets with just a few voice or text commands**. Industry experts foresee a future where a director might simply say the words, and the AI system will re-dress a virtual set or change the time of day on the LED wall in real-time【28†L199-L207】. For example, if a scene needs a different skyline or an imaginary creature in the background, AI could generate those assets on the fly. This convergence of AI and virtual production could dramatically reduce the cost and time of set design, location scouting, and even VFX rendering. It enables “in-camera” visual effects – complex shots captured on set, thanks to the AI-assisted virtual backdrop, rather than added months later in post-production.

**Implications for studios:** If AI and virtual production continue to advance, traditional filmmaking workflows will shift. On one hand, studios may not need to build as many elaborate physical sets or travel to as many exotic locations – a lot can be done digitally on a stage. This could make **some older soundstages (without high-tech upgrades) less essential**, while increasing demand for fewer but more advanced stages. On the other hand, the total volume of content production isn’t necessarily shrinking – in fact, the ease of creating content with AI might spur *more* production (for instance, generating multiple versions of scenes, or enabling smaller creators to produce film-quality visuals). Major studios are already investing in virtual production facilities (for example, Disney’s StageCraft or Warner’s LED stages in Leavesden) to keep up with these trends. The new studio campuses in development are likely to include virtual production stages and green-screen suites to be future-proof. AI tools like Sora and Firefly also raise **workflow questions**: Will studios need as many crew for set design, matte painting, or even extras casting if an AI can generate backgrounds and crowds? These technologies could eventually streamline crew sizes and costs, but they also provoke ethical and creative debates (e.g. about the role of human labor and originality). In summary, generative AI and virtual production are pushing studios to adapt – influencing decisions about what kind of facilities to build. The expansions in NV, AZ, NJ might be seen as “future-ready” investments if they incorporate these technologies, ensuring the studios can produce cutting-edge content efficiently. But they also carry the risk of becoming underutilized if production methods radically change beyond the need for large physical spaces.

# Changing Audience Behavior and Industry Trends

The calculus behind studio investments must account for how audiences are consuming content in an evolving media landscape. Over the past decade, **streaming platforms and digital media have fundamentally altered viewing habits**. Theatrical attendance has been on a long-term decline, especially in the U.S. (even pre-pandemic). In 2004, Americans bought on average about 5 movie tickets per person annually; by 2019 that was down to roughly 4, and by 2024 it’s projected to be under 2 tickets per person【31†L105-L113】. In fact, a Gallup survey found that about **3 in 5 Americans did not go to a movie theater at all in the past year**, as home-viewing options multiplied【42†L459-L467】. The COVID-19 pandemic accelerated this decline – forcing audiences to get comfortable with streaming premieres and further entrenching at-home entertainment habits. Although 2023 and 2024 saw box office rebounds (thanks to hits like *Barbie* and *Top Gun*), the domestic box office remains **~20% below pre-pandemic 2019 levels**【31†L85-L93】. Theatrical releases are now largely dominated by big franchise tentpoles, with mid-budget dramas and comedies finding it harder to succeed in cinemas.

Conversely, **streaming and on-demand content consumption have soared**. Subscribers have myriad choices – Netflix, Amazon Prime, Disney+, Max, Hulu, and more – leading to fierce competition in the “streaming wars.” This drove an explosion of content spending in the late 2010s and early 2020s as each platform raced to build a content library. For a time, it meant nearly every soundstage in Los Angeles was booked solid to film new series and movies for streaming. Private equity investors started buying studio lots (for example, Hackman Capital bought CBS’s Studio City lot for \$1.8 billion in 2021) because streaming players like Apple, Amazon, and Netflix were desperate for production space【18†L147-L156】【18†L153-L158】. **Social media and short-form video** (YouTube, TikTok, Instagram) have also impacted viewing habits – younger audiences spend hours on user-generated or algorithmically served short videos, which competes for their attention versus watching a two-hour film. This doesn’t directly hit studios’ core business the way streaming does, but it creates a generational shift where traditional Hollywood content has to fight harder for relevance and buzz. We see studios trying to tap into social trends (through marketing on TikTok, or creating bite-sized spinoff content) to stay culturally relevant.

Another trend is the **globalization of content**. Non-U.S. markets (like China, India, Latin America) are contributing a larger share of box office and streaming growth. Major studios are increasingly tailoring some content to these markets or launching international productions. This globalization means content production is spreading out – a show might shoot in multiple countries to save cost or appeal to local audiences. It’s another factor driving studios to have production hubs in various locations (like Netflix building studios in New Mexico, New Jersey, or investing in South Korean productions) rather than doing everything in Hollywood.

Crucially, **streaming, while growing, has proven less profitable per dollar than the old theatrical and TV model**. The third-quarter 2024 earnings of major studios underscore this: streaming revenue is rising but hasn’t yet compensated for the collapse of traditional TV income【22†L69-L74】. Linear TV (broadcast and cable) was once a cash cow for studios like Disney, Warner, and Paramount – now cord-cutting is shrinking that revenue dramatically each quarter. Studios are trying to pivot, but the transition period is painful financially (as seen with WBD and Paramount’s big losses and write-downs). This has led to belt-tightening: **industry-wide layoffs** (not just at Paramount; Disney cut 7,000 jobs in 2023, Warner Bros. Discovery cut several thousand post-merger, Netflix had layoffs, etc.), and a pullback on the relentless content spending. Dozens of projects were canceled or delayed in 2023, and production slowed further during the Writers’ and Actors’ strikes. Interestingly, that content slowdown coincided with a glut of studio space coming online, especially in places like New York and London – leading to a short-term mismatch of supply and demand for soundstages. This kind of statistic raises red flags about potential overcapacity: if audience behaviors are reducing the volume of traditional production (fewer TV series orders, fewer mid-budget films), will there be enough work to fill all these new studios?

# Strategic Outlook: Expansion Gamble vs. Industry Evolution

The big question: are these ambitious studio expansions a forward-thinking investment positioning for a content-hungry future, or an expensive miscalculation in light of technological and audience shifts? The answer may be a bit of both, and it likely varies by company.

On one hand, **there is logic to these investments** as a forward-looking play. The content arms race of the past few years revealed a shortage of quality soundstages – streaming services needed more studio space to produce the endless slate of shows and movies, and top-tier stages in LA, New York, London were booked out. Building modern production facilities in new locales could alleviate bottlenecks and give studios more control over their production pipeline (instead of renting space ad hoc). Additionally, the **tax incentives** greatly de-risk these projects financially. When New Jersey is covering nearly 40-50% of Netflix’s build cost in subsidies【39†L83-L90】, or Nevada is prepared to hand Sony/WBD tens of millions per year in credits【7†L271-L279】【7†L299-L307】, the calculus changes – it’s essentially “cheap money” to expand. These companies are thinking 10-20 years out; even if the industry is in a slump now, having additional state-of-the-art studios could be a competitive advantage when the next production surge comes. The facilities can also serve as revenue generators on their own by renting to third-party productions. For instance, a new Las Vegas studio could attract projects from other Hollywood studios or even international productions that want to take advantage of Nevada’s incentives. In that sense, Sony and Warner might profit not just from their own use of Summerlin Studios but also by operating it as a business venture (with partner Howard Hughes Corp) to host outside projects. If content demand continues globally – including new formats like interactive media, VR, etc. – these multipurpose studio campuses could find many uses.

Moreover, **the new studios are being built with future technology in mind**. They’re likely to be equipped for virtual production, volumetric capture, motion capture, high-speed data workflows, and other advanced needs that older stages lack. This means as AI and digital production methods grow, these facilities can accommodate them. A soundstage is still needed for filming actors and staging scenes, even if the background is computer-generated; the volume of production might not drop, but the nature of it changes. It’s conceivable that studios will produce *more* content pieces (including shorts, VR experiences, spinoffs for streaming) even if each individual project has a smaller footprint – and having more stages allows parallel productions. In sum, if one believes that “content is king” and the appetite for video entertainment will only increase (on various platforms globally), then owning more production infrastructure is a wise long-term bet.

On the other hand, **there are significant risks and signs that this could be a miscalculation** if trends don’t pan out favorably. The current industry struggles are very real: every major legacy studio is reining in spending, not ramping it up. If streaming has taught studios anything in the last few years, it’s that chasing subscriber growth without regard to profit leads to peril. Now Wall Street is pressuring media companies to focus on profitability, which means fewer greenlights and more caution in content investment. The era of “blank check” content budgets is ending. If studios produce fewer shows and films per year than the peak of 2018–2021, they simply won’t need all those extra soundstages. We are already seeing hints of **overcapacity** – as mentioned, the New York market is experiencing a wave of new stage construction just as production activity declined, causing utilization to drop and rental rates to soften【32†L7-L15】. If Las Vegas, Arizona, and New Jersey all build massive facilities around the same time, they’ll be competing with each other (and with established hubs like Georgia, Louisiana, Vancouver, the U.K., etc. that also have incentives and stages). There’s a genuine concern that some of these gleaming new studios could sit underused, at least in the short term, if the content gold rush doesn’t continue.

Another factor is the **technological wildcard**: generative AI. While the current consensus is that big-budget productions will still require human filmmakers and actors, the rapid improvement of AI-generated content could create alternative forms of entertainment. It’s not unreasonable that in a decade, a new type of studio might emerge that produces AI-driven animated content or interactive experiences with far fewer physical sets and more cloud computing infrastructure instead. If some entertainment shifts to fully digital creation, traditional soundstage demand could plateau or even decline for certain genres. The large physical studio backlot might become less critical for, say, sci-fi or fantasy projects if those can be done largely via virtual environments. **Virtual production** itself means you can do more on one stage (with LED walls) rather than needing multiple sets or locations – potentially reducing the total number of stages needed for a given slate of productions. Thus, studios that over-expand concrete-and-steel facilities could be left with high fixed costs in an era where leaner, tech-driven production is the norm.
