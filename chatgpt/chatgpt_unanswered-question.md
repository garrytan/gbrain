## Assistant

Mr. White, he is **partly right on subway capacity**, but his conclusion is too broad.

## Where he’s wrong

### 1. He treats Boring tunnels as a subway replacement

The better argument for The Boring Company is: **subway-grade capacity is unnecessary on most routes**. NYC is an extreme edge case. MTA says NYC Transit operates one of the largest public transportation systems in the world, with about **3.6 million subway rides per day** and **472 subway stations**. That is a Manhattan trunk-line problem. citeturn911523view5

Vegas, Nashville, Dubai, airports, convention centers, stadiums, resorts, campuses, and many downtown connectors need **medium-capacity point-to-point service**, not 10-car trains every 2 minutes.

### 2. He ignores the diameter economics

His “why not build subway-sized tunnels?” point misses the core engineering tradeoff: **tunnel cost and difficulty scale heavily with diameter**.

TBC’s newer Loop projects describe **12-foot internal-diameter tunnels**. citeturn911523view3turn911523view4 The Gateway/Hudson rail tunnel he cites uses tubes with **25 ft 2 in inside diameter**, and TBM cutterheads **28 ft 8 in** across. citeturn459145view0

Rough geometry: a 25-foot rail tube has about **4.4×** the cross-sectional area of a 12-foot Loop tunnel; using the 28 ft 8 in cutterhead comparison, it is about **5.7×**. That means far more spoil removal, liner material, machine mass, launch/retrieval complexity, and station integration. His “just make it subway-sized” solution gives away much of the cost/speed advantage.

### 3. He compares vehicle capacity instead of system capacity

A subway train beats a Tesla on passengers per vehicle. That is obvious.

But transport capacity is:

> **passengers per vehicle × vehicles per hour × number of parallel paths × station throughput**

TBC’s argument is not “one car beats one subway train.” It is “many cheap, small, point-to-point tunnels can be layered and expanded.” TBC describes Loop as direct-to-destination service with no intermediate stops, and describes tunnels as expandable with additional levels. citeturn782394view0turn782394view1

The current LVCC Loop has demonstrated **over 4,500 passengers/hour** and **over 32,000 passengers/day**; the broader Vegas Loop is planned as **68 miles / 104 stations** with claimed final capacity up to **90,000 passengers/hour**. citeturn911523view1turn911523view0

That does not make one Loop tube equal to a subway trunk. It means his “cars can never scale” claim is too simplistic at the network level.

### 4. He assumes “car” means private car needing parking

That is a weak point in his argument. Loop vehicles can be fleet vehicles. They do not require every rider to own, park, maintain, or store a private car at the destination. TBC’s stated Loop model is an underground public transportation system moving passengers directly to final destinations. citeturn782394view0

A fleet of EV shuttles, vans, robotaxis, or higher-occupancy vehicles changes the math materially. His parking critique applies to private car commuting; it is much less relevant to a managed shuttle/robotaxi network.

### 5. He dismisses the real tunneling innovation

He says speed is mainly a function of diameter. Diameter matters, but TBC’s Prufrock page lists other process innovations: launching without large pits, “porpoising,” continuous mining while installing liners, remote/ZPIT operation, vertical integration, and standardizing tunnel/TBM design. citeturn911523view2

Those are exactly the boring parts of boring that matter: less mobilization, fewer custom designs, fewer giant station boxes, fewer utility conflicts, and more repeatable construction.

## The clean rebuttal

He is arguing against **“replace the NYC subway with private cars in one tunnel.”** That is an easy target.

The actual Boring case is:

> Small standardized tunnels can be built cheaper and faster than conventional rail tunnels, then networked into point-to-point systems for routes that need more than surface traffic but less than NYC-subway capacity.

## Caveat

He is right that **a single car tunnel does not match a high-frequency subway line**. If TBC claimed it could replace the Lexington Avenue Line or the 7 train at rush hour with Teslas, that would be nonsense. His error is converting that valid narrow point into “this cannot scale in sizable cities.” It can scale for many urban use cases; it just scales differently from heavy rail.
