## Assistant

Short version, Mr. White:  

- On **your own landing page**, “Look better naked” is generally *not* illegal or obscene in the U.S.  
- **Biggest real risk** is *trademark*, not decency. That exact phrase is a registered fitness tagline.  
- **App Store / Play Store / ad platforms** may flag or limit it as “adult / sexual” and make your life annoying.  
- So: great hook conceptually, but I’d *tweak the wording* rather than ship that exact phrase as your main hero line.

Let me break it down quickly.

---

## 1. Pure legality (U.S. / California)

For a standalone marketing site:

- “Look better naked” is nowhere near the legal definition of obscenity.  
- Gyms and fitness brands have been using that phrase in normal commerce (gyms, classes, etc.) for years. citeturn2search3turn2search13  
- U.S. advertising law (FTC) mainly cares that your *objective* claims (fat loss %, health benefits, disease risk, etc.) are truthful and **substantiated**. citeturn1search11turn1search3turn1search31turn1search7  

“Look better naked” is basically **puffery**: subjective, not a precise, testable promise. That’s usually fine. The second you start saying “Lose 10% body fat in 4 weeks,” that’s where the FTC standard of “competent and reliable scientific evidence” kicks in hard.

So from a *pure law / regulator* angle: the phrase itself on a website is not your main problem.

---

## 2. Trademark: where this *does* get spicy

“LOOK BETTER NAKED” is actually a **registered U.S. trademark** owned by DB 85 GYM CORP. for fitness-adjacent goods (hats and activewear, David Barton gyms). citeturn3search0turn3search7turn3search4  

Key points:

- It’s a well-known tagline in the fitness space for those gyms (“our trademark tagline is ‘look better naked’”). citeturn3search4  
- Trademarks are registered in specific classes, but enforcement in practice is about **likelihood of confusion**:  
  A fitness/physique app using the identical slogan in the *same* general industry (body aesthetics / gyms / training) is exactly where they’d plausibly argue confusion or brand association.
- Even if you’d ultimately win on some narrow legal argument, a C&D letter from a litigious brand is a headache you don’t need.

**Conclusion:**  
Using **that exact three-word phrase as your hero line and core brand slogan is a non-trivial trademark risk.** If you like the concept, rephrase it.

---

## 3. Apple App Store / Google Play

### Apple

Apple’s App Store Review Guidelines:

- Ban “overtly sexual or pornographic material,” defined as explicit descriptions/depictions intended to stimulate erotic feelings. citeturn2search0turn2search2  
- Also say that if you’re “looking to shock and offend, the App Store isn’t the right place.” citeturn2search14  

“Look better naked”:

- It’s **suggestive**, but not explicit.  
- Whether it flies depends heavily on the **overall context** (screenshots, copy, visuals) and the **age rating**. With a line like that, expect to be treated as at least “mature / suggestive themes” and bumped into a higher age rating bucket. citeturn2search20turn2search22  

Where it’s more likely to bite you is **Apple Search Ads / Apple advertising**:

- Apple’s advertising content rules don’t allow ads with text that “explicitly or implicitly references any adult subject.” citeturn2search16  
- “Look better naked” is almost the dictionary definition of “implicitly references an adult subject.”

So:

- **Inside the app / app description:** might pass if the rest is clinical and non-sleazy, but risk of rejection/extra scrutiny is real.  
- **Apple Search Ads:** that exact line is very likely to be rejected as “adult-related”.

### Google Play

Google Play doesn’t allow apps that “contain or promote sexual content or profanity,” especially anything that could be seen as sexually gratifying. citeturn1search5turn1search9  

- Your app is clearly about physique, not sexual services, so you’re not in “porn / escort” territory.  
- But the *copy* “look better naked” pushes things into **“suggestive / adult”** territory; they can require a higher age rating and/or push back if the whole brand feels like a thirst-trap app rather than a fitness tool.

Bottom line:  
- **App name and store metadata:** I would **not** put that exact phrase in title/subtitle. Maybe in a lower-level screenshot caption at most, but you’re asking for a review fight.  
- Keep the **store-facing messaging more neutral** (optimize your physique, pro-level body metrics, etc.), and save the spicier copy for web/email where you’re in control.

---

## 4. Ad platforms (Meta, Google Ads, etc.)

If you ever want to run paid:

- **Meta (IG/FB)** bans ads with adult nudity and **“sexually suggestive content”**; they’re pretty touchy about anything that can be read as sexualized body presentation. citeturn1search2turn1search26turn1search14turn1search18turn1search22  
- **Google Ads** has a sexual content policy that restricts “moderately sexual” ads to limited placements and forbids more explicit content entirely. citeturn1search1turn1search25  

“Look better naked” will almost certainly:

- Get flagged as **adult/sexual** content.  
- Limit where and how often you can run the ad, or be outright rejected depending on creative.

That doesn’t kill the idea, but it **shrinks your acquisition channels** if it’s front-and-center.

---

## 5. Practical recommendation

If I were in your chair:

1. **Website landing page (your own domain):**  
   - Concept is fine. The *exact phrase* has trademark risk.  
   - If you really want to test the “naked” angle, I’d use something like:  
     - “Train for the body you’re proud to show off.”  
     - “Physiques that look good in bad lighting.”  
     - “Build a body that looks unreal with your shirt off.”  
   - These keep the aesthetic-first honesty without stepping on a registered tagline.

2. **App Store / Play Store listing:**  
   - Go cleaner and more clinical:  
     - Title: **Log Your Body – Aesthetics & Body Comp Tracker**  
     - Subtitle: **Track FFMI, body fat, and symmetry to build your best physique.**  
   - Body copy can hint at the real motive (“Train for a physique that turns heads”) but avoid the naked line.

3. **Ad accounts:**  
   - Assume “naked” in ad text = **review headaches, account flags, or limited delivery**.  
   - Use that more in **owned channels** (email, landing pages, community) rather than in ad creatives.

4. **Trademark / legal hygiene:**  
   - Before you invest in any variant using that exact wording as a core line or brand name, get a **short consult with an IP/advertising lawyer**. They can quickly check classes, current enforcement history, and risk tolerance for your specific use case.
   - It’s a much bigger pain to rebrand after a C&D once you’re in production.
