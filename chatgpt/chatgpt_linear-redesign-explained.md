### What *linear redesign* means
A *redesign* is when you take an existing product or system and intentionally revise its design to **fix problems, improve usability, or better align with current goals and usage patterns** — not just patch around the edges but rethink important structural or visual elements. citeturn0search1

If the term is specifically *linear redesign* (as sometimes used in UX/UI/product discussions), it usually means **applying that redesign in a straightforward, logical, sequential way** rather than in an unpredictable or chaotic way. This can mean:

- **Evaluating the current design debt** (the visual and interaction inconsistencies that have built up over time) and recognizing when a full redesign is necessary. citeturn0search1  
- **Planning the redesign in clear steps**, often from high-level structure down to details (e.g., update information architecture → then visual hierarchy → then UI elements).  
- **Maintaining continuity** with what already works while improving clarity, hierarchy, and usability, rather than ripping everything up and starting from scratch unexpectedly. citeturn0search0

### How it differs from other redesign approaches
“A linear redesign” in contrast to non-linear, chaotic, or exploratory redesign processes emphasizes:

- **Planned sequencing** (one logical step at a time).  
- **Clear goals and priorities** rather than open-ended experimentation.  
- **Iterative refinement within a defined path** instead of bouncing between unrelated elements.  
- **Maintaining usability and familiarity** while improving form and function.

This is especially important for complex products where users rely on consistency and predictability. citeturn0search1
