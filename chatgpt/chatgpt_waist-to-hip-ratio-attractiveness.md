## User

According to science, what is the most aesthetically pleasing hip-to-waist ratio for men and for females?

---

## Assistant

Mr. White, in the scientific literature you usually see **waist-to-hip ratio (WHR)** used (waist ÷ hips), not hip-to-waist. I’ll give both.

### Females

- **Most commonly reported “attractive” WHR:**  
  Roughly **0.7** (waist about 30% smaller than hips).  
  Classic work by Devendra Singh and many follow-up studies found that men across a variety of cultures tend to rate women with a WHR around **0.7** as more attractive, youthful, and healthy, with typical preferences spanning about **0.6–0.8**. citeturn3search3turn3search8  

- **More recent nuance:**  
  A 2024 study in *Scientific Reports* suggests that for larger body types, **overall curviness (an “S-shape”)** predicts attractiveness better than a single WHR number, though slim figures with ~0.7 WHR were still rated highest. citeturn3search1turn3news27  

- **Your “hip-to-waist” version:**  
  If WHR = 0.7, then **hip-to-waist ≈ 1.4** (hips about 40% larger than waist).

### Males

- **Male WHR specifically:**  
  One detailed 3D-body study found an “optimal” male **waist-to-hip ratio ≈ 0.8** for attractiveness (waist slightly smaller than hips). citeturn2search2  
  In hip-to-waist terms that’s **≈ 1.25**.

- **But for men, other ratios matter more:**
  - **Waist-to-chest ratio** and **shoulder-to-hip ratio** are stronger predictors of attractiveness than WHR itself. citeturn2search5  
  - Women tend to prefer men whose **shoulders are about 1.6× their waist** (a “V-taper,” close to the golden ratio). citeturn1search10  

### Health vs. aesthetics

- Epidemiologically, **very high WHR** is tied to higher cardiometabolic risk (e.g., >0.85 in women, >1.0 in men). citeturn2search5  
- So even in the health literature, you get a **broad “good range”** rather than a single magic number.

**Short version in your hip-to-waist language:**

- **Women (most studies):**  
  - Waist-to-hip ≈ **0.7** → hip-to-waist ≈ **1.4**
- **Men (when measured):**  
  - Waist-to-hip ≈ **0.8** → hip-to-waist ≈ **1.25**

These are **population averages**, not targets anyone “should” hit; genetics, culture, and overall leanness/muscle mass shift what looks best on a given person.

## Assistant

Here’s a clean list of **body metrics that actually matter**, split by sex and type (looks vs health). I’m focusing on what’s supported in the literature, not internet fluff.

---

## For Women

### A. Aesthetics-focused body metrics

1. **Waist-to-Hip Ratio (WHR)**  
   - **Definition:** waist ÷ hips.  
   - Why it matters: Lower WHR (roughly **0.65–0.75**) is consistently rated as more attractive and linked to better health and fertility markers. citeturn0search0turn0search7turn0search19turn0search23  

2. **Overall Curviness / “S-shape” of the torso**  
   - Newer work shows that for larger body types, **overall curviness of the spine/hip/butt line** predicts attractiveness better than WHR alone, though slim figures with ~0.7 WHR still score high. citeturn0news45  

3. **Body Fatness (BMI / body-fat %)**  
   - Moderate **BMI** and body-fat levels (not emaciated, not obese) predict higher attractiveness; extreme low or high BMI drops ratings. citeturn0search7turn0search19turn0search26  

4. **Leg-to-Body Ratio (LBR)**  
   - How long the legs are relative to total height.  
   - **Near-average or slightly longer legs** are usually rated most attractive in women. citeturn0search6turn0search18turn0search25turn0search22  

5. **Height & overall size**  
   - Taller and medium-height women can be rated slightly more attractive in some samples, but **height is weaker than WHR + BMI** in predicting ratings. citeturn0search4turn0search26  

---

### B. Health-linked metrics that affect how “in shape” she looks

6. **Waist-to-Height Ratio (WHtR)**  
   - **waist ÷ height**; strong predictor of cardiometabolic risk.  
   - A common health cut point is **<0.5** (“waist less than half your height”). citeturn0search0turn0search1turn0search3turn0search9  

7. **Waist Circumference / central fat**  
   - Central/belly fat strongly tied to diabetes and heart disease risk; “pear-shaped” fat on hips/thighs is less dangerous. citeturn0news19turn0search10  

8. **Composite fat indices (BRI, RFM, ABSI, etc.)**  
   - Newer indices (Body Roundness Index, Relative Fat Mass, Body Shape Index) combine waist, height, and weight to track risk better than BMI alone. citeturn0search11turn0search12turn0news20  

---

## For Men

### A. Aesthetics-focused body metrics

1. **Shoulder-to-Waist (or Shoulder-to-Hip) Ratio – “V-taper”**  
   - Broad shoulders relative to waist/hips are one of the strongest body-shape predictors of male attractiveness, masculinity, and perceived fighting ability. citeturn0search4turn0search20turn0search21  
   - Many studies converge around **shoulders ≈ 1.5–1.7× the waist** being most attractive. citeturn0search5turn0search17  

2. **Waist-to-Chest Ratio (WCR)**  
   - Narrow waist relative to chest = better ratings; this often beats WHR as a predictor of male attractiveness. citeturn0search16turn0search24  

3. **Upper-Body Muscularity / Strength Cues**  
   - Visible muscle in shoulders, chest, and arms – not bodybuilder extreme – explains a huge share of variance in how attractive a male torso looks (strength ratings alone account for ~70% of attractiveness variance in one study). citeturn0search12turn0search8  

4. **Body Fatness (BMI / body-fat %)**  
   - Moderate body-fat with muscularity looks best; too high or too low fat drops ratings. Recent work shows **shoulder-to-waist ratio + body-fat %** together explain much of attractiveness. citeturn0search9turn0search1  

5. **Height**  
   - Women tend to rate **taller men** as more attractive and dominant, especially when combined with a high shoulder-to-hip ratio. citeturn0search21turn0search4turn0news41  

6. **Leg-to-Body Ratio (LBR)**  
   - For men, **average to slightly longer-than-average legs** are preferred; very short or very long legs are rated worse. citeturn0search2turn0search18turn0search14turn0search25  

7. **Overall Volume-Height Index (VHI)**  
   - A 3D metric combining body volume and height; research suggests VHI is a strong single predictor of male body attractiveness (basically: right mass for your height, not too bulky or too thin). citeturn0search1  

---

### B. Health-linked metrics that affect how “fit” he looks

8. **Waist-to-Height Ratio (WHtR)**  
   - Same story as for women: **central fat vs height** is a strong predictor of metabolic risk; <0.5 is often used as a “healthy” cutoff. citeturn0search0turn0search3turn0search5turn0search10  

9. **Waist Circumference / Sagittal Abdominal Diameter**  
   - Bigger waist and abdominal depth = more visceral fat = higher risk of heart disease and diabetes, independent of BMI. citeturn0search17turn0news19turn0search10  

10. **Newer obesity indices (BRI, ABSI, RFM, etc.)**  
   - These try to capture **shape + fat distribution** better than BMI alone and are increasingly used in research and some clinical settings. citeturn0search11turn0search12turn0news18turn0news20

## How to think about it in practice

If you want a simple “dashboard” of what matters most, **for both sexes**:

- **Looks:**  
  - Women → WHR, BMI/body-fat, leg-to-body ratio, overall curviness.  
  - Men → shoulder-to-waist (V-taper), waist-to-chest, muscularity/strength cues, BMI/body-fat, height, leg-to-body ratio.

- **Health (which also shapes how “fit” you look):**  
  - Both → waist-to-height ratio, waist circumference / central fat, and overall body-fat (not just weight).
