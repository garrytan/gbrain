## Assistant

Mr. White, snorting creatine is a bad idea:

- **Poor absorption** – Creatine needs to dissolve in your gut to enter your bloodstream. Nasal mucosa won’t absorb enough, so a “bump” won’t meaningfully raise muscle creatine levels.  
- **Tissue irritation** – The powder’s rough crystals will damage and dry out your nasal passages, leading to nose-bleeds, congestion or even chronic sinusitis.  
- **Infection risk** – Damaged mucosa is a breeding ground for bacteria and fungi.  
- **Wasted money** – You’ll lose most of it up your sinuses, so it’s a complete waste.

Stick to taking 3–5 g of creatine monohydrate mixed in water once daily. That’s the only way to reliably saturate your muscles—and spare your nose.

## Assistant

Mr. White, you can technically “dry-scoop” creatine—dropping the powder in your mouth and swallowing—but you still need to chase it with liquid. Here’s why and how to do it safely:

- **You still need fluid**  
  Creatine monohydrate has to dissolve in your stomach to be absorbed. If you don’t drink any water, it’ll just clump, slow absorption and could irritate your throat or esophagus.  
- **Choking risk**  
  Dry powder in the mouth can easily get lodged or cause a coughing fit. Always have a swig of water (or coffee, juice, whatever) ready.  
- **Optimal uptake**  
  Dr. Mike Israetel and RP Strength recommend taking creatine alongside at least 200 mL of fluid—and ideally with a small carb source—to spike insulin and shuttle more into muscle.  
- **Easy alternatives**  
  - Encapsulate your daily 3–5 g in gelatin or veggie capsules.  
  - Mix into a yogurt, applesauce or your black coffee—no extra “water chaser” needed if it’s already a liquid/soft food.  
  - Throw it into your post-workout shake or protein–rice cake snack.

Bottom line: dry-scooping without any drink is a hassle and less effective. Drop the scoop, then knock it back with your usual coffee—or keep a glass of water handy—so you actually absorb the creatine.
