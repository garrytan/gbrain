## Assistant

Mr. White, treat this as **do-not-drive-unless-necessary** until you pull the code and inspect the left side/wheel area.

A check engine light after a crash usually means one of these:

## Most likely causes

### 1. **Sensor or wiring damage**
Even if the engine bay looks fine, impact can yank or crush wiring. Common affected areas:

- oxygen sensor wiring
- MAF/MAP sensor connector
- coolant temp sensor
- EVAP lines/sensors
- front crash/air intake-related wiring
- loose battery terminal or ground strap

This can trigger the check engine light without obvious fluid leaks.

### 2. **Air intake got disturbed**
If the intake box, hose, or MAF sensor got knocked loose, the car can run weird, vibrate, hesitate, or throw a CEL.

Look for:

- cracked plastic intake hose
- loose airbox clips
- unplugged sensor on the intake tube
- hissing sound
- rough idle

### 3. **Wheel/suspension/axle damage on the left side**
“Weird vibes on the left side” after a crash is more concerning than the CEL. It could be:

- bent wheel
- tire bubble/internal tire damage
- alignment knocked out
- bent control arm
- damaged wheel bearing
- CV axle issue
- brake rotor/caliper rubbing
- fender liner or splash shield rubbing the tire

This can get worse quickly and can become unsafe.

### 4. **Exhaust damage**
A hit can shift the exhaust or damage an oxygen sensor. Symptoms:

- louder engine sound
- rattling
- burning smell
- vibration under load
- CEL

## Is driving it going to damage it more?

**Possibly, yes.** Especially if the vibration is from the wheel/suspension/axle area.

Do **not** drive it if any of these are true:

- CEL is **flashing**
- car is shaking at idle
- steering wheel vibrates
- car pulls left/right
- tire looks bulged, cut, or low
- wheel looks bent
- you hear scraping, grinding, clunking, or rubbing
- temperature gauge rises
- oil pressure/battery/brake light is also on
- burning smell
- reduced power

A **solid** check engine light with no leaks and normal temperature is less immediately catastrophic, but combined with post-crash vibration, I would not casually drive it around LA.

## What I’d do now

1. **Check if the CEL is flashing or solid.**
   - Flashing = stop driving. Tow it.
   - Solid = still get it scanned ASAP.

2. **Scan the OBD-II code.**
   - AutoZone/O’Reilly can usually scan free.
   - A cheap Bluetooth scanner is worth owning.

3. **Inspect the left front tire/wheel/suspension area.**
   - Tire bulges/cuts
   - Wheel bend
   - Plastic liner rubbing
   - Leaking strut
   - Anything hanging
   - Uneven wheel gap compared to the other side

4. **Send it to insurance/body shop/mechanic as crash-related.**
   Since the light came on after the accident, document it as part of the claim.

## My practical recommendation

**Do not drive it beyond a short, slow trip to a nearby mechanic or parts store for a scan.** If it vibrates, pulls, scrapes, clunks, or the CEL flashes, **tow it**. The risk is less “engine instantly explodes” and more “damaged wheel/suspension turns into a dangerous failure or ruins tires/axle/brakes.”
