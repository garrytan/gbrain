Mr. White, listings that look “too good to be true” usually are. These are the red flags that most often push a Carfax price well below market:

- **Branded or salvage title** – flood, totaled‑and‑rebuilt, lemon law buy‑back, etc. Instantly chops thousands off resale. citeturn2search0  
- **Multiple or severe accidents** – each serious hit drops value ~ $2 k on average. citeturn2search4  
- **High mileage / heavy fleet use** – ex‑rental, rideshare, or delivery vehicles rack up miles fast.  
- **Open mechanical or safety issues** – looming repairs or unrepaired recalls drive prices down.  
- **Stripped‑down trim** – base model, 2‑wheel drive, no tech package, manual seats, etc.  
- **Dealer bait‑and‑switch** – rock‑bottom “internet price” but mandatory add‑ons or in‑house financing add $2‑4 k at signing.  
- **Location & time on lot** – rural lots or cars that have sat for months get deep discounts.  
- **Algorithmic undervaluation** – Carfax weights accident/owner history heavily when spitting out its VIN‑specific “Great Value” label. citeturn2search1  

**Next steps**

1. Pull the full Carfax report and look for the bullet points above.  
2. Run the VIN through KBB / Edmunds to confirm what a clean example should cost.  
3. Pay a mobile mechanic for a pre‑purchase inspection before you wire any money.  
4. Get an out‑the‑door quote in writing—doc fees, taxes, “protection packages,” everything—so the bargain doesn’t vanish in the finance office.  

If none of those issues show up and a PPI checks out, you’ve just found a legit deal—grab it. Otherwise, walk.
