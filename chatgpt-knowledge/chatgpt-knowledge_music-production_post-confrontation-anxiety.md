## Question

Sometimes when I have a heated or uncomfortable exchange, very often when I’m like arguing to get a refund or something with a manager who is a dick, I get uncomfortable after the conversation is well over. Like it could be a phone conversation but then for hours after I hang up I’m physically feeling unsafe like someone is going to attack me physically even though logically I know that person is hundreds of miles away and doesn’t care about me. Is this normal? Is there a name for it?

## Answer

Yes, what you’re describing is **normal** for a subset of people and falls under a known psychological pattern. It’s often related to:

- **Hypervigilance** or a **heightened stress response**: Your nervous system is still on high alert after the confrontation, even though the threat is gone.
- **CPTSD traits** (without needing a formal diagnosis): If you grew up in a chaotic or hostile environment, your brain may interpret conflict as a physical threat even in safe contexts.
- **Autonomic dysregulation**: Your sympathetic nervous system (fight-or-flight) was triggered, and your parasympathetic (rest-and-digest) system hasn’t kicked in to calm you down.
- **Somatic flashbacks** (sometimes): Your body reacts as if it’s in past situations where conflict *was* dangerous.

It doesn’t have to mean something is wrong with you. But if it interferes with your daily life or you want to change it, somatic therapy, EMDR, or other trauma-informed approaches can help retrain your nervous system to respond appropriately.

No single clinical label perfectly matches your experience, but terms like “**post-adrenaline anxiety**, **threat overgeneralization**, or **conflict-triggered hyperarousal**” may come up in therapy or research.

## Question

When working on a startup, I find it particularly problematic because I'm having to talk to investors and customers and employees, and etc., and it can get triggered quite often. Anything that can be done to mitigate that?

## Answer

Yes, Mr. White. What you’re describing is a nervous system that’s *overfiring after high-stakes interactions*—especially common in founders. Here’s a concise mitigation plan tailored to your situation:

---

### 🔧 **Pre-Interaction Prep (Buffer the System)**

1. **Preload the Parasympathetic System**:  
   - 2–3 minutes of deep nasal breathing (4-7-8 method works well).  
   - Slow exhales *before* the call lowers baseline arousal.

2. **Script the Stakes**:  
   - Write one sentence: *“If this goes badly, the worst outcome is [realistic result].”*  
   - Remind your body this isn’t life or death—it’s a pitch, not a lion attack.

3. **Power posture or cold exposure**:  
   - A quick cold splash on face or neck triggers the vagus nerve and helps regulation.

---

### 💬 **During the Interaction**

1. **Slow your speech slightly** – it slows your brain’s threat processing.
2. **Sip water** – activates the vagus nerve, interrupts stress buildup.
3. **Anchor item** – hold a physical object that reminds you you’re safe (coin, ring, etc.).

---

### 🧠 **Post-Interaction Recovery**

1. **Full-body shakeout** (seriously – like animals do after a threat).
2. **10-minute walk + sunlight** – proven cortisol reducer.
3. **Name the feeling**: “My body feels unsafe. That doesn’t mean I *am* unsafe.” Labeling emotion reduces amygdala activation.

---

### 🧱 **Long-Term Armor Building**

- **Somatic therapy** or **nervous system retraining** (e.g., TRE, EMDR, vagal toning).
- **Deliberate exposure** to low-stakes confrontations (safe reps).
- **HRV training apps** like *Othership*, *Wim Hof*, or *Apollo Neuro* device.

---

This isn’t about being soft. It’s about keeping the machine tuned for performance. Founders with regulated nervous systems outlast the ones constantly running hot.

## Question

It also puts me in a shitty mood for hours after. How can I avoid this 

## Answer

What’s happening isn’t just anxiety—it’s **post-confrontation emotional residue**. Your body thinks you narrowly survived a threat, so it dumps cortisol and puts you in “stay alert” mode. That’s what poisons your mood. Here's how to **clear it fast and reclaim your day**:

---

## 💥 Post-Confrontation Mood Reset (15–20 min total)

### 1. **Flush the Cortisol (Physical first, not mental)**
- **5–10 min fast walk or stairs**
  - Gets adrenaline out of your bloodstream
- Or **20–30 jumping jacks** if indoors
  - You’re tricking your body into thinking the “threat” ended in survival and escape

---

### 2. **Physiological Sigh (x5)**
- Inhale through nose → sharp second inhale → slow, long exhale  
- This is the **fastest** way to flip out of sympathetic overdrive (backed by Stanford’s Dr. Andrew Huberman)

---

### 3. **“Mood Interrupt” Ritual**
Pick one you like. Do it *immediately* after hard convos:
- Cold face splash
- Eat a mint or gum (oral stimulation calms the limbic system)
- Watch a 60s funny or sexy video clip
- Play a hype song and air drum for 30s  
→ It **forces a pattern break** in your brain’s stress loop

---

### 4. **Cognitive Override (But short)**
Say out loud:  
> “That convo sucked. But I won’t let it hijack my day.”  
This is not toxic positivity—it's reclaiming agency.

---

## 🚫 Don’t Do:
- Don’t *replay* the convo unless it’s for a strategic debrief
- Don’t vent unless it's short and purposeful (venting too long = re-triggering)
- Don’t isolate—get some normal social contact or movement
