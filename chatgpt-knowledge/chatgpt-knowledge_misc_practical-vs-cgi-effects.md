# Practical vs CGI effects

## Question

Are the particulars in stranger things ever created as apractical effect or are they always cgi

## Answer

The Stranger Things team uses a mix of **practical effects** and **CGI**, depending on the scene:

- **Practical effects**: Many props, sets, and certain creatures have physical elements built on set. For example, the Demogorgon’s original suit in Season 1 was largely a man-in-a-suit animatronic with mechanical petals. Blood splatter, flickering lights, and most of the Upside Down’s slimy set dressing are practical.

- **CGI enhancements**: Digital effects extend or enhance the practical work—adding extra movement to monsters, creating large-scale environments like the vast Upside Down landscapes, or making supernatural elements like psychic powers and particle effects.

In short, they often **start practical for realism**, then **layer CGI** to polish or achieve what’s impossible in-camera.

## Question

I need specifics on the floating dust particles not all effects

## Answer

The Upside Down’s signature “floating dust” (the slow-drifting, glowing motes you see whenever a portal is open) is **almost entirely a practical effect**:

* **On-set snow machines** – The crew uses custom fine-particle snow/fog machines that eject very light, almost weightless cellulose or paper pulp “ash.” These are lit from the side or back so each flake catches the light and appears to glow.

* **Fans and air currents** – Gentle floor fans keep the particles suspended and drifting in random patterns to get that slow, eerie float.

* **Lighting & lens choices** – Hard backlights and a bit of haze in the room make the particles stand out on camera. Cinematographers shoot with wide apertures so the specks shimmer in and out of focus.

* **Minimal CGI touch-up** – VFX is usually limited to cleanup (removing wayward flakes, adding a few extra layers for density in wide shots, or matching continuity between cuts). The base look is captured in-camera.

So the “dust” you see is **real material filmed live**, with only light digital augmentation when needed.

## Question

Give me the exact specifics on how to recreate

## Answer

Here’s a concise recipe used on set to mimic the Upside Down “ash” exactly:

---

### Materials
- **Snow/Foam Machine:** Use a professional fine-particle “evaporative snow” unit (e.g., Antari S-500 or Le Maitre Arctic). These produce dry, tissue-light flakes that hang for minutes.
- **Snow Fluid:** Choose an extra-dry formula (high cellulose content, low residue). It creates thin, slow-falling particles that don’t quickly dissolve.
- **Atmospheric Haze:** A water-based hazer such as a DF-50 or MDG Atmosphere to create a light mist so particles catch light.
- **Fans:** Quiet, variable-speed floor fans to keep air moving gently without strong drafts.
- **Lighting:** High-intensity back or side lighting—LED panels or fresnels—gelled cool blue for the classic “dim Upside Down” hue.

---

### Setup
1. **Dark Environment:** Shoot in a dim space or at night for contrast.
2. **Pre-Haze:** Run the hazer 10–15 minutes before rolling to build a thin, even mist.
3. **Snow Output:** Set the snow machine to its lowest output for a steady, sparse fall. Mount it high (overhead grid or ladder) and slightly upwind so flakes drift across the camera frame.
4. **Air Flow:** Angle two or three fans on low around the room to create gentle circulating currents. You want turbulence that keeps flakes hovering rather than dropping straight down.
5. **Lighting Angles:** Place strong backlight(s) behind the particle field and slightly off camera axis. Add a soft front fill if you need to catch glints without flattening the scene.

---

### Camera
- **Lens:** Fast prime (f/1.4–f/2.8) to get shallow depth and bokeh sparkles.
- **Shutter:** Standard 180° (1/48 at 24 fps) for natural motion blur.
- **White Balance:** Slightly cool (around 4000 K) to emphasize blue tones.

---

### Optional VFX Touch
- In post, duplicate the particle layer for density or mask stray blobs, but capture most of the effect in-camera for the authentic “Stranger Things” float.

Follow those steps and you’ll get nearly the same slow-drifting, glowing particles you see in the Upside Down without heavy CGI.
