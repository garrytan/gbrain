## Answer

Mr. White, the Instagram post is accurate. Key points from veterinary toxicology literature and regulatory sources:

- **Birch sugar = xylitol (E 967, “wood sugar”)**  
  Xylitol is marketed under names like birch sugar, wood sugar or E 967 on ingredient lists. citeturn0search3

- **Rapid, dose-dependent toxicosis in dogs**  
  • Doses ≥0.1 g/kg body weight trigger a massive insulin surge → life-threatening hypoglycemia.  
  • Doses ≥0.5 g/kg often lead to acute liver failure (coagulopathy, elevated liver enzymes) and can be fatal. citeturn0search2turn0search7

- **Onset & clinical signs**  
  Signs appear 20–60 minutes post-ingestion. Early vomiting may be followed by weakness, ataxia, tremors, seizures and, in severe cases, coma or sudden liver collapse. citeturn0search0turn0search5

- **Common sources**  
  Xylitol is found in sugar-free gum, mints, toothpaste, mouthwash, cough syrups, vitamin supplements, sugar-free jams/jellies, nut butters (including some “sugar-free” peanut butters), and sugar-free desserts such as ice cream. citeturn0search0

- **Action**  
  Even small amounts can be deadly. If ingestion is suspected, induce veterinary care immediately (activated charcoal is ineffective; early decontamination and IV glucose support are critical).

**Bottom line:** The warning (“birch sugar” is xylitol; highly toxic to dogs; can cause liver failure or death) is fully supported by peer-reviewed studies and FDA guidance.

## Answer

Mr. White, in real-world pet treats the odds of a dog getting a toxic xylitol dose are negligible—provided you stick to mainstream brands and read labels. 

- **Regular peanut butter (Jif, Skippy, grocery-store brands):** 0 % contain xylitol; safe. citeturn1view0  
- **Sugar-free nut butters:** very rare (e.g. Nuts ’N More); only a handful of niche brands use xylitol—always check for “xylitol” or “E 967.” citeturn1view0  
- **Sugar-free gum/candy/toothpaste:** almost all contain 0.22–1.0 g xylitol per piece—a single piece can toxify a small dog. citeturn0search1turn0search12  
- **Sugar-free ice cream:** virtually never sweetened with xylitol (McDonald’s soft-serve, for example, does not). citeturn0search14  

**Bottom line:** if you’re giving dogs normal treats (peanut butter, ice cream, dog-safe toothpaste) and avoid products explicitly labeled “sugar-free” human formulas without vet-grade certification, the likelihood of a meaningful xylitol exposure is essentially zero.
