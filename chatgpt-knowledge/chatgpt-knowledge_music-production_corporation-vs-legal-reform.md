## Fact check

**What’s true:**

- Uber is backing a proposed California ballot measure that would change rules for **all vehicle-crash injury cases**, not just Uber crashes. CalMatters says it would cap personal-injury lawyers’ contingency fees and limit medical damages for all vehicle crashes in California. citeturn272208view1
- The measure would generally require crash victims to keep at least **75% of the total recovery**, which effectively caps plaintiff contingency fees at **25%** in these cases. citeturn272208view0
- It would also limit recovery of certain medical expenses and restrict certain attorney/medical-provider referral arrangements. citeturn272208view0
- Critics argue this could make complex cases harder to bring because attorneys may reject expensive, risky, or lower-dollar cases if fees are capped. Axios notes law professors have raised that concern. citeturn272208view3

**What’s overstated:**

- The measure does **not appear to eliminate the right to sue Uber, robotaxi companies, or manufacturers**. Axios reported Uber’s position that the measure does not change the right to sue or the types of claims people can bring, including negligence and product liability. citeturn272208view3
- Framing it as purely “Uber wants to kill people with robotaxis and escape liability” is advocacy, not a clean factual summary.
- Framing it as purely “protecting victims from greedy lawyers” is also advocacy. The financial incentive for Uber is obvious: reducing litigation cost and settlement leverage in crash cases.

## Answer

Mr. White, **you’re directionally right, but probably overstating it.**

AI will make parts of litigation cheaper, especially:

- legal research
- drafting complaints, motions, demand letters
- summarizing medical records
- reviewing documents
- finding inconsistencies
- estimating case value
- preparing deposition outlines

That gives individual plaintiffs and smaller firms more leverage. Reuters has reported that AI is already improving speed and reducing cost in research, document analysis, and litigation support, especially in data-heavy cases. citeturn730625news47turn730625news48

But AI does **not** eliminate the expensive parts of personal-injury litigation:

- expert witnesses
- depositions
- medical causation disputes
- accident reconstruction
- court filing costs
- discovery fights
- trial prep
- attorney risk
- years of delayed payment
- the chance of losing and getting nothing

That is why contingency fees matter. In California personal-injury cases, standard contingency fees are commonly around **33% before litigation** and can rise toward **40% if the case proceeds deeper into litigation or trial**, with case costs separate. citeturn730625search4turn730625search13

So the cleaner argument is:

:::writing{variant="chat_message" id="61508"}
I agree AI changes this somewhat. Over time, it should make parts of litigation much cheaper: research, drafting, document review, medical-record summaries, deposition prep, and case analysis. That could give individuals and smaller firms more leverage against companies like Uber.

But AI doesn’t make litigation free. You still have expert witnesses, depositions, medical causation fights, accident reconstruction, court costs, attorney risk, and years of delayed payment. A 25% cap might be fine for simple cases, but it could make complex or lower-dollar cases harder to bring.

That’s why I don’t trust either slogan. “Protect accident victims” sounds nice, but the proposal may weaken some victims’ ability to get lawyers. “Stop greedy trial lawyers” also sounds nice, but corporate liability costs are real and ultimately flow into prices, insurance, driver pay, hiring, layoffs, and service availability.

So I’m trying to evaluate the mechanism, not the team jersey. The real question is whether this balances fees and access to representation, or whether it quietly tilts the system too far toward large defendants.
:::
