## **Core Argument: Celebrating failure is not a virtue**
Framing someone’s box-office flop as a “good thing” misunderstands both economics and basic decency. A bad opening weekend isn’t proof of some moral reckoning—it’s just a bad opening weekend. Turning that into a moment for public celebration is petty, performative, and intellectually lazy.

Consumers choosing not to see a movie is not “cancel culture.” It’s just… the movie business. Thousands of films underperform every decade. Almost none of those failures are treated as symbolic punishments.

---

## **Box-office ≠ moral referendum**
If the premise is “people didn’t show up because she was canceled,” that claim falls apart immediately:

- **The genre itself is box-office poison.**  
  Non-franchise biopics have been struggling for years. Studios themselves know this—these films only survive during Oscars season or on streaming. Expecting strong theatrical performance is unrealistic, regardless of the star.

- **Historical data backs this.**  
  Sports biopics, especially boxing ones, barely draw audiences unless they’re attached to an iconic figure *and* have prestige backing. Even then, it's hit-or-miss.

- **No evidence audiences were boycotting her.**  
  There was no mass outrage, no organized pressure, no platform bans. You can’t call something “cancel culture” when there’s no cancellation mechanism present.

The far simpler explanation—the one Occam’s razor supports—is that the movie would have underperformed no matter who starred in it.

---

## **If the free market matters, so does humility**
If someone truly believes the free market is the ultimate judge, then they should also accept this:

**A movie failing is not a moral endorsement; it’s just the market being indifferent.**

Celebrating indifference is strange. It’s not a “win” for culture, society, or accountability. It’s just schadenfreude packaged as principle.

---

## **The real contradiction in his take**
He says:

> Consumers voting with their wallets is cancel culture in its purest form, and that’s good.

But if that’s the standard, then:

- Every flop is “cancel culture.”  
- Every failing business is “canceled.”  
- Every unsuccessful album, book, startup, or idea is a moral referendum.

That’s obviously absurd. Markets don’t morally judge people—they reward or punish *products*.

To pretend otherwise is to project meaning where none exists.
