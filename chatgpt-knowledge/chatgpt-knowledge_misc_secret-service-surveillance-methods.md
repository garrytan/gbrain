Mr. White — great line of questioning. Let’s break it down rigorously and realistically, without Hollywood contamination.

# **1) Live satellite video: technical feasibility**
Today, live video from a satellite is **absolutely feasible**, but **not the way pop culture imagines it**.

Two different technical scenarios exist:

### **A. Satellites designed for communications (Starlink, Iridium, etc.)**
- They can **transmit and receive real-time video**, but **they do not image the Earth**.
- They’re data pipes, not cameras.

### **B. Satellites designed for imaging (NRO, reconnaissance)**  
- They can take **very high-resolution imagery of the Earth**, including rapidly refreshed passes.
- Continuous *video-grade* imaging of a small area **is not the default mission profile**.

True, live imaging from orbit is technologically possible, but the bottleneck has historically been:

- **revisit rate** (how often the satellite passes over the spot)
- **optics and stabilization for high-frame-rate ground video**
- **bandwidth to downlink the video continuously**
- **tasking priority** (satellites are expensive; you don’t assign one to shadow a sedan)

The **“persistent live drone-like video from orbit”** is still not a standard satellite capability.

Think of orbital satellites as:
> **amazing cameras with intermittent access**, not persistent hovering platforms.

---

# **2) Latency**
For modern comms satellites (Starlink-like) with a video feed:

- **Round-trip latency** is extremely low: ~30–60 ms for LEO systems
- GEO satellites (TV satellites) are ~600 ms

Latency is **not the barrier** anymore.

If you had a satellite already capable of producing video, transmitting it is trivial.

---

# **3) The true barrier: orbital geometry**
Satellites are *not stationary above arbitrary points*, unless they are in **geostationary orbit**, which is 35,786 km up.

You can’t put an ultra-high-resolution Earth-imaging camera in GEO and get facial-level detail or rooftop-level resolution — the physics don’t allow it.

So:
- **High resolution = low orbit**
- **Low orbit = moving relative to the ground**
- Therefore, **a given spot is only visible for a short window**, unless you have a massive fleet.

If you want actual persistent “Predator drone style” video coverage from space:

> You need many satellites dedicated to one area — **extremely expensive**, even today.

This is why **military persistent surveillance is done by aircraft or drones, not satellites**.

---

# **4) Cost feasibility**
Today, cost has dropped dramatically, but feasibility depends on **persistence**, not the one-off ability.

To get **continuous video of one moving motorcade**, you would need:

- multiple satellites in coordinated orbits
- real-time handoff
- a ground control tasking system
- bandwidth allocation
- extremely precise optics and stabilization

This is **orders of magnitude more expensive** than using:

- airborne drones
- tethered aerostats
- helicopters
- ground camera systems

# **1) Latency**
Your understanding is correct:

For real-time **surveillance viewing**, both are acceptable. Latency is NOT the limiting factor for using satellites as live tactical video.

**The bottleneck is NOT transmission speed — it’s persistent optical coverage and cost.**

# **2) Helicopter surveillance**
### **Altitude**
Typical law-enforcement surveillance helicopters operate between:

- **1,000–5,000 feet AGL** for tactical urban work
- Higher if airspace or safety requires it

For **presidential protection**, the altitude varies based on:

- mission profile
- type of optics
- FAA restrictions

### **Video Connectivity**
Helicopter feeds are normally:

- **digitally downlinked directly to ground command posts**
- OR **relayed via microwave link**
- IF operating far away, they may **backhaul via satellite uplink**, but the imaging itself is NOT done by satellite

**Satellite is just a comms pipe, not a camera.**

# **3) Aerostats**
### **Are they feasible for protection?**
Absolutely. They’re one of the best persistent surveillance platforms ever created:

- Stationary
- Cheap to operate
- Very high resolution
- All-day/all-night
- No orbital mechanics
- Live video
- Broadcast via microwave or satellite uplink

### **Does Secret Service use them?**

- **DoD and DHS absolutely use tethered aerostats for border security, base protection, and critical infrastructure**
- There is **no technical or cost reason** protection agencies could not deploy aerostats at major events

In fact, when the U.S. military needed persistent wide-area surveillance overseas (Afghanistan, Iraq), **aerostats became the go-to solution**, not drones, because they were cheaper, calmer, and always on.

### **Oil companies**
Large industrial operators use **aerostats, mast towers, or fixed cameras** for:

- perimeter monitoring
- theft prevention
- pipeline protection
- critical asset security
- spill detection
- remote camp safety

If you run a refinery or pipeline in a high-risk region, a tethered aerostat is vastly cheaper than drones or aircraft.
