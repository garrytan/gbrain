## Answer

Mr. White, yes, there is real data on **perceived facial attractiveness**. No, there is **not** a serious scientific dataset that can defensibly name one single “most attractive woman ever.” The research-grade work measures **consensus ratings on standardized face images**, and one of the major benchmark papers explicitly says an absolute definition of facial beauty remains elusive. citeturn462465view0turn831014view1

The closest thing to what you want is this: SCUT-FBP5500 uses **5,500 frontal faces**, split across Asian/Caucasian and male/female groups, with **60 raters per face** and ages **15–60**. The Chicago Face Database gives standardized photos of people aged **17–65** with attractiveness norms from independent judges. The 10k US Adult Faces Database gives a much broader pool of **10,168 natural face photos**, though only part of it has the extra rated/annotated measures. Said and Todorov also built a statistical attractiveness model from **4,000 faces** rather than a celebrity list. citeturn462465view0turn831014view1turn831014view6turn831014view7turn831014view2

What the science does support is **shared signal plus noise**. There is meaningful agreement across raters and even across cultures, and recurring predictors include things like **averageness, symmetry, and female facial dimorphism/femininity**. That agreement is real, but it is not perfect, and it gets weaker when raters judge faces from groups they are less familiar with. citeturn831014view4turn831014view5

Your complaint about internet lists is valid. Those lists usually have a **bad sampling frame**: celebrities, magazine readers, social-media fame, or a tiny celebrity-only dataset. A 2024 paper, for example, analyzed **115 female celebrities**. That can tell you something about celebrities under a chosen metric. It cannot tell you who is most attractive among all women. citeturn906563search16turn462465view0turn831014view1

So the clean answer is: **there is objective-ish data on consensus attractiveness, but there is no scientifically credible all-time female GOAT ranking.** The best existing work can rank faces **within a defined dataset**, under defined conditions, with error bars. That is a real result. “Most attractive woman ever” is not.

Mr. White, the useful way to think about it is this:

The serious datasets usually do **not** spit out a magical “most beautiful woman” label. They give you **standardized face images**, **consensus attractiveness ratings**, and often **landmarks**. In SCUT-FBP5500, for example, there are **5,500 frontal neutral faces**, each with a **1–5 attractiveness score from 60 raters**, plus **86 facial landmarks**. So the “trait extraction” is mostly: take geometry, skin cues, age cues, and shape cues, then see which ones predict the consensus rating. citeturn588398view0

The strongest recurring trait cluster in the literature is:

**1. Averageness / facial harmony**  
Faces closer to the population average tend to be rated as more attractive. This is one of the most replicated findings in the field. citeturn681966search8turn530669search11

**2. Symmetry**  
More symmetrical faces are generally rated as more attractive, though newer work suggests symmetry’s effect may be smaller than people think once other cues are accounted for. citeturn681966search8turn530669search8turn681966search18

**3. Female-typical facial shape / femininity**  
For female faces, higher perceived femininity tends to predict higher attractiveness. In plain English, that usually means the shape pattern associated with **larger eyes, fuller lips, higher or more prominent cheekbones, and a smaller nose, chin, and lower-face mass**. A classic feature-metric study found exactly that pattern. citeturn681966search15turn530669search4

**4. Youthfulness**  
Younger-looking faces are usually judged as more attractive, and recent reviews still treat youthful appearance as a persistent component of facial attractiveness. citeturn530669search12turn530669search0

**5. Skin quality**  
Smooth, even, homogeneous skin matters a lot. Skin homogeneity has been shown to increase perceived attractiveness, health, and youth. Reviews also treat skin texture and color/texture quality as major inputs to attractiveness judgments. citeturn681966search4turn681966search7turn530669search1turn530669search11

**6. Facial adiposity**  
Lower facial adiposity, within a normal healthy-looking range, tends to increase attractiveness ratings, especially in female faces. Multiple papers and reviews find a negative relationship between perceived facial adiposity and attractiveness. citeturn681966search15turn681966search3turn681966search19

Where the evidence gets weaker or more mixed:

**Skin color by itself** is less clean than skin quality. Some studies find redness/yellowness or related color cues matter, but at least one large predictor paper found skin color did **not** predict attractiveness once other factors were modeled. That suggests **evenness, texture, radiance, and overall health appearance** are more reliable than a simple “tone = attractive” claim. citeturn681966search15turn681966search22turn530669search3
