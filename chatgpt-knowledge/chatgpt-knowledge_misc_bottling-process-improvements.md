## Answer

Got it, Mr. White. Your current setup is well thought out for batching, but the bottling stage is where your labor and inefficiency stack up. Here’s how to make that step faster, safer, and more consistent — without going into full industrial territory.  

---

### 🧰 **1. Switch to a Food-Grade Transfer Pump**
Your hand pump is the weakest link. Replace it with a **12V food-grade diaphragm pump** or **peristaltic pump**.  
- **Example:** Seaflo 33-Series or Topsflo TD5.  
- These can move 2–3 L/min and can be powered by a small adapter or battery.  
- Add an inline shutoff valve or foot switch for control.  
- Fit the output hose with a short rigid spout (½" OD) to fit your bottle mouths.  

**Result:** 18 liters pumped in 6–9 minutes, hands-free, consistent fill.  

---

### ⚙️ **2. Add an Inline Flow Meter or Stop Timer**
If precision matters, add a **digital flow meter** (0.5–5 L/min) between pump and spout. It counts volume, so you can stop at 1.0 L exactly.  
- Or, time your fill: run the pump for 20 seconds per bottle using a **foot pedal timer**.  

**Result:** consistent volume, less spillage, faster rhythm.  

---

### 🧴 **3. Use a Bottling Wand or Gravity-Fed Spigot Setup**
If you prefer non-electric, a **gravity-fed bottling wand** (like for homebrewing) works beautifully.  
- Place your Cambro on a counter, attach a short hose to a spigot or quick-connect fitting at the base.  
- Insert a bottling wand (spring-tip or self-sealing).  
- It fills from the bottom up—less foam, no splashing.  

**Result:** Cheap, fast, precise, and no spraying.  

---

### 🪣 **4. Modify the Cambro**
- Drill a **bulkhead fitting** near the base of your Cambro (½" NPT).  
- Add a **quick-connect tap** or **spigot** (stainless or HDPE).  
- You can then connect directly to either a gravity setup or your pump hose.  

**Result:** No more lifting, pouring, or pump-priming.  

---

### 🧃 **5. Batch Workflow Optimization**
- **Pre-label** your bottles and caps before you start.  
- **Group batching**: fill all bottles from one batch before starting another (avoid cross cleanup).  
- **Cleanline:** use dedicated food-safe hoses and pump for alcohol; run sanitizer through afterward.

Love that vision, Mr. White. You’re basically describing a **compact rotary (carousel) filler**. Here’s a right-sized, bar-back version you can actually build without going full factory.

# Option A — Rotary “Lazy-Susan” Filler (1 fill head)
**What it is:** A 10–12-position turntable that indexes one step at a time. A single fill nozzle dispenses a fixed volume as each bottle pauses under it.

**Core components (tabletop, ~$900–$1.8k total):**
- 16–18" food-grade **rotary table** (manual index or low-RPM geared motor + detents)
- **Peristaltic pump** (food-safe) with **pinch tubing** and quick-change head  
  - Flow: ~2.5 L/min (1 L ≈ 24 s), self-priming, easy sanitation
- **No-drip bottom-fill nozzle** (or bottling wand) + **anti-foam vent**
- **Photoeye** (or micro-switch) to confirm bottle present
- **Timer/PLC** (very small) to run: stop → fill X seconds → index
- **Bulkhead fitting** near Cambro base → silicone line → pump → nozzle
- **Drip tray** + **guard** (plexi) around the fill station

**Cycle:**
1) Operator loads bottle at Station 1.  
2) Table indexes; bottle arrives under nozzle.  
3) Sensor sees bottle → pump runs **T seconds** (e.g., 23–25 s for 1 L) → stop.  
4) Table indexes; operator unloads filled bottle two stations later; reloads an empty.  
5) Repeat rhythmically.

**Throughput:**
- 1 head @ ~2.5 L/min → **~30 s/bottle** including index = **~120 bottles/hour**  
- Your 18-L batch (≈18 bottles) ≈ **9–10 min** per batch of active filling  
- Six batches (108 bottles) ≈ **~54–60 min** filling time (plus setup/cleanup)

**Why peristaltic?** Gentle on product, accurate with a simple time-fill, and the fluid only touches tubing (cheap to replace, quick to sanitize).

**Trade-offs:** More heads (cost), slightly more plumbing, but **fastest** and easier than a mechanical indexer.
