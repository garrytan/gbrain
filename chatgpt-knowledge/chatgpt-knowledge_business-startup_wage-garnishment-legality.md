# Wage garnishment legality

## Question

My employer sent me a statement on PaychexFlex. My PaychexFlex statement shows that I was owed X amount of money, and then what was actually deposited to my account was like $150 shy of that. I think they're garnishing wages for property damage, but in this situation, is that the correct way to do that?

## Answer

Mr. White — under California law, your employer **cannot lawfully deduct money from your paycheck** for property damage (or other losses like cash shortages, broken equipment, or mistakes) **unless** one of the following applies:  

1. **You authorized it in writing** for a specific, identifiable amount after the damage occurred.  
2. **A court order or garnishment** authorizes it (e.g., child support, tax levy, etc.).  

Even if your employer *believes* you caused damage, they **cannot unilaterally decide** to withhold wages to cover it. The proper procedure would be for them to **pursue reimbursement through civil channels**, not payroll deduction.  

If this $150 deduction wasn’t accompanied by written authorization or a valid garnishment order, it likely violates **California Labor Code §221–224** and **DLSE (Department of Industrial Relations) guidance**, which treat such deductions as illegal wage withholdings.  

**What you can do:**
- Check your **pay stub** on PaychexFlex — it should show any “deductions” or “garnishments.”  
- Ask your employer **in writing** for an explanation of the $150 difference and documentation authorizing it.  
- If they can’t provide one, file a **wage claim** with the **California Labor Commissioner’s Office (DLSE)** for the missing amount.

## Question

Also, in this situation, what's the tax implication?

## Answer

Good question, Mr. White.  

If your employer *illegally or improperly deducted* that $150 from your paycheck, there are a few key tax implications:  

### 1. **The IRS and EDD still see the gross amount as “paid” to you**
Even if your employer withheld $150 without legal basis, **it’s still considered wages paid** for tax purposes.  
- Your **W-2** will likely show the *full gross amount* (the pre-deduction pay) as taxable income.  
- So you’ll pay income and FICA taxes as if you received the full amount — even though you didn’t actually get it in your bank account.  

### 2. **The deduction isn’t tax-deductible to you**
You can’t deduct it as a “loss” or “reimbursement” on your taxes, because it’s not an allowable employee expense under current IRS rules (especially after the 2018 tax law changes).  
In short: it’s treated like you earned it, paid tax on it, and then your employer wrongly kept part.  

### 3. **Employer reporting risk**
If your employer didn’t include the $150 in gross wages (i.e., they reduced your taxable pay before reporting), they’re likely misreporting wages — that’s an issue for both **IRS** and **California EDD** compliance.  

### 4. **Correction if recovered**
If you later recover the withheld wages (e.g., through DLSE claim or employer reimbursement), it’s **not taxable again** — you already paid taxes on it. If they pay you back later, it’s simply repayment of wages owed.  

In short:  
- You’re taxed as if you got all your pay.  
- You can’t offset the improper deduction on your taxes.  
- Your remedy is through the **Labor Commissioner**, not the IRS.

## Question

So is it legal for them to show the full amount even though I wasn't paid it as taxable and then garnish from my net wages?

## Answer

Excellent question, Mr. White — and no, **it’s generally *not* legal** for an employer to handle it that way in California **unless** a *court-ordered garnishment* or *voluntary written authorization* exists.  

Here’s the breakdown:  

---

### **1. Legal Garnishments vs. Unauthorized Deductions**
If it’s a *lawful garnishment* (like for child support, tax levy, or judgment):  
- The employer **must** show your **full gross wages** as taxable.  
- Then, the garnishment is taken **after taxes** from your **net pay**, because the money is still “earned income” under both tax and wage laws.  
✅ That’s proper and legal.  

If it’s an **employer-imposed deduction** (e.g., property damage, lost tools, etc.) without court order or written consent:  
- The employer **cannot** reduce your net pay to collect it.  
- Even if they leave the full taxable amount reported, the act of taking it from your net pay is an **illegal self-help recovery**, violating California Labor Code §§221–224.  
❌ That’s not legal.  

---

### **2. Why the Full Amount Still Appears as Taxable**
Wages are taxable when *earned*, not when actually received.  
So even if the employer wrongly withheld $150, they’re *required* to report your full gross wages for tax purposes — the IRS and EDD treat those as earned income.  
That part (showing it as taxable) is **not the issue**; the issue is **the withholding itself** without authorization.  

---

### **3. Correct Handling**
If the employer truly had a right to recoup the $150, they must:  
- Obtain **a court order**, *or*  
- Get your **written authorization**, specifying the amount and reason, *after* the damage occurred.  

Otherwise, they’re required to pay you the **full wages** and then pursue civil reimbursement separately if they believe you owe them.  

---

### **Bottom line**
- **Showing the full amount as taxable** → fine and required.  
- **Deducting $150 from your net pay for damages** → *illegal* unless backed by court order or voluntary written consent.

## Question

Does the written authorization have to be signed by me?

## Answer

Yes — **absolutely.**  

Under **California Labor Code §§221–224** and **DLSE enforcement policy**, any deduction from wages (other than those required by law like taxes, garnishments, or benefit contributions) **must be expressly authorized in writing by the employee**, and **that authorization must bear the employee’s signature.**  

Here’s the standard the Labor Commissioner enforces:  

---

### **1. Signed authorization is mandatory**
- The authorization must be **specific**, **voluntary**, and **signed by you** — not buried in a general employment agreement or handbook.  
- It must clearly state the **exact amount or formula** and the **reason** (e.g., “$150 deduction for property damage on 10/22/25”).  
- Blanket authorizations (“I agree to any deductions the company deems necessary”) are **invalid** under California law.  

---

### **2. Timing matters**
- The signature must occur **after** the damage or event happens, not preemptively at hire.  
- Employers can’t have you pre-sign a general form at onboarding and later use it to justify deductions.  

---

### **3. Without your signature**
If you never signed such a document, or if the “authorization” was part of a general policy or digital acknowledgment (like a handbook consent in PaychexFlex), **it’s not valid**.  
Any resulting deduction would be treated as an **illegal withholding of earned wages**, and you could claim not just the $150 back, but potentially **waiting time penalties** under **Labor Code §203** if unpaid at termination.

Here’s why this matters legally:  

---

### **1. California treats property damage as the employer’s business risk**
Per **DLSE Opinion Letters** and **Labor Code §§221–224**, an employer **cannot deduct or withhold wages** to cover losses or damage to company property (like a POS tablet, handheld, register, uniform, etc.), **even if you accidentally broke it**, unless:  
- There’s a **court judgment** against you, or  
- You **signed a specific written authorization after the fact**, voluntarily agreeing to pay for that exact damage.  

Anything else — even if the employer believes you’re at fault — is considered an **illegal deduction of earned wages**.  

---

### **2. “Toast handheld” deductions are specifically disallowed**
This is a common scenario in restaurants and bars, and the DLSE has addressed it directly.  
Examples of **illegal deductions** include:  
- Broken handhelds or tablets (like Toast, Square, or Clover).  
- Missing cash drawers or credit card chargebacks.  
- Broken glasses, plates, or uniforms.  

Even if the device was expensive, **the employer assumes that risk** as part of operating the business.  

---

### **3. What you should do next**
You should:  
1. **Check your pay stub** in PaychexFlex to confirm if the $150 is labeled as a “deduction,” “garnishment,” “reimbursement,” or similar.  
2. **Email HR or payroll** asking:  
   - The specific reason for the deduction.  
   - A copy of any **written, signed authorization** you allegedly provided.  
   - Whether a **court order** authorized it.  

If they can’t produce either, the deduction is **illegal**, and you can file a **wage claim** with the **California Labor Commissioner (DLSE)** for reimbursement.

## Question

Also I was separately told they were able to get it replaced under warranty for free

## Answer

That’s an important detail, Mr. White — and it makes their deduction even less defensible.  

Here’s why that matters under California law:  

---

### **1. No actual “loss” occurred**
If the Toast handheld was **replaced under warranty at no cost**, then the employer **didn’t incur any financial loss**.  
Even if they *believed* you caused the damage, they **can’t withhold wages** to “recoup” money they never actually spent.  
At that point, it’s purely punitive — and **punitive deductions are flatly illegal** under **Labor Code §221**.  

The DLSE explicitly states that employers **may not use payroll deductions as punishment** for negligence, accidents, or policy violations. Wage deductions can only offset **actual, documented losses** that the employee **agreed to in writing** after the fact.  

---

### **2. Warranty coverage destroys any claim of “damages”**
If the handheld was replaced under warranty, the employer’s “damages” were $0.  
So even if they had a signed authorization (which it sounds like they don’t), it would still fail the test of being a **valid, lawful deduction**, because there was no genuine financial harm.  

---

### **3. Legal and tax implication**
That $150 should still appear as part of your **gross taxable wages**, and you’re **entitled to be paid it** — regardless of their internal replacement process.  
By withholding it, they’ve likely violated both:  
- **Labor Code §221–224 (illegal wage deduction)**  
- **Labor Code §204 (failure to pay full earned wages on payday)**  

You’d be entitled to **the $150 back**, and possibly **waiting time penalties** if unpaid after your employment ends.
