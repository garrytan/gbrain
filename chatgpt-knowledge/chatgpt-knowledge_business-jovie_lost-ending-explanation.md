These are the rules the writers chose for Season 5:

Desmond’s “time travel” is *not* his body moving through time.

- **Only Desmond’s consciousness jumps.**  
  In *“Flashes Before Your Eyes”* and *“The Constant,”* his mind slips into his past self, then back to present, while his body stays put. The writers highlight this distinction in production notes and the episode text. citeturn1search31turn1search28turn1search7

## What the writers *had in mind early* (2004-ish, before / during Season 1)
From original “think tank” meetings before the pilot was even finished:

- **Core premise was set early:** a survival show **plus** a mystery show about a weird Island. Lindelof/Cuse say they knew early it would be “two shows”: on-Island plot and off-Island flashbacks that emotionally comment on it. citeturn1view2
- **The Island as a “light vs dark / good vs evil” nexus was pitched in the earliest mythology sessions.** That basic spiritual/metaphorical frame was there from day one. citeturn3view0
- **A science/experiment organization and stations were conceived very early** (a proto-Dharma idea, called “Medusa Corporation” in early notes), including why polar bears were on the Island. citeturn3view0

So: **the Island is real, special, tied to a big moral/spiritual conflict, and has scientific stations** — that skeleton is early.

---

## What was *not* planned at the beginning / clearly evolved later
The writers are open about this:

- **They did *not* have the full mythology mapped in Season 1.**  
  Cuse/Lindelof say they didn’t even know if the show would survive, and only started “fashioning the mythology” once it was a hit. They used S1→S2 to build it out in detail. citeturn1view2
- **The Hatch’s exact purpose was not locked on day one.**  
  In the early room, multiple totally different hatch ideas were pitched; Lindelof later “declared” the button/108-minutes concept after S1 was underway. citeturn3view0turn1view2
- **Major later-season pillars like Jacob were *not* part of the early staff’s plan.**  
  A key early writer (Grillo-Marxuach) notes Jacob wasn’t even in the room conversation during the first two seasons. citeturn3view0

So yes: **a lot of specific lore (especially Seasons 4–6 mythology) was invented later**, even though it was built to fit the early “science stations + moral conflict” frame.

---

## When did they decide/plan the ending?
You can separate “ending image” from “ending *story*.”

- **They knew the final *image* very early:**  
  Lindelof says they always knew the show would end with **Jack’s eye closing** (mirroring the pilot’s opening eye). citeturn2search0turn2search2
- **They did *not* know the detailed route to get there early on.**  
  Lindelof says explicitly that knowing the final image isn’t the same as knowing the whole ending structure. citeturn2search0turn1view2
- **They couldn’t fully architect the endgame until ABC gave them a fixed end date in May 2007.**  
  ABC ordered 48 more episodes over three seasons and set the finale for 2010; Cuse said that only then could they “map out the rest of the series in confidence.” citeturn4view0

So: **concept of the end shot early; actual end-arc planning starts in earnest after the May 7, 2007 end-date deal.**

## Question

What do they know if there was gonna be mythical shit from day one like supernatural parts of the island beyond just the shady scientific organization or did that come later?

## Answer

Yeah Mr. White — **the writers knew from day one that the Island had “mythical/supernatural” stuff baked into the concept, not just shady science.** What they *didn’t* know from day one was the *exact form* that mythology would take.

Here’s the sourced breakdown:

- **Day-one think-tank pitch (Feb 24, 2004): the Island was explicitly framed as a mystical / metaphysical place.**  
  Early writer Javier Grillo-Marxuach says that **on the very first day** Lindelof told the room the Island was a *nexus of conflict between good and evil* with a *mysterious force at its core* drawing people there for a “light vs dark” contest. That’s supernatural mythology as a foundational premise. citeturn1view0

- **That same day-one pitch also paired the mystical core with a scientific-experiment layer.**  
  Grillo-Marxuach says Lindelof pitched “The Medusa Corporation” running **scientific stations and polar-bear experiments** *because* of the Island’s special nature. So the show started as **mythic Island + science trying to exploit it**, not one or the other. citeturn1view0

- **J.J. Abrams (co-creator) confirms the earliest series outline was “the island wasn’t just an island.”**  
  In a 2006 interview, Abrams says when ABC first wanted a simple crash-survival show, he pushed a version where the Island was “weirder” and more than a normal island — and that was the show that got greenlit. citeturn2view0

- **What came later was the *specific mythology*, not the fact of mythology.**  
  Even Grillo-Marxuach (who supports the “mythic from day one” claim) notes that while the **core notion was there**, the writers only decided the *exact nature* of many mystical elements “much, much later.” Example he gives: Locke’s “mystical experience” wasn’t defined early. Same pattern applies to things like what the Monster *is*, Jacob/MIB details, protector rules, etc. citeturn1view0

**Bottom line:**  
- **Planned from the start:** the Island is a real place with a supernatural/metaphysical core; a battle of light vs dark; destiny/faith themes — and science organizations poking that bear. citeturn1view0turn2view0  
- **Made up/locked later:** the *technical identities and mechanics* of the supernatural pieces (Smoke Monster origin, Jacob/MIB backstory, candidate system, cork/heart specifics). citeturn1view0
