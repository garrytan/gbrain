## Best data points

### Los Angeles / West LA

A UCLA/PLOS One study looked at **1,354 e-scooter injuries** treated across UCLA Health clinics, ERs, and hospitals from **2014–2020**. After shared scooters rolled out, injuries jumped from **at most 13 per year** before 2018 to **595 in 2018** and **672 in 2019**. The estimated injury rate was **115 injuries per 1 million e-scooter trips**. UCLA compared that with older national benchmarks: **104/million for motorcycles, 15/million for bicycles, 8/million for passenger cars, and 2/million for walking**. citeturn840075view1turn978580view0

That study also found the injuries were not trivial: **29% required advanced imaging, 6% required hospital admission, and 2 people died**. Injured people included riders and non-riders, including pedestrians hit by moving scooters or people tripping over parked scooters. citeturn978580view0turn840075view1

### Helmet use

The helmet situation is exactly as bad as it looks. A CDC investigation in Austin found that **less than 1% of injured dockless scooter riders were wearing helmets**. The CDC specifically noted that helmet use would likely reduce head and brain injury risk, based on bike injury evidence. citeturn840075view3

A 2026 UCLA Health release on scooter collisions reported that researchers observed **about 94% of 193 riders were not wearing helmets**. citeturn346804search9

### NYC

NYC has transportation-injury dashboards, but the public city dataset is less useful for isolating stand-up e-scooters because it groups traffic injury categories and uses hospital discharge data, not exact crash-location data. NYC DOH does explicitly include “e-bikes, mopeds, stand-up scooters and other micromobility devices” in its prevention guidance and recommends helmets and predictable riding with traffic. citeturn840075view0

NYC’s bigger acute problem appears to be the broader **micromobility chaos bucket**: e-bikes, mopeds, high-speed illegal scooters, delivery vehicles, and bike-lane congestion. A current example: on **May 28, 2026**, a high-speed illegal stand-up scooter capable of over **50 mph** reportedly collided head-on with a cyclist on the Queensboro Bridge bike lane, killing both riders. That is not a normal rental scooter scenario, but it shows the failure mode when fast electric devices mix into narrow dense infrastructure. citeturn346804news77turn346804news79

## Answer

Mr. White, yes — **it is statistically defensible to say e-scooters are more injury-prone than bicycles and cars in dense urban use.** It is **directionally defensible, but more nuanced, to say they can have a higher per-trip injury rate than motorcycles.** The key caveat: **motorcycles are still far more fatal when crashes happen.**

## The cleanest comparison

The best LA-area study found:

| Mode | Estimated injuries per 1M trips |
|---|---:|
| **E-scooter** | **115** |
| Motorcycle | 104 |
| Bicycle | 15 |
| Passenger car | 8 |
| Walking | 2 |

That comes from a UCLA/PLOS One study of West LA-area e-scooter injuries. The authors estimated **115 e-scooter injuries per 1 million trips** and explicitly said that was higher than prior national estimates for motorcycles, bicycles, cars, and walking. They also said the e-scooter injury rate was likely an underestimate. citeturn936896view3

But fatalities are different:

| Mode | Fatalities per 100M trips |
|---|---:|
| **E-scooter** | **19** |
| Bicycle | 21 |
| Motorcycle | 537 |

So the right statement is:

> **In dense urban use, e-scooters appear to have motorcycle-like or worse injury rates per trip, but motorcycle crashes are vastly more likely to kill you.**

That is the most accurate framing.

## Your friend’s crash is the classic e-scooter failure mode

What you described is exactly why they are sketchy in cities:

- **Small front wheel hits pothole**
- Front wheel deflects sharply
- Handlebar turns instantly
- Rider has almost no recovery window
- Standing posture launches body sideways/forward
- Face, wrists, clavicle, knees, tib/fib, ankles take the hit

A bike has bigger wheels, a longer wheelbase, more gyroscopic stability, and the rider is seated. A scooter has tiny wheels, twitchy steering, a high standing center of gravity, and almost no ability to absorb road defects. So potholes, plates, cracks, curb cuts, wet paint, grates, and debris are much more catastrophic.

That also explains why a helmet only solves part of the problem. A helmet reduces head-injury risk. It does not protect your face, teeth, wrists, clavicle, knees, tibia, fibula, ankle, or hip.

## The “casualness” is the deadly part

People treat rental scooters like toys because they are app-unlocked, cheap, quiet, and slow-looking. But legally and biomechanically, they are closer to **standing motorcycles for bad pavement** than “walking with wheels.”

The bad behavior you’re seeing is specifically illegal in LA/California:
