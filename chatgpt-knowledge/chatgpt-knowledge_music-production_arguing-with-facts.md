Comment on how people argue like he’s wrong. But facts don’t. And use facts to substantiate.

## Answer

Mr. White,

Some people *argue emotionally* that “theaters are still the cultural default,” that “streaming cheapens cinema,” or that “people miss the communal experience.” But **arguments aren’t the same as evidence.**

**Actual market facts don’t support those claims.**

---

## **U.S. Consumer Behavior**
- **U.S. streaming subscriptions are now in the vast majority of households** (over 85%+), versus **only ~12% attending a movie theater monthly**. Home viewing is no longer fringe—it’s normalized.
- **The average American watches 2–3 hours of streaming per day**, while **the average theater attendance per person is under 3–4 times per year.**  
  Reality: People *watch movies at home constantly* and go to the theater as a rare event.

---

## **Box Office vs. Streaming Economics**
- Adjusted for inflation, **the U.S. box office has fallen dramatically since pre-2020** and never returned to trend growth.
- Meanwhile, **global streaming revenue has surpassed global theatrical revenue**, and has been compounding at double-digit annual growth.

You don’t get those financial curves unless **consumer preference has already shifted at scale**.

---

## **Convenience and Cost**
People don’t choose behaviors by ideology—they choose them by utility:

- A night at the theater for two people is often **$40–$80** when you add tickets, parking, snacks.
- A streaming platform is **$10–$20 per month**, unlimited content, no travel, no schedule, no talking strangers, no crying babies, no overpriced popcorn.

The median consumer optimizes for **convenience + cost**, and that **predictably drives home viewing over theaters**.

---

## **Content Availability**
For decades, the only way to see new movies was theaters. Now:
- Many films debut **direct to streaming**.
- Studios shorten theatrical windows because **home release monetizes faster**.
- Heavy hitters like Apple, Amazon, and Netflix increasingly invest in premium streaming-first releases.

If the “theaters are king” narrative were true, studios wouldn’t compress windows and shift capital toward streaming originals.

---

## **Consumer Choice = Revealed Preference**
People may *say* they love theaters, but economics has a brutal test:

**Where do they actually spend time and money?**

Answer: overwhelmingly at home.

---

### **Bottom Line**
Arguments can be nostalgic or romantic, but **markets measure real preference**, and the market shows:

> **People prefer watching movies at home.**  

You don’t have to like it, but you can’t deny it.
