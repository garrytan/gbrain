## Question

## Answer

It’s likely you’re seeing observant Jewish men heading to or from synagogue for weekday prayers. Many wear a suit and a black hat—often a fedora or wide-brim style—especially in Orthodox and Hasidic communities.  
Today isn’t a major Jewish holiday on the calendar, so it’s probably just regular daily or evening services (Shacharit, Mincha, or Maariv), which require a minyan (quorum of ten men) and often draw groups dressed formally.

## Answer

Many Orthodox and Hasidic Jews treat their clothing as part of the religious practice itself, not just a dress code for an event. A few key points explain why they keep it even in hot weather:

**Religious meaning**  
- **Tradition as worship:** Wearing a dark suit and black hat is seen as a sign of respect before God, like entering a royal court. The discomfort isn’t the point; the consistency and reverence are.  
- **Halachic modesty:** Jewish law encourages men to dress modestly and with dignity, which for many communities means a jacket, long pants, and a hat.

**Identity and continuity**  
- **Uniform of belonging:** The look signals “I’m part of this group,” preserving identity across centuries and countries.  
- **Link to ancestors:** Dressing the same way their great-grandparents did creates a living connection to Jewish history.

**Practical adaptations**  
- Suits and hats are often lightweight wool or linen, more breathable than they appear.  
- They’re used to walking to synagogue (driving is avoided on the Sabbath and often for daily prayers too), so they pace themselves and stay hydrated.

In short, the discomfort is accepted—sometimes even valued—as a small sacrifice for faith, community, and tradition. The goal isn’t personal comfort; it’s honoring something larger than oneself.

Whether God exists is a matter of personal belief. What’s observable is that the clothing functions as a shared symbol and a way to maintain tradition, independent of anyone’s stance on the supernatural.

## Question

Where are the hazard lights on a U-Haul truck? I got a 10-foot U-Haul truck right now I'm driving and I can't find the button for the hazard lights anywhere.

## Answer

On the 10-ft U-Haul (Ford or GMC chassis), the hazard flashers use the **red triangle button** on the steering-column area:

* Look **directly behind or on top of the steering wheel**, right at the base of the turn-signal stalk.  
* It’s a **small red button with a white triangle**, sometimes pulled up like a knob.  
* Pull it out or push it in (varies by model) to turn the hazards on; push/pull again to turn them off.

If you don’t see it on the column, check the **center of the dash near the climate controls or radio**—still a red triangle. It’s never hidden in a glove box or side panel; it’s always within the driver’s reach.
