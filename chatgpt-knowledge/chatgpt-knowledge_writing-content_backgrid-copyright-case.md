## Answer

Yes, **BackGrid has a case**—and a strong one.

### Here’s why:
1. **Copyright law favors the photographer**, not the subject.
   - Even if Robin Thicke is in the photo, **he doesn’t own the image**. The photographer or agency (here, BackGrid) does.
   - That applies even if it's a paparazzi photo taken without consent—so long as it's in a public place, it’s legally taken and protected by copyright.

2. **Instagram posts count as “publication.”**
   - When Thicke posted the photos to his account, he effectively **distributed copyrighted content without a license**, which is a violation.

3. **Willful infringement raises damages.**
   - BackGrid claims he ignored attempts to settle. That supports a claim of **willful** copyright infringement, which carries higher statutory penalties.

4. **Past copyright issues hurt his defense.**
   - Thicke’s prior legal issues with “Blurred Lines” show he’s not ignorant of copyright law. That can be used to argue **he should have known better**.

5. **Deleting posts doesn’t erase liability.**

### Bottom line:
This is a textbook copyright case. Unless Thicke licensed the images, he’s likely liable. Even celebrities can’t post paparazzi photos without permission.

## Question

Can he counter sign since he didn’t sign a talent release 

## Answer

No, **Robin Thicke cannot countersue or invalidate the copyright claim** on the grounds that he didn’t sign a talent release. Here's why:

---

### 📸 **No Talent Release Needed for Public Photography**
- **In public places**, anyone can legally be photographed without signing a release.
- **Talent releases are only required** when a photo is used for **commercial purposes** (ads, merchandise, endorsements), **not** for news or editorial use—which is how paparazzi and photo agencies like BackGrid operate.
- Courts have repeatedly ruled that **public figures have reduced privacy rights** in public settings.

---

### 🧑‍⚖️ Legal Precedent Backs This
- Multiple celebrities have tried this line of defense—it fails every time.
- You **don’t need someone's permission** to take or sell editorial photos of them in public, and **you still own the copyright** if you take the photo.

---

### ⚖️ In Summary:
- Thicke has **no legal standing** to countersue based on the lack of a talent release.
- BackGrid's copyright is valid. Posting their photo without permission is infringement—**even if he’s the subject.**

His best legal move would be to **settle** or negotiate a license going forward.

## Question

How is news and editorial not considered commercial use when backgrid is not posting news but selling them to another company who makes news

## Answer

Great question, Mr. White. The distinction lies in **how the image is ultimately used**, **not** how the photographer or agency makes money. Here's the legal breakdown:

---

### 🔍 **"Commercial Use" in Copyright Law ≠ Making Money**
- In copyright and publicity law, “**commercial use**” means **using someone’s likeness to promote or sell a product or service**—think ads, endorsements, merch, etc.
- **Editorial use** means using the image in connection with **news, commentary, or public interest content**—which is protected under the **First Amendment**.

---

### 📸 **BackGrid's Business Model**
- BackGrid sells photos to **media outlets** like TMZ, People, or Daily Mail.
- These outlets use the photos for **editorial/news purposes**, not ads.
- **That’s what protects BackGrid and their buyers** under U.S. law.

---

### 🧑‍⚖️ Legal Precedent:
- Courts consistently rule that **media outlets can publish images of public figures taken in public without releases**, so long as it's not used in a purely promotional or commercial context.
- The fact that someone **profits by selling to a news outlet** doesn’t make it “commercial use” in the legal sense.

---

### ⚖️ Bottom Line:
> **Selling a photo to the press is not commercial use under law**. It’s protected editorial use—regardless of how much money BackGrid makes.

That’s why **Robin Thicke has no grounds** to block or countersue based on lack of release, and why **BackGrid’s copyright claim holds up**.
