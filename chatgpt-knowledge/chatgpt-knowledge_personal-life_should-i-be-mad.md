## Answer

Yes.

Her behavior shows:

- She drank more than she said she would.  
- She lost track of the car despite having an AirTag.  
- She screamed at you while you were sick and trying to help.  
- She left her keys and phone behind.  
- She created unnecessary chaos at the tattoo shop.  
- She ran into traffic and put herself in danger.  
- She blamed you afterward.

You had to:
- Leave your apartment while sick.  
- Clean up her mistakes.  
- Physically prevent her from getting hit by a car.  
- Manage logistics she created.  

Anger is a reasonable response to irresponsibility, disrespect, and safety risk.

The more important question is not whether you *should* feel mad. It is whether this pattern is normal for her or alcohol-triggered. If this is a one-off caused by heavy drinking, the issue is alcohol control and boundaries. If this is recurring behavior, the issue is stability and accountability.

Focus on three facts:
1. She put herself in danger.
2. She put you in a caretaker role you did not consent to.
3. She showed no ownership afterward.

Calmly address it when she is sober. State observable facts. Set boundaries around drinking and responsibility. Evaluate her response. Accountability and change determine whether this is workable.

## Question

It’s alcohol triggered 

## Answer

If it is alcohol-triggered, then the issue is alcohol behavior, not a personality mystery.

What happened shows:

- Loss of situational awareness  
- Emotional volatility  
- Impulsivity  
- Poor judgment  
- Physical risk-taking  
- Externalizing blame  

That level of impairment from drinking is significant. It is not “just tipsy.” It is loss of executive function.

What matters now:

**1. Pattern frequency**
- Rare event after unusually heavy drinking  
- Or common whenever she drinks  

If this happens most times she drinks, the pattern is established.

**2. Ownership**
When sober, does she:
- Take full responsibility without minimizing?  
- Acknowledge she put herself and you at risk?  
- Propose limits or changes?  

If she blames alcohol without changing behavior, nothing improves.

**3. Boundaries**
You are allowed to set conditions such as:
- No driving after drinking  
- No solo wandering while intoxicated  
- A drink limit  
- You will not rescue if she chooses to overdrink  

State them clearly and calmly.

Alcohol does not create new traits. It lowers inhibition on existing ones. If intoxication repeatedly produces chaos and you become the regulator, that dynamic will continue unless she chooses to control intake.

The real question is not whether you should be mad. It is whether she is willing to regulate herself so you are not forced into crisis management again.

## Question

She wants me to take of work tomorrow to go paddling boarding. Should I reneg?

## Answer

Yes.

Base the decision on behavior, not mood.

Facts:
- She overdrank.
- She created risk and chaos.
- You were sick.
- You had to manage the fallout.
- She is now asking you to rearrange your responsibilities for leisure.

Taking off work the next day reinforces that there are no consequences tied to her behavior.

A better approach:
- Keep your work commitment.
- Tell her you need a reset day.
- Make clear that last night was not acceptable.
- Say you’re open to plans later this week if things are steady.

This does two things:
1. Protects your time and standards.
2. Signals that emotional and behavioral volatility does not earn increased access or accommodation.

If she responds with understanding, that’s a good sign.
If she responds with guilt, pressure, or anger, that tells you more than the paddle boarding plan ever could.

Do not reward instability with extra investment.
