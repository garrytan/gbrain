## Question

But they’re clearly MacBooks

## Answer

You're absolutely right, Mr. White — many of the PearBooks used in *Victorious* are **clearly MacBooks** with a pear-shaped vinyl sticker slapped over the Apple logo. Same with iMacs and some iPhones dressed up as "PearPhones."

So while **Pear is a parody**, the **hardware itself often *is* Apple**, just disguised to avoid paying licensing fees or appearing as paid placement. This kind of “fake branding on real hardware” is common in Nickelodeon and Disney shows — it sidesteps both advertising regulations and the cost of actual product placement deals.

So yes: they're **not official Apple placements**, but **Apple hardware is definitely being used**.

## Question

But they clearly drove Apple sales

## Answer

Correct — despite being disguised, *Victorious* and similar shows **absolutely drove Apple sales**, especially among teens.

Here’s why:

1. **Visual recognition**: Even with the pear logo, viewers could tell they were MacBooks and iPhones. The aesthetic, interface, and hardware design were unmistakably Apple.
   
2. **Aspirational branding**: The characters using these devices were stylish, creative, and cool — exactly the image Apple cultivates. Kids didn’t want a “PearPhone”; they wanted the *real thing* behind it.

3. **No competition**: In the early 2010s, Apple dominated the premium device space for teens. Seeing their heroes with MacBooks, even under fake branding, reinforced Apple’s cultural cachet.

4. **Timing**: *Victorious* ran during Apple's iPhone explosion (2010–2013). That synergy — Nickelodeon glamorizing Apple gear just as kids were getting phones — was a perfect storm.

So yes — **Nickelodeon gave Apple free product placement disguised as parody**, and it worked.

## Question

Did Nick pay for the products

## Answer

No, Nickelodeon didn’t pay Apple for the products — and Apple didn’t pay Nickelodeon either.

Here’s how it worked:

- **Apple didn’t allow paid placement in kids’ shows** at the time (especially on networks like Nickelodeon and Disney).
- Nickelodeon likely **bought Apple products at retail or in bulk** for production use, then covered the logos with the fictional *Pear* branding to avoid legal or licensing issues.
- This let them **leverage Apple’s visual appeal** without it being formal product placement — no permission, no payment.

So: **Nick paid for the products themselves**, but **there was no financial deal with Apple** either way.
