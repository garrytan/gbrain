## Question

I got a $300 speeding ticket in Clackamas County, Portland, Oregon. I live in Los Angeles. If I'm driving, I'm driving in California, and occasionally I drive to Vegas. If I'm flying and renting a car, it's probably going to be in New York or Miami. Most other places I travel, I wouldn't drive. I would just Uber around. I might drive throughout Southern California, possibly even through Northern California, but I don't think I would ever drive north of California. If I was flying, I'd probably be Ubering everywhere. If I never decide to drive in Oregon again, what happens if I don't pay the ticket?

## Answer

Mr. White, here’s the short version:

- **Oregon will suspend your Oregon driving privileges** if you ignore the ticket. You don’t need an Oregon license for them to do that.  
- **California and most other states will honor the suspension** through the Driver License Compact. That means California can suspend *your* California license once Oregon reports the unpaid ticket. If that happens, you can’t legally drive anywhere—Vegas, SoCal, New York rentals, etc.—until it’s cleared.  
- **Collections and credit impact:** The court can send the debt to collections, add late fees, and potentially report it to credit agencies.  
- **Arrest warrant risk:** For a simple speeding citation it’s usually a civil violation, so no warrant just for non-payment, but the DMV consequences are real.

If you truly never pay and never drive, you avoid the license issue but you’d still have a growing debt in collections. The clean way out is either (1) pay the $300, (2) request a hearing or mitigation by mail, or (3) try to get it reduced/paid on a plan. Ignoring it will almost certainly cost more and can block you from renewing your California license.

## Answer

Here’s how it works if you want to fight or reduce this ticket, based on Oregon law and how Clackamas County handles out-of-state drivers:

---

### 1. What You’re Charged With
- **Violation**: 86 → 85 mph in a 55 mph zone is a *Class B speeding violation* under Oregon law (ORS 811.111).  
- **Base fine**: about \$265–\$325, which matches the \$300 you were quoted.

---

### 2. Your Options  
You have to act **within the deadline on the citation (usually 30 days)**.

**A. Pay (no contest)**  
- You mail payment or pay online.  
- It’s reported to your home state (California) and will hit your CA driving record.

**B. Written “Trial by Affidavit” (most common for out-of-state drivers)**  
- You plead “not guilty” or “no contest with explanation” by mail.  
- You can submit a written statement and evidence (photos, diagrams, calibration arguments).  
- The officer submits a written statement.  
- The judge decides without you appearing.  
- If you lose, you pay the fine; if the officer doesn’t respond, it’s usually dismissed.

**C. In-person hearing**  
- You (or a lawyer you hire) appear in Clackamas County traffic court.  
- You can cross-examine the trooper on the radar use, calibration, rear-facing accuracy, etc.  
- If the officer fails to appear, the case can be dismissed.

**D. Attorney / Local Traffic Lawyer**  
- Many Oregon traffic attorneys offer a flat fee (often \$250–\$400) to appear and negotiate for a dismissal or a “violation of basic rule” with a lower speed so it’s a non-moving violation.

---

### 3. Viable Defenses / Mitigation Angles  
- **Radar challenge**: Rear-facing radar while the patrol car is moving *does* exist and is admissible. But you can request proof of calibration and training.  
- **Speedometer inaccuracy**: Hard to win unless you have a shop certification showing it was off.  
- **Need to drive a friend**: Explains circumstances but doesn’t excuse speeding; works only for fine reduction.

---

### 4. Practical Playbook  
1. **Before the deadline**, enter a *not-guilty* plea by mail and request a trial by affidavit.  
2. Ask the court for **discovery**: officer’s radar calibration records, training certs, and the citation notes.  
3. Decide whether to argue technical errors (radar accuracy, pacing, improper signage) or to negotiate a reduction.  
4. If you want the lowest hassle, hire a local traffic attorney—they often get it cut to a non-moving “failure to obey a traffic control device,” which avoids points on your CA record.

---

### Key Takeaway  
Ignoring it risks a suspension that follows you to California. Your best shot to keep your record clean without flying back is a written trial or hiring a local lawyer to appear and negotiate.

## Question

If I do the B trial by affidavit, can I request the radar training and radar calibration stuff?

## Answer

Yes. In Oregon you can ask for discovery even if you choose a written (trial-by-affidavit) hearing.  

**How to do it:**
- After you plead *not guilty* and the court sets a hearing date (even for a written trial), send a **written discovery request** to the prosecutor or the court clerk—Clackamas County typically routes it to the DA’s traffic division.  
- Cite **ORS 135.805–135.873** (Oregon’s discovery statutes) and specifically request:  
  * the officer’s radar/lidar calibration and maintenance records,  
  * the officer’s certification/training for that device,  
  * the officer’s notes or reports for your stop.

Do this as soon as you get a hearing date; give them a clear deadline (e.g., 10 days before the hearing).  

**What to expect:**  
- They must provide those records or explain why not.  
- If they fail to provide required calibration or training proof, you can move to exclude the radar evidence or ask for dismissal.  
- You can reference the missing or late discovery in your written affidavit.

This gives you the same leverage you’d have at an in-person trial, just on paper.
