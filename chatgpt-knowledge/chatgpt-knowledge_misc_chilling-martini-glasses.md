## Question

at the bar can inuse a normal freezer to chill martini glasses

## Answer

Yes, Mr. White. You can use a normal freezer to chill martini glasses. Just make sure:

- They’re **dry** before freezing to prevent ice buildup.
- You place them in for at least **30 minutes** for a proper chill.
- You handle them carefully to avoid cracking from sudden temp changes.

For speed during service, also consider storing a **dozen or so pre-chilled glasses** and rotating them out as needed.

   - Many pros do it between pours for speed citeturn0search4turn0search1.

3. **Avoid thermal shock**  
   - Don’t freeze a hot glass or pour hot liquid into a frozen one—stress causes cracking citeturn0search6turn0search1.

## 🔧 Step-by-step at the bar or home:
- **Option 1: Quick chill with ice-water**  
  1. Fill glass with ice + a bit of water.  
  2. While building drink, let the glass chill.  
  3. Dump contents & pour.

- **Option 2: Pre-chill in freezer**  
  1. After cleaning and drying, chill glasses ≥30 minutes in freezer.  
  2. Freeze spirit too if going full “bone-dry” citeturn0search0turn0search2.  
  3. Avoid hot liquids, sudden shifts.

## ✅ Recommendation for your bar  
- **High-volume setting?** Ice-water chilling is fast and scalable.
- **If space allows and you want presentation points?** Pre-chill glasses in freezer at least 30 min.
- **Best practice:** Combine both—store a few glasses in freezer, top up with ice‑water chilling during peak service.

## Question

bone dry?

## Answer

**"Bone dry"** means a martini with **little to no vermouth** — often just rinsed or even skipped entirely.

### Typical breakdown:
- **Dry martini**: Light vermouth (e.g., 5:1 gin to vermouth)
- **Extra dry**: Just a few drops or a rinse
- **Bone dry**: No vermouth at all — just chilled gin or vodka

Some bartenders swirl vermouth in the glass then dump it, or spritz it in the air over the drink for aroma only. It's all about showcasing the base spirit with zero dilution from modifiers.
