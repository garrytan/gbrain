## Question

Yo dawg, I just read a parking meter that had a max time of 15 minutes. So I put in the first quarter, and it was like 6 minutes. Then I put in the second one, and it was like, oh, 15 minutes. Then I put in the next one, and it was like, oh, 15 minutes. And then I realized, after putting in excessive amounts of money, that it was only 15 minutes. But it wouldn't then at that point give me a refund or anything, because there's no mechanism for the thing to give you money back. What's the legality on that?

## Answer

Mr. White, here’s the deal:

### Why it works that way
- Parking meters are designed with a **maximum time limit** (in your case, 15 minutes) to keep cars turning over.  
- Once you hit that limit, the machine won’t extend beyond it, even if you keep feeding it quarters.  
- They also **don’t give refunds**—by design. The mechanism only accepts coins; there’s no way to spit them back.

### Legality
- In California (and most states), this is perfectly legal.  
- Parking meter rules are covered by the **California Vehicle Code** and local municipal codes. Cities are allowed to set maximum time limits and configure meters so they won’t exceed that limit.  
- As long as the meter is clearly marked (“15 min max”), the city isn’t required to refund you if you insert excess coins.  
- It’s treated like any other vending transaction where you overpay for a capped product (e.g., you pay $1.50 for a $1 soda; the machine isn’t required to refund the extra).

### Bottom line
It feels like a scam, but it’s legal. You’re essentially paying for the *opportunity to park for up to 15 minutes*, not for each coin beyond that. The responsibility falls on the driver to read the posted max-time limit before overfeeding the meter.

## Question

A vending machine isn’t required to refund overpayment?

## Answer

Correct, Mr. White — a vending machine isn’t legally required to refund an overpayment.  

### Why:
- **Uniform Commercial Code (UCC)** and **California Civil Code** say: if you insert money into an unattended vending device, you’re making an *offer to purchase*. The “price” is whatever the machine sets.  
- If you overpay (say the soda costs $1.25 and you put in $2), the machine can either give you change *if it’s designed to* or simply keep the excess. There’s no general legal duty to refund unless the machine falsely advertises a price.  
- Parking meters are a special case of this — they’re explicitly capped by **municipal code**. Once you hit max time, any extra payment is just wasted. The city is not obligated to return it.

### Exceptions:
- If a machine malfunctions (e.g., eats your money and gives nothing), you can sometimes claim a refund under consumer protection rules.  
- But with overpayment, courts treat it as **your choice**, since the limit or price is posted.

## Question

So if a soda is 1.50 and I put 2 1 dollar bills in. It can just take the extra 50 cents?

## Answer

Exactly, Mr. White. If the soda is listed at **$1.50** and you feed the machine **$2**, the machine is *not legally required* to give you $0.50 back **unless** it’s advertised or designed to give change.  

Most modern vending machines do return change (because otherwise they’d get constant complaints), but the **law doesn’t force them to**. The transaction is still valid even if you overpay, because you got the item you agreed to buy at the posted price.  

The only time you’d have a legal claim is if:  
- The machine said it gives change and didn’t.  
- Or it failed to give you the product at all (that’s a malfunction, not overpayment).  

So yeah — legally speaking, the machine can just pocket the extra 50 cents.
