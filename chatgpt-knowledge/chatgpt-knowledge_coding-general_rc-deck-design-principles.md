## Answer

Mr. White, the world-class Jovie RC deck should look like a **YC-style fundraising deck that is aggressively plain, huge, readable, and proof-oriented**.

Michael Seibel’s core frame is: be clear and concise, explain what you do in simple language, cover what you do / team / traction / unique insight / market size / ask, and make the slides boring enough that the investor listens to you instead of studying the design. citeturn607928view1turn607928view0

Jovie’s official docs currently describe it as “the all-in-one platform for musicians to manage their career, connect with fans, and grow their audience,” with features around profiles, releases, smart links, audience tools, chat/AI, tips, and analytics. citeturn884748view0 The deck should compress that into a much simpler investor sentence:

# Jovie is the AI manager for music artists.

That is the headline. Everything else proves it.

## Design rules

Use **10–12 slides max**. White or black background. One font. No decorative design system. No tiny screenshots. No paragraphs.

Each slide gets:

**1 big headline**  
**1 proof point or visual**  
**1 short supporting line max**

Recommended type sizes:

| Element | Size |
|---|---:|
| Slide headline | 52–72 pt |
| Supporting line | 30–40 pt |
| Metric | 72–110 pt |
| Screenshot labels | 24–32 pt |
| Footer/source text | 12–16 pt |

The screenshot principle is exactly right: a product shot on the cover proves, instantly, that **this is software**, **it exists**, and **it is music-related**. That removes cognitive risk before the investor hears the pitch.

## The deck

### 1. Cover

**Purpose:** Make the company impossible to misunderstand in 5 seconds.

**Layout:**  
Left side: text.  
Right side: large product shot, ideally showing an artist profile, audio player, fan/chat interaction, or AI manager interface.

**Copy:**

**Meet Jovie**  
**The AI manager for music artists**

Subline:  
**Artists use Jovie to grow fans, manage releases, and run their career.**

No logo-only intro. No abstract mission. No “future of music” language.

---

### 2. What Jovie does

**Purpose:** Give the investor the clean product model.

**Copy:**

**Jovie helps artists do the work a manager would do.**

Then three plain words or phrases:

**Plan releases**  
**Talk to fans**  
**Grow revenue**

This slide should feel almost too simple. Seibel’s YC guidance is to start with what the company does in the simplest possible language and avoid making the idea sound overly impressive. citeturn607928view1

---

### 3. Specific example

**Purpose:** Make the product concrete.

**Copy:**

**A 50k-listener artist releases a single.**

Jovie helps them:

**Build the release page**  
**Message fans**  
**Track what worked**

Use a real example if you have one. Even better:

**“Artist X used Jovie to launch Y and got Z fan actions in N days.”**

This is the Airbnb-style “specific example” slide. It turns “AI manager” from concept into a workflow.

Jovie should be pitched as software that performs that loop.

## Answer

Mr. White, the disconnect is economics.

The dangerous version is:

**“We sell $300/month software to artists.”**

The stronger version is:

**“Jovie replaces creative-business labor. Artists are the first operating surface. Managers, labels, agencies, and media companies are the economic buyers.”**

The company should price like an operator, not like a creator tool.

# The core answer

Jovie should become:

## **The AI operator for cultural businesses, starting with music artists.**

The universal loop is:

**Detect signal → identify opportunity → create asset → launch action → measure result → improve the next action**

For an artist, that looks like:

**No merch → designs generated → storefront launched → fans messaged → revenue collected**

For Disney, Netflix, a sports team, a label, or a media brand, it looks like:

**Viral moment detected → brand-safe creative generated → approval routed → post/campaign launched → performance measured**

Same underlying product. Different buyer. Different price point.

# Your pricing concern is correct

A $299/month artist plan equals **$3,588/year**. Spotify says the **100,000th-highest-earning artist generated more than $7,300 in Spotify royalties in 2025**, and more than a third of artists generating $10,000+ were DIY or began DIY. That means $299/month can make sense for a narrow professional artist segment, but it is too expensive as the default mass-market artist motion. citeturn466012view1

So the deck should never imply:

**“Millions of artists will all pay us $299/month.”**

The credible version is:

**“Artists use it. Businesses pay for it.”**
