## Question

Yo, so I'm trying to map the TV, um, what there's, um, what do we think the width of the studs are, and what do we think the distance between them is?

## Answer

Mr. White, standard wood-stud walls use nominal 2×4s (actual 1.5″ thick × 3.5″ wide) set 16″ on center.  

- **Stud width** (across the face): 3.5″  
- **Stud thickness** (depth into the wall): 1.5″  
- **Center-to-center spacing**: 16″ (so about 12.5″ between the inside faces of adjacent studs)  

Some walls (especially newer or commercial) use 24″ OC or metal studs (≈1″ channel width), so always verify with a stud-finder.

## Question

What about in a building that was built in 1927?

## Answer

Mr. White, in a 1927 wood-frame building you’ll typically find:

- **Stud size**: Nominal 2×4 lumber (actual ≈1.5″ thick × 3.5″ wide).  
- **Spacing**: Often 24″ on-center for exterior walls and some non-bearing partitions; load-bearing or interior walls may be 16″ OC.  
- **Wall assembly**: ¾″ of plaster over ⅜–½″ wood lath strips (about 1″ wide) nailed to the studs, so you’re looking at ~1.25″ of wall before you hit the stud face.  

Use a deep-scan stud-finder (or rim joist/attic access) to verify spacing; older framing can vary from bay to bay.

- **Span a blocking board**

## Question

Would using toggle bolts on the right side and a stud on the left side be an option?

## Answer

Mr. White, you can—but it’s a compromise:

- **Stud side**: rock-solid.  
- **Toggle side**: only as strong as your anchor’s rating (often 50–100 lb shear per toggle).  

A 65″ TV + bracket often loads each bolt with 80–100 lb in shear/pull-out. If you go toggle on one side:

1. Use **metal snap-toggles** (not plastic) rated ≥150 lb each.  
2. Install **two toggles** on that side (spread vertically) so the load’s shared.  
3. Verify the bracket manufacturer OKs a single-stud + toggle install.

**Better**: add a horizontal blocking board or switch to a sliding-rail bracket so both ends hit studs. Toggle+stud can work, but only if you over-engineer the toggle side.

## Question

What about if I mount it only using one stud, but place the stud center on the mount?

## Answer

Mr. White, you can’t safely hang a standard two-stud bracket on a single stud—even if you center it—because the TV’s weight and moment will twist the mount out of the wall.  

If you really need a one-stud solution, you must:

- Switch to a **single-stud TV rail** (vertical channel) that uses **3–4 heavy-duty lag screws** spaced 8–12″ apart into that one stud.  
- Use at least **3″–3½″ exterior-grade lag bolts**, pre-drilled, to get full thread engagement.  
- Confirm the rail/bracket is **rated for your TV’s weight** and torque.  

Anything less (two lag bolts, a one-stud two-bolt mount) risks pull-out or rotation. For a 65″ TV, the rail-style single-stud mount is the only safe one-stud option.

Here’s how to tell:

---

### 🔨 **Tap Test**
- **Hollow + dull thud** → wood stud or lath-backed plaster
- **Solid + sharp clack** → brick or concrete
- **Metallic ping** → steel stud or conduit

---

### 🧲 **Magnet Test**
- Stick a strong magnet around the hole:
  - **Sticks everywhere** = metal stud or metal lath
  - **Sticks only in patches** = metal lath over something else
  - **No stick at all** = wood or masonry

---

### 🪚 **Drill Test**
Use a fresh 1/8″ drill bit:
- **Goes in with gradual resistance, yellow/tan shavings** = wood
- **Drills easy, then hits metal** = metal lath + wood or metal stud
- **Dusty white powder + very hard** = concrete
- **Red/orange gritty dust** = brick

---

### 🔍 Likely Case for 1927 Building:
You’re probably hitting **metal lath and plaster over wood studs**.  
The metal edges = expanded steel mesh. The center wood = actual stud.
