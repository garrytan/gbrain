## Question

I filed a trademark opposition. I attempted to settle but we were unable to. we have a discovery conference coming up. I can not afford a lawyer to handle the conference.  

Im going to withdraw the opposition because i cant afford to litigate. what are my options to oppose again in the future if i do? what reason should i give for withdrawing

## Answer

I'm not a lawyer, but in general, if you're thinking about managing trademark oppositions or other legal procedures on your own, it's essential to know your rights, responsibilities, and the consequences of your actions. Here are some points to consider based on your situation:

1. **Withdrawing Your Opposition**: 
    - If you choose to withdraw your opposition, the trademark office will generally accept your withdrawal and allow the application process for the other party to proceed.
    - If the trademark is ultimately registered, you might find it more challenging to oppose it later, as you'll then be dealing with a registered trademark rather than an application. However, depending on the jurisdiction, you might still have post-registration remedies such as cancellation proceedings. 

2. **Reason for Withdrawing**:
    - You don't always have to provide a detailed reason for your withdrawal. You can often simply state that after careful consideration, you have decided not to proceed with the opposition. 
    - However, if you're considering opposing again in the future, it might be prudent not to reveal too much about your reasons, as this can be used against you in future litigation or opposition.

3. **Opposing Again in the Future**:
    - Depending on the jurisdiction and specific rules of the trademark office, there might be restrictions on refiling an opposition on the same grounds once you've withdrawn it.
    - Even if you can refile, you may be at a disadvantage if the mark has since been registered.
    - Remember that if the mark gets registered, you might still have other remedies available, like seeking cancellation of the registration on specific grounds. But each remedy has its own set of requirements and challenges.

4. **Consider Mediation or Alternative Dispute Resolution (ADR)**:
    - Before withdrawing, see if the other party is willing to engage in mediation or another form of ADR. This can be a less costly way to resolve the dispute.
    - Some trademark offices even offer mediation services, which might be worth exploring.

5. **Pro Bono or Low-Cost Legal Assistance**:
    - Look into local legal aid organizations or law schools that might provide pro bono or low-cost assistance for trademark disputes.
    - Some professional associations related to intellectual property law may also have resources or offer clinics where individuals can get advice.

6. **Document Everything**:
    - Keep detailed records of all your communications and actions related to the opposition. This will be important if you decide to re-oppose in the future or if any other legal issue arises.

Lastly, although you can't afford a lawyer for the discovery conference, it might still be beneficial to at least consult with one to discuss the implications of withdrawing your opposition and your potential future options. They can provide specific guidance tailored to your situation and jurisdiction.

## Question

do i need this line: and no agreement, understanding, or promise made between the parties has influenced this choice.

## Answer

No, you don't necessarily need that line. The line "and no agreement, understanding, or promise made between the parties has influenced this choice" is often included to clarify that the decision to withdraw wasn't a result of any backdoor dealings or understandings between the parties. It's a way to ensure transparency.

However, if you're not comfortable with it or if it doesn't apply to your situation, you can absolutely exclude it from your withdrawal notice. Removing the line won't affect the main purpose of the letter, which is to notify the Trademark Trial and Appeal Board of your intention to withdraw the opposition.
