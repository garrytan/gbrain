# Estate Sale and Inheritance Tax

## Question

If I sell a house in the name of an estate of my uncle who died, then I'm assuming I would, the profits would go into the estate's bank account, and then if I were to inherit the estate, I would then inherit that money and not pay taxes on it. If I were to inherit the house and then sell the house in my name, I'm assuming that the house is an inheritance which is tax-free, but then when I sell the house, I pay taxes on it. Is that correct, or am I wrong? I am a resident of California, but my uncle was a resident of Nevada and the house is in Nevada and the estate is based in Nevada.

## Answer

You're mostly right, Mr. White. Here's a breakdown for clarity, based on federal and California/Nevada-specific rules:

### 1. **Selling Through the Estate**
- **Estate sells the house.**
- Proceeds go into the **estate’s bank account**.
- After debts and expenses, **you inherit the net proceeds**.
- **You pay no income tax** on the inheritance.
- **The estate may owe capital gains tax** if the sale price exceeds the **stepped-up basis** (value at date of death), but usually this is minimal if sold soon after death.

### 2. **Inheriting the House, Then Selling It**
- You **inherit the house at a stepped-up basis** (its fair market value on your uncle’s date of death).
- If you sell it for **that amount or close to it**, **no capital gains tax**.
- If it appreciates **after** you inherit it and **you sell it for more than the stepped-up basis**, you owe **capital gains tax** on the difference.

### Summary:
| Scenario                       | Taxable?                          |
|-------------------------------|-----------------------------------|
| Estate sells house, you get cash | **No tax for you** (estate may owe minimal gains) |
| You inherit house, then sell   | **Capital gains** on appreciation *after* date of death |
| You inherit either way         | **No inheritance tax** (no estate tax unless estate > ~$13.6M) |

**State-specific note:** Neither California nor Nevada has an inheritance tax.

## Question

If, for example, someone inherited one billion dollars in the state of California, how much taxes would they pay?

## Answer

If someone inherits $1 billion in California in 2025, the tax implications are as follows:

### Federal Estate Tax

- **Exemption**: $13.99 million per individual in 2025.
- **Tax Rate**: 40% on amounts exceeding the exemption.

**Calculation**:

- **Taxable Estate**: $1,000,000,000 - $13,990,000 = $986,010,000
- **Estate Tax Due**: $986,010,000 × 40% = $394,404,000

This tax is paid by the estate before distributing assets to beneficiaries.

### California State Taxes

- **Inheritance Tax**: None. California does not impose a state inheritance tax.
- **Estate Tax**: None. California does not have a state estate tax.

### Beneficiary Tax Implications

- **Inheritance**: Not considered taxable income; beneficiaries do not pay income tax on inherited assets.
- **Capital Gains**: If inherited assets (e.g., real estate, stocks) are sold, capital gains tax applies only to the appreciation from the asset's value at the time of inheritance (stepped-up basis).

**Example**: If you inherit a property valued at $5 million and later sell it for $5.5 million, you'd owe capital gains tax on the $500,000 gain.

**Summary**:

- The estate pays approximately $394.4 million in federal estate taxes.
- Beneficiaries in California receive their inheritance tax-free.
- Future taxes may apply if inherited assets appreciate and are sold.

For estates of this magnitude, consulting with an estate attorney or tax advisor is advisable to explore strategies like trusts or charitable donations to potentially reduce tax liabilities.
