Under California law, **the claim outcome is irrelevant to license suspension**. What matters is **whether you were insured at the moment of the accident**.

## California suspension rules (facts)
- California uses **electronic insurance verification** (DMV + insurers).
- If an accident occurs and **coverage was not in force at that exact time**, the DMV can treat it as **uninsured operation**, even if you bought insurance later the same day.
- A denied claim does **not** cause suspension by itself.
- **Driving uninsured at the time of loss does.**

## Your situation
- Accident: **3:00 PM PT**
- Coverage began: **3:25 PM PT**
- That is a **hard gap**.

If the accident time is **provable** as before 3:25 PM PT, the DMV can:
- Suspend your license (typically **1 year**, mitigable)
- Require **SR-22** filing
- Impose reinstatement fees

## When suspension is avoided
You avoid suspension **only if one of these is true**:
1. **The accident is never reported** to DMV (by police, insurer, or other party), or
2. The time of loss is **not provable** and cannot be fixed before coverage, or
3. No one makes an uninsured motorist claim or files an SR-1 triggering DMV review

## Critical practical point
Insurers often **do not report pre-bind accidents** if:
- No claim is pursued
- No third-party injury/property claim is made
- You do not affirmatively state uninsured operation

But if a police report or third party exists, **DMV usually finds out**.

## Bottom line
- **Coverage gap = suspension risk**
- **Claim denial ≠ suspension**
- **Proof of uninsured operation = suspension**
- Silence and non-pursuit often matter more than arguing coverage
