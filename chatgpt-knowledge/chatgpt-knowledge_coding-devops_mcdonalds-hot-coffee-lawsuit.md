## Answer

In the famous **McDonald’s “hot coffee” lawsuit** (formally *Liebeck v. McDonald’s Restaurants*), the defendant was **McDonald’s itself as a corporate entity**, not an individual franchise owner. The suit was brought against **McDonald’s Restaurants, P.T.S., Inc. and McDonald’s International, Inc.** — corporate McDonald’s entities — on a product liability and negligence theory after Stella Liebeck suffered severe burns from coffee purchased at a McDonald’s drive-thru in Albuquerque, New Mexico. citeturn0search1turn0search6

Franchisees were not the primary named defendants in the landmark litigation; rather, the case focused on **McDonald’s corporate policies and product standards** (specifically how hot the coffee was served) and whether those standards made the product unreasonably dangerous. citeturn0search1

## Answer

Under U.S. tort law (including California), **both a franchisor and a franchisee *can* be liable**, but **who actually gets sued—and who pays—depends on control**.

### How courts decide liability (the short, factual version)

1. **Franchisor liability (e.g., McDonald’s corporate)**  
   A franchisor is liable when the injury is tied to **corporate-mandated policies or products**, such as:
   - Product design or formulation  
   - Required temperatures, recipes, packaging  
   - National safety standards or training manuals  

   In *Liebeck*, the coffee temperature was **set by entity["company","McDonald's","global fast food company"] corporate policy**, which is why **corporate McDonald’s was sued and held liable**.

2. **Franchisee liability (the local owner/operator)**  
   The franchisee is liable when the harm results from **local operational negligence**, such as:
   - Failure to clean spills  
   - Poor maintenance  
   - Employee misconduct  
   - Deviating from required procedures  

3. **Both can be sued simultaneously**  
   Plaintiffs often sue **both**, then let the court allocate fault. This is common because:
   - The franchisee controls day-to-day operations  
   - The franchisor controls branding, products, and system rules  

### Key legal test courts use
- **Degree of control**  
  The more control the franchisor exerts over the specific activity that caused harm, the more likely it is to be liable.

### Practical reality
- Plaintiffs usually target **corporate** because:
  - Deeper pockets  
  - Centralized policies  
  - Easier to prove systemic negligence  

### Bottom line
- **Liebeck** → Corporate McDonald’s liable  
- **Slip-and-fall, local error** → Franchisee liable  
- **Corporate rule causes injury** → Franchisor liable  
- **Gray area** → Both get sued

## Answer

**Outcome of the McDonald’s hot coffee case (*Liebeck v. McDonald’s Restaurants*):**

- **Jury verdict (1994):**
  - **$200,000 compensatory damages** to Stella Liebeck  
    - Reduced to **$160,000** for 20% comparative fault
  - **$2.7 million punitive damages** against **entity["company","McDonald's","global fast food company"]**  
    - This equaled ~2 days of McDonald’s coffee sales

- **Post-trial reduction by judge:**
  - Punitive damages reduced to **$480,000**
  - Total judgment: **~$640,000**

- **Final resolution:**
  - The case **settled confidentially** for **less than $640,000** (exact amount not public)

**Key facts that drove the verdict (not opinion):**
- Coffee was served at **~180–190°F**
- McDonald’s had **700+ prior burn complaints**
- Burns were **third-degree**, requiring skin grafts
- Plaintiff initially asked only for **medical costs (~$20,000)**
