## Answer

I’ll separate this into practical insurance paths, likely out-of-pocket costs, and what to do today. Since this is California/insurance-specific, I’m checking the current rules rather than relying on memory.

## Answer

Mr. White, given your facts, **this should be his insurance problem, not yours**. Your car was parked, unoccupied, and hit by another driver. Since he also has GEICO, it should be administratively easier, though GEICO still needs to open a **separate liability claim against his policy**.

## Best path

### 1. File against **his GEICO liability coverage**
Tell GEICO you want to file a **third-party property damage claim** against the BMW driver’s policy.

This usually means:

| Item | Likely outcome |
|---|---|
| Deductible | **$0** |
| Repairs | Paid by his liability coverage |
| Rental car / loss of use | Should be covered if your car is not safely drivable |
| Rate impact | Should be minimal or none if GEICO codes you as not-at-fault |
| Delay risk | Higher than using your own collision coverage |

California minimum property-damage liability is currently **$15,000**. Liability insurance pays for damage to someone other than the policyholder. citeturn900328search3

GEICO’s own claim guidance says when you are not at fault, you file with the at-fault driver’s insurer, and their liability coverage should cover vehicle repairs and other losses. citeturn900328search15

## Your other path: use your own collision coverage

This only exists if your policy includes **collision**. “Minimum insurance” often means liability-only, so check.

If you have collision:

| Item | Likely outcome |
|---|---|
| Deductible | You pay your deductible upfront, often $500–$1,000 |
| Repairs | Faster approval through your own policy |
| Deductible recovery | GEICO may reimburse later through subrogation |
| Rental | Only covered if you bought rental reimbursement |
| Rate impact | Should be coded not-at-fault, but still confirm |

GEICO says collision can apply whether you are at fault or not, but you are responsible for your selected deductible. citeturn900328search6 GEICO also says after settling your claim, it may seek to recover your deductible and its payment from the negligent party or their insurer. citeturn900328search20

## What it likely costs you out of pocket

### If his GEICO accepts liability
**Likely $0 out of pocket**, assuming damage is under his property-damage limit and GEICO pays the shop directly.

You may temporarily pay for:

- towing, if needed
- rental, if GEICO delays authorization
- storage, if the car is sitting somewhere charging fees

Keep every receipt.

### If you use your own collision
Likely **your deductible upfront**. Common range: **$500–$1,000**. You may get it back later.

### If he has only minimum property-damage coverage and repairs exceed $15,000
Then the problem becomes coverage limits. Options:

1. Use your own collision if you have it.
2. Ask GEICO whether he has higher property-damage limits.
3. Pursue him personally for the gap.
4. If your car is totaled, negotiate the actual cash value.

## Question

## Answer

Mr. White, **yes, you can probably wait until morning** if all of this is true:

- Your car is parked somewhere legal and safe.
- It is not leaking fluid.
- No sharp debris is creating an immediate hazard.
- The car is not blocking traffic, a driveway, or the restaurant.
- It is not in a tow-away zone.
- You already have the police report/event number or officer info.
- You have photos/videos of the damage and scene.

If the car is unsafe to drive, leave it parked and call GEICO in the morning. If it is at risk of being towed or vandalized, call GEICO’s 24/7 claims line tonight and ask for a tow.

## Payment: will you pay upfront?

### If GEICO accepts his liability
Usually **GEICO pays the repair shop directly** after the estimate is approved.

You generally should **not** need to front the full repair bill.

Typical flow:

1. You open the claim against **his GEICO policy**.
2. GEICO inspects the car or reviews shop photos.
3. GEICO approves an estimate.
4. The shop repairs the car.
5. GEICO pays the shop directly.
6. You pay **$0 deductible** because it is his liability claim.

You might only pay out-of-pocket temporarily for things like:

- towing, if you arrange it yourself before approval
- rental car, if GEICO has not authorized it yet
- emergency parking/storage
- small incidental costs

Keep receipts.

### If you use your own collision coverage
Then you may pay your **deductible upfront**, likely $500–$1,000 depending on your policy, and GEICO may reimburse it later after they recover from the at-fault driver’s side.

Since he also has GEICO, they may streamline it, but still ask them to handle it as a **third-party claim under his policy**.

## Do not drive it if

- the tire rubs the wheel well

## The two normal workflows

### Option A — Use a GEICO network shop / Auto Repair Xpress

This is usually the least annoying path.

**Flow:**

1. File claim against the BMW driver’s GEICO policy.
2. GEICO gives you approved shops nearby.
3. You schedule the car at one of those shops.
4. Shop + GEICO handle the estimate.
5. Shop finds hidden damage and sends GEICO a supplement.
6. GEICO pays the shop directly.
7. You pick up the car.

**Pros:**

- Fastest / least back-and-forth.
- GEICO coordinates directly with the shop.
- GEICO says Auto Repair Xpress repairs are guaranteed for as long as you own the vehicle. citeturn403912search0
- Rental may be easier if authorized.

**Con:**

- You are using their preferred network, so quality depends on the specific shop.

### Option B — Use your own body shop

Also allowed.

**Flow:**

1. Open claim with GEICO.
2. Tell GEICO: “I’m using my own shop.”
3. GEICO may do a photo estimate, inspection, or send an adjuster.
4. Your shop writes its estimate.
5. GEICO compares/approves the covered repair amount.
6. Shop starts repairs.
7. If the shop finds hidden damage, it submits a **supplement** to GEICO.
8. GEICO pays the shop directly or sends payment to you/the shop depending on how they process it.

**Pros:**

- You control the shop choice.
- Better if you already trust a collision shop.

**Con:**

- More coordination.
- If your shop charges above GEICO’s approved labor rates or insists on OEM parts GEICO does not approve, you could have a dispute or possible out-of-pocket gap.

## Do you need to get a quote first?

Usually **no**, you do not need to run around getting quotes before opening the claim.

The better order is:

1. **Open the claim first.**
2. Get the claim number.
3. Ask GEICO what inspection method they want:
   - photo estimate
   - GEICO inspection location
   - network shop inspection
   - your chosen shop submitting estimate/photos

GEICO says after reporting the claim, you can schedule an inspection, do a photo estimate, schedule only an estimate, or schedule repair through a GEICO network shop. citeturn403912search5
