Using the best arguing to convince tactics explain why raising minimum wage selectively by industry harms almost everyone it’s intended to help.

## Answer

Here’s how to break it down in a way that’s persuasive, logical, and hard to dismiss:  

---

**1. It kills entry-level jobs in that industry**  
When you target just one sector—like hotels and airports—with a much higher wage floor, employers there face a sudden labor cost spike their competitors in other industries don’t. Businesses respond by:  
- Cutting hours or staff  
- Automating roles faster (kiosks, app-based check-ins)  
- Outsourcing tasks to lower-wage contractors  

That means fewer openings for the very people the law claims to help—new entrants, part-timers, or those without formal training.

---

**2. It makes the industry avoid local hires**  
If an LA hotel has to pay $30/hr but an Orange County one pays $17–20/hr, expansion, investment, and job growth move outside the city limits. Businesses will schedule more work outside LA or relocate altogether, shrinking the local job pool.

---

**3. It punishes small operators the hardest**  
Big hotel chains can absorb higher wages by raising rates or redistributing costs across hundreds of properties. Smaller, independent hotels can’t—so they’re more likely to cut service, sell, or shut down entirely. The corporate giants win, mom-and-pop owners lose.

---

**4. It inflates prices for customers—hurting the workers, too**  
Higher labor costs get passed to guests through higher room rates, food prices, and fees. That kills tourism volume, which reduces tips and hours for the same employees the policy is supposed to benefit.

---

**5. It creates resentment between industries and workers**  
If you’re making $18/hr in retail and see a hotel housekeeper making $30/hr for similar skill/effort, morale drops. Other sectors push for matching hikes, leading to a wage spiral without productivity gains—fueling inflation and eroding the real value of those raises.

---

**6. It risks political blowback and reversals**  
Because it’s selective and not broad-based, it’s easier for coalitions to challenge the law (as they’re doing now). Workers may celebrate the promise of $30/hr, only to see it paused, overturned, or watered down—creating instability instead of security.

---

**In short:**  
Selective industry hikes distort the local labor market, accelerate automation, and drive jobs away. They help a fraction of workers in the short term but cost opportunities

## Question

Explain how it raises the skill required for an industry because skilled workers fight for non skilled positions 

## Answer

When wages jump in one industry, people with more skills than the job requires start applying—because the pay is suddenly better than their own field. Employers then pick those higher-skilled applicants, raising the bar for hiring.  

That means jobs that used to be entry-level now demand more experience, shutting out the lower-skilled workers the raise was supposed to help.
